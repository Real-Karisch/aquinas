      Super Psalmo 38 Psalm 38 Custodiam vias meas I will take heed to my ways 38:1 In finem, pro Idithun. Canticus David. 38:2 Dixi: ‘Custodiam vias meas: ut non delinquam in lingua mea. Posui ori meo custodiam, cum consisteret peccator adversum me.’ 38:3 Obmutui, et humiliatus sum, et silui a bonis; et dolor meus renovatus est. 38:4 Concaluit cor meum intra me; et in meditatione mea exardescet ignis. 38:1 Unto the end, for Jeduthun, a song of David. 38:2 I said, “I will take heed to my ways that I sin not with my tongue. I have set a guard to my mouth, when the sinner stood against me.” 38:3 I was dumb, and was humbled, and kept silence from good things and my sorrow was renewed. 38:4 My heart grew hot within me and in my meditation a fire shall flame out. 38:5 Locutus sum in lingua mea: Notum fac mihi, Domine, finem meum, et numerum dierum meorum quis est, ut sciam quid desit mihi. 38:5 I spoke with my tongue: “O Lord, make me know my end and what is the number of my days, that I may know what I lack.” 38:6a Ecce mensurabiles posuisti dies meos, et substantia mea tamquam nihilum ante te. 38:6a Behold, you have made my days measurable, and my substance is as nothing before you. 38:6b Verumtamen universa vanitas, omnis homo vivens. 38:6b Indeed, all things are vanity, every man living. 38:7 Verumtamen in imagine pertransit homo; sed et frustra conturbatur: thesaurizat, et ignorat cui congregabit ea. 38:7 Surely man passes as an image: yea, and he is disquieted in vain. He stores up, and he knows not for whom he shall gather these things. 38:8 Et nunc quae est exspectatio mea: nonne Dominus? Et substantia mea apud te est. 38:8 And now what is my hope? Is it not the Lord? And my substance is with you. 38:9 Ab omnibus iniquitatibus meis erue me: opprobrium insipienti dedisti me. 38:10 Obmutui, et non aperui os meum, quoniam tu fecisti; 38:11 amove a me plagas tuas. A fortitudine manus tuae ego defeci in increpationibus: 38:12 propter iniquitatem corripuisti hominem. Et tabescere fecisti sicut araneam animam ejus: verumtamen vane conturbatur omnis homo. 38:9 Deliver me from all my iniquities; you have made me a reproach to the fool. 38:10 I was dumb, and I opened not my mouth, because you have done it. 38:11 Remove your scourges from me. The strength of your hand has made me faint in rebukes. 38:12 You have corrected man for iniquity. And you have made his soul to waste away like a spider: surely in vain is any man disquieted. 38:13 Exaudi orationem meam, Domine, et deprecationem meam; auribus percipe lacrimas meas. Ne sileas, quoniam advena ego sum apud te, et peregrinus sicut omnes patres mei. 38:14 Remitte mihi, ut refrigerer priusquam abeam et amplius non ero. 38:13 Hear my prayer, O Lord, and my supplication; give ear to my tears. Be not silent, for I am a stranger with you and a sojourner, as all my fathers were. 38:14 O forgive me, that I may be refreshed before I go from here and be no more. 407. Psalmista confessus est se passum afflictionem suam pro peccato suo; hic autem promittit cautelam in posterum. 407. The psalmist confessed that he suffered affliction for his sin; now here he promises caution in the future. Titulus talis est, in finem canticus David pro Idithum. The title is unto the end, for Jeduthun, a song of David. Et hoc non est novum, licet sit novus titulus: quia ut habetur in historia 1 Paral. 15, quando David arcam foederis quae fuerat capta a Philistaeis reduxit, quatuor millia cantorum instituit, qui Psalmos canerent; quos posuit intus, quos Asaph, Itamar, et Idithum vocavit, et hic ponitur in titulo. And this is not new, although this is a new title, since this is in the history of 1 Chronicles 15. When David led back the ark of the covenant which had been captured by the Philistines, he appointed four thousand singers who sang the Psalms, among whom were those called Asaph, Ithamar, and Jeduthun, and here he is placed in the title (cf. 1 Chron 15:3–17; 24:4; 25:1). Et sicut Origenes dicit, haec consuetudo erat etiam apud Graecos, quod si aliquando aliqui poetae erant qui carmina recitabant in populo, si non erant ab ipsis recitantibus facta, sed ab aliis, victoria non dabatur recitatori, sed poetae. And as Origen says, it was the custom among the Greeks that when poems were sung to the people, if they were not written by the ones reciting but by others, the glory of the victory was not given to the reciter but to the poet. Sic etiam fit de Psalmis; nam omnes Psalmos fecit David, et quosdam ipse cantavit coram arca, ut habetur 2 Reg. 6: quosdam dabat aliis cantandos: et ideo illi quibus dabantur, erant quasi cantores, et eis intitulantur: Now it was so with the Psalms, for David wrote all the Psalms, and he sang certain of them before the ark, as is found in 2 Samuel 6. He gave some to others to sing, and so these were given to those who were singers, and they are titled from them. tum propter dignitatem, quia fuerunt prophetae, tum propter mysterium. Unde hoc competit mysterio Idithum, quod interpretatur intus vir sciens, vel transiliens; et signat transilientem vanitatem mundi, de qua multum in hoc psalmo agitur. Further, the titles were given because of their dignity, since they were prophets, and because of their mystery. So this fits the mystery of Jeduthun, which is interpreted to mean “a man knowing interiorly” or “leaping across” and it signifies the leap from the vanity of the world which much of this psalm treats. Dividitur ergo psalmus iste in partes duas. Therefore, this psalm is divided into two parts. In prima ponit cautelam quam habet ad evadendum pericula. In the first part, he describes the caution which he has to avoid danger. In secunda ostendit necessitatem cautelae, ibi, ecce mensurabiles. In the second, he shows the necessity of this caution, at behold, you have made my days measurable. Circa primum duo facit. He makes two points about the first. Primo praemittit cautelam. First, he puts forward the caution. Secundo ostendit periculum ex ea incidens, ibi, obmutui. Second, he shows the danger from which it comes, at I was dumb. Circa primum duo facit. He makes two points regarding the first. Primo praemittit cautelam. First, he mentions his caution. Secundo ponit rationem cautelae, ibi, posui ori meo. Second, he adds the reason for caution, at I have set a guard to my mouth. In cautela consideranda sunt tria: In caution, three things should be considered: scilicet cavendi propositum; unde ait, dixi, id est cum deliberatione in corde meo statui et proposui: Eccl. 2: dixi in corde meo, vadam et affluam deliciis. the proposal to be cautious, so he says, I said, that is, I established and proposed in my heart with deliberation. I said in my heart, “I will go, and abound with delights” (Eccl 2:1). Secundo considerandum est quid proponat: quia, custodiam vias meas, id est processus meos. Et debet homo custodire vias Dei ut imitetur: Job 23: vestigia ejus sequutus est pes meus. Viam ejus custodivi, et non declinavi ex ea. Item debet custodire vias suas ne erret: Deut. 4: custodite vias vestras et studia vestra. Second, what is proposed must be considered, that I will take heed to my ways, that is, my progress. And a man should take heed to the ways of God so he may imitate them. My foot has followed his steps. I have kept his way and have not turned away from it (Job 23:11). Further, he should take heed to his ways so he will not err. Guard your ways and your doings (Jer 18:11; Deut 4:15). Tertio considerandus est fructus qui ex hoc sequitur: ut non delinquam in lingua mea. Homo debet diligenter se custodire ab omnibus peccatis, et praecipue a peccato linguae, quia faciliter in eis prolabitur: lingua enim est mobilis, et de facili prorumpit in malum. Third, the fruit which follows from this should be considered: that I sin not with my tongue. A man should diligently guard himself from all sin, and principally from the sins of the tongue, since one falls into them easily. For the tongue is active and easily breaks forth with evil. Vel alia ratio est, quia lingua est immediatum organum ad interiorem conceptum cordis explicandum. Unde quam cito verbum est in corde, est in lingua; et ideo oportet diligenter custodiri. Or another reason is that the tongue is the organ in which the interior thought of the heart is immediately expressed. So whatever is in the heart is quickly expressed by the tongue, and one should diligently guard it. Consequenter ponitur ratio hujus custodiae, posui ori meo custodiam, cum consisteret peccator adversum me; quasi dicat: haec est ratio quare sic custodio vias meas, quia semper adversarius est contra me. Afterwards, he gives the reason for this guarding: I have set a guard over my mouth when the sinner stood against me. As if to say, “This is the reason why I so guard my ways, because my adversary is always against me.” Alibi dictum est, quod homo ponat custodiam cordi: Prov. 4: omni custodia serva cor tuum; There is another saying that a man should place a guard over his heart: with all watchfulness, keep your heart (Prov 4:23). hic dicit quod ponat custodiam ori, quod non claudatur totaliter, sed debet custodiri, id est servari a malis verbis quantum ad tempus, et quantum ad ea quae dicit: Eccl. 8: omni negotio tempus est, et opportunitas. He says that he places a guard over his mouth, not that he totally closes it, but that he should guard it, that is, watch for words which are evil either because of their time or because of what he says. There is a time and opportunity for every business (Eccl 8:6). Et quare posui ori meo custodiam? Cum consisteret peccator adversum me. And in what way have I put a guard over my tongue? When the sinner stood against me. Hoc exponitur tripliciter. This can be explained in three ways. Unus sensus est: adversum me, ad insidiandum; et tunc magis necessaria est cautela: Eccl. 5: si est tibi intellectus, responde proximo: sin autem, sit manus tua super os tuum: Matth. 22: ut caperent eum in sermone. One sense is, against me, to set an ambush, and then caution is especially necessary. If you have understanding, answer your neighbor, but if not let your hand be upon your mouth (Sir 5:14). To ensnare him in speech (Matt 22:15).
