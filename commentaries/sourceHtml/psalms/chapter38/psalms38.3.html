      Secundo rationem assignat, ibi, verumtamen universa. Second, he gives a reason, at indeed, all things are vanity. Miseriam hujus vitae ostendit quantum ad ejus brevitatem, et quantum ad ejus debilitatem. He shows the misery of this life in regard to its brevity and in regard to its weakness. Quantum ad primum dicit, domine petivi, ut annuntiares mihi numerum dierum meorum. Sed nunquid sunt numerabiles dies mei? Ita, quia, ecce mensurabiles posuisti dies meos. Speaking about the first he says, “O Lord, I have asked that you announce to me the number of my days.” But can the days of my life be numbered? Yes, since behold, you have made my days measurable. Homo est sic conditus, quod si nunquam peccasset, non moreretur, et ita dies ejus non mensurarentur; sed peccando moritur, et sic numerari possunt. Job 14: breves dies hominis sunt, numerus mensium ejus apud te est. Man was made so that if he had not sinned, he would not have died, and so his days were not measurable, but by sinning he came to die, and so they can be numbered. The days of man are short and the number of his months is with you (Job 14:5). Sed esto quod vita esset brevis, et posset esse ita pretiosa quod multum reputanda esset: tamen hoc nihil est: quia substantia mea et natura mea et vita mea tanquam nihilum ante te est, id est in comparatione tui; licet aliquid videatur esse in comparatione creaturarum infimarum. Isa. 40: omnes gentes quasi non sint, sic sunt coram eo. But if life is short, it should for this reason be precious, as many consider it to be. Nevertheless, it is not so, since my substance and my nature and my life are as nothing before you, that is, in comparison with you, although it would seem to be precious in comparison with lower creatures. All nations are before him as if they had no being at all (Isa 40:17). Potest autem dici sic: substantia mea, id est dum considero illud quod est ante te, id est bona aeterna, quae dabis sanctis, substantiam meam nihil reputo. Now, it can be said in this way: my substance, that is, when I consider what is before you—that is, the eternal goods which you give to the saints—I consider my substance as nothing. Vel sic. Qui considerant res hujus mundi ante te, id est oculo divino, nihil reputant eas. Or it can be taken this way: those who consider the things of this earth before you—that is, with a divine eye—consider them to be nothing. 410. Verumtamen universa. Hic assignat rationem secundum litteram. Universa, secundum litteram nostram, vel est nominativi casus singularis numeri, vel est nominativi casus neutri generis, et pluralis numeri. 410. And indeed all things are vanity. Here he gives the reason according to the literal sense. All things, according to our text, is either in the nominative case and singular in number or in the nominative case, neuter in gender, and plural in number. Si secundo modo, sic est sensus, verumtamen universa; quasi dicat, non est enim mirum si vita hominis est nihil ante te, quia, universa, id est inferiora omnia sunt vanitas. If read in the second way, this is the sense: indeed, all things are vanity, as if to say, “It is not surprising that human life is as nothing before you, since all things, that is, the things below, are vanity.” Et ideo Hieronymus habet, omnia enim vanitas, ut sit quasi ratio praecedentium. Eccl. 1: omnia vanitas. And so Jerome has, for all is vanity, as though for the preceding reason. All is vanity (Eccl 1:2). Et dicuntur vanitas, quia vanitas opponitur soliditati et stabilitati: omnia enim quae sunt in mundo, sunt subjecta mutabilitati, et ideo sunt vana: et etiam inter ea, omnis homo vivens est subjectus mutabilitati, et sic est vanitas. Rom. 8: vanitati enim creatura, id est homo, subjecta est non volens. They are called vanity because vanity is opposed to solidity and stability, for all things which are in the world are subject to change and so they are vain. And among them, every man living is subject to change, and so is vanity. The creature, that is, man, was made subject to vanity, not willingly (Rom 8:20). Vel secundum litteram. Universa, si sit singularis numeri, sic est sensus, universa vanitas, id est omnis vanitas, est omnis homo vivens, id est est in omni homine mundane vivente. Vanus dicitur homo ex hoc quod sequitur res mutabiles Jer. 2: ambulaverunt post vanitates, et vani facti sunt. Or, according to the literal sense, all, if it is taken as singular in number, means all vanity, that is, entirely vain, is every living man; that is, it is so in regard to every man living in a worldly way. A man is said to be vain because he follows after changeable things. They walked after vanity and became vain (Jer 2:5). Vel sic, omnis homo vivens, id est carnaliter: est omnis, id est perfecta vanitas. Or it is so, every man living carnally is totally, that is, completely, vanity. 411. Verumtamen in imagine. Hic ostendit miseriam conditionis humanae, quantum ad occupationem: 411. Surely man passes as an image. Here he shows the misery of the human condition in regard to its occupation. et circa hoc tria facit. He makes three main points about this. Primo ostendit quod aliquid sit in homine quod vanitati debet resistere. First, he shows that there is something in man which should resist vanity. Secundo ostendit in generali vanitatem occupationis ejus, ibi, sed frustra. Second, he shows in general the vanity of his occupation, at yet in vain. Tertio ostendit hoc idem in speciali, ibi, thesaurizat. Third, he shows the same things in particular, at he stores up. Dicit ergo, omnis homo vanitas; tamen aliquid est in homine fixum, quod semper manet in eo dum transit; et hoc est imago. Therefore, he says, every man is vanity; nevertheless, there is something fixed in man which always remains while he changes, and this is the image. Et potest hoc tripliciter exponi. And this can be explained in three ways. Uno modo de imagine Dei, quae est in quantum homo est praeditus ratione. Gen. 1: faciamus hominem ad imaginem et similitudinem nostram. Et haec imago debet resistere vanitati; scilicet ratio, et debet eam, scilicet vanitatem, excludere. Et hoc est quod ait. Quamvis homo sit subjectus vanitati, semper tamen est praeditus ratione. In one way, it is about the image of God, which is in man insofar as he is elevated by reason. Let us make man in our image and likeness (Gen 1:26). And this image—namely, the reason—should resist vanity, and it should exclude it, that is, vanity. And this is what he says. Although man is subject to vanity, nevertheless he is always elevated by reason. Sed quia non determinatur hic cujus sit imago, ideo nota hic duplicem imaginem: But it is not clarified here whose image he is, since image is understood in two ways. unam caelestis hominis, One is that of the heavenly man, aliam vero hominis terreni. but the other is of the earthly man. 1 Cor. 15: sicut portavimus imaginem terreni, portemus et imaginem caelestis. Dico ergo quod omnis homo est vanitas; tamen omnis homo pertransit in aliquam imaginem, quia, vel per bona opera repraesentat in se imaginem hominis caelestis, vel per mala imaginem terreni. As we have borne the image of the earthly, let us bear also the image of the heavenly (1 Cor 15:49). I say, therefore, that every man is vanity, nevertheless every man passes into some image, since he either represents the image of the heavenly man in himself through good works, or the image of the earthly through evil. Vel aliter. Imago importat similitudinem. Cursus autem vitae istius dirigitur per aliquam cognitionem, quia affectus movetur secundum cognitum. Or it can be understood otherwise. Image implies similarity. Now, the course of this life is directed through thought, since the affections are moved according to thought. Est autem duplex cognitio. Now there is a twofold way of thinking. Una, qua cognoscitur veritas: et haec est certa. One is that by which the truth is known, and this is certain. Alia est per verisimilitudinem: et haec est quasi cognitio imaginis: quia non attingit ad ipsam veritatem, sed ad similitudinem veritatis. Another is knowing through a likeness, and this is like the understanding of an image, since it does not attain to the truth itself, but to the likeness of the truth. Si homo attingeret ad ipsam veritatem sicut est, non turbaretur, quia consideraret conditionem hujus vitae, et quid erimus post hanc vitam; et sic non occuparetur in ista vita, sed ad ea quae sunt in alia vita tenderet. If a man reaches to the truth as it is, he shall not be disturbed, since he considers the condition of this life and what we will be after this life, and so he is not preoccupied with this life but with those things through which we tend to the other life. Et ideo Hieronymus habet, quantum ad imaginem pertransit homo, tantum turbatur, id est quantum deficit homo a vera cognitione veritatis, tantum turbatur per occupationem hujus vitae, sive in bonis sive in malis. Luc. 10: Martha, Martha, solicita es, et turbaris erga plurima. And so Jerome has, to the extent that a man passes through according to the image, he is disquieted, that is, to the extent that a man fails to attain a true knowledge of the truth, he is disturbed by being occupied in this life, either in good things or evil things. Martha, Martha, you are careful and are troubled about many things (Luke 10:41). Quando ergo facit bona opera, tunc non turbatur frustra; sed quando non occupat se in bonis, tunc frustra turbatur: Isa. 49: vane consumpsi fortitudinem meam. Therefore, when he does good works, then he is not disquieted in vain, but when he does not occupy himself with good works, then he is disquieted in vain. I have spent my strength without cause (Isa 49:4). Et ponit exemplum de occupatione quae conturbat homines, quae est occupatio avaritiae; haec autem maxime conturbat homines. And he gives an example of the occupation which disquiets men, which is being occupied with avarice. This most greatly disquiets men. Concupiscentia autem ad horam occupat homines, similiter et ira. Sed, ut dicitur Eccl. 5, avarus non impletur pecunia; et ideo de eo ponit exemplum dicens, thesaurizat et ignorat cui congregabit ea, id est congregat thesauros superfluos in hac vita. Matth. 6: nolite thesaurizare vobis thesauros in terra, scilicet vane et frustra, quia ignoratis cui congregatis ea. Now concupiscence does occupy a man for a period, as does anger. But, as is said, a covetous man shall not be satisfied with money (Eccl 5:9), and so he gives an example of this, saying, he stores up and he knows not for whom he shall gather these things, that is, he gathers together treasures in this life vainly. Do not lay up for yourselves treasures on earth (Matt 6:19), vainly and uselessly, for he knows not for whom he shall gather these things. Sibi non, quia Job 27: dives cum dormierit, nihil secum affert. Ergo aliis, sed nescit cui; quia aliquando non congregat pueris, quia non remanent post eum; et si remanent, non tamen scit quales sint, quia aliquando sunt contra parentes. It is not for yourself, since the rich man, when he shall sleep, shall take nothing with him (Job 27:19). Therefore, it is for others, but he does not know for whom, since they do not remain after him, and if they should remain, he would nevertheless not know what sort they are, since sometimes children act contrary to their parents. 412. Et nunc quae est expectatio mea et cetera. Hic petit Dei auxilium contra miseriam. 412. And now what is my hope? Here he asks for the help of God against his misery.
