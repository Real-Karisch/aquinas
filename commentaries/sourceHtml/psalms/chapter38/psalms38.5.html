      Causa est, quoniam tu fecisti. Et potest referri ad hoc quod dicitur, opprobrium. Causa quod sustineat patienter injurias, est quando cogitat quod divino judicio fit sibi hoc. Hoc fecit David cum dixit 2 Reg. 16: dimitte eum ut maledicat. Prov. 3: disciplinam domini, fili mi non dimittas, ne deficias cum ab eo corripieris. The reason is because you have done it. And this can be connected to what was said above: you have made me a reproach. The reason why he endures injuries patiently is that he thinks that the divine justice brought them about. David did this when he said, let him alone that he may curse (2 Sam 16:11). My son, reject not the correction of the Lord and do not faint when you are chastised by him (Prov 3:11). Vel potest referri ad hoc quod dicit, obmutui quoniam tu fecisti, scilicet patientiam. Or it can be connected to what was said, I was dumb because you have caused it, namely, my patience. Secunda petitio sua est de amotione poenae. The second petition regards the removal of punishment. Et primo ponit petitionem; First, he makes the petition; secundo rationem petitionis. second, gives the reason for the petition. Dicit, amove a me plagas tuas, id est flagella tua: He says, remove your scourges, that is, your whips, from me. et potest dupliciter exponi. This can be explained in two ways. Vel de corporalibus flagellis: et haec sunt exteriora, sicut flagella ceteraeque adversitates, quibus homo ponitur pro peccatis suis a Deo. Jerem. 30: plaga inimici percussi te. Ergo haec flagella amove a me. It may be about corporeal scourges, and these are external things, such as the scourge of certain adversaries, under which a man is placed by God for his sins. I have wounded you with the wound of an enemy (Jer 30:14). Therefore, remove these scourges from me. Item sunt plagae spirituales, id est interiores, sicut remorsus conscientiae. Ps. 49: arguam te, et statuam contra faciem tuam. Et has petit removeri. Likewise, there are spiritual scourges, that is, those which are interior, such as remorse of conscience. I will reprove you and set against your face (Ps 49:21). He asks for these to be removed. Secundum autem Origenem, causa hujus petitionis est a fortitudine. Flagellatio exterior multum utilis est in quantum patienter sustinetur; sed quando non fertur patienter, sed deficit et desperat se, sunt nocivae. 2 Cor. 2: consolemini eum ne abundantiori tristitia absorbeatur. Et ideo timens hoc, petit removeri plagas, quia defecit a fortitudine, id est a violentia percussionis. Isa. 8: in forti manu erudivit me. 1 Reg. 5: fiebat manus domini gravissima. Now, according to Origen, the cause of this petition is a lack of fortitude. Exterior scourges are very useful as long as they are endured patiently, but when they are no longer borne patiently, but someone weakens and despairs of himself, they become harmful. You should comfort him, lest such a one be swallowed up with overmuch sorrow (2 Cor 2:7). And so, fearing this, he asks that the scourges be removed, since he is failing in fortitude—that is, because of the violence of the beating. He has taught me with a strong hand (Isa 8:11). The hand of God was exceedingly heavy (1 Sam 5:12). Deficit homo tripliciter in flagellis. A man fails under scourges in three ways. Unus modus est communis bonis et malis; qui est corporalis. Ps. 89: et in ira tua defecimus. One way is common to both the good and the evil, which is bodily. In your wrath we have fainted away (Ps 89:9). Alius est impatientiae; et hic est malorum. Job 4: nunc venit super te plaga et defecisti. Another is by impatience, and this happens to the evil. Now the scourge is come upon you and you faint (Job 4:5). Alius modus est bonorum, quo quis deficit a se, et tendit in Deum. Ps. 118: defecit in salutare tuum anima mea. Another way happens to the good, when someone becomes weak himself but reaches out towards God. My soul has fainted after your salvation (Ps 118:81). Et quolibet istorum modorum potest intelligi quod dicitur hic; quasi dicat: ideo peto quod amoveas a me flagella tua, quia sum correptus quoad tertium modum defectionis: quia, ego defeci a fortitudine manus tuae in increpationibus. And in whatever way what is said here is understood, as if he says, “Therefore, I ask that you remove your scourges from me, since I am corrected because I have reached the third mode of failure, since the strength of your hand has made me faint in rebukes.” Vel quia despero, quoad secundum modum. Or, “since I despair,” in the second way. Vel quia non possum sustinere, quoad primum modum. Or, “since I can no longer endure,” in the first way. Nec est vis si dicatur, secundum aliam litteram, in increpationibus ego defeci et cetera. Nor is there any strength if this other line is said: the strength of your hand has made me faint in rebukes. Propter iniquitatem corripuisti hominem. Haec est ratio quare proponit duplicem petitionem: You have corrected man for iniquity. This is the reason why he makes his twofold petition, quia primo removetur causa, since first, the cause is removed, et postea removetur effectus. and afterwards, the effect is removed. Culpa est causa plagarum: et ideo dicit, quod homo flagellatur propter peccata. Unde dicit, tu corripuisti, id est flagellasti hominem, propter iniquitatem, id est propter peccata sua. Guilt is the cause of scourging, and so he says that man is scourged on account of sins. So he says, you have corrected, that is, you scourged man, for iniquity, that is, for his sins. Et ideo primo petit removeri peccata, quia poenae futurae sunt ad damnationem, sed poenae hujus vitae ad purgationem, quantum est de se, et sunt ad correptionem. Ps. 31: multa flagella peccatoris. And so first he asks for the sins to be removed since future punishment is damnation, but the punishments in this life are for purgation, insofar as they concern this and they are for correction. Many are the scourges of the sinner (Ps 31:10). Et tabescere fecisti sicut araneam animam ejus. Hic ponitur modus sive terminus correctionis, tabescere, id est desiccare. And you have made his soul to waste away like a spider. Here he describes the mode or the end of correction: to waste away, that is, to dry up. Potest ergo exponi corporaliter, quando refertur ad tribulationem corporalem; quia humiditas et pinguedo corporalis desiccatur, et sic vita tabescit. Job 33: tabescet caro ejus. Item ibidem: consumpta est caro ejus a suppliciis. It can be explained corporeally when it is referred to bodily tribulation, since the moisture and fat of the body are dried up, and so life wastes away. His flesh shall be consumed away (Job 33:21). Likewise: his flesh is consumed with punishment (Job 33:25). Alio modo, ut referatur ad animam. In another way, it can be referred to the soul. In homine spiritualiter est duplex pinguedo. Now, in man there is a twofold spiritual fatness. Una mala per delectationes corporales. Deut. 32: impinguatus est, dereliquit Deum factorem suum: et ab hac pinguedine desiccare bonum est; et hoc est quod dicit, tabescere fecisti, id est desiccasti, animam meam sicut araneam, ab amore carnalium rerum: quia aranea est animal calidum, et non est pingue, quia facit subtilissima fila; ita anima separata a carnalibus delectationibus, inhaeret per affectum rebus invisibilibus, et facit operationes et affectiones invisibiles. 2 Cor. 4: non contemplantibus nobis quae videntur et cetera. One is evil and comes through bodily delights. He grew fat . . . and forsook the God who made him (Deut 32:15). And it is good for this fatness to be dried up, and this is what he says: you have made my soul waste away, that is, dry up, like a spider, from the love of carnal things, since the spider is a hot animal and it is not fat since it makes most subtle filaments; so the soul that is separated from fleshly delights clings in its affection to invisible things and has invisible actions and affections. While we look not at the things which are seen but at the things which are not seen (2 Cor 4:18). Vel, tabescere, id est desiccare a pinguedine devotionis, sicut dicit Ps. 62: sicut adipe et pinguedine repleatur anima mea: et haec est mala desiccatio. Hoc fit, quia anima efficitur indevota, et inhaeret peccatis, et caret pinguedine devotionis sicut aranea. Or you have made it to waste away, that is, to be dried up in regard to the richness of devotion, as is said: let my soul be filled as with marrow and fatness (Ps 62:6), and this is a bad drying up. This comes about because the soul is no longer devout and clings to sins and, like a spider, lacks the fatness of devotion. Item tela araneae est fragilis, ita cogitationes peccatorum sunt vanae. Isa. 59: telas araneae, id est inutilia et vana, texerunt, id est fecerunt. Likewise, the web of a spider is fragile, so the thoughts of sinners are vain. They have woven, that is, made, the webs of spiders, that is, useless and vain things (Isa 59:5). Item aranea tantum emittit de filis illis, quod moritur; ita homines moriuntur spiritualiter propter peccata quae faciunt. Jac. 1: peccatum cum consummatum fuerit, generat mortem. Et legitur sic. Animam ejus existentem, sicut araneam, fecisti tabescere, id est desiccare bona siccitate. Likewise, a spider sometimes emits its children so that it dies; likewise men are killed spiritually because of the sins which they make. When sin is completed it begets death (Jas 1:15). And so it is written, you have made his soul to waste away like a spider, that is, to dry up with a good aridity. Verumtamen vane conturbatur omnis homo. Hic est defectus correptionis; quia licet faciat sic tabescere, tamen multi manent in malitia sua, vel redeunt ad malum. Et quod aliqui non conturbantur vane, hoc non est in quantum sunt homines, sed in quantum sunt elevati a Deo a rebus terrenis ad considerationem caelestium. Surely in vain is any man disquieted. This is a defect in correction, since although he makes him to waste away in this way, nevertheless many remain in their evil or return to evil. And that some are not disquieted in vain does not come from the men, but because they are elevated by God from earthly things to consider heavenly things. 414. Exaudi orationem meam domine, et deprecationem et cetera. Hic petit suam petitionem admitti: ubi petit ut exaudiatur. 414. Hear my prayer, O Lord, and my supplication. Here he asks that his petition be accepted; he asks that it be heard. Et ponit tria, quae faciunt acceptam petitionem Deo. He describes three things which make a petition acceptable to God. Primum est elevatio mentis in Deum: et haec est oratio, quae est ascensus mentis in Deum: unde dicit, exaudi orationem meam domine. Ps. 68: ego vero orationem meam ad te domine. First is the elevation of the mind to God, and this is prayer, which is the lifting of the mind to God, so he says, hear my prayer, O Lord. But as for me, my prayer is to you, O Lord (Ps 68:14). Secundum est continuatio petendi: et hoc cum dicit, et deprecationem meam. Jac. ult.: multum valet deprecatio justi assidua. Second is that he ask continuously, and this is found where he says, and my supplication. The continual prayer of a just man avails much (Jas 5:16).
