      Secundum est oratio sive laus Dei: unde addit, cantabo domino, id est laudabo eum: Ps. 21: qui timetis dominum, laudate eum. Second is the prayer or praise of God, so he adds, I will sing to the Lord, that is, I will praise him. You who fear the Lord praise him (Ps 21:24). Laus enim Dei multum valet ad expugnandum Diabolum: Matth. 17: hoc genus Daemoniorum non ejicitur nisi per orationem et jejunium: Habac. 3: ego autem in domino gaudebo, et exultabo in Deo Jesu meo. For the praise of God is very powerful for fighting the devil. This kind of demon is not cast out except by prayer and fasting (Matt 17:20). But I will rejoice in the Lord, and I shall joy in God my Jesus (Hab 3:18). Et hoc pro beneficiis concessis: et ideo sequitur. Qui bona tribuit mihi, id est qui bona tribuit. Jacob. 1: omne datum optimum et cetera. And this praise comes from blessings bestowed upon him, and so it follows, who gives me good things, that is, who has given good things. For every best gift and every perfect gift is from above (Jas 1:17). Et dicit, bona, quia sunt quaedam bona temporalia quae dominus tribuit: Matth. 15: tradidit illis bona sua, et uni dedit quinque talenta et cetera. And he says, good things, since there are some temporal goods which the Lord gives. He delivered to them his goods, and to one he gave five talents (Matt 15:14–15). Item, bona spiritualia, quae sunt bona gratiae et virtutes: 1 Cor. 12: divisiones gratiarum sunt et cetera. He also gives spiritual good things, which include the goods of grace and the virtues. There are diversities of grace (1 Cor 12:4). Haec omnia operatur unus atque idem spiritus, dividens singulis prout vult: Job 2: si bona suscepimus de manu domini et cetera. These are all accomplished by one and the same Spirit, distributing to each one as he wills. If we have received good things at the hand of God, why should we not receive evil? (Job 2:10). Item bona gloriae: Ps. 26: credo videre bona domini in terra viventium. He also gives the good things of glory. I believe I will see the good things of the Lord in the land of the living (Ps 26:13). Haec omnia bona tribuit dominus; et ideo merito laudandus est: Eccl. ult. danti mihi sapientiam, dabo gloriam. The Lord gives all of these good things, and so he deserves to be praised. To him who gives me wisdom, I will give glory (Sir 51:23). Tertium est bonum opus, de quo subjungitur, psallam, id est operabor. Psallere est manu tangere psalterium. Third is the good work, about which he adds, I will sing a psalm, that is, I will work. To sing a psalm involves touching the psaltery with one’s hand. Hieronymus: semper aliquid boni facito, ut te Diabolus inveniat occupatum; Matth. 12: cum immundus spiritus exierit ab homine et cetera usque ingressi habitant ibi et cetera. Fiunt et cetera. Jerome: always do something good, so the devil may find you occupied. When an unclean spirit is gone out of a man . . . and coming back he finds it empty . . . and they enter in and dwell there. . . . So it shall be also to this wicked generation (Matt 12:43–45). Nomen domini, id est ad laudem nominis domini altissimi: Luc. 1: gloria in altissimis Deo: Matth. 5: sic luceat lux vestra coram hominibus, ut videant et cetera. He will sing to the name of the Lord, that is, in praise of the name of the Lord, the Most High. Glory to God in the highest (Luke 2:14). So let your light shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father who is in heaven (Matt 5:16). Super Psalmo 13 Psalm 13 Dixit insipiens in corde suo: ‘Non est Deus’ The fool has said in his heart, “There is no God” 13:1 In finem. Psalmus David. Dixit insipiens in corde suo: ‘Non est Deus.’ Corrupti sunt, et abominabiles facti sunt in studiis suis; non est qui faciat bonum, non est usque ad unum. 13:1 Unto the end, a psalm for David. The fool has said in his heart, “There is no God.” They are corrupt, and are become abominable in their ways; there is no one who does good; no, not one. 13:2 Dominus de caelo prospexit super filios hominum, ut videat si est intelligens, aut requirens Deum. 13:3a Omnes declinaverunt, simul inutiles facti sunt. Non est qui faciat bonum, non est usque ad unum. 13:2 The Lord has looked down from heaven upon the children of men, to see if there are any who understand and seek God. 13:3a They are all gone aside, they are become unprofitable together. There is no one who does good; no, not one. 13:3b Sepulchrum patens est guttur eorum; linguis suis dolose agebant. Venenum aspidum sub labiis eorum. 13:3b Their throat is an open sepulcher; with their tongues they acted deceitfully; the poison of asps is under their lips. 13:3c Quorum os maledictione et amaritudine plenum est; veloces pedes eorum ad effundendum sanguinem. 13:3c Their mouth is full of cursing and bitterness; their feet are swift to shed blood. 13:3d Contritio et infelicitas in viis eorum, et viam pacis non cognoverunt; non est timor Dei ante oculos eorum. 13:3d Destruction and unhappiness in their ways, and the way of peace they have not known; there is no fear of God before their eyes. 13:4 Nonne cognoscent omnes qui operantur iniquitatem, qui devorant plebem meam sicut escam panis? 13:5 Dominum non invocaverunt; illic trepidaverunt timore ubi non erat timor. 13:6 Quoniam Dominus in generatione justa est: consilium inopis confudistis, quoniam Dominus spes ejus est. 13:4 Shall not all those who work iniquity know, who devour my people as they eat bread? 13:5 They have not called upon the Lord; there have they trembled for fear, where there was no fear. 13:6 For the Lord is in the just generation: you have confounded the counsel of the poor man, but the Lord is his hope. 13:7 Quis dabit ex Sion salutare Israel? Cum averterit Dominus captivitatem plebis suae, exsultabit Jacob, et laetabitur Israel. 13:7 Who shall give out of Zion the salvation of Israel? When the Lord shall have turned away the captivity of his people, Jacob shall rejoice and Israel shall be glad. 96. Supra psalmista exprobravit dolositatem inimicorum; hic ponit eorum malitiam. 96. Above, the psalmist reproached the deceit of his enemies; here he describes their wickedness. Titulus, in finem psalmus David. The title is unto the end, a psalm for David. Et circa hoc duo facit. And he considers two points regarding this. Primo manifestat eorum malitiam. First, he shows their wickedness. Secundo ponit spem liberationis suae ab eis, ibi, nonne cognoscent et cetera. Second, he describes his hope to be delivered from them, at shall not all those know. Et circa primum duo facit. He discusses two aspects of their wickedness. Primo proponit eorum malitiam. First, he shows their wickedness. Secundo certificat hanc malitiam eis inesse, ibi, dominus de caelo et cetera. Second, he makes certain that this wickedness really is within them, at the Lord has looked down. Prima in duo. He shows their wickedness in two steps. Primo proponit radicem malitiae eorum. First, he describes the root of their wickedness; Secundo processum malitiae, corrupti sunt et cetera second, the progress of their wickedness: they are corrupt. sicut dicitur Eccl. 10: initium omnis peccati superbia, et initium superbiae hominis apostatare a Deo. Quod homo ergo non habeat Deum in corde, principium malitiae est; et ideo dicit, dixit insipiens in corde suo, non est Deus: Sapien. 1: in malevolam animam non introibit sapientia, aut habitabit in corpore subdito peccatis. The beginning of every sin is pride, and the beginning of the pride of man is to fall away from God (Sir 10:13). For a man to not have God in his heart is the beginning of wickedness, and so he says, the fool has said in his heart, “There is no God.” For wisdom will not enter a malicious soul, nor dwell in a body subject to sins (Wis 1:4). Sed numquid potest dicere? Dicere in corde est cogitare. Sed numquid potest cogitare Deum non esse? Anselmus dicit, quod nullus potest. Item Damascenus. Cognitio Dei naturaliter omnibus est inserta: naturaliter cognita nullus potest cogitare non esse. But can this really be said? To speak in the heart is to think. But can it really be thought that there is no God? Anselm says that no one can do this. Damascene does also. The knowledge of God is naturally implanted in all, but no one is able to naturally think what is not. Sed sciendum, quod de cognitione Dei dupliciter loqui possumus: scilicet secundum se, vel quoad nos. But it must be known that we can speak about the knowledge of God in two ways: either in itself or in relation to us. Si primo modo, sic procul dubio non potest cogitari non esse: nulla enim propositio potest cogitari falsa ex sua natura, cujus praedicatum includitur in definitione subjecti. If we mean it in the first way, it is without a doubt true that no one can think that he is not. For no proposition can be thought false which is of the nature that the predicate is included in the definition of the subject. Notandum est autem, quod in Deo est aliter esse quam in aliis; quia esse Dei est ejus substantia. Ergo qui dicit Deum secundum se, dicit ipsum esse; et ideo secundum se non potest cogitari non esse. But it must be noticed that in God, existence is different than in other things, since the being of God is his very substance. Therefore, he who speaks about God as he is in himself says that he is being itself; and so as he is in himself no one can think that he is not.
