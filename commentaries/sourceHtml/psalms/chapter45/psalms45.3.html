      jucunditatis est unus: et hoc designat cum dicit, laetificat civitatem Dei. Et hoc manifestatur ex auctoritate sacrae Scripturae, quae dicit Rom. 14: regnum Dei non est esca et potus, sed justitia et pax et gaudium in Spiritu Sancto. Gal. 5: fructus spiritus est pax, gaudium et cetera. Joyfulness is one, and this is signified when he says: it makes the city of God joyful. And this is made clear from the authority of Sacred Scripture, which says: the kingdom of God is not food and drink but justice, and peace, and joy in the Holy Spirit (Rom 14:17). The fruit of the Spirit is peace, joy (Gal 5:22). Et quia facit amare Deum. Et in hoc amore semper est jucunditas, quia quilibet delectatur in praesentia amati: et qui diligit Deum, habet Deum praesentem. 1 Joan. 4: qui manet in caritate et cetera. And the reason is because it makes one love God. And there is always joyfulness in this love, since one always delights in the presence of the beloved, and he who loves God has God present. He who abides in charity, abides in God (1 John 4:16). Haec civitas est Ecclesia. Ps. 86: gloriosa dicta sunt de te, civitas Dei et cetera. This city is the Church. Glorious things are said of you, O city of God (Ps 86:3). Tria sunt in ista civitate, quae sunt de ejus ratione. There are three things in this city which belong to its essence. Primum est, quod sit ibi multitudo liberorum: quia si est ibi unus vel pauci, non est civitas: et similiter si sunt servi. Et hoc maxime invenitur in Ecclesia. Gal. 4: non sumus ancillae filii, sed liberae. First is that there is a multitude of the free, since if there is only one, or a few, it is not a city, and so also if they are slaves. And this is particularly found in the Church: we are not children of the bondswoman, but of the free (Gal 4:31). Secundum est quod habeat sufficientiam per se. In vico enim non inveniuntur omnia necessaria vitae humanae sanis et infirmis; sed in civitate oportet invenire omnia necessaria ad vitam. Et haec sufficientia est in Ecclesia: quia quicquid necessarium est ad vitam spiritualem, invenitur in ea. Ps. 64: replebimur in bonis domus tuae. The second is that it is sufficient unto itself. For a village does not have all that is necessary for human life, either healthy or sick, but a city has all that is necessary for life. And this sufficiency is in the Church, since whatever is necessary for the spiritual life is found in it. We shall be filled with the good things of your house (Ps 64:5). Tertium est unitas civium: quia ab hoc, scilicet ab unitate civium, civitas nominatur; quia civitas quasi civium unitas. Et haec est in Ecclesia. Joan. 17: ut sint unum in nobis, sicut et nos unum sumus. Haec ergo civitas laetificatur per gratiam Spiritus Sancti in eam descendentem. The third is the unity of the citizens, since it is called a city because of the unity of its citizens, since a city is, as it were, a unity of citizens. And this is true of the Church. That they may be one as we also are one (John 17:22). Therefore, this city is gladdened by the grace of the Holy Spirit descending upon it. Secundus effectus est sanctitatis: unde dicit: sanctificavit tabernaculum suum altissimus. Hoc tabernaculum uno modo est ipsa civitas. In civitate habitant quiescentes; in tabernaculo militans Ecclesia, quae hic habet pacem, scilicet ex Deo: et tamen patitur inquietem a mundo. Joan. 16: in mundo pressuram habebitis; in me autem pacem. The second effect of the Holy Spirit is holiness, so he says: the Most High has sanctified his tabernacle. This tabernacle is, in one sense, the city itself. In a city, peaceful people dwell; in the tabernacle, the Church Militant, which has peace here on earth from God, nevertheless suffers disquietude from the world. In the world you shall have distress, . . . but in me peace (John 16:33). Et ideo propter primum dicitur civitas: propter secundum dicitur tabernaculum. And therefore, something is called a city from the first quality, and from the second a tabernacle. Hoc ergo sanctificavit altissimus per proprium sanguinem. Hebr. ult.: Jesus ut sanctificaret per suum sanguinem et cetera. Therefore, the Most High has sanctified it through his own blood. Jesus, that he might sanctify by his own blood (Heb 13:12). Item per sacramenta in quibus operatur virtus sanguinis Christi. 1 Cor. 6: sed abluti estis, sed sanctificati estis, sed justificati estis. Likewise, he sanctifies it through the sacraments, in which the power of Christ’s blood is at work. But you are washed, you are sanctified, but you are justified (1 Cor 6:11). Vel aliter. In civitate et in exercitu est principale tabernaculum regis vel ducis: ita in hac civitate, id est Ecclesia, principale tabernaculum est corpus Christi. Et corpus tabernaculum dicitur. 2 Pet. 1: sciens quod velox est depositio tabernaculi mei. Or it can be taken otherwise. The principal tabernacle of a king or of a leader exists in a city or in the army; so in this city, that is, the Church, the principal tabernacle is Christ’s body. And this body is called a tabernacle: knowing that the laying away of my tabernacle is at hand (2 Pet 1:14). Et sic corpus Christi est tabernaculum, quia in eo est tota plenitudo divinitatis. Hoc tabernaculum sanctificavit altissimus: non quod aliquando non fuerit sanctus, sed quia in ipsa conceptione sanctum ipse formavit: quod fuit singulare in Christo. And the body of Christ is a tabernacle, because in it exists the entire fullness of divinity. The Most High sanctified this tabernacle, not because it was not holy before, but because he himself formed it as holy in its conception; which was unique to Christ. 465. Deus in medio ejus. Hic ponitur consolatio ex praesentia. 465. God is in the midst of it. Here, he describes the consolation from his presence. Et primo ponit beneficium hujus praesentiae; First, he describes the favor of this presence; secundo effectus, ibi, non commovebitur. second, its effect, at it shall not be moved. Dicit ergo, Deus in medio, scilicet Ecclesiae. 1 Cor. 14: vere protestans quod Deus est in vobis. Therefore he says: God is in the midst of it, namely, of the Church. Indeed, asserting that God is in you (1 Cor 14:25). Et dicit, in medio, ut ostendat quod non est acceptor personarum, ut dicitur Act. 10, et Eph. 6. Medium autem dicitur quod aequaliter distat ab extremis. Et Deus quantum est in se aequaliter se habet ad omnes: Luc. 24: stetit Jesus in medio discipulorum: Gen. 2: lignum vitae erat in medio Paradisi. And he says, in the midst, to show that he is not a respecter of persons (Acts 10:34; Eph 6:9). Now, the “middle” is what is equally distant from two extremes. And God, insofar as he is in himself, is the same toward all. Jesus stood in the midst of the disciples (Luke 24:36). The tree of life in the midst of paradise (Gen 2:9). Vel ideo dicit, in medio, quia cor dicitur esse in medio hominis. Quia ergo habitat in cordibus nostris, ideo dicitur esse in medio. Or he says, in the midst, because the heart is said to be in the middle of a man. Therefore, he is said to be in the midst because he dwells in our hearts. Effectus est duplex: The effect of God’s presence is twofold: stabilitas contra mala, steadfastness against evil things, et firmitas ad bona. and strength for good ones. Dicitur ergo, quia Deus est in medio ejus, scilicet istius civitatis, non commovebitur, id est firma reddetur et stabilis: Ps. 124: qui confidunt et cetera. Matth. 16: portae Inferi non praevalebunt adversus eam. Therefore he says that God is in the midst of it, namely, of the city: it shall not be moved, that is, it is strong and steadfast. Those who trust in the Lord shall be as Mount Zion (Ps 124:1). The gates of hell shall not prevail against it (Matt 16:18). Quantum ad secundum dicit, adjuvabit eam Deus: quod non solum oportet quod detur stabilitas contra mala, sed auxilium ad bona implenda. Dicit ergo, adjuvabit eam Deus, id est Ecclesiam, mane diluculo. In regard to the second effect, he says: God will help it, for it is needs not only steadfastness against evil things, but help to accomplish good ones. Therefore he says: God will help it, that is, the Church, in the morning, early. Per mane intelligitur principium boni operis. Quidam dicunt, quod principium boni est a naturali principio, sed finis et consummatio est a Deo. Sed contra est, quod hic dicit quod mane, id est ab exordio boni operis. Morning means the source of good works. Some say that good begins from a natural principle and that its end and consummation are in God. But the morning is contrary to this: from its beginning, a good work is from God. Unum exordium boni operis ex parte intellectus est cogitatio, aliud ex parte voluntatis: et haec duo sunt a Deo, quia, non sufficientes sumus cogitare aliquid a nobis (ut Apostolus dicit 2 Cor. 3), sed sufficientia nostra ex Deo est. Item in ipso est velle et perficere, ut idem Apostolus dicit Phil. 2. One source of a good work is on the part of the intellect, understanding; another is on the part of the will, and these are both from God, since we are not sufficient to think anything of ourselves, as the Apostle says, but our sufficiency is from God (2 Cor 3:5). Likewise in him is the capacity both to will and to accomplish (Phil 2:13), as the same Apostle says. Et per hoc quod additur, diluculo, designat quod Ecclesia juvatur per spiritualem illuminationem spiritus sancti: Isa. 60: surge, illuminare et cetera. And by the added early, he signifies that the Church is helped by the spiritual illumination of the Holy Spirit. Arise, be enlightened (Isa 60:1). Alia littera habet, vultu suo: et hoc refertur vel ad statum praesentem; et sic signat praesentiam auxilii ejus, quasi principaliter ei subvenientis, secundum illud Psalm. 79: ostende nobis, domine, faciem tuam, et salvi erimus. Vel ad statum futurum; quasi dicat: modo est in medio ejus: et permittit eam conquassari, sed in futuro praebebit ei auxilium aspectu vultus sui: Ps. 20: laetificabis eum in gaudio cum vultu tuo. Another text has by his countenance, and this could refer either to the present state—and then it signifies the presence of the help of the one who principally comes to his aid: show us your face, O Lord, and we shall be saved (Ps 79:4)—or to the future state, as if to say, “He is now in her midst and permits her to be shaken, but in the future he will furnish her with help through the vision of his countenance.” You shall make him joyful in gladness with your countenance (Ps 20:7). In Hebraeo est talis littera. Et sic pro eodem accipiatur mane et diluculo, scilicet considerationis diluculi, quia optima et ad contemplandum hora matutina; Ps. 5: mane astabo tibi. Item Ps. 62: in matutinis meditabor in te; quasi dicat, adjuvabit eam Deus vultu suo, id est in contemplatione suae altitudinis. This is the text in Hebrew. And thus morning and early are understood to mean the same thing, namely, the musing of early morning, since the best hour for contemplation is in the morning. In the morning I will stand before you (Ps 5:5). Likewise: I will meditate on you in the morning (Ps 62:7), as if to say, “God will help him with his countenance, that is, in the contemplation of his majesty.” Et possunt haec referri ad b. virginem, quia ipsa est civitas, in ipsa habitavit, ipsam fluminis impetus, scilicet Spiritus Sanctus, laetificavit, ipsam sanctificavit in utero matris suae, postquam formatum fuit corpus et creata anima. Tunc primo operuit gloria domini tabernaculum, ut dicitur Exod. 40. And these things can be referred to the Blessed Virgin Mary, since she is the city: he dwelt in her; the river’s stream, that is, the Holy Spirit, rejoiced her; he sanctified her in the womb of her mother, after her body was formed and her soul created. Then, at the beginning, the glory of the Lord came down upon the tabernacle (Exod 40:32). Et est alia sanctificatio b. virginis et aliorum sanctorum: quia alii sic sanctificati fuerunt quod nunquam mortaliter peccaverunt, tamen venialiter sic: 1 Joan. 1: si dixerimus quia peccatum non habemus et cetera. Beata autem virgo nec mortaliter nec venialiter umquam peccavit: Cant. 4: tota pulchra es amica mea et cetera. Et ideo dicit, non commovebitur, nec veniali peccato. And the sanctification of the Blessed Virgin was different from that of the other saints, since they were sanctified in such a way that they did not sin mortally but did sin venially. So: if we say that we have no sin, we deceive ourselves (1 John 1:8). But the Blessed Virgin never sinned either mortally or venially. You are all fair, O my love (Song 4:7). And this is why he says: it shall not be moved, not even to venial sin. Et ideo dicit, adjuvabit eam Deus mane diluculo, id est adhuc ea existente in utero. Et hoc est quod dicit, quod auxiliatus est ei dominus in ipso ortu matutino. And so he also says: God will help her in the morning, early, that is, from her coming to be in the womb. And this is why he says that the Lord helped her in the early morning. 466. Conturbatae sunt gentes. Supra psalmista posuit afflictionem aliorum et consolationem propriam sub similitudine; hic autem exponit primo quod dictum est 466. Nations were troubled. Above, the psalmist laid out metaphorically the affliction of others and his own consolation; but here he explains plainly what was said. de afflictione; First, about affliction, secundo de consolatione, ibi, dominus virtutum; and second, about consolation, at the Lord of armies; et ordinate exponit omnia. and he explains it all in an orderly way.
