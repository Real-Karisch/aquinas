      et ordinate exponit omnia. and he explains it all in an orderly way. Supra loquens de tribulatione, dicit, sonuerunt et turbatae sunt: ubi tetigit turbationem et causam, in fortitudine. Above, speaking about trouble, he says: they roared and were troubled, where he touched on the trouble and its cause, with his strength. Item turbationem aquae: et exponit cum dicit, conturbatae sunt gentes: Apoc. 17: aquae multae, populi multi. Likewise, he treated the troubling of the waters, and he explains it when he says: nations were troubled. The many waters are many peoples (Rev 17:15). Conturbati sunt, in malo, quia insanierunt contra Christum. They were troubled, in an evil sense, because they raged against Christ. Vel in bono: quia qui ad bonum convertitur, sentit afflictionem propter consuetudinem praeteritam. Or in a good sense, because those who are converted to the good experience affliction because of their former way of life. Item turbati sunt montes. Et dicit, inclinata sunt regna, id est reges terrae humiliati sunt ad fidem Christi: Isa. 49: vultu in terram demisso adorabunt te. Likewise, he explains the mountains were troubled by saying that kingdoms were bowed down, that is, the kings of the earth were subjected to the faith of Christ. They shall worship you with their face towards the earth (Isa 49:23). Et causam exponit, cum dicit, dedit vocem suam. And he explains the reason when he says: he uttered his voice. Supra dixit, in fortitudine: in veteri autem testamento dicit, dedit vocem, sed per prophetas sed postea per se ipsum. Psalm. 17: intonuit de caelo et cetera. Cant. 2: sonet vox tua in auribus meis. Above, he said: with his strength. In the Old Testament it says: he uttered his voice—this was first through the prophets and afterwards through himself. He thundered from heaven (Ps 17:14). Let your voice sound in my ears (Song 2:14). Ad hanc vocem, mota est terra, primo Judaeae, postea tota terra universaliter: Isa. 24, commotione commovebitur terra. At this voice the earth trembled, first Judea and afterwards the whole earth universally. With trembling shall the earth be moved (Isa 24:19). Quidam autem commoti sunt in malum per vocem Christi: sicut Pharisaei et alii multi. Quidam in bonum, sicut apostoli, et qui conversi sunt ad fidem. Now, some were moved to evil by Christ’s voice, like the Pharisees and many others. Some were moved to good, like the apostles and those who were converted to the faith. 467. Dominus virtutum. Hic exponit quod dicit de consolatione, scilicet quod dicit, Deus in medio ejus. Et describit Deum adjuvantem, et ejus praesentiam et ejus auxilium. 467. The Lord of armies. Here, he explains what he says about consolation, namely: God is in its midst. And he describes God helping, both his presence and his help. Deum describit dupliciter: quia dominus virtutum, He describes God in two ways, since he is the Lord of powers, quia dominatur non solum creaturis inferioribus, for he rules not only the lower creatures, sed etiam virtutibus caelestibus. but also the heavenly powers. Non erat sufficiens homini quod mitterentur angeli, quos misit ad dationem veteris testamenti; sed oportebat quod ipse veniret, ut innotescat, quod anima hominis est tantae excellentiae quod non nisi in Deo virtutum potest beatificari: Ps. 79: domine virtutum respice et vide et cetera. It was not enough for man that angels were sent, whom he sent to give the Old Testament, but it was necessary that he come himself, so that he might make known that the human soul is of such excellence that it cannot be made happy except by the God of powers. Lord, look down from heaven and see (Ps 79:15). Et est nobiscum primo per carnis similitudinem: Phil. 2: in similitudinem hominum factus. Item per familiarem conversationem: Baruch 3: post haec in terris visus est et cetera. Item per gratiam inhabitans nos: Ephes. 3: inhabitare Christum per fidem et cetera. Et ideo dicitur Emmanuel, id est nobiscum Deus. And he is with us through the likeness of our flesh, taking the form of a servant (Phil 2:7); and likewise through a shared life, afterwards he was seen upon earth (Bar 3:38); and likewise through grace dwelling in us: that Christ may dwell by faith in your heart (Eph 3:17). And so he is called Emmanuel, that is, “God with us.” Susceptor noster Deus Jacob. Hic ostendit auxilium quod habet a Deo, quia ipse susceptor noster est, qui suscepit nos in suam curam: Ps. 3: tu autem, domine, susceptor meus es. The God of Jacob is our protector. Here, he shows the help which he has from God, since he is our protector, who protects us with his care. You, O Lord are my protector (Ps 3:4). Vel ostendit quomodo sit nobiscum, quia scilicet suscepit nostram naturam: Hebr. 2: nusquam angelos apprehendit, sed semen Abrahae. Ideo dicitur Deus Jacob. Or he shows how he is with us by taking on our nature. Nowhere does he take hold of angels, but of the seed of Abraham (Heb 2:16). Therefore, he says: the God of Jacob. Deus virtutum dicitur propter gentiles, ne credant quod non habeamus alium Deum quam ipsi: Hebr. 2: non confunditur vocare eos fratres dicens: nuntiabo nomen tuum fratribus meis, in medio Ecclesiae laudabo te (Ps. 21). He is called the God of powers because of the gentiles, lest they believe that we have another God than him. He is not ashamed to call them brethren, saying, “I will declare your name to my brethren; in the midst of the Church I will praise you (Heb 2:11; Ps 21:23). 468. Venite et videte opera. Hic exprimit tranquillitatem subsequentem: et facit tria. 468. Come and behold. Here, he describes the resulting tranquility, and he uses a tripartite structure. Primo excitat attentionem. First, he arouses attention. Secundo proponit tranquillitatem. Second, he describes the tranquility. Et tertio inducit conclusionem. And third, he brings in the conclusion. Primo ergo inducit in considerationem divinorum operum; et ideo dicit, venite per fidem: 2 Cor. 5: per fidem ambulamus. Et videte, per studium et diligentiam: Matth. 11: venite ad me omnes et cetera. First, he leads us into consideration of the divine works, and so he says: come by faith. We walk by faith (2 Cor 5:7). And behold, by eagerness and diligence. Come to me all you who labor (Matt 11:28). Et videte, id est considerate: opera domini: Isa. 5: opus domini non respexistis. Dicitur peccatoribus Ps. 138: mirabilia opera tua et cetera. Quae posuit prodigia super terram. Haec opera sunt prodigia, quae praetendunt aliquid in futurum. And behold, that is, by considering the works of the Lord. The work of the Lord you regard not (Isa 5:12). This is said to sinners. Wonderful are your works (Ps 138:14). What wonders he has done upon earth. These works are wonders, which foreshadow something in the future. Et mirabilia quae tempore Christi fuerunt, sive circa mortem Christi, sive in vita quae fecit: quae omnia fuerunt signa alicujus futuri. Et sunt super terram, id est super omnem naturalem virtutem. They are also the wonders which were done in the time of Christ, either those surrounding the death of Christ or those which he worked during his life, which were all signs of future things. And they are upon earth, that is, above all natural power. Et quae sunt ostendit, auferens bella. And he indicates what they are when he says: making wars cease. Primo fuit opus pacis. First was the work of peace. Secundo ostendit magnitudinem operis hujus. Second, he indicates the magnitude of this work. Tertio utilitatem, abstulit bella, quia in tempore nativitatis Christi in toto mundo facta est pax: Ps. 71: orietur in diebus ejus justitia, et abundantia pacis: Ps. 75: ibi confregit potentias. Third, its usefulness, at he made wars cease, since at the time of the birth of Christ, peace had been achieved in the whole world. In his day justice shall spring up, and abundance of peace (Ps 71:7). There he has broken the powers (Ps 75:4). Et haec significabant pacem quam venit facere Christus inter Deum et humanam naturam. And these works signify this peace, which Christ effected between God and human nature. Magnitudo pacis ostenditur quantum ad locum, quia, usque ad finem terrae, quia fuit quasi universalis pax. The physical extent of magnitude of this peace is shown by saying: even to the end of the earth, since there was universal peace. Ad litteram, tempore nativitatis Christi: quia jam quiescentibus bellis civilibus, Octavianus dominabatur toti mundo. Et haec pax praetendebat quod pax Christi derivari debebat in omnibus hominibus. Luc. 2: gloria in altissimis Deo, et in terra pax hominibus. In the literal sense, this was at the time of the nativity of Christ, since then civil wars had ceased and Octavius had subjugated the whole world. And this peace foreshadowed that peace which Christ would extend to all men. Glory to God in the highest and on earth peace to men (Luke 2:14). Secundo quantum ad tempus, quia diuturna. Et hanc diuturnitatem describit, quia si homo timet bellum, conservat arma. Et ideo dicit, arcum conteret, et confringet arma; quasi dicat, tamdiu durabit pax, quod omnes tradent oblivioni arma: quia illa pax diu durabit: Isa. 2: conflabunt gladios suos in vomeres, et lanceas suas in falces. The magnitude of this peace in time is also shown, since it is enduring. And he describes this ending nature: if a man fears war, he preserves his weapons. And so he says: he shall destroy the bow, and break the weapons, as if to say, “This peace will last so long that all will forget weapons. This peace will last so long that they shall turn their swords into plowshares, and their spears into sickles (Isa 2:4).” Et describit arma impugnationis quae a remotis percutiunt: unde dicit, arcum conteret: Osee 1: conteram arcum Israel in valle Jesrael. And he describes the weapons of attack which wound from afar: he shall destroy the bow. I will break in pieces the bow of Israel in the valley of Jezreel (Hos 1:5). Et quandoque de prope: et hic est gladius et lancea. And some wound from nearby, and these are the sword and lance.
