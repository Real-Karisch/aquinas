      Dicit ergo, non salvatur rex. Sed quia potestas saecularis est triplex: Therefore, he says, the king is not saved. Secular power is threefold. una quae consistit in multitudine subditorum, There is one type which consists in having many subordinates, alia in robore corporis, another in strength of body, et alia in divitiis exterioribus; and another in exterior riches. ideo ostendit quod nullum eorum potest perducere ad salutem. And so he shows that none of these can bring someone to salvation. Et primo de prima potestate, et haec est regia; et ideo dicit, non salvatur rex per multam virtutem. Hieronymus habet, in multitudine et cetera. Ps. 145: nolite confidere in principibus, in filiis hominum, in quibus non est salus. Immo si aliquando habent salutem, hoc est per Deum: Ps. 143: qui das salutem regibus. First, he treats the first power, and this is that of ruling. So he says, the king is not saved by a great army. Jerome has, in a multitude. Psalm 145:2–3 says: put not your trust in princes, in the children of men in whom there is no salvation. On the contrary, if some are saved, this comes through God, who gives salvation to kings (Ps 143:10). Secundo ostendit quod non est salus in robore corporis; unde dicit, et gigas non salvabitur in multitudine virtutis suae, id est roboris: Baruch 3: ibi fuerunt gigantes nominati, illi qui ab initio et cetera. Second, he shows that salvation is not found in strength of body, so he says, nor shall the giant be saved by his own great power, that is, by his strength. There were the giants, those renowned men that were from the beginning (Bar 3:26). Tertio, quod non in divitiis. Et ponit duo adminiculativa; scilicet equum, et abundantiam rerum. Third, he shows that it is not found in riches. And he mentions two types of property: horses and an abundance of things. Quantum ad primum dicit, fallax equus et cetera id est quantumcumque habeat bonum equum, tamen non potest salvari corporaliter vel spiritualiter: Prov. 21: equus paratur ad diem belli, dominus autem salutem tribuet. In regard to the first, he says, vain is the horse, that is, even though someone may have a good horse, he cannot be saved either corporeally or spiritually. The horse is prepared for the day of battle, but the Lord gives safety (Prov 21:31). Quantum ad secundum dicit, in abundantia autem virtutis suae non salvabitur, id est rerum exteriorum: Prov. 11: qui confidit in divitiis suis, corruet: Isa. 31: vae qui descendunt in Aegyptum ad auxilium in equis sperantes. In regard to the second, he says, neither shall he be saved by the abundance of his strength, that is, by his abundance of exterior things. He who trusts in his own riches shall fall (Prov 11:28). Woe to those who go down to Egypt for help, trusting in horses (Isa 31:1). Mystice, moraliter et allegorice sic exponitur, quod homo non salvatur propria virtute, quodcumque bonum obtineat. It can be explained mystically, morally, and allegorically that man is not saved by his own power, whatever good he may obtain. Est enim triplex bonum per quod videtur quis consequi posse salutem. For there are three goods through which he might seem to possess salvation. Primum est potentia; et quantum ad hoc dicit, non salvatur rex per multam virtutem. Si vero sit potens ut regat alios, hoc non est per virtutem suam, sed habet a Deo. The first is power, and in regard to this he says, the king is not saved by a great army. It may be true that he has the power to rule others, but this does not come from his own power; rather, he has it from God. Secundum est constantia; et hanc non habet per virtutem suam: unde dicit, et gigas non salvabitur in multitudine virtutis suae. The second is firmness, and he does not have this from his own power, so he says, nor shall the giant be saved by his own great strength. Tertium est bona dispositio corporis et fortitudo; unde dicit, fallax equus, scilicet corpus forte et robustum est fallax. Third is a good disposition of body and courage, so he says, vain is the horse; namely, a strong and robust body is vain. Vel est universaliter, in abundantia virtutis suae, id est undecumque habeat aptitudinem ad bonum, non salvatur nisi ei Deus salutem tribuat: Ps. 29: ego dixi in abundantia mea, non movebor in aeternum. Avertisti et cetera. Hoc est quod dicitur Hier. 9: non glorietur sapiens in sapientia sua, et non glorietur fortis in fortitudine sua, et non glorietur dives in divitiis suis. Or, by the abundance of his strength is said in general to mean that, no matter what aptitude for the good he has, he will not be saved unless God gives him salvation. I said in my abundance, I shall never be moved. You turned away your face . . . and I became troubled (Ps 29:7–8). This is what is said, let not the wise man glory in his wisdom, and let not the strong man glory in his strength, and let not the rich man glory in his riches (Jer 9:23). 321. Deinde cum dicit, ecce oculi, ostenditur efficacia divinae misericordiae ad salvandum. 321. Afterwards, when he says, behold, the eyes of the Lord, he shows the effectiveness of the divine mercy to save. Et primo ponit misericordiam salvantem. First, he describes the saving mercy; Secundo affectum sanctorum ex hac consideratione conceptum, ibi, anima nostra. second, the affection of the saints which is engendered by considering this, at our soul waits for the Lord. Circa primum tria facit. He makes three points about the effectiveness of mercy. Primo enim ostendit divinam misericordiam. First, he shows the divine mercy; Secundo, in quibus habet effectum divina misericordia, ibi, super metuentes eum. second, those things in which the divine mercy has an effect, at on those who fear him; Tertio, quem effectum habet, ibi, ut eruat. third, what effect it has, at to deliver. Dicit ergo, ecce oculi domini. Divinam enim misericordiam insinuat per respectum Dei. Ps. 118: aspice in me, et miserere mei. Therefore, he says, behold, the eyes of the Lord. He treats the divine mercy in respect to God. Look upon me and have mercy on me (Ps 118:132). In quo autem respicit, subdit, super metuentes et cetera. Habac. 1: mundi sunt oculi tui ne videas malum, et respicere ad iniquitatem non poteris. Respice ergo super eos, qui timorem habent et spem. Unum sine altero non sufficit; quia timor sine spe desperat, et spes sine timore praesumit. In regard to those upon whom he looks down, he adds, on those who fear him. Your eyes are too pure to behold evil and you cannot look upon iniquity (Hab 1:13). Therefore, look down upon those who have fear and hope. One without the other does not suffice, since fear without hope despairs, and hope without fear presumes. Timor autem consurgit ex consideratione divinae potestatis. Hier. 10: quis non timebit te o rex gentium? Spes vero consurgit ex Dei misericordia. Ex primo consurgit fuga peccati, ex secundo spes veniae. Now fear arises from the consideration of the divine power. Who shall not fear you, O king of the nations? (Jer 10:7). But hope arises from the mercy of God. Fear of sinning arises from the first; from the second, hope of forgiveness. Effectum autem divinae misericordiae ostendit cum dicit, ut eruat a morte et cetera. Now he shows the effect of the divine mercy when he says, to deliver their souls from death and feed them in famine. Ubi duplicem effectum ostendit: Here he shows a twofold effect: quia liberat a malo; et quantum ad hoc dicit, ut eruat a morte. since it delivers from evil, and in regard to this he says, to deliver from death. Item confirmat in bono; et quantum ad hoc dicit, et alat eos. It also confirms in the good, and in regard to this he says, and feed them. Dicit ergo, ut eruat a morte animas eorum, a morte corporali, et morte peccati, et a morte futurae damnationis in resurrectione. Oseae 13: de manu mortis liberabo eos et cetera. Therefore, he says, to deliver their souls from death, from corporeal death and from the death of sin and from the death of future damnation in the resurrection. I will deliver them out of the hand of death (Hos 13:14). Confirmat etiam in bono: unde ait: et alat eos in fame, id est in necessitate; et loquitur de alimento corporali. Ps. 144: oculi omnium in te sperant, domine, et tu das illis escam in tempore opportuno. Now, he confirms them in the good, so he says, and feed them in famine, that is, in their need, and this is said about the nourishment of the body. The eyes of all hope in you, O Lord, and you give them food in due season (Ps 144:15). Et de alimento spirituali. Deut. 8: non in solo pane vivit homo, sed in omni verbo quod procedit de ore Dei. And it is about spiritual nourishment. Not in bread alone does man live, but in every word that proceeds from the mouth of God (Deut 8:3; Matt 4:4). Et de alimento sacramentali. Jo. 6: caro mea vere est cibus. In loco pascuae ibi me collocavit: Ps. 22. And it is about sacramental nourishment. For my flesh is food indeed (John 6:56). He has set me in a place of pasture (Ps 22:2). 322. Consequenter cum dicit, anima, ostendit quis effectus sequitur in istis ex hac consideratione. 322. Afterwards, when he says, our soul waits for the Lord, he shows the effect which follows in them from this consideration. Et est duplex. And it is twofold.
