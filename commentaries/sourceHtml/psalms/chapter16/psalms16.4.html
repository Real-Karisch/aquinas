      Sed est quaestio contra psalmum (Ps. 75): tu terribilis es, et quis resistet tibi? Nullus ergo suae voluntati potest contradicere: Job 9: Deus cujus irae nullus potest resistere. But there is a question in a psalm that contradicts this: you are terrible, and who shall resist you? (Ps 75:8). Therefore, no one can contradict his will. God, whose wrath no man can resist (Job 9:13). Et dicendum, quod nullus efficaciter potest resistere ei, sed potest habere voluntatem sive propositum resistendi. And it should be said that no one can resist him effectively, but someone can have the will or purpose to resist. Consequenter ponit modum liberationis, quia diligenter et tute: ideo dicit custodi me ut pupillam oculi. Pupilla oculi cum diligentia custoditur, quia nihil quod laedere possit permittitur appropinquare; sic et facit Deus in custodia servi sui: Deut. 32: circumduxit eum, et docuit et custodivit quasi pupillam oculi sui: Zach. 2: qui vos tetigerit, tangit pupillam oculi mei. Afterwards, he puts down the manner of deliverance, which is carefully and safely, and so he says, keep me as the apple of your eye. The apple of the eye is guarded carefully, since nothing which could damage it is allowed to come close to it; so also God acts in guarding his servant. He led him about and taught him, and he kept him as the apple of his eye (Deut 32:10). He who touches you touches the apple of my eye (Zech 2:8). Vel secundum glossam, pupilla oculi dicitur Christus dirigens: Eccl. 3: virtus visiva est in pupilla qua discernimus bonum a malo, et Christus discernit fideles ab infidelibus, et a bonis malos, Or, according to the gloss, Christ who directs is called the apple of the eye. The power of sight by which we discern good from evil is in the pupil, and Christ distinguishes the faithful from the faithless and the evil from the good. ad hanc diligentem custodiam manifestandam utitur duplici metaphora: scilicet umbrae et alarum. To show this careful guarding a twofold metaphor is used: that of a shadow and that of wings. Umbra enim refrigerat ab aestu, sic et tutela Dei refrigerat dans securitatem. For a shadow refreshes in the heat, just as the guardianship of God refreshes by giving safety. Item alis gallina pullos contra milvum custodit; sic et Deus suis alis, quae sunt caritas et misericordia, justos defendit a rapacitate Daemonum. Matth. 23: quoties volui congregare vos, quemadmodum gallina congregat pullos sub alas, et noluistis? His ergo alis Deus nos elevat ad superna: Ps. 88: misericordia et veritas praecedent faciem tuam, beatus populus et cetera. Hier. 31: in caritate perpetua dilexi te, ideo attraxi te miserans. Also, just as a hen guards her chicks against the bird of prey, so also God’s wings (which are love and mercy) defend the just from the rapacious demon. How often would I have gathered you together as the hen gathers her chicks under her wings, and you would not? (Matt 23:37). Therefore, these wings of God lift us up to the highest things. Mercy and truth shall go before your face, blessed is the people (Ps 88:15). I have loved you with an everlasting love; therefore I have drawn you, taking pity on you (Jer 31:3). Vel pupilla oculi dicitur anima; quia sicut pupilla quae est in medio oculi, circumdatur multis pellibus ad defensionem, et homo apponit manum, et fere omne quod habet, ne laedatur; sic debet homo facere pro anima: Job 2: pellem pro pelle et cetera: quia, ut dicitur Marc. 8, quid prodest homini si mundum universum lucretur et animae et cetera. Or the apple of the eye is said about the soul, since, just as the pupil which is in the middle of the eye is encircled by much skin to protect it, and a man uses his hand and almost everything he has so that it will not be hurt, so a man should act for the sake of his soul. Skin for skin, and all that a man has he will give for his life (Job 2:4), since, as is said, what shall it profit a man, if he gain the whole world, and suffer the loss of his soul? (Mark 8:36). Vel custodi me, ut pupillam oculi, id est ut Christum, sub umbra alarum tuarum protege me, id est sub custodia angelorum: Ps. 90: angelis suis mandavit de te et cetera. Or, keep me as the apple of your eye, that is, as Christ, protect me under the shadow of your wings, that is, under the protection of the angels. He has given his angels charge over you (Ps 90:11). Vel duae alae sunt duo brachia Christi extenta in cruce: Deut. 32: expandit alas suas, et assumpsit eos atque portavit in humeris suis. Or the two wings are the two arms of Christ extended on the Cross. He spread his wings, and has taken him and carried him on his shoulders (Deut 32:11). Consequenter ostendit a quibus competit liberari, quia a facie impiorum qui me afflixerunt; id est a potestate et praesentia Daemonum, vel falsorum fratrum. 2 Cor. 11: periculis in falsis fratribus. Afterwards, he shows from what he needs to be delivered: from the face of the wicked who have afflicted me, that is, from the power and presence of the demons or from false brothers. In perils from false brothers (2 Cor 11:26). Qui me afflixerunt, tentationibus et persecutionibus. Exod. 1: oderant filios Israel Aegyptii, et affligebant illudentes eis. Who have afflicted me, by temptation and persecution. The Egyptians hated the children of Israel and afflicted them and mocked them (Exod 1:13). Sic nos debemus petere liberari a peccato: Eccl. 21: quasi a facie colubri fuge peccatum. So we should ask to be freed from sin. Flee sin as from the face of a serpent (Sir 21:2). 118. Inimici. Hic ponit necessitatem liberationis: et circa hoc duo facit. 118. My enemies have surrounded. Here, he describes his need to be delivered, and he considers two things about this. Primo proponit afflictionem quam patitur. First, he describes the affliction which he is suffering; Secundo afflictionis similitudinem, ibi, susceperunt me. and second, gives an image of the affliction, at they have taken me as a lion. Circa primum duo facit. He describes two aspects of his affliction. Primo praemittit afflictionem. First, he mentions the affliction; Secundo afflictionis modum, ibi, adipem et cetera. and second, the manner of the affliction, at their fat. Dicit ergo, inimici, Daemones, sive peccata ita affligunt me, quod, circumdederunt animam meam, id est sic undique concludunt, quod non inveniam viam liberationis. Therefore, he says, my enemies, the demons or the sins, so afflict me that they have surrounded my soul, that is, they have so enclosed me that I cannot find the way to freedom. Et dicit, animam, quia nihil quaerunt nisi animam. Hostes corporales quaerunt tollere vitam; hostes vero spirituales quaerunt animam. And he says, my soul, because they seek nothing but the soul. Bodily enemies seek to take away the life; spiritual enemies seek the soul. Vel potest intelligi de Christo, cujus animam Judaei suis malitiis circumdabant; Ps. 117: circumdederunt me sicut apes et cetera. Item Ps. 21: circumdederunt me canes multi, Concilium malignantium obsedit me. Or, this can be understood about Christ, whose soul the Jews surrounded with their wickedness. They surrounded me like bees (Ps 117:2). Likewise: many dogs have encompassed me; the council of the malignant has besieged me (Ps 21:17). Consequenter ponit modum; unde dicit: adipem. Adeps in Scriptura quandoque in bono, quandoque in malo accipitur. Afterwards, he describes the manner of his affliction, so he says, they have shut up their fat. In Scripture, fat is sometimes understood to be good, and sometimes understood to be bad. In bono, secundum quod signat devotionem mentis: Ps. 62: sicut adipe et pinguedine repleatur anima mea. When good, it signifies devotion of the mind. Let my soul be filled as with marrow and fatness (Ps 62:6). In malo. It is understood to be bad when it signifies wickedness: Primo secundum quod signat nequitiam cordis. first, of the heart; Secundo oris. second, of the mouth; Tertio operis: and third, of deeds. et ideo designat detestabilem malitiam: Job 21: viscera impii plena sunt adipe, et medullis ossa illius irrigantur. And so it designates detestable evil. His bowels are full of fat, and his bones are moistened with marrow (Job 21:24). Et hoc est multiplex. And this is complex. Quandoque delectatio de peccato quod faciunt: Prov. 2: qui laetantur cum male fecerint, et exultant in rebus pessimis. Sometimes it is delight in the sin which they do. Who are glad when they have done evil, and rejoice in most wicked things (Prov 2:14). Item superbia et falsitas: Job 11: vir vanus in superbiam erigitur, et quasi pullum onagri se liberum natum putat. Likewise, sometimes there is pride and falsity. A vain man is lifted up into pride and thinks himself born free like a wild ass’s colt (Job 11:12). Item carnalis sensus. Dicit ergo, adipem suum, id est carnalem sensum, vel superbiam, vel delectationem: concluserunt, in se, ut non capiant spiritualem sensum. Likewise, there is sometimes a carnal sensibility. Therefore, he says, they have shut up, that is, in themselves, so they might not grasp the spiritual sense, their fat, that is, their carnal sensibility or pride, or delight. Hieronymus habet, adipe suo, id est abundantia temporalium et saecularis potestatis, concluserunt me. Jerome has, by their fat, that is, by their abundance of temporal things and worldly power, they have shut me up. Secundo, quoad os, quia, os eorum locutum est superbiam. Et hoc quando Judaei dicebant contra Christum; Matth. 27: si es rex Israel et cetera. Second, fat can refer to the mouth: their mouth has spoken proudly. And this took place when the Jews spoke against Christ. If you are the king of Israel, save yourself (Luke 23:37; Matt 27:42). Tertio quoad opus. Third, fat may refer to work.
