      Tertio quoad opus. Third, fat may refer to work. Et primo ostendit quomodo procedit ad opus. And first, he shows how one advances to work. Secundo causam hujus, ibi, oculos suos. Second, he shows its cause: their eyes. In operatione autem duo ponit. Now, in their working two things are described: Primo defectum. first, a defect; Secundo solicitudinem nocendi: and second, the intention to harm. et tamen quando quis contemnit, non solicitatur nocere: et ideo dicit, projicientes, id est despicientes: Isa. 33: projecit civitates, non reputavit homines, et tamen circumdederunt me undique solicite. Et hoc fecerunt Judaei Christo, quando projecerunt eum extra civitatem: Luc. 4. Now, although someone may hold someone else in contempt, he need not intend to do him harm, and so he says, they have cast me forth, that is, they look down on me. He has rejected the cities, he has not regarded the men (Isa 33:8); they compassed me with anxiety on every side (Sir 51:10). And the Jews did this to Christ when they cast him out of their city (Luke 4:29). Et circumdederunt me, convenientes ad spectaculum ut irriderent, Act. 7. Et hujus ratio est, quia non respiciunt ad Deum, sed ad terrena: Ps. 3: non est salus illi in Deo ejus. And they have surrounded me, gathering to look and to mock as in Acts 7. And the reason for this is that they did not look to God but to earthly things. There is no salvation for him in God (Ps 3:3). Statuerunt oculos suos declinare in terram, scilicet peccatores statuerunt intentionem cordis sui declinare in terram, cum deliberatione et mora: Prov. 17: oculi stultorum, id est peccatorum, in finibus terrae, et ideo non recipiunt lumen gratiae: Eccl. 2: oculi sapientis in capite ejus; stultus, id est peccator, in tenebris id est in peccatis, ambulat. Dan. 13: declinaverunt oculos suos ut non viderent caelum. Et hoc ad litteram fuit in Judaeis, cum dicebant, Joan. 11: ne forte veniant Romani, et tollant locum nostrum et gentem. They have set their eyes bowing down to the earth, that is, sinners have fixed the intention of their heart bowing down towards the earth with deliberation and delay. The eyes of fools, that is, of sinners, are in the ends of the earth (Prov 17:24), and so they do not receive the light of grace. The eyes of a wise man are in his head; the fool, that is, the sinner, walks in darkness, that is, in sins (Eccl 2:14). They turned away their eyes that they might not look to heaven (Dan 13:9). And this literally took place among the Jews, when they said, lest the Romans come and take away our place and our nation (John 11:48). Vel in terram, id est in carnem Christi, cujus infirmitatem tantum considerabant, et non ejus divinitatem: quasi dicat: statuerunt oculos suos et cetera. Or, they have set their eyes bowing to the earth, that is, to the flesh of Christ, whose weakness they considered and not his divinity—as if to say, they have set their eyes bowing down to the earth by thinking Christ is man and not God. De industria similitudo ponitur quantum ad violentiam, quia, sicut leo paratus ad praedam susceperunt me, vel a Deo, vel a Pilato milites: quantum ad fraudulentiam, quia, sicut catulus leonis habitans in abditis. Leo in agro invadit: sed catulus ejus in occulto morans, raptam praedam comedit vel invadit: Matth. 26. Osculo enim fuit traditus, de nocte captus, per falsos testes condemnatus, et princeps sacerdotum scidit vestimenta sua. A likeness to their activity is given that expresses violence, since as a lion prepared for the prey they have taken me, either from God or the soldiers from Pilate. The likeness expressed deception, since as a young lion dwelling in secret places. A lion attacks in an open field, but a young lion cub waits hidden to feed or to attack the prey already seized. For he was betrayed with a kiss on the night he was captured, condemned by false testimony, and the chief priest tore his garments (Matt 26). 119. Exurge. Hic ponit aliam petitionem, id est dejectionem inimicorum; 119. Arise, O Lord. Here he adds another petition, that is, for the downfall of his enemies. et ponit tria. He puts forward three things: Primo petitionem. first, his petition; Secundo expositionem, ibi, eripe animam meam. second, an explanation, at deliver my soul; Tertio petitionis rationem, ibi, de absconditis. and third, the reason for his petition, at from your hidden stores. Circa primum duo facit. He does two things in making his petition. Primo petit occultationem auxilii. First, he asks concerning the concealment of his help; Secundo destructionem adversarii. second, for the destruction of his adversaries. Dicit ergo: dormire videris dum pateris me affligi, sed, exurge domine, praeveni eum, ut citius subvenias quam nocere possit: et supplanta eum, id est destitue eum quasi astute: Job 5: qui apprehendit sapientes in astutia eorum: Prov. 19: astutia hominis supplantat gressus ejus. Therefore, he says, you seem to sleep while you allow me to be afflicted, but arise, O Lord, disappoint him, so you may help more quickly than he is able to harm, and supplant him, that is, make him fail as though by cunning. Who catches the wise in their craftiness (Job 5:13). The cunning of a man supplants his steps (Prov 19:3). Supplanta eum, in duobus: Supplant him in two ways: scilicet in mei liberatione; et quantum ad hoc dicit, eripe animam meam ab impio, quia contra justitiam persequitur me, et ideo impius est: Ps. 42: ab homine iniquo et doloso eripe me. First, by freeing me. In regard to this he says, deliver my soul from the wicked one, since he persecutes me unjustly and so is wicked. Deliver me from the unjust and deceitful man (Ps 42:1). Et hujus ratio est, quia anima mea est framea, id est gladius acutus ex utraque parte, qua destructus est Diabolus. Et hoc proprie dicitur de anima Christi: Isa. 27: in die illa visitabit dominus super Leviathan in gladio duro et forti. Dicit, frameam tuam ab inimicis manus tuae, supple, eripe: Ps. 68: intende animae meae, et libera eam. And the reason for this is that my soul is a sword, that is, a sword sharpened on both sides by which the devil is destroyed. And this is properly said about the soul of Christ. In that day the Lord, with his hard and strong sword, shall visit Leviathan (Isa 27:1). He says, your sword from the enemies of your hand. Add: deliver. Attend to my soul, and deliver it (Ps 68:19). Vel, eripe frameam tuam ab inimicis, id est aufer gladium et potestatem, quam habent a te: Sap. 6: data est vobis a Deo potestas. Et voluntatem quam habent a se: Zach. 13: framea suscitare super pastorem meum. Or, deliver your sword from the enemies, that is, remove the sword and the power which they have from you. Power is given you from the Lord (Wis 6:4). And they have their will from you. Awake, O sword, against my shepherd (Zech 13:7). Vel, supplanta eos, in eorum frustratione, et eripe animam meam ab inimicis manus tuae, id est Christi Filii tui; nam filius dicitur manus patris: Deut. 32: tollam in caelum manum meam, id est filium meum. Or, supplant them by their frustration, and deliver my soul from the . . . enemies of your hand, that is, of Christ your Son, for the son is said to be the hand of the father. I will lift up my hand, that is, my son, to heaven (Deut 32:40). Domine, a paucis de terra divide eos; quia propter hoc persequuntur me, ut regnum suum stabiliant. O Lord, divide them from the few of the earth in their life; since they persecute me in order to establish their own kingdom. Haec est duplex littera: There are two versions of this text. in psalmo Romano sic, o domine dispartire eos in vita eorum; quasi dicat, ipsi oculos habent ad terram, et ideo mala faciunt; sed tu exclude eos de terra quam dedisti eis. In the Roman version of the psalm, it has, O Lord, disperse them in their life, as if to say, “They have eyes on the earth and so they do evil, but exclude them from the earth which you have given to them.” Sed quomodo? Numquid ut vadant in unum locum? Non, sed dispartire eos per totum mundum. But how? Should they only go into one place? No, rather disperse them throughout the whole earth. Alia littera, domine a paucis de terra dimitte eos; quasi dicat: divide eos de terra, et a paucis, id est a societate electorum, in vita eorum, id est dum vivunt. Another text has, O Lord, send them away from the few of the earth, as if to say, “Divide them from the earth, and from the few (that is, from the society of the elect), in their life, that is, while they live.” Vel quia legitur quod imminente destructione sunt admoniti per angelum, quod fideles recederent et irent in regnum Agrippae. Et ideo, divide eos a paucis, id est Christianis, qui sunt reservati. Or, as is written, it is because they were warned of imminent destruction through the angel, so that the faithful left and went to the kingdom of Agrippa. And so, divide them from the few, that is, from the Christians, who are set aside. De absconditis tuis adimpletus est venter eorum, id est de peccatis non confessis: Prov. 28: qui abscondit scelera sua, non dirigetur: Job 31: si abscondi quasi homo peccatum meum, et celavi in sinu meo iniquitatem meam. Their belly is filled from your hidden stores, that is, it is full of unconfessed sins. He who hides his sins shall not prosper (Prov 28:13). If as a man I have hid my sin, and have concealed my iniquity in my bosom (Job 31:33). Vel hic ponitur ratio petitionis, et hujusmodi ratio est duplex: videlicet quia potest referri ad peccata, vel ad beneficia de quibus sunt ingrati. Or this is put here as a petition, and the reason for it is twofold, since it can refer to sins or to the favors for which they were ungrateful. Si primo modo, sic. Primo ponit abundantiam peccatorum. If it is understood in the first way, it is like this: first it describes the abundance given to sinners. Si secundo modo, ostendit quomodo beneficia Dei derivabantur ad filios. If it is taken in the second way, it shows how the blessings of God have flowed down to the sons. Dicit ergo quantum ad primum, de absconditis tuis adimpletus est venter eorum, id est de peccatis quae sibi abscondita sunt: non quod non videat, sed quia non vult ea videre: Habac. 1: mundi sunt oculi tui, domine, ne videat malum; et ad iniquitatem respicere non possunt. Therefore, he says in regard to the first, their belly is filled from your hidden stores, that is, from the sins which they have hidden—not that he does not see them, but that he does not desire to see them. Your eyes are too pure, Lord, to look upon evil, and they cannot gaze on iniquity (Hab 1:13).
