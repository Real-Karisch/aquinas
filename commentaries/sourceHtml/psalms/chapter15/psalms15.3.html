      Hieronymus habet: multiplicata sunt idola eorum: post tergum sequentium non libabo libamina eorum de sanguine, neque assumam nomina eorum in labiis meis; quasi dicat, te colo, non idola. Sed illorum idola multa sunt. Oseae 8: multiplicavi post dorsum recedentes a te. Hierem. 2: verterunt ad me tergum et faciem, nec ero particeps libaminum eorum. Deut. 32: de quorum victimis comedebant adipes, et bibebant vinum libaminum. Nec jurabo per illa idola. Jerome has, their idols have been multiplied; I will not offer their libations of blood behind the back of those following, nor take their names upon my lips, as if to say, “I worship you, not idols.” But their idols were many. I have multiplied (Hos 2:10) those going away from (Jer 17:3) behind your back. They have turned their back to me and not their face, nor will I participate in their libations (Jer 2:27). Of whose victims they ate the fat and drank the wine of their drink offerings (Deut 32:38). Nor will I swear by those idols. 112. Dominus. Hic assignat rationem suae inhaesionis soli Deo, quia scilicet ipse solus est hereditas sua; quasi dicat: ideo solum huic inhaereo, quia haec est hereditas mea. 112. The Lord is the portion. Here he gives the reason why he clings to God alone: that he alone is his inheritance, as if to say, “I cling to him alone because this is my inheritance.” Primo dicit Deum esse suam hereditatem. First, he says that God is his inheritance. Secundo commendat eam, quod est contentus de ea, ibi, etenim hereditas mea et cetera. Ipsa est bonum nostrum quo fruimur: homines in mundo isto quaerunt possessiones et usus earum, sed possessio sua est Deus; unde dominus, pars hereditatis meae, intransitive, id est hereditas quae venit mihi in partem. Second, he commends it, because he is content with it, at for my inheritance is goodly to me. This is the good of us who enjoy it. Men in this world seek possessions and their use, but his possession is God. Thus, the Lord is the portion of my inheritance, which cannot be given to another, that is, the heritage which comes to me as my portion. Quidam habent pro hereditate delectationes carnis. Sap. 2: haec est pars et haec sors nostra. Alii autem alia delectabilia mundi; sed Deus est sors mea. Thren. 3: pars mea dominus, dixit anima mea. Sed non solum est hereditas mea, sed, pars calicis mei, id est calix meus veniens mihi in sortem; quia tota delectatio mea et potus est Deus. Ps. 22: calix meus inebrians quam praeclarus est. Some have the pleasures of the flesh as their inheritance. For this is our portion and this our lot (Wis 2:9). Others have other delights of the world, but God is my lot. The Lord is my portion, said my soul (Lam 3:24). But he is not only my inheritance, but also the portion of my cup, that is, my cup coming to me as my lot, since my whole delight and drink is God. My chalice which inebriates me, how goodly it is (Ps 22:5). Vel Christus habet hereditatem fideles; et hujusmodi hereditatis, scilicet fidelium, Deus est pars, sicut dictum est, dominus pars calicis mei, quia passio mea ordinatur ad Deum. Ipse etiam est dator hujus hereditatis: tu es qui restitues hereditatem meam mihi, scilicet aeternae gloriae. Et sic Christus loquitur ex persona suorum, qui eam perdiderunt peccante primo parente. Or, Christ has the faithful as his inheritance, and God is the portion of this sort of the faithful’s inheritance, as it was said that the Lord is the portion of my cup, because my suffering is ordered to God. For he is the giver of this inheritance. It is you who will restore my inheritance to me, namely, eternal glory. And so Christ speaks in the person of those who lost it through the sin of the first parent. Vel, hereditatem, id est claritatem corporis, quam perdidit homo peccando. Or it is his inheritance, that is, the glory of the body, which man lost by sinning. Funes extenderunt mihi in praeclaris. Divites terram mensurant fune. Deut. 32: Jacob funiculus hereditatis ejus. Et ideo portio dicitur quasi funiculus funis, id est portio mea cedit mihi in rebus optimis, quia nihil melius ipso Deo. Hier. 3: tribuam tibi terram desiderabilem, praeclaram hereditatem. The lines are fallen for me in goodly places. The rich measure the land with a line. Jacob the lot of his inheritance (Deut 32:9). And so his portion is said to be the lot of lots, that is, my portion fell to me in the best things, since nothing is better than God. I will give you a lovely land, a goodly inheritance (Jer 3:19). Secundo ostendit quod sit ea contentus: etenim hereditas mea praeclara est mihi; quasi dicat: non solum hereditas mea in se praeclara est; sed est ita praeclara mihi, quod nullo modo mutarem eam. Ps. 131: haec requies mea in saeculum saeculi: hic habitabo, quoniam elegi eam. Second, he shows that he is content with this: for my inheritance is goodly to me, as if to say, “My inheritance is not only goodly in itself, but it is goodly to me, so that I would not change it at all.” This is my rest for ever and ever; here will I dwell, for I have chosen it (Ps 131:14). 113. Benedicam. Supra psalmista posuit rationem quare soli Deo inhaeret, quia scilicet ipse est pars hereditatis suae; hic recognoscit beneficia. 113. I will bless the Lord. Above, the psalmist gave the reason why he clings to God alone, which is because he is the portion of his inheritance; here he recognizes his favors. Et primo proponit beneficia suscepta; First, he describes the favors he has received; secundo speranda, ibi, insuper et caro mea. and second, those he hopes for, at my flesh also shall rest in hope. Circa primum duo facit. He makes two points about the favors received: Primo commemorat beneficia suscepta; first, he remembers the favors received; secundo ostendit gaudium quod habet ex eis, ibi, propter hoc laetatum est cor meum. second, he shows the joy that has come to him from them, at therefore, my heart has been glad. Commemorat ergo duplex beneficium: Now, he remembers two favors: unum in adeptione boni, first, in that he has achieved the good; aliud conservando contra mala. the other, in that he has been preserved from evil. Quantum ad primum dicit, benedicam dominum et cetera ut intelligam quam praeclara sit illa hereditas aeterna. Ps. 118: da mihi intellectum, et scrutabor et cetera. Ps. 31: intellectum tibi dabo, et instruam te. Eccl. ult.: danti mihi sapientiam dabo gloriam. Dedit autem dominus homini rationem ad sapientiam, sed non totaliter abstulit infirmitatem; sed hoc erit in gloria. In regard to the first, he says, I will bless the Lord, who has given me understanding, so that I understand that his eternal inheritance is goodly. Give me understanding, and I will search your law (Ps 118:34). I will give you understanding, and I will instruct you (Ps 31:8). To him who gives me wisdom will I give glory (Sir 51:23). Now, the Lord gave reason and wisdom to man but did not totally remove all weakness from him; but this will take place in glory. Et primo proponit eam; In regard to his preservation from evil, he first describes his weakness, secundo ponit auxilium contra eam, ibi, providebam dominum. and second, he describes the help given against it, at I set the Lord always in my sight. Omnis homo habet a Deo secundum rationem lumen intellectus, et justus reformatur lumine gratiae. Sed adhuc infirmitas carnis restat; et ideo dicit, insuper adhuc increpuerunt me renes mei, id est infirmitates meae, scilicet culpae, vel peccata. Every man has from God intellectual light according to the reason, and the just one is renewed by the light of grace. But the weakness of the flesh remains even then, and so he says, my reins—that is, my weaknesses (namely, my faults or sins)—also have corrected me. Et hoc, usque ad noctem, id est usque ad mortem, increpuerunt me renes mei, id est reprehensibilem me ostenderunt. And this was even until night, that is, until death, my reins also have corrected me, that is, they have shown me to be worthy of blame. Alia littera, quia in renibus incentivum luxuriae sedem habet, et sic delectationem tentando molestat. 2 Cor. 12: ne magnitudo revelationum et cetera. Another text has, that the incitement to lust has its seat in the reins, and so it disturbs inclination by tempting. Lest the greatness of the revelations should exalt me (2 Cor 12:7). Sed in Christo non sunt infirmitates culpae, vel infectionis, quia caro ejus non repugnat adversus spiritum; et ideo intelligitur solum de poena. Heb. 4: tentatum per omnia, quantum ad infirmitates corporales. But in Christ there was no weakness of fault nor of corruption, since his flesh does not rebel against the Spirit, and so this is understood to apply to him only in regard to punishment. Tempted in all things (Heb 4:15), in regard to bodily weaknesses. Sed si de nobis intelligatur, dicendum quod homo qui donum intellectus habet, vel gratiam, dicat adhuc cum apostolo, Rom. 7: video aliam legem in membris meis repugnantem legi mentis meae. But if it is understood about us, it should be said that a man who has the gift of understanding or grace may say with the apostle, but I see another law in my members, fighting against the law of my mind (Rom 7:23). Vel renes, id est Judaei sibi conjuncti, usque ad noctem, id est usque ad passionem, sive usque ad passibilitatem carnis. Or, the reins are the Jews who remained with him, even until night, that is, even to the suffering of his flesh. Et quia caro timebat passionem, ego in illa passione, providebam dominum, erectis oculis in caelum, non in mundo. And since the flesh fears suffering, I, in this suffering, set the Lord in my sight, by lifting my eyes to heaven, not to the world. Providentia est praevisio rerum fiendarum in futuro; sed visio sive conspectus est rerum praesentium. Sed si adhuc tales renes impugnant, non est timendum, quia paratum habet auxilium Dei. Now, providence is the foreseeing of things that will come about in the future, but vision or seeing is of present things. But if the reins now fight in this way, it should not be feared, since help from God has been prepared. Et ideo primo ponit recordationem auxilii, dicens, providebam dominum et cetera quando scilicet increpuerunt me renes mei. Ps. 24: oculi mei semper ad dominum quoniam et cetera. And so he first describes the remembrance of help, saying, I set the Lord always in my sight when my reins have corrected me. My eyes are ever towards the Lord (Ps 24:15). Et hoc ideo quia a dextris est mihi ne commovear, non ad sinistram. Prov. 3: dominus erit in latere tuo, et custodiat pedem tuum ne capiaris. Isa. 50: stemus simul et cetera. And so it is that he is at my right hand that I be not moved, and not at my left. For the Lord will be at your side, and will keep your foot so you be not taken (Prov 3:26). Let us stand together (Isa 50:8). Propter hoc laetatum est cor meum. 1 Reg. 2: exultavit cor meum in domino. Ps. 63: laetabitur justus in domino et cetera. Therefore, my heart has been glad, and my tongue has rejoiced. My heart is exalted in the Lord (1 Sam 2:1). The just shall rejoice in the Lord (Ps 63:11). Et exultavit lingua mea et cetera, exterius, cum exteriori gaudio prorumpit in laudem vocis. Isa. 12: cantate domino quoniam magnifice fecit. Ps. 80: exultate Deo et cetera. And my tongue has rejoiced, externally, since joy rushes forth exteriorly in vocal praise. Sing to the Lord for he has done great things (Isa 12:5). Rejoice to God (Ps 80:2). 114. Insuper. Hic enumerat beneficia speranda. 114. Moreover, my flesh also shall rest. Here he lists the favors to be hoped for: Primo quantum ad resurrectionem carnis; first, the resurrection of the flesh; secundo quantum ad animam, ibi, notas. and second, that of the soul, at you have made known to me.
