      scilicet signum spei, namely, the sign of hope, et ipsam spem. and hope itself. Signum spei, ibi, conserva me domine: quasi: non confido per me servari posse: sed tu, domine, conserva me, vel in se, vel in membris suis: Joan. 17: Pater, serva eos in nomine tuo, quos dedisti mihi. The sign of his hope is at preserve me, O Lord, as if to say, “I do not hope to be able to save myself, but you, O Lord, preserve me, either by yourself directly, or through your members.” Father, keep them in your name whom you have given to me (John 17:11). Et hoc, quoniam speravi in te. And there is this: for I have put my trust in you. Sed numquid speravit Christus? Dicendum est quod sic: speravit quidem pro aliis vitam aeternam, pro se autem claritatem corporis. Claritatem autem animae habuit in instanti suae conceptionis. But surely Christ did not hope? It should be said that indeed he hoped for eternal life for others and for the glory of the body for himself. He had the glory of the soul from the moment of his conception. Dixi et cetera. Hic primo ponit actum fidei. I have said to the Lord, “You are my God.” Here, he first describes the act of faith. Secundo rationem assignat, ibi, quoniam bonorum et cetera. Second, he assigns the reason: for you have no need of my goods. Actus fidei est confiteri Deo, vel in corde credendo, vel exterius laudando, factis approbando: Roman. 10: corde creditur ad justitiam, ore autem confessio fit ad salutem; et ideo, dixi, corde, ore, et opere, quia Deus meus es: Gen. 28: erit mihi dominus in Deum. It is an act of faith to confess God either by believing in the heart, or by praising him outwardly, or by acclaiming him through deeds. With the heart, we believe unto justice, but with the mouth, confession is made unto salvation (Rom 10:10). And so, I have said, in my heart, word, and deed, you are my God. The Lord shall be my God (Gen 28:21). Et ideo est, quia bonorum meorum non eges. Et hoc est proprium Dei: quia infinitae bonitatis est, et nihil ei addi potest, quia est substantiale bonum ad omnia extendens bonitatem sicut sol lumen, non per participationem, sed per ipsum esse illuminans omnia. And it is so, for you have no need of my goods. And this is proper to God, since his goodness is infinite and nothing can be added to him, since he is substantial goodness reaching out to every goodness as the sun does through its light, not by participation but through his very being illuminating all. Cuilibet autem alii creaturae potest addi, etiam sanctis, et propter hoc aliquid eis accrescit, et ideo aliquo modo indigent nobis: sed Deus solus non indiget bonis nostris: Job 35: porro si juste egeris, quid donabis ei, aut quid de manu tua accipiet? Now, addition can be made to any creature, even to the saints, and on account of this something increases in them, and so in some way they lack, but God alone does not lack our goods. And if you do justly, what shall you give him? Or what shall he receive from your hand? (Job 35:7). Hieronymus habet, quoniam bene non est nobis sine te: quasi, ex hoc apparet quia tu es Deus meus, quia tu es bonitas, nec mihi bene est sine te. Jerome has, for nothing is well for us without you, as if to say, “From this it is clear that you are my God, since you are goodness and nothing is well for me without you.” 109. Sanctis. Hic ostendit quomodo se habet ad sanctos, etiam ut legitur ex persona Christi. 109. To the saints. Here he shows how he is related to the saints, for this is read as though from the person of Christ. Et sciendum, quod voluntas Patris sicut voluntas Christi est, et in quantum homo, ut impleat voluntatem Patris: Ps. 39: ut faciam voluntatem tuam, Deus meus volui: Thess. 4: haec est voluntas Dei sanctificatio vestra: Jo. 6: descendi de caelo non ut faciam voluntatem meam, sed voluntatem ejus qui misit me, Patris, ut omne quod dedit mihi, non perdam, sed resuscitem illud in novissimo die. And it should be known that the will of the Father is the same as the will of Christ, and insofar as he is man, it is to fulfill the will of the Father. That I should do your will, O God, I have desired it (Ps 39:9). This is the will of God, your sanctification (1 Thess 4:3). I came down from heaven, not to do my own will, but the will of him who sent me . . . of the Father, that of all he has given to me, I should lose nothing, but should raise it up again on the last day (John 6:38–39). Christus autem multa voluit: et hoc propter nostram utilitatem. Now Christ desired many things, and this was because of their usefulness to us. Sed quid voluit? Pati, mori, resurgere, ut nos vivificaret. Dicit ergo: Deus Pater, omnes voluntates meas mirificavit, id est mirifice adimplevit, in eis, in quibus? Eis, in sanctis qui sunt in terra ejus, id est in Ecclesia militante et triumphante. Hieronymus habet sic, sanctis qui sunt in terra et magnificis, omnis voluntas mea in eis. But what did he desire? To suffer, to die, to rise, and to give us life. Therefore, he says, “God the Father has made wonderful all my desires, that is, he has fulfilled them wonderfully, in them.” In whom? In them, in the saints who are in his land, that is, in the Church Militant and Triumphant. Jerome has, the saints who are in the land and the noble, all my will is in them. Alia littera habet, robusti. Et dato quod aliquis terrenus confidat in potestate robusti exercitus, sed David dicit, ego speravi in te: Eccl. 2: nullus speravit in domino, et confusus est. Another translation has, the strong. And even though having been given what any earthly man would hope in—the power of a strong army—David says, I have put my trust in you, O Lord (Ps 30:15). No one has hoped in the Lord and been confounded (Sir 2:11). Et robusti mei sunt sancti tui, qui magna faciunt: Ps. 100: oculi mei ad fideles, dicit Christus, ut sedeant mecum. Quod ipse Christus diligat sanctos, patet: Prov. 8: ego diligentes me diligo. And my strong ones are your saints, who do great things. My eyes were upon the faithful (Ps 100:6), says Christ, that they may sit with me. Since Christ himself loves the saints, he shows himself. I love those who love me (Prov 8:17). 110. Multiplicatae. Hic ostendit quomodo se habeat ad adversarios Dei, sive ad peccatores. 110. Their infirmities were multiplied. Here he shows how he stands in regard to the enemies of God, or sinners. Et primo ponit eorum conversionem; First, he describes their conversion; secundo conversionis modum, ibi, non congregabo. second, the manner of their conversion, at I will not gather together. Circa primum duo facit. He makes two points regarding the first. Primo proponit statum praecedentis culpae; First, he describes their previous guilty state. secundo ponit statum subsequentis gratiae, ibi, postea acceleraverunt. Second, he describes their state after having received grace, at they made haste. Dicit ergo, multiplicatae sunt infirmitates eorum, id est diversa peccata. Therefore, he says, their infirmities were multiplied, that is, their various sins. Vel infirmitates, id est poenalitates ex peccato consequentes. Thren. 1: multi gemitus mei. Or, their infirmities, that is, the penalties following upon sin. My sighs are many (Lam 1:22). Et ex hoc sequitur, postea acceleraverunt, id est conati sunt ad curandum vitam, ut redimerent tempus perditum. Et signanter hoc faciunt, quia, ubi abundat delictum superabundat gratia: ut dicitur Rom. 5. Item post tribulationes homo currit ad Deum. Osee 6: in tribulatione sua mane consurgent ad me, venite, revertamur ad dominum. Ps. 82: imple facies eorum ignominia, et quaerent nomen tuum, domine. And from this it follows that afterwards they made haste, that is, they made an effort to improve their life so that they might redeem the time that had been lost. And that they do this is shown, since where sin abounded, grace did more abound (Rom 5:20). Likewise, after troubles man runs to God. In their affliction they will rise early to me, “Come, and let us return to the Lord” (Hos 6:1). Fill their faces with shame and they shall seek your name, O Lord (Ps 82:17). 111. Non. Hic ponit modum conversionis. 111. I will not gather together. Here he describes the manner of their conversion. Et primo ostendit ad quem ritum convertantur; And first, he shows to what rite they are converted; secundo, quomodo perfecte, ibi, nec memor ero et cetera. and second, how perfectly, at nor will I be mindful. Dicit ergo, acceleraverunt. Therefore, he says, they made haste. Sed quomodo convertentur? In veteri lege illi qui convertebantur, offerebant diversa sacrificia. Sed ego adunabo ex diversis partibus ad fidem; sed non ut effundant sanguinem: quia, ut Apostolus dicit Hebr. 10, impossibile est per sanguinem taurorum aut hircorum, auferri peccata. But how will they be converted? Under the Old Law, those who were converted offered various sacrifices. But I will make them one in faith from the various regions; but they will not shed blood, for, as the Apostle says, it is impossible that with the blood of oxen and goats sin should be taken away (Heb 10:4). Non congregabo conventicula eorum de sanguinibus, id est de ritu legis: sed haec congregatio est de sanguine novo, id est Christi. Hebr. 9: Christus semel oblatus est ad multorum exhaurienda peccata. Heb. 10: una oblatione consummavit in aeternum sanctificatos et cetera. Zach. 9: tu autem in sanguine testamenti tui eruisti vinctos tuos de lacu in quo non est aqua et cetera. I will not gather together their meetings for blood offerings, that is, by the rite of the Law, but this gathering comes about through the new blood, that is, the blood of Christ. Christ was offered once to exhaust the sins of many (Heb 9:28). By one oblation, he has perfected forever those who are sanctified (Heb 10:14). You also by the blood of your testament have sent forth your prisoners out of the pit wherein is no water (Zech 9:11). Sed quomodo perfecta erit ista congregatio? Quia non ero memor nominis illorum, quod habebant in statu peccati, quia unus dicebatur fornicator, alius latro. Sed nullus post conversionem debet sic nominari, quia hujusmodi nomina sunt deleta. But how will this gathering be made perfect? I will not be mindful of their names, which they had in the state of sin, since then one was called a fornicator, another a thief. But no one should be named this way after his conversion, since these sorts of names have been wiped out. Vel, non ero memor peccatorum in judicio, cum congregabo justos, venite benedicti, Matth. 25. Or, I will not be mindful of their sins in the judgment, when I will gather together the just: come, ye blessed (Matt 25:34). Et hoc per labia mea, vel per praedicatores meos. Sed dicetur eis, ite maledicti et cetera. Hier. 15: si separaveris pretiosum a vili, quasi os meum eris. And this is by my lips, or by my preachers. But it is said to them, go, you cursed (Matt 25:41). If you will separate the precious from the vile, you shall be as my mouth (Jer 15:19). Hieronymus habet: multiplicata sunt idola eorum: post tergum sequentium non libabo libamina eorum de sanguine, neque assumam nomina eorum in labiis meis; quasi dicat, te colo, non idola. Sed illorum idola multa sunt. Oseae 8: multiplicavi post dorsum recedentes a te. Hierem. 2: verterunt ad me tergum et faciem, nec ero particeps libaminum eorum. Deut. 32: de quorum victimis comedebant adipes, et bibebant vinum libaminum. Nec jurabo per illa idola. Jerome has, their idols have been multiplied; I will not offer their libations of blood behind the back of those following, nor take their names upon my lips, as if to say, “I worship you, not idols.” But their idols were many. I have multiplied (Hos 2:10) those going away from (Jer 17:3) behind your back. They have turned their back to me and not their face, nor will I participate in their libations (Jer 2:27). Of whose victims they ate the fat and drank the wine of their drink offerings (Deut 32:38). Nor will I swear by those idols.
