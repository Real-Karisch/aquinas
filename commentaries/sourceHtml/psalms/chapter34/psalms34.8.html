Obscuratus est sol et cetera. Unde revertentes percutiebant pectora sua. The first is that they were separated, that is, they became foolish in heart so they did not know the reason for what occurred, that is, for the miraculous elements in the Passion. Many bodies of the saints who had slept arose, and coming out of the tombs came into the holy city and appeared to many (Matt 27:52–53). The sun was darkened, . . . so they returned, striking their breasts (Luke 23:45, 48). Vel, dissipati, id est divisi: quia Joan. 7: alii dicebant, quia bonus est: alii autem dicebant, non, sed seducit turbas. Or, they were separated, that is, divided, since some said, “He is a good man.” And others said, “No, but he seduces the people.” (John 7:12). Et non sunt compuncti, id est conversi ad poenitentiam. Et hoc patet per effectum, quia tentaverunt me dicentes: Joan. 10: si tu es Christus, dic nobis palam. And they repented not, that is, by being converted to repentance. And this is shown through the effect, since they tempted me, saying, if you are the Christ, tell us plainly (John 10:24). Vel ad passionem Christi non sunt conversi. Nam post mortem ipsius adhuc insultabant ei dicentes verba blasphemiae contra Christum, quia dicebant verba irrisionis. Matth. 27: vah qui destruis templum Dei et cetera. Or they were not converted by the Passion of Christ. For after his death they insulted him saying blasphemous words against Christ, since they spoke words of derision. Vah, you who destroy the temple of God (Matt 27:40). Et quantum ad hoc dicit, subsannaverunt me. Subsannatio est irrisio quae fit rugato naso. Isa. 37: cui exprobrasti, et quem blasphemasti, et super quem exaltasti vocem tuam et cetera. Item verba indignationis. Matth. 27: recordati sumus, quia seductor ille dixit adhuc vivens. Et quantum ad hoc dicit, frenduerunt et cetera. Hoc est aprorum qui crudeles sunt, sic et Judaei. Thr. 2: frenduerunt dentibus, et dixerunt: devorabimus. And in regard to this he says, they scoffed at me. Scoffing is derision that involves wrinkling the nose. Whom have you reproached and whom have you blasphemed, against whom have you exalted your voice? (Isa 37:22). It also involves words of indignation. We have remembered what the seducer said while he was alive (Matt 27:63). And in regard to this he says, they gnashed upon me with their teeth. This is appropriate for those who are cruel, as were the Jews. They have gnashed their teeth and have said, “We will swallow her up” (Lam 2:16). 357. Domine quando respicies. Supra psalmista ex persona Christi, vel justi, nequitiam persecutorum et pertinaciam ostendit; hic autem contra utrumque invocat Deum. 357. Lord, when will you look upon me? Above, the psalmist, in the person of Christ or of the just man, showed the wickedness and obstinacy of his persecutors; here he invokes God against them. Et primo petit divinum auxilium; First, he asks for the divine help, secundo ostendit divini auxilii fructum, ibi, confitebor. and second, he shows the fruit of the divine help, at I will give thanks. Circa primum duo facit. He makes two points regarding the divine help. Primo petit accelerationem; First, he asks for him to hasten; secundo ipsum auxilium, restitue. second, for the help itself: rescue. Dicit ergo, domine quando respicies? In hoc ergo auxilii accelerationem exprimit, et affectum animi non valentis amplius pati moram; quasi in anxietate positi. Et ideo dicit, domine quando respicies? Hoc est enim proprie non ferentis moram. Ps. 41: quando veniam, et apparebo ante faciem domini? Therefore, he says, Lord, when will you look upon me? He asks for the help to hasten, and desires that his soul may suffer delay no longer, as though he is suffering in anxiety. And so he says, Lord, when will you look upon me? For this would be said by one able to bear no delay. When shall I come and appear before the face of God? (Ps 41:3). Et potest hoc dupliciter intelligi, secundum quod duplex est respectus Dei. And this can be understood in two ways following two different relations to God. Unus est misericordiae quoad justos ad salutem. Sap. 4: gratia Dei et misericordia ejus in sanctos ejus, et respectus ejus in electos illius. One is in regard to his mercy, which brings the just to salvation. The grace of God and his mercy is with the saints and he has respect to his chosen (Wis 4:15). Alius est quoad malos ad puniendum. Judith 9: respice castra Assyriorum nunc castra Aegyptiorum videre dignatus es, quando post servos tuos armati currebant. Another is in regard to him bringing the evil to punishment. Look upon the camp of the Assyrians now, as you were pleased to look upon the camp of the Egyptians, when they pursued armed after thy servants (Jdt 9:6). Et de utroque respectu potest intelligi si de Christo exponatur, domine quando respicies me, ut resuscites me. Ps. 12: respice et exaudi me domine Deus meus. Illumina oculos meos, ne unquam obdormiam in morte: ne quando dicat inimicus meus; praevalui adversus eum. And either way can be understood as meant about Christ: Lord, when will you look upon me, to raise me up. Consider and hear me, O Lord my God. Enlighten my eyes that I never sleep in death, lest at any time my enemy say, “I have prevailed against him” (Ps 12:4). Vel, quando respicies super adversarios meos. Habac. 1: quare respicis super iniquitates, sive inique agentes, et taces devorante impio justiorem se. Or, when will you look upon my enemies? Why do you look upon those who do unjust things, or act unjustly, and hold your peace when the wicked devour the man who is more just than himself? (Hab 1:13). Deinde cum dicit, restitue, ipsum auxilium quod petit, exponit: et in hac petitione dat duo intelligere: Afterwards, when he says, rescue, he expounds upon the help for which he asks, and he gives two things to be understood in this petition. primo scilicet necessitatem quam patiebatur; First is the need which he suffered; secundo malitiam adversariorum, ibi, a malignitate. second is the malice of his adversaries, at from their malice. Dicit ergo quantum ad primum, restitue, quia necessitas grandis est, nam periculosa res est, quia in periculo animae. Restitue, inquam, animam meam, corpori scilicet a quo separata fuit in morte, licet a divinitate non fuerit separata: Joan. 10: potestatem habeo ponendi animam meam et cetera. Hoc poterat potentia divinitatis, quae non est alia quam potentia Patris: unde ita facit a se quidquid facit, quod tamen habet a Patre. Therefore, in regard to the first he says, rescue my soul from their malice, since my need is great, for this is such a dangerous situation that my life is in danger. Rescue, I say, my soul, which was separated from my body in death, although it was not separated from the divinity. I have power to lay down my life (John 10:17–18). He could do this by the power of the divinity, which is nothing other than the power of the Father, since he acts so that whatever he does he has from the Father. Hoc etiam dicat quilibet vir justus, quando est in periculo, vel corporali vel spirituali; quasi dicat, libera me a periculis. This could also be said about any just man, when he is in either corporeal or spiritual danger, as if to say, “Deliver me from dangers.” Malitia hostium duplex. The malice of the enemy is twofold. Primo quantum ad fraudulentiam: unde dicit, a malignitate eorum, quia malitiose contra me procedunt: 1 Joan. 2: vicistis malignum. First is that which comes from deceit, so he says, from their malice, because they acted against me maliciously. You have overcome the wicked one (1 John 2:13). Item quantum ad crudelitatem, et a leonibus unicam meam, quia anima unica liberatur a leonibus, id est a Daemonibus, vel a tyrannis: Ps. 56: animam meam eripuit de medio catulorum leonum: Eccl. 51: a rugientibus praeparatis ad escam. Likewise is their cruelty: my only one from the lions, since his only soul is delivered from the lions, that is, from the demons or the tyrants. He has delivered my soul from the midst of young lions (Ps 56:5). From those who roared, prepared to devour (Sir 51:4). 358. Confitebor. Hic ponit fructum auxilii. 358. I will give thanks to you in a great church. Here he describes the fruit of help. Et primo ex parte sua. First, in regard to himself; Secundo ex parte hostium, ibi, non supergaudeant. second, in regard to the enemy, at let them not rejoice over me; Tertio ex parte justorum, ibi, exultent. third, in regard to the just, at let them rejoice. Fructus ex parte liberati est laus Dei; unde dicit, confitebor tibi, et laudabo te, quia liberasti me: Ps. 65: reddam tibi vota mea et cetera. Et si referatur ad Christum, fructus resurrectionis Christi est instructio Ecclesiae, et fides qua Ecclesia confitetur Deum: Rom. 10: corde creditur ad justitiam, ore autem confessio fit ad salutem. The fruit in regard to the one delivered is the praise of God, so he says, I will give thanks to you and I will praise you because you have delivered me. I will pay you my vows (Ps 65:13). And if this is referred to Christ, the fruit of the Resurrection of Christ is the instruction of the Church and the faith by which the Church praises God. With the heart we believe unto justice, but with the mouth confession is made unto salvation (Rom 10:10). Vel describitur Ecclesia ex multitudine credentium. Or, the Church is described as great because of the multitude of believers. Secundo ex virtute eorum. Second, this is because of their virtue. Quantum ad primum dicit, in ecclesia magna quidem numero et diffusione terrarum: Malach. 1: magnum est nomen meum in gentibus. In regard to the multitude he says, in a church great in number and spread throughout the earth. My name is great among the gentiles (Mal 1:11). Item magna constantia et virtute, quia portae inferni non praevalebunt adversus eam, Matth. 16. Ps. 39: annuntiavi justitiam tuam in ecclesia magna. Likewise it is great in constancy and virtue, since the gates of hell shall not prevail against it (Matt 16:18). I have declared your justice in a great church (Ps 39:10). Quantum ad secundum dicit, in populo gravi laudabo te. Gravitas quandoque sumitur in bono, quandoque in malo; quia similitudinarie est duplex proprietas in corporali gravitate. In regard to the second, he says, I will praise you in a strong, weighty people. Weight is sometimes taken in a good way and sometimes in a bad way, since it has two properties similar to corporeal weight. Una est ponderositatis, quae tendit deorsum; et haec est mala: quia homo dicitur gravis, secundum quod ad terrae ponderositatem et similitudinem tendit: Ps. 4: filii hominum usquequo gravi corde? Isa. 1: populo gravi iniquitate, semini nequam, filii sceleratis. One is ponderousness, which tends to sink down, and this is evil, since a man is said to be heavy when his ponderousness makes him like the earth and makes him tend towards it. O you sons of men, how long will you be heavy of heart? (Ps 4:3). A people laden with iniquity, a wicked seed, ungracious children (Isa 1:4).
