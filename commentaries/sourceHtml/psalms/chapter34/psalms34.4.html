      ex pulveris scilicet dispersione; quia siccus est pulvis, et in minima divisus: et ideo facile dispergibilis est. Et ideo dicit, fiant tamquam pulvis: ut sicut pulvis comminutus est in multis propter defectum humoris, ita sint et impii. First, the dispersal of the dust, since dust is dry and divided into tiny bits, and so is easily blown away. And so he says, let them become as dust, so that, as dust has crumbled into many bits because of its lack of water, even so are the wicked. Alia ratio est ventus commovens: unde ait, ante faciem venti. Ventus quandoque significat tribulationem: Joan. 27: tollet eum ventus urens et cetera. Quandoque ipsam tentationem peccati; Is. 64: iniquitates vestrae quasi ventus abstulerunt vos. Another aspect is the blowing wind, so he says, before the wind. The wind often signifies tribulation. A burning wind shall take him up (Job 27:21). Sometimes it signifies the temptation to sin. Our iniquities, like the wind, have taken us away (Isa 64:6). Tertio ex impulsu alicujus impellentis ventum: unde ait, et angelus domini coarctans eos, scilicet ad hoc ut totaliter dispergantur: nam sicut per auxilium angelorum aliqua prosperantur virtute majori, ita ex angelis bonis vel malis contingit quod tribulationes magis gravantur: nam Deus quandoque punit etiam per bonos angelos, peccatores: Isa. 37: egressus est angelus domini, et percussit in castris Assyriorum centum octogintaquinque millia et cetera. Third is the force of something pushing the wind, so he says, and let the angel of the Lord straiten them, namely so that they may be totally dispersed, for as others prosper with great strength through the help of the angels, so it is from the angels, either good or evil, that great troubles befall others. For God often punishes sinners through the good angels. The angel of the Lord went out, and slew in the camp of the Assyrians one hundred and eighty-five thousand (Isa 37:36). Deinde cum dicit, fiant viae illorum, ponitur similitudo de secundo impedimento, id est de processu viae; Afterwards, when he says, let their ways become dark and slippery, he uses an image about the second impediment, that is, to progress in their path, et tria impedimenta ponit in via. and he places three obstacles in the way. Primum est tenebrae: quia tunc de facili quis impingit. Fiant ergo viae illorum, id est processus, tenebrae. The first is darkness, since in it someone easily bumps into things: let their way, that is, their progress, become dark. Secundum est, quod sit lubrica et disposita ad casum: Thren. 4: lubricaverunt vestigia nostra in itinere viarum nostrarum. The second is that it be slippery and easy to fall. Our steps have slipped in the ways of our streets (Lam 4:18). Et tertium est, quod sit aliquis persequens et cogens, ut magis corruat. Et angelus domini et cetera. Prov. 17: Angelus crudelis contra eum mittetur. The third is that there be someone persecuting and pushing towards a great fall: and let the angel of the Lord straiten them. A cruel angel shall be sent against him (Prov 17:11). Dicit ergo, fiant viae illorum tenebrae, ignorantiae: Ps. 81: nescierunt neque intellexerunt, in tenebris ambulant. Et lubricum, id est vitia carnalia in quibus de facili cadunt. Et angelus domini persequens eos. Ipse Daemon tentans ad peccandum, vel angelus bonus permittens cadere, ut humiliati fortiores resurgant. Therefore, he says, let their ways become dark with ignorance: they have not known nor understood; they walk on in darkness (Ps 81:5). And slippery, that is, with the carnal vices into which they so easily fall. And let the angel of the Lord straiten them. This is a demon tempting them to sin or a good angel allowing them to fall so that they may rise up stronger in humility. 349. Quoniam gratis absconderunt. Supra psalmista petiit quod nocentes judicarentur, et expugnantes in expugnationem venirent; hic autem ostendit, quomodo merito haec patiantur. 349. For without cause they have hidden their net. Above, the psalmist asked that those harming him be judged and those attacking him be repulsed; now here he shows how they deserve to suffer these things. Et primo ponit culpam. First, he describes their guilt; Secundo poenam, ibi, veniat illis. second, their punishment, at let the snare . . . come upon him. Circa primum ponit primo malitiae interpretationem. About their guilt he first gives an explanation of their malice; Secundo executionem, ibi, supervacue. second, its execution, at without cause they have hidden their net. In primo ponuntur tria, quae aggravant culpam. In regard to their malice, three things are described which aggravate their guilt. Primum est iniquitas; et ideo dicit, gratis. The first is their iniquity, and so he says, without cause. Secundum est fraus; et ideo dicit, absconderunt. The second is their fraud, and so he says, they have hidden. Tertium est crudelitas: et ideo dicit, interitum laquei sui. The third is their cruelty, and so he says, their net unto destruction. Dicit ergo, quoniam gratis. Si aliquis intentat malum contra se nocentem, non videtur totaliter iniquum; sed quando contra eum qui in nullo nocuit, hoc omnino est iniquum: et hoc est quod dicit, gratis, scilicet sine offensa quam fecerim: Jo. 15: odio habuerunt me gratis: Isa. 52: Assur absque ulla causa calumniatus est eum. Therefore, he says, for without cause. If someone intends evil against someone harming him, it does not seem to be totally iniquitous, but when he intends it against someone who has not harmed him at all, it is completely iniquitous. And this is what he says, without cause, that is, without any offense I committed. They hated me without cause (Job 15:25). The Assyrian has oppressed them without any cause (Isa 52:4). Fraus vero notatur, cum dicit, absconderunt mihi, quia scilicet per fraudem intentant mala mihi: Thren. 3: ursus insidians factus est mihi, leo in absconditis: Psalm. 9: insidiatur quasi leo in spelunca sua. Now, fraud is noted where he says, they have hidden for me, since they intend to do evil to me through deception. He is become to me as a bear lying in wait, as a lion in secret places (Lam 3:10). He lies in wait like a lion in his den (Ps 9:30). Crudelitatem autem ostendit, quia parant insidias ad mortem: unde ait, in interitum laquei sui, id est abscondunt laqueum ad mortem: Prov. 1: veni nobiscum, insidiemur sanguini, abscondamus tendiculas contra insontem frustra, deglutiamus eum et cetera. Now he shows their cruelty, since they prepared ambushes to cause his death, so he says, their net for me unto destruction, that is, they have hidden a net to bring about death. Come with us; let us lie in wait for blood; let us hide snares for the innocent without cause (Prov 1:11). Secundo cum dicit, supervacue exprobraverunt, ponitur executio, similiter sine causa. Dicit autem exprobraverunt, quia opprobria intulerunt. Est autem opprobrium, quando quis imponit crimen alicui quod est contra ejus honorem. Next, when he says, without cause they have upbraided, their execution of their plan also is said to be without cause. Therefore, he says, they have upbraided, since they brought about reproach. Now, a reproach occurs when someone makes a charge against another which is against his honor. Aliquando imponitur crimen alicui, ut confundatur et abjiciatur. Sometimes when a charge is made against another, it is so that he may be confounded and cast down. Aliquando vero ut perdat vitam; et hoc est contra animam ejus: et ideo dicit, animam meam; quasi dicat, propter hoc fecerunt ut totaliter me vita privarent. Sic Judaei Christo imposuerunt crimina morte digna: Luc. 23: commovet populum, docens per universam Judaeam: Psalm. 68: improperia exprobrantium tibi ceciderunt super me. At other times it is so that his life may be destroyed, and this is against his soul, and so he says, my soul, as if to say, “They have done this so as to totally deprive me of life.” So the Jews imposed a charge deserving of death upon Christ. He stirs up the people, teaching throughout all Judea (Luke 23:5). The reproaches of those who have reproached you have fallen upon me (Ps 68:10). Sed hoc, supervacue, id est sine causa, sicut supra dictum est, scilicet gratis: Jo. 8: quis ex vobis arguet me de peccato? Ergo, supervacue, imposuerunt ei peccatum. But this is without cause, that is, with no cause, as was said above: without cause. Which of you shall convict me of sin? (John 8:46). Therefore, without cause they have imputed sin to him. Vel, supervacue, id est frustra, quantum ad eorum intentionem: quia intendebant fidem ejus destruere: Jo. 11: quid facimus quia hic homo multa signa facit? Si dimittimus et cetera. Tamen hoc consilium fuit supervacue, quia totus mundus post ejus mortem ad fidem conversus est. Or, it is without cause, that is, in vain, in regard to their intention, since they intended to destroy his faith. What do we do, for this man does many signs? If we let him alone so, all will believe in him (John 11:47–48). Nevertheless, this plan was without effect, since the whole world was converted to faith after his death. 350. Consequenter cum dicit, veniat illi laqueus quem et cetera, ponitur poena conveniens culpae: 350. Afterwards, when he says, let the snare come upon him, he describes the punishment which fits their guilt. et hoc, quia eodem judicio judicantur, quo judicare volebant: Matth. 7: in quo judicio judicaveritis, judicabimini. Isti fraudulenter intendebant mortem ipsius; ideo psalmista petit contra eos, vel conformando voluntatem suam voluntati divinae, vel praenuntiando. So they are judged with the same judgment by which they desired to judge. For with what judgment you judge, you shall be judged (Matt 7:2). These intended to bring about his death by deception, so the psalmist petitions against them either by conforming his own will to the divine will or by prophecy. Et petit tria: And he asks for three things: scilicet quod laqueus eis praeparetur, that a net be prepared for them, quod capiantur, that they will be caught, quod non evadant. and that they will not escape. Quantum ad primum dicit, veniat illi laqueus, scilicet populo, vel ei qui principalis est inter eos. In regard to the first, he says, let the snare come upon him, namely, the people or the leaders among them. Laqueus hic tripliciter potest intelligi. Snare can be understood here in three ways. Vel propter laqueum poenae, quia propter mortem Christi illaqueati sunt: Isa. 8: offendent ex eis plurimi, et cadent et conterentur et irretientur et capientur: quia captivi sunt apud omnes gentes. Quem ignoraverunt, dicit, quia nec suspicari quidem poterant talem poenam: Luc. 21: erit enim pressura magna super terram, et ira populo huic; et cadent in ore gladii, et captivi ducentur in omnes gentes: Matth. 24: erit enim tunc tribulatio magna, qualis nunquam fuit ab initio mundi usque modo, neque fiet. It is either the snare of punishment, since they were snared by the death of Christ. Very many of them shall stumble and fall and shall be broken in pieces and shall be snared and taken (Isa 8:15), since they were taken captive among the nations. He says, which he knows not, since they did not suspect this punishment. For there shall be great distress in the land, and wrath upon this people. And they shall fall by the edge of the sword and shall be led away captives into all nations (Luke 21:23–24). For there shall be then great tribulation such as has not been from the beginning of the world until now, neither shall be (Matt 24:21). Vel de laqueo culpae: 1 Tim. ult. qui volunt divites fieri, incident in tentationem, et in laqueum Diaboli. Quasi dicat, veniat illi laqueus culpae quem praevidere non potest; quia peccatori non videtur quod sit ligatus, sed quod faciat voluntatem suam; in veritate tamen laqueatus est, quia Prov. 5: funibus peccatorum suorum constringitur. Or it is said about the snare of guilt. Those who become rich fall into temptation and into the snare of the devil (1 Tim 6:9). As if to say, “This snare of guilt shall come upon those who could not foresee it,” because the sinner does not realize that he is bound, but what he believes to be his own will really is a snare because he is fast bound with the ropes of his own sins (Prov 5:22).
