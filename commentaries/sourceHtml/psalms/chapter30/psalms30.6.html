      Et signum hujus subdit, qui videbant me, id est qui considerabant hujusmodi opprobria culpae et poenae, fugerunt a me foras, quia noluerunt converti. Job 6: fratres mei pertransierunt quasi torrens. Ps. 87: longe fecisti notos meos a me. And the sign of this is added below: those who saw me, that is, who thought about the reproach of this sort of guilt and punishment fled from me without, since they did not want to be converted. My brethren have passed me by as the torrent (Job 6:15). You have put away my acquaintance far from me (Ps 87:9). Tertium est oblivio. Et hoc est quod ait, oblivioni datus sum. Sed quia homo quantumcumque diligat aliquem post mortem tradit eum oblivioni, ideo dicit, tamquam mortuus. Ps. 87: sicut vulnerati dormientes in sepulcris, quorum non es memor amplius. Third is being forgotten. And this is what he says, I am forgotten as one dead from the heart. But since every man who loves someone forgets him after death, he says, as one dead. Like the slain sleeping in the sepulchers whom you remember no more (Ps 87:6). Haec ad Christum referuntur in persona sua: quia factus est in opprobrium. Ps. 68: propter te sustinui opprobrium, operuit confusio faciem meam. These things can be applied to Christ in his own person, since he was brought into reproach. For your sake I have borne reproach, shame has covered my face (Ps 68:8). Item timor, quia fugerunt, discipuli. Likewise he has caused fear, since the disciples fled. Item, oblivioni datus, quia, sperabamus quod ipse redempturus esset Israel, Luc. 24. Likewise: I am forgotten, since we hoped that it was he who should have redeemed Israel (Luke 24:21). 282. Deinde adducit exemplum, factus et cetera. Factus sum tamquam vas perditum, id est inutile de quo non curatur. Hier. 51: reddidit me quasi vas inane, absorbuit me quasi draco et cetera. 282. Afterwards, he adds an example: I am become as a vessel that is destroyed, that is, useless because of what cannot be healed. He has made me as an empty vessel; he has swallowed me up like a dragon (Jer 51:34). Tertio probationem: et circa hoc duo facit. Third, he gives the evidence, and he makes two points about this. Primo enim inducit contemptum; First, he mentions the contempt; secundo persecutionem quam patitur, ibi, in eo dum convenirent. and second, the persecution which he suffers, at while they assembled together. Dicit ergo, factus sum opprobrium: quia audivi vituperationem, a viris meis. Hier. 20: audivi contumelias multorum et cetera. In circuitu. Augustinus: quandoque Christiani peccant; et infideles non dicunt, hoc faciunt mali; sed, hoc faciunt Christiani. Et si religiosi peccant, similiter. Et dicit in circuitu, quia non intrant ad veritatis considerationem: quia tunc dicerent, hoc faciunt mali Christiani. Ps. 11: in circuitu impii ambulant. Therefore, he says, I am become a reproach . . . for I have heard the blame from my men. I heard the reproaches of many (Jer 20:10) who dwell round about. Augustine says, when Christians sin, the unbelievers do not say, “The evil do this,” but “Christians do this.” And if religious sin, it is the same. And he says, who dwell round about, since they do not enter into the understanding of the truth, since then they would say, “Evil Christians do this.” The wicked walk around (Ps 11:9). 283. Consequenter cum dicit, in eo, ostendit quomodo patitur persecutionem, et aggravat ex multitudine persequentium, cum dicit, in eo dum convenirent simul, id est unanimiter, adversum me: hoc timendum esset valde. Eccl. 26: a tribus timuit cor meum; delaturam civitatis, collectionem populi, et calumniam mendacem et cetera. 283. Afterwards, when he says, while they assembled, he shows how he suffered persecution, and it becomes heavier because of the multitude of persecutors when he says, while they assembled together, that is, unanimously, against me. This should be greatly feared. Of three things my heart has been afraid: . . . the accusation of a city, and the gathering together of the people, and a false calumny (Sir 26:5–7). Ex crudelitate ipsorum, non enim ad leve convenerunt, sed ad occidendum: unde, accipere animam meam, id est vitam, consiliati sunt. Hier. 18: tu scis, domine, omne consilium eorum adversum me in mortem. Gen. penul.: in consilio eorum non veniat anima mea. They have not gathered together lightly, but out of their cruelty, to kill: to take away my soul, that is, my life. You, O Lord, know all their counsel against me unto death (Jer 18:23). Let not my soul go into their counsel (Gen 49:6). 284. Ego. Supra psalmista narravit mala quae patiebatur: hic autem refugium habet ad orationem. 284. But I have put my trust in you, O Lord. Above, the psalmist described the evils which he had suffered; now here he takes refuge in prayer. Et quia oratio vacua est nisi certae spei innitatur; ideo praemittit spem, et subjungit orationem, ibi, eripe me de manu et cetera. And since prayer is empty unless it rests upon a certain hope, he puts forward his hope and then joins a prayer to it, at deliver me from the hands. Circa primum duo facit. He makes two points about his hope. Primo enim ponit spem. First, he describes his hope. Secundo assignat spei rationem, ibi, dixi Deus meus. Second, he assigns a reason for hope, at I said, “You are my God.” Continuatio talis est, non dicit simpliciter, ego in te speravi, sed, ego autem et cetera. Glossa Augustini dicit, consuetum est, quod pueri vocantur mammontreca, quia volunt diu sugere mammas, sed matres opponunt amaritudinem ut abhorreant. Ubera nostra, quibus a domino lactamur, sunt prosperitas temporalis, et solatium amicorum. Quando videt Deus quod homines confidunt nimis in his et alliciuntur ab his, subtrahit ea, et ponit amaritudinem, ut ponant in solo Deo spem suam. The connection is such that he does not simply say, I have hoped in you, but rather, but I have hoped in you. The gloss of Augustine says, it is the custom that children who want to suck the breast for a long time are called nurslings, but the mothers put something bitter there so that they will be disgusted. Our breasts, by which we are nursed by the Lord, are temporal prosperity and the consolation of friends. When God sees that men trust either in these or are bound by these, he removes them and replaces them with bitterness so that they may place their hope in God alone. Sic ille advertens dicit, audivi, inquit, vituperationem: et ideo dicit, ego autem et cetera: quasi dicat: omnibus aliis contradicentibus et pugnantibus adversum me, solum in te fuit spes mea: Hier. 17: benedictus vir qui confidit in domino. But here, turning, he says, I have heard the blame of many who dwell round about. And so he says, but I have put my trust in you, as if to say, “Although all these are opposing me and fighting against me, my hope is in you alone.” Blessed be the man who trusts in the Lord (Jer 17:7). Ratio spei duplex assignatur. A double reason for hope is given. Una ex cura et providentia Dei. One comes from the care and providence of God. Alia ex potestate ejus. The other comes from his power. Confidit ergo in eo primo, quasi commissus divinae curae, et a Deo susceptus: Deus enim ex sua virtute curam habet universorum: Sapient. 12: nec enim est alius Deus quam tu, cui cura est de omnibus. Item ejusdem 14: tu ergo, Pater, cuncta gubernas providentia. Therefore, he trusts first in this, as though entrusted to the divine care and supported by God. For God, by his strength, has the care of all things. For there is no other God than you, who has care of all (Wis 12:13). Likewise: but your providence, Father, governs all (Wis 14:3). Unde Deus omnium, specialiter tamen aliquorum habet curam: 1 Pet. ult.: omnem solicitudinem vestram projicientes in eum, quoniam ipsi cura est de vobis. Et ideo specialiter Deus est eorum. Et ideo dicit, speravi in te, quia Deus meus es tu. The God of all nevertheless especially cares for some. Casting all your care upon him, for he has care of you (1 Pet 5:7). And so he is especially their God. And so he says, I have put my trust in you, because you are my God. Secundo ex potestate; quia nisi esset potens, deficeret spes. Sed non ita de Deo sentit; unde ait, in manibus tuis sortes meae, id est omnia bona quae mihi donantur, sunt in potestate tua: Ephes. 1: in quo et sorte vocati sumus praedestinati et cetera. The second reason for hope comes from his power, since if he were not powerful, hope would fail. But he does not think this about God, so he says, my lots are in your hands, that is, all the goods which you have given to me are in your power. In whom we are also called by lot, being predestined (Eph 1:11). Sciendum est autem, quod triplex est sors: Now, it should be known there are three types of lots: quaedam consultoria, some are cast for consultation, quaedam divinatoria, some for divination, et quaedam derisoria. and some for amusement. Communiter loquendo, sors nihil aliud est nisi quoddam indicium divinae voluntatis requisitum. Et ideo in glossa Augustini, sors non est aliquid mali, sed res in humana dubitatione divinam indicans voluntatem. Et ideo projicere sortes nihil aliud est, quam exquirere voluntatis divinae indicium. Ergo sors de sui ratione non dicit aliquid mali; sed efficitur mala ex duobus. It is commonly said that a lot is nothing but a certain requested indication of the divine will. And so in the gloss of Augustine he says, a lot is not anything evil, but is something indicating the divine will in human doubt. And so to throw lots is nothing other than to seek an indication of the divine will. Therefore, lots are not evil from their very nature but can be made evil by two things. Primo quando aliquis exquirit hoc indicium a quo non debet, ut a Daemone: et secundum hoc sortilegia damnantur inter alia quae pertinent ad infidelitatem. The first is when someone seeks some indication from one from whom he should not, as from a demon, and so this type of fortune telling is condemned with the other things that belong to infidelity. Secundo, si inquirat indicium in rebus, in quibus non est necesse. The second is if he seeks an indication in regard to things for which it is not necessary. In duobus debet inquiri hoc indicium: He should seek this indication in two things: in necessariis, in necessities, et in non valentibus aliter sciri. and in those he cannot know otherwise. Si enim non fuerit de necessariis, tunc est curiositas: et hoc est in sorte divinatoria: quia in hac solicitantur de futuris, et ideo vana, et reprobatur. For if it is not about necessary things, then it is curiosity, and this is what takes place in lots used in divination, since in this they seek to know future things, and so it is vain and to be condemned.
