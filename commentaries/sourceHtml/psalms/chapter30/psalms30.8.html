      287. Secundo, cum subdit, quae, assignat triplex illorum peccatum: et praecipue oris: quod ex tribus aggravatur: 287. Next, when he adds, which speak iniquity, he assigns a threefold reason for their sins, and especially those of the mouth, which are made more serious by three things: scilicet ex persona contra quam dicitur, the person who is spoken against, ex eo quod dicitur, what is spoken, et ex radice ejus quod dicitur. and the rooted intention of the one who speaks. Quantum ad primum dicit, adversus justum, quod pejus quam si contra peccatorem; In regard to the first, he says, against the just, which is worse than if it were against a sinner. quasi dicat: illa obmutescent quae modo loquuntur, de eo quod dicunt, quia injustitiam dicunt et falsa: Ps. 35: verba oris ejus iniquitas et dolus. In regard to the second, it is as if he were to say, “Let those be made dumb who spoke in this way, because of what they say, since they speak what is unjust and false.” The words of his mouth are iniquity and guile (Ps 35:4). Ex radice vero, quia aliquid aliquando dicitur contra virum justum ex ignorantia et deceptione; sed si ex prava radice procedit, tunc est malum. Et haec prava radix est duplex: scilicet superbia et abusio: Prov. 11: ubi fuerit superbia, ibi erit et contumelia. And it truly comes from the root, since sometimes something is said against the just man out of ignorance and misunderstanding, but if it comes from a depraved root, then it is evil. And this depraved root is double: pride and abuse. Where pride is, there shall also be reproach (Prov 11:2). Item abutuntur bonis sibi datis; quia data sunt eis ut humilientur, et ipsi inde extolluntur. Likewise, the goods given to them are abused, since they are given to some so that they may be humiliated and to others so that they may be lifted up. Hieronymus habet, et despectu, quia stulte alios contemnunt. Et haec est abusio, quia non utitur justo ut debet contemnendo ipsum. Jerome has, from contempt, since they condemn others foolishly. And this is abuse, since it is not just that he should condemn him. 288. Quam. Psalmista supra praemisit tribulationem et orationem; hic autem accedit ad gratiarum actionem, tamquam exauditus. Ubi duo facit. 288. O how great is the multitude. Above, the psalmist told about his affliction and prayer; now, having been heard, he comes at last to thanksgiving, where he makes two points. Primo commemorat universaliter beneficia, quae Deus humano generi et sanctis confert. First, he recalls universally the favors which God conferred on the human race and the saints. Secundo prorumpit ad gratias, ibi, benedictus dominus, quoniam et cetera. Second, he breaks out into thanks, at blessed be the Lord. Et circa primum duo facit. He makes two points about the favors given to the human race. Primo enim ostendit Dei misericordiam quantum ad dona collata sanctis. First, he shows the mercy of God in regard to the gifts given to the saints; Secundo quantum ad mala a quibus tuetur eos, ibi, abscondes eos. second, in regard to the evils from which he has saved them, at you shall hide them. Circa primum duo facit. He makes two points about the gifts given. Ostendit enim primo praeparationem bonorum. First, he shows the preparation of their goods; Secundo eorum adimpletionem, ibi, perfecisti eis. second, their fulfillment, at you wrought for those. Dicit ergo, quam magna et cetera. Dulcedo in spiritualibus transumptive dicitur: sicut enim dulcedo corporalis delectat gustum carnis, ita quod mentem delectat interius, dicitur dulcedo. Therefore, he says, O how great is the multitude of your sweetness, O Lord, which you have hidden for those who fear you! Sweetness in spiritual things is said by transference, for as physical sweetness delights the bodily taste, so what interiorly delights the mind is called sweetness. Contingit autem quandoque quod gustus carnis non bene dispositus delectatur in corrupto sapore, et tunc falso delectatur: sic affectus hominis quando non est bene ordinatus, delectatur in re quae non vere est delectabilis: sed si bene sit dispositus, delectatur in vero bono, scilicet divino. Et ideo bonitas substantialis Dei dicitur dulcedo Dei: Sap. 16: substantiam tuam et dulcedinem tuam quam in filios habes ostendebas. Now, it sometimes happens that a bodily taste that is not well-disposed takes delight in a corrupted taste, and then it delights falsely; so also, when the affection of man is not well-ordered, it delights in something that is not truly delightful. But if it is well-disposed, it delights in the true good, namely the divine. And so the substantial goodness of God is called the sweetness of God. You showed your sustenance and your sweetness to your children (Wis 16:21). Vel dulcedo Dei dicitur eo modo quo aliquis dicitur amarum habere affectum, quando scilicet cogitat quomodo alios contristet: Eph. 4: omnis amaritudo tollatur a vobis. Ergo per contrarium dulcedo animi, quando proponit alios consolari. Unde dulcedo Dei propositum dicitur quo vult omnes homines salvos fieri, 1 Tim. 2: gustate et videte quoniam suavis est dominus; Ps. 33, item dulcis et rectus dominus. Ps. 24. Or the sweetness of God is spoken of in a case when someone is said to have a bitter attitude when he considers how to make others sad. Let all bitterness . . . be put away from you (Eph 4:31). Therefore, sweetness of soul is recognized to be the contrary, when one intends to console others. So the sweetness of God is said to be found in that he will have all men to be saved (1 Tim 2:4). Likewise: O taste and see that the Lord is sweet (Ps 33:9). Likewise: the Lord is sweet and righteous (Ps 24:8). Et quocumque modo dulcedo dicatur, continet delectationem: quia quamvis sit una et simplex in se, tamen est radix et fons omnis bonitatis. Et ideo quidquid delectat in mundo, totum est in Deo; ut sapientia, veritas, honores, excellentia, voluptas, et omnia hujusmodi in excessu sunt ibi. Et ideo dicit, quam magna multitudo, superexcedens secundum magnitudinem, infinite a nobis incomprehensibilis. And in whatever way sweetness is said, it contains delights, since although it is one and simple in itself, nevertheless it is the root and fount of every goodness. And so whatever delights exist in the world are complete in God: wisdom, truth, honors, excellence, pleasure and all such things are there in excessive abundance. And so he says, O how great is the multitude of your sweetness, O Lord, superexceeding in greatness, infinitely incomprehensible to us. Item similiter multitudo quantum ad dulcedinem effectus; quia quidquid homini potest provenire, totum est ex dulcedine bonitatis Dei: Sap. 12: o quam suavis est domine spiritus tuus in nobis. Isa. 54, ubi agitur de multitudine justorum: multi filii desertae et cetera. Likewise, the effects of this sweetness are like a multitude, since whatever can be provided for a man comes completely from the sweetness of the goodness of God. O how . . . sweet is your spirit, O Lord, in us (Wis 12:1). Here he treats the great numbers of the just: many are the children of the desolate (Isa 54:1). Quod vero ait, quam abscondisti et cetera dupliciter potest intelligi. Now, what he says truly, which you have hidden, can be understood in two ways. Uno modo: quod Deus abscondit se ab eis ut non cognoscant eum, ut scilicet crescat eorum desiderium. One way is that God hides himself from those who do not know him, so that they may grow in desire for him. Alio modo, quod in abscondito reposuisti ut timentes te habeant eam: et hoc melius; quasi dicat: in abscondito reposuisti propter eos. In another way, you have hidden it so that those fearing you may have it, and this is a better interpretation. It is as though he says, “You have hidden yourself for them.” Et quare? Quia illa dulcedo non est in sensibilibus bonis quae manifesta sunt nobis, sed est in invisibilibus; et non in rebus creatis, sed in invisibilitate ipsius Dei: Apoc. 2: vincenti dabo manna absconditum: 1 Cor. 2: oculus non vidit, nec auris audivit, nec in cor hominis ascendit, quae praeparavit dominus diligentibus se. And why? Since that sweetness is not among the sensible goods which are manifest to us, but in the invisible; and not in created things, but in the invisible God himself. To him who overcomes I will give the hidden manna (Rev 2:17). Eye has not seen, nor ear heard, neither has it entered into the heart of man, what things God has prepared for those who love him (1 Cor 2:9). Praeparatio ergo bona est in abscondito; sed consummatio et expletio est manifesta; unde subdit, perfecisti eis et cetera. Inter illam praedestinationem et ejus effectum, ejus scilicet qui est in gloria, in medio est status gratiae, qui est inchoatio gloriae: et hac praetermissa tendit ad perfectum et dicit, perfecisti, id est perficies, et incepisti perficere id absconditum: 1 Cor. 13: cum venerit quod perfectum est, evacuabitur et cetera. Therefore, the good preparation is in secret, but the consummation and fulfillment is manifest, so he adds, you have wrought. In the middle between predestination and its effect (which is in glory) is the state of grace, which is the beginning of glory, and this, passed through, tends towards perfection. And he says, you have wrought, that is, you perfect and you begin to perfect that which is hidden. When that which is perfect is come, that which is in part shall pass away (1 Cor 13:10). Sed quibus? Eis qui sperant in te, ad quos perveniet haec perfectio, non ad eos qui in se ipsis confidunt: Rom. 5: gloriamur in spe gloriae filiorum Dei. But for whom? For those who hope in you, to these this perfection will come, not to those who trust in themselves. We glory in the hope of the glory of the sons of God (Rom 5:2). Sed tunc non erit abscondita, immo erit in conspectu filiorum hominum: quia illam gloriam omnes videbunt, boni et mali: Rom. 8: existimo quod non sunt condignae passiones hujus temporis ad futuram gloriam quae revelabitur in nobis: Sap. 5: videntes mirabuntur in subitatione et cetera usque quomodo computati sunt inter filios Dei, et inter sanctos sors illorum est. But then it will not be hidden, but it will be in the sight of the sons of men, because all will see their glory, the good and bad. For I reckon that the sufferings of this time are not worthy to be compared with the glory to come, that shall be revealed in us (Rom 8:18). Those seeing it . . . shall be amazed at the suddenness, . . . how they are numbered among the children of God and their lot is among the saints (Wis 5:2, 5). Vel, filiorum, id est sanctorum qui plene videbunt: Ps. 106: videbunt recti et cetera. Or, of the sons, that is, of the saints who see fully. The just shall see (Ps 106:2). Vel, qui sperant in te, et hanc manifestant in conspectu filiorum hominum: Rom. 1: non enim erubesco Evangelium. Or, they hope in you, and then they will show it clearly in the sight of the sons of men. For I am not ashamed of the Gospel (Rom 1:16). 289. Deinde cum dicit, abscondes, ostendit beneficia Dei in sanctos, per liberationem a malo. 289. Afterward, when he says, you shall hide them in the secret of your face, he shows the favors of God in the saints through their deliverance from evil. Sed quia duplex malum potest homo pati: scilicet in opere, et in verbo: But since man can suffer from a twofold evil, namely, in works and in deeds, primo ostendit quando protegit a primo malo. first, he shows when he protects from the first evil; Secundo quomodo a secundo, ibi, proteges. and second, how he protects from the second: you shall protect.
