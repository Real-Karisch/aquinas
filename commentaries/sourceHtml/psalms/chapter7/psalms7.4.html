      Tertio judicis idoneitatem, scrutans corda et renes Deus. third, the particular characteristics of the judge: the searcher of hearts and reins is God. Dicit ergo, habeo inimicos persequentes: et peto divino auxilio liberari. Et de hoc confido. Quia dominus judicat populos: judicat enim orbem terrae in aequitate, et populos in veritate sua: Ps. 95. Isa. 3: stat ad judicandum dominus, et stat ad judicandos populos. Therefore, he says, “I have enemies persecuting me, and I ask for divine help to be freed. And I have confidence about this because the Lord judges the people. For he judges the world with justice, and the people with his truth (Ps 95:13).” The Lord stands up to judge, and he stands to judge the people (Is 3:13). Et nota quod postquam dixit, propter hanc in altum, subjungit de judicio: quia post ascensionem revertetur ad judicandum: Act. 1: quemadmodum vidistis eum ascendentem in caelum, ita veniet ad judicandum. Modus judicii ponitur in forma orationis, quia orando ostendit quid fiet in judicio, dicens, judica me. And note that after he has said, and for their sakes you return on high, he adds something about judgment, since after the Ascension he will return to judge. Just as you have seen him going up into heaven, so he shall come to judge (Acts 1:11). He describes the manner of judgment in the form of a prayer, since by praying he shows what will happen in the judgment, saying, judge me. Et primo ponit bonorum retributionem. And first, he describes the repayment of good people; Secundo malorum punitionem, consumetur. second, the punishment of evil ones: sinners shall be brought to naught. In judicio duo retribuuntur: quia bonis bona, et malis mala. Qui ergo bonus est, et qui caret malis, habebit abundantiam bonorum, et sublationem malorum: Prov. 1: qui me audierit, abundantia perfruetur, timore malorum sublato et cetera. Et ideo quantum ad primum dicit, judica me secundum justitiam meam, scilicet quam dedisti mihi, secundum quod sum justus: et haec justitia est, ut mihi retribuantur bona: Matth. 5: beati qui esuriunt et sitiunt justitiam, quoniam et cetera. In judgment, two things are repaid, since good things will be given to the good and evil to the evil. Therefore, he who is good and free from evil will have an abundance of good things and a removal of evil things. He who shall hear me . . . shall enjoy abundance, without fear of evils (Prov 1:33). And so in regard to this first aspect, he says, judge me, O Lord, according to my justice, which you have given to me, according to which I am just; and it is justice that you should repay good things to me. Blessed are those who hunger and thirst after justice: for they shall have their fill (Matt 5:6). Quantum ad secundum dicit: secundum innocentiam meam: et haec justitia est ut nihil mali patiatur: Job 22: salvabitur innocens: salvabitur autem in munditia manuum suarum. In regard to the second aspect, he says, according to my innocence, and it is justice that they should suffer no evil. The innocent shall be saved, and he shall be saved by the cleanness of his hands (Job 22:30). Et nota, quod petit hoc judicium secundum justitiam. Supra dicit in poenitentiali psalmo secundum misericordiam, nullum meritum commemorans: nunc autem quia jam justificatus fecerat aliqua bona, quibus debebatur praemium, petit judicari secundum justitiam. Quod ait, super me, designat quia justitia hominis et innocentia non sunt ab homine, sed a Deo. And note that he asks for this judgment to be in accordance with justice. Above, in the penitential psalm, he speaks that it should be in accordance with mercy, not remembering his own merit; but now, since, already justified, he has done some good things for which reward is owed, he asks to be judged according to justice. His saying upon me expresses that the justice and innocence of man are not from man, but from God. Contra hoc quod petit, ut sibi sint bona, et mali nihil patiatur, videtur aliquando bonum esse et utile opprimi a malis, et etiam ultra boni habent mala in mundo isto, et mali bona: Luc. 16: recordare quia recepisti bona in vita tua et cetera. Contrary to his asking that he receive goods and suffer no evil is the argument that sometimes it is good and useful to be oppressed by evils, and further, that the good have evils in this world, and the evil have goods. Remember that you did receive good things in your lifetime (Luke 16:25). Et ratio hujus assignatur. Consumetur, id est ad finem deveniet: nequitia peccatorum: Apoc. ult.: qui in sordibus est, sordescat adhuc. And a reason is given for this. The wickedness of sinners shall be fulfilled, that is, shall reach an end. He who is filthy, let him be filthy still (Rev 22:11). Nota quod quandoque Deus differt poenam ut praedestinati convertantur: Isa. 30: propterea expectat vos Deus, ut misereatur vestri. Unde tunc. Consumetur nequitia peccatorum, id est deficiet. Note that sometimes God delays punishment so that the predestined may be converted. Therefore the Lord waits that he may have mercy on you (Isa 30:18). So, then, the wickedness of sinners shall be brought to naught, that is, fail. Quandoque ut mali malitiam suam amplius manifestent, et judicia Dei appareant justa: et tunc, consumetur, id est perficietur, nequitia peccatorum, ut justior appareat vindicta: propter quod dicit Gen. 15: scito praenoscens quod peregrinum futurum sit semen tuum, et cetera usque necdum completae sunt iniquitates Amorrhaeorum et cetera. Act. 7: et non dedit ei hereditatem in ea nec passum pedis et cetera usque dixit dominus. And sometimes this is so that the evil may manifest their malice more clearly and the justice of God’s judgments be apparent, and then the wickedness of sinners shall be consumed, that is, completed, so that the justice of the vindication may appear more clearly, which is why he says, know beforehand that your seed shall be a stranger in a land not their own . . . for as yet the iniquities of the Amorrhites are not at the full until this present time (Gen 15:13, 16). And he gave him no inheritance in it; no, not the pace of a foot: but he promised to give it him in possession, and to his seed after him, when as yet he had no child (Acts 7:5). Sic ait, consumetur; quasi dicat: faciant quidquid possunt, quia tandem consumetur nequitia ipsorum peccatorum. So he says, it shall be consumed, as if to say, “They shall do whatever they can, since then the wickedness of these same sinners shall be consumed.” Sed numquid mali gravabunt bonos? Non: quia Deus diriget justos. Unde, diriges justum, Prover. 11: simplicitas justorum dirigit eos. But shall the evil, then, severely oppress the just? No, because God directs the just. So, you shall direct the just. The simplicity of the just shall guide them (Prov 11:3). 46. Deinde cum addit, scrutans, ostendit judicis idoneitatem. 46. Afterwards, when he adds, the searcher, he shows the suitability of the judge. Et primo ponit idoneitatem judicis. And first, he describes the suitability of the judge; Secundo confidentiam de eo conceptam, ibi, justum adjutorium meum. and second, he describes the confidence aroused by him: just is my help. Duo autem requiruntur ad idoneitatem judicis: scilicet ut sit sapiens, et ut sit justus. Now two things are required for a judge to be suitable: that he be wise, and that he be just. Haec autem sunt in Deo: et ideo judex est idoneus: Prov. 20: rex qui sedet in solio judicii, dissipat omne malum intuitu suo. Est enim sapientissimus, omnia cognoscens, etiam interiora: Heb. 4: omnia nuda et aperta sunt oculis ejus. Est etiam justissimus: Hier. 2: tu autem domine Sabaoth, qui judicas juste et cetera. Et ideo dicit, scrutans corda. Now these are found in God, and so he is a suitable judge. The king, who sits on the throne of judgment, scatters away all evil with his look (Prov 20:8). And he is most wise, knowing all things, even those that are interior: all things are naked and open to his eyes (Heb 4:13). And he is most just: but you, O Lord of Sabaoth, who judge justly (Jer 11:20). And so he says, the searcher of hearts. Hieronymus habet sic, probator cordis et renum, Deus justus: et post sequitur alius versus: clypeus meus in Deo. Ubi littera nostra habet, justum adjutorium et cetera. Jerome has, the examiner of hearts and reins, the just God, and the other verse follows, my shield is in God, while our text has, just is my help. Dicit ergo, scrutans. Therefore, he says, the searcher. Tria sunt in homine: Now, there are three things in man: unum apparens, scilicet exterius opus: one which is apparent, namely, his external work; et duo quae latent, scilicet intentio et delectatio. and two which are hidden, his intention and his delight. Haec duo nobis latent, sed Deo patent. Et quia Deo sunt nota, licet nobis occulta, ideo dicit, scrutans corda, quia novit intentionem, et renes, id est delectationem, utrum scilicet delecteris in laude Dei vel hominum. These two are hidden to us but open to God. And since they are known to God, although hidden to us, he says, the searcher of hearts, since he knows the intention, and reins, that is, the delight—whether you delight in the praise of God or of men. Sed quia scrutari est inquirere, inquirere autem est ignorantis, hoc a Deo removet. Et ut ostendat quod Deus evidenter scit; cum dixit, consumetur et cetera convenienter dixit, scrutans: quia in tribulatione maxime apparet conditio hominum. But since to search is to inquire, and to inquire belongs to the ignorant, he removes this from God. And in order to show that God knows clearly, when he said, shall be consumed, he appropriately said, the searcher, since the condition of man appears most clearly in tribulation. Deinde subinfertur, justum adjutorium meum a domino et cetera. Ubi ponitur fiducia concepta de judice, a quo adjutorium est sperandum. Afterwards, he adds, just is my help from the Lord. Here he discusses the confidence inspired by the judge, from whom help is expected. Est enim duplex adjutorium Dei: For there are two kinds of help from God: scilicet misericordiae, that of mercy et aliud justitiae. and that of justice. Adjutorium quo liberatur a malis et peccatis, est misericordiae: et non est justum, quia non ex meritis. The help by which one is freed from evils and from sins is that of mercy, and it is not just since it does not come from merits. Sed quando quis justificatur, Deus perficit: et hoc est justitiae, quia respondet aliqualiter merito. But when someone is justified, God perfects him, and this belongs to justice, since it corresponds in some way to merit. De primo dicitur Ps. 9: adjutor in opportunitatibus et cetera. It is said about the first, a helper in due time (Ps 9:10). De secundo Ps. 45: adjuvabit eam Deus et cetera. About the second, God will help it (Ps 45:6). Sed quare? Quia, salvos facit et cetera. Prov. 2: custodiet rectorum salutem. Rectos corde id est qui intentione tendunt in Deum. But why? Since he saves the upright of heart. He will keep the salvation of the righteous (Prov 2:7). The upright of heart, that is, those who incline toward God in their intention. Sed quaerit Cassiodorus. Quare non dicit, qui salvos facit rectos renibus, sed rectos corde. But Cassiodorus asks why he does not say, “Who saves those with upright reins,” but rather the upright of heart.
