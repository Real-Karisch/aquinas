      Ex parte justorum est alia causa, quae petit vindictam de peccatoribus. Sunt autem in justis duo quae exigunt vindictam: On the part of the just, there is another reason, which asks for vindication against sinners. Now there are two things in the just which require vindication, scilicet abjectio temporalis, namely, their temporal state of being cast down, et virtus spiritualis. and their spiritual virtue. Quantum ad primum dicit: quoniam non in finem oblivio erit pauperis. Res viles habentur despectui, sermo traditur oblivioni; sed non sic traduntur oblivioni justi apud Deum: Jac. 2: Deus elegit pauperes divites in fide, heredes regni quod repromisit Deus diligentibus se. Matth. 5: beati pauperes spiritu, quoniam ipsorum est regnum caelorum. Ergo licet hic videantur oblivioni traditi, hoc tamen non est in finem, scilicet finaliter: Isa. 54: punctum in modico dereliqui te, et in miserationibus magnis congregabo te. Et sequitur: et in misericordia sempiterna misertus sum tui. Quando enim recordabitur, tunc puniet opprimentes: Ps. 76: non obliviscetur misereri Deus, neque continebit in ira sua misericordias suas. In regard to the first, he says, for the poor man shall not be forgotten to the end. Vile things happen to the scorned: their words are forgotten, but the just are not forgotten by God. Has God not chosen the poor in this world, rich in faith, and heirs of the kingdom which God has promised to those who love him? (Jas 2:5). Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven (Matt 5:3). Therefore, although they seem to have been forgotten, this is not so to the end, that is, ultimately. For a small moment have I forsaken you, but with great mercies will I gather you; . . . with everlasting kindness have I had mercy on you (Isa 54:7–8). For when he remembers, then he will punish the oppressors. God will not forget to show mercy, nor will he in his anger shut up his mercies (Ps 76:10). Quantum ad secundum, patientia pauperum et cetera. Vel expectatio secundum Hieronymum. Cum enim hic sustineant oppressionem patienter et paupertatem, ex patientia merentur vindictam: nec expectatio peribit in finem, quia consequentur bonum quod expectant. Aliter et in finem, qui scilicet est vita aeterna. In regard to the second, the spiritual virtue of the just, he says, the patience of the poor. Or, according to Jerome, their hope. For when they endure oppression and poverty patiently, they merit vindication through their patience. Nor will the hope of the poor perish forever, since the good that they hope for will follow. Or forever means eternal life. Sed numquid patientia est in patria? Dicendum, quod non secundum essentiam, sed secundum fructum: secus de caritate et justitia, quae erunt etiam secundum essentiam. But surely there is no patience in the fatherland? It should be said that it is not there in its essence but in its fruit, unlike charity and justice, which are there in their essence. 69. Consequenter cum dicit, exurge, praenuntiat divinum judicium: et circa hoc tria facit. 69. Afterwards, when he says, arise, he declares the divine judgment, and he makes three points concerning this. Primo excitat judicem. First, he stirs up the judge. Secundo implorat judicium. Second, he begs for judgment. Tertio ostendit judicii fructus. Third, he describes the fruit of judgment. Dicit quod non est oblitus in finem pauperum: unde ipse quasi unus de pauperibus dicit, rogo ut non differas usque in finem praemiare; sed exurge. He says that the poor man is not forgotten forever, so he speaks as though he is one of the poor: I ask that you do not delay reward forever, but arise. Et circa hoc tria facit. And he makes three points regarding this. Primo repudiat pravum sive humanum judicium. Homo enim humanitus absque ratione opprimens non confortetur, id est non valeat facere quod vult: Prov. 29: cum impii sument principatum, gemet populus. First, he rejects a perverse or human judgment. For let man, acting humanly, oppressing without reason, not be strengthened, that is, let him not be able to accomplish what he wishes to do. When the wicked shall bear rule, the people shall mourn (Prov 29:2). Secundo exposcit judicium alterius: judicentur gentes, non secundum voluntatem humanam, sed in conspectu tuo, scilicet tuo judicio, quasi, appello ad te. Act. 25: ante tribunal Caesaris sto, ibi oportet me judicari. Ps. 25: judica me domine quoniam et cetera. Second, he asks for the judgment of another: let the gentiles be judged, not according to human will, but in your sight, that is, by your judgment, as if to say, “I appeal to you.” I stand at Caesar’s judgment seat, where I ought to be judged (Acts 25:10). Judge me, O Lord (Ps 25:1). Tertio petit adjutorem: constitue legislatorem, scilicet filium tuum: Isa. 33: dominus legifer noster. Vel aliter constitue legislatorem, id est punitorem secundum legem tuam; secundum enim legem poena infertur. Third, he asks for a helper: appoint a lawgiver, that is, your son. The Lord is our lawgiver (Isa 33:22). Or, appoint a punisher according to your law, for punishment is brought to bear under the law. Hieronymus habet: pone eis terrorem; alia translatio: mitte eis amaritudinem. Glossa: non confortetur homo, id est Antichristus: judicentur, id est puniantur. Jerome has, put terror upon them. Another translation has, send bitterness to them. The gloss says, let not man, that is, the Antichrist, be strengthened; let them be judged, that is, punished. Fructus judicii, quia recognoscunt se esse homines: sciant gentes quoniam homines sunt, fragiles, peccatores et mortales. Isa. 28: vexatio dat intellectum. Sic Alexander quando fuit percussus, recognovit se non filium Jovis, sed mortalem, ut ipse ait suis militibus. This is the fruit of judgment, because they recognize they are men: that the gentiles may know themselves to be but men, fragile, sinners, and mortal. He gives vexation to the intellect (Isa 28:19). So Alexander, when he was beaten, realized that he was not the son of Jove but mortal, as he himself said to his troops. 70. Hic ponit processum impiorum. 70. Here, he describes the progress of the wicked. Et primo ponit causam nequitiae eorum. And first, he lays out the cause of their wickedness. Secundo describit nequitiam, ibi, cujus maledictione et cetera. Second, he describes their wickedness, at his mouth is full of cursing. Tertio contra eam implorat divinum auxilium, ibi, exurge domine Deus. Third, he implores the divine help against it, at arise, O Lord God. Causa nequitiae est duplex: The cause of their wickedness is twofold: scilicet permissiva a permissive cause, et inductiva. Secunda, ibi, quoniam laudatur peccator. and an inducing cause. The second is where he says, for the sinner is praised. Circa primum tria facit. He makes three points about the permissive cause. Primo ponit divinam dissimulationem, quae videtur causa male agendi malis. Eccl. 8: quia non profertur cito contra malos sententia, absque timore filii hominum perpetrant mala;unde subdit: ut quid domine recessisti longe a nobis? In quantum non punis affligentes nos, et in hoc videris nos despicere, in opportunitatibus, id est tempore in quo deberes ferre auxilium. First, he describes the divine concealment, which seems to be a cause for the evil of acting in an evil way. For because sentence is not speedily pronounced against the evil, the children of men commit evils without any fear (Eccl 8:11). And so he adds, why, O Lord, have you retired afar off from us, insofar as you do not punish those afflicting us, and seem to slight us in our wants, that is, at the time at which you should bring help. Vel in opportunitatibus, id est opportune hoc facis: quia sancti inde proficiunt ad meritum vitae aeternae. Or in our wants, that is, it is opportune that you do this, since by it the saints advance to merit eternal life. Secundo ponit dissimulationis effectum, dum superbit impius. Second, he puts down the effect of his concealment: while the wicked man is proud. Et ponit duplicem effectum in malis. And he describes two effects upon evil persons. Superbiam, eo quod non statim puniuntur a Deo; et ideo dicit: superbit impius, in potestatibus, There is pride because they are not immediately punished by God. And so he says, the wicked man is proud, in his powers. incenditur pauper, id est affligitur. The poor one is set on fire, that is, he is afflicted. Vel incenditur spiritualiter ex consideratione superbiae impiorum et eorum peccatis: Ps. 68: zelus domus tuae comedit me. Or he is set on fire spiritually when he considers the pride of the wicked and their sins. The zeal of your house has eaten me up (Ps 68:10). Tertius effectus est, quia comprehenduntur in consiliis quibus cogitant, quia consilia eorum finaliter destruent eos: Prov. 5: iniquitates suae capiunt impium, et funibus peccatorum suorum constringitur. The third effect is that they are caught in the counsels which they devise, since their counsels will destroy them in the end. His own iniquities catch the wicked, and he is fast bound with the ropes of his own sins (Prov 5:22). 71. Quoniam. Hic ponuntur tres causae inducentes ad peccatum. 71. For the sinner is praised. Here he puts down three causes that induce one to sin. Prima est adulatio. The first is adulation. Secunda contemptus Dei, ibi, dixit enim in corde suo. The second is contempt of God, at for he has said in his heart.
