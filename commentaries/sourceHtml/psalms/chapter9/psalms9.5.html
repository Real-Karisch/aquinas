      Tertio, inducit ad secundum fructum, scilicet ad laetitiam. Psallite domino qui habitat in Sion. Dominus dicitur habitare in loco non corporaliter: 2 Reg. 6: David posuit arcam in Sion; sed secundum veritatem habitat in Ecclesia. Sion interpretatur specula, et in Ecclesia speculamur aeterna. Isti ergo debemus psallere, id est gaudere corde et ore et operibus, pro beneficiis datis. Third, he proposes the second fruit, namely joy. Sing to the Lord, who dwells in Zion. The Lord is said to dwell in in a place in an incorporeal way. David placed the ark in Zion in 2 Samuel 6:2, but according to the reality, he dwells in the Church. Zion means “mirror,” and we see eternal things mirrored in the Church. Therefore, we should sing praise to it—that is, we should rejoice in heart and word and works for the blessings given. Tertio inducit ad tertium fructum, annuntiate. 1 Pet. 4: unusquisque prout accepit gratiam in alterutrum illam administrantes. Third, he proposes the third fruit: declare. As every man has received grace, ministering the same one to another (1 Pet 4:10). Et ideo primo inducit quod debeant annuntiare. Gregorius: ille uberes fructus suae praedicationis colligit, qui semina bonae operationis praemittit. Inter gentes, id est inter gentiliter viventes, id est inter peccatores: Isa. 21: occurrentes sitienti ferte aquam. Studia, id est curam ejus sive solicitudinem de salute humani generis: Hier. 29: ego cogito cogitationes pacis. And so he first proposes what they should declare. Gregory: he gathers the plentiful fruits of his preaching, who sows the seeds of good works. Among the gentiles, that is, among those living like gentiles (that is, among sinners). Meeting the thirsty bring him water (Isa 21:14). His ways, that is, his care or concern for the salvation of the human race. I think thoughts of peace (Jer 29:11). Secundo haec studia exponit ibi, quoniam requirens et cetera. Illud quod aliquis cum studio et diligentia facit, non obliviscitur quin illud faciat; Deus autem studiosus est ad salutem hominum: et ideo non obliviscitur. Second, he explains this eagerness: for requiring their blood he has remembered them. One does not forget to do what one is eager and diligent about doing, and God is eager for the salvation of mankind and so does not forget it. Duo faciunt oblivionem: Two things cause forgetfulness: scilicet mors: Ps. 30: oblivioni datus sum tamquam mortuus a corde, namely, death: I am forgotten as one dead from the heart (Ps 30:13), item paupertas: Prov. 19: fratres hominis pauperis oderunt eum: Apoc. 6: quare non vindicas et cetera. and poverty: the brethren of a poor man hate him (Prov 19:7). Why, O Lord, do you not judge (Rev 6:10). Sed Deus non obliviscitur mortuorum? Quoniam requirens sanguinem eorum, scilicet te quaerentium; etiamsi contingat eos occidi; et hoc judicando. But surely God does not forget the dead? For requiring their blood, namely, that of those who seek you, even if it should happen that they are killed; and he does this by judging. Vel requiret sanctorum sanguinem, in resurrectione reparando illum: Sap. 5: ecce quomodo computati sunt et cetera. Or, he will “seek” the blood of his saints by restoring it in the resurrection. Behold how they are numbered among the children of God (Wis 5:5). Dicitur autem recordatus, non quod sit oblitus, sed quia videtur oblitus propter dilationem. Now, he has remembered means not that he has forgotten, but that, because of a delay, it seems as though he has forgotten. Item non obliviscitur pauperum et parvorum: unde, non est oblitus clamorem pauperum: Ps. 33: iste pauper clamavit, vel in periculis, vel in oratione: Jac. 5: clamor eorum et cetera. Ps. 21: non sprevit neque despexit deprecationem pauperis: Ex. 3: videns vidi afflictionem populi mei qui est in Aegypto, et descendi liberare eum. Likewise, he does not forget the poor and the little: he has not forgotten the cry of the poor. The poor man cried, either in danger or in prayer, and the Lord heard him (Ps 33:7). Their cry has entered into the ears of the Lord of Sabaoth (Jas 5:4). He has neither slighted nor despised the supplication of the poor man (Ps 21:25). I have seen the affliction of my people in Egypt, and I have come down to free them (Exod 3:7–8). 65. Miserere. Hic ponit fructum quantum ad se. 65. Have mercy on me. Here he describes the fruit in regard to himself. Et primo commemorat beneficium. And first, he remembers the favor. Secundo ponit fructum, ibi, ut annuntiem et cetera. Second, he describes its fruit, at that I may declare. Beneficium duplex: The favor is double: unum futurum: one is in the future, aliud habitum. Qui exaltas et cetera. the other already possessed: you who lift me up. Primo duo facit. Primo ponit misericordiam. Regarding the first, he first describes the mercy; Secundo misericordiae motivum. second, the motivation for mercy. Futurum beneficium est misericordia. Miserere mei: Ps. 32: misericordia domini plena est terra. The future favor is mercy. Have mercy on me. The earth is full of the mercy of the Lord (Ps 32:5). Motivum ad eam, consideratio Dei. Vide, id est considera, humilitatem meam. Haec humilitas non importat virtutem, sicut illud, si non humiliter sentiebam, sed dejectionem. Unde Hieronymus habet afflictionem de inimicis meis, quia scilicet affligunt. The motivation for it is God’s consideration. See, that is, consider, my humiliation. This humiliation is not a virtue, as this is: if I was not humbly minded (Ps 130:2), but it is the state of being downcast. So Jerome has, the affliction of my enemies, since they are afflicting me. Vel aliter: vide humilitatem, quia humilibus dat gratiam, Jac. 4. Sed hoc potest considerare ex consideratione inimicorum, qui sunt superbi et mali. Or in another way: see my humiliation, that is, take note of it, because God gives grace to the humble (Jas 4:6). But he can consider this by considering the enemies, who are proud and evil. Consequenter confitetur beneficium habitum: qui exaltas me et cetera: quasi dicat: de tanto periculo eruisti me, quod non restabat nisi ut morerer: Hier. 9: ascendit mors per fenestras nostras; ingressa est domos nostras disperdere parvulos deforis. Next, he confesses the favor which he already has: you who lift me up, as if to say, “You have raised me up from such great danger, in which I could not continue lest I die.” For death has come up through our windows, it has entered into our houses to destroy the children from without (Jer 9:21). Spiritualiter autem portae mortis sunt haeretici: Matth. 16: portae inferi non praevalebunt adversum eam. Et sensus hominis: Hier. 9: mors introivit per fenestras. Odium verbi Dei: Ps. 126: omnem escam abominata est anima eorum, et appropinquaverunt usque ad portas mortis. Tentationes et vitia: Sap. 16: deducis ad portas mortis, et reducis. Qui ergo liberatur ab istis, dicat, exaltas me, id est liberasti me de portis mortis. Now, spiritually, the gates of death are the heretics: the gates of hell shall not prevail against it (Matt 16:18). And they are man’s senses: death has come up through our windows (Jer 9:21); hatred of the word of God: their soul abhorred all manner of food and they drew nigh even to the gates of death (Ps 106:18); and temptations and vices: you lead down to the gates of death, and bring back (Wis 16:13). He, therefore, who is freed from these says, you lift me up, that is, you have freed me from the gates of death. 66. Consequenter cum dicit, ut ponit fructum; sed ordine retrogrado. 66. Afterwards, when he says, that I may declare, he describes the fruit, but in reverse order: Primo annuntiationis. first, declaration; Secundo exultationis, ibi, exultabo. second, rejoicing: I will rejoice; Tertio cognitionis, ibi, cognoscetur. third, knowledge: the Lord shall be known. Dico quod misertus es, et peto quod miserearis; et hoc ut annuntiem praedicationes. Antiquitus judicia fiebant in portis; et ideo dicit, in portis filiae Sion, id est Hierusalem, quia subjecta erat arci quae vocabatur Sion; quasi dicat: in multitudine populi Hierosolymitani, annuntiem omnes laudationes, non quod omnes, sed de omni genere laudis. I say that you have been merciful, and I ask that you be merciful, and so I may declare all your praises. In ancient times court was held at the gates, and so he says, in the gates of the daughter of Zion, that is, Jerusalem, because it was built on the mountain called Zion; as if to say, “Among the multitude of the inhabitants of Jerusalem, I shall declare all your praises,” not that he can express all your praises, but every type of praise. Item portae filiae Sion dicuntur doctores Ecclesiae: Isa. 54: ponam portas ejus in lapides sculptos; universos filios tuos doctos a domino. Further, the gates of the daughter of Zion are said to be the doctors of the Church. I will make your gates of graven stones; . . . all your children shall be taught of the Lord (Isa 54:12–13). Item justitiae: haec porta domini, justi intrabunt in eam, ut dicitur Ps. 117: Likewise, they are gates of justice: this is the gate of the Lord; the just shall enter into it (Ps 117:20). item bonae cogitationes: Ps. 147: seras portarum. In his ergo portis annuntiabo laudes tuas. They are also good thoughts: the bolts of your gates (Ps 147:2). Therefore, in these gates I will announce your praises. 67. Exultabo. Posito uno fructu, scilicet praedicatione divinae laudis, hic ponit secundum, scilicet laetitiam spiritualem a Deo. 67. I will rejoice. He described one fruit, namely the preaching of the divine praise; here he describes a second: spiritual joy from God. Et primo ponit suam exultationem. And first, he describes his rejoicing; Secundo occasionem exultandi, ibi, infixae sunt gentes. second, the occasion for his rejoicing: the gentiles have stuck fast in the destruction which they have prepared. Dicit ergo: annuntiem omnes laudes tuas. Sed hae essent steriles, si ita essent in ore quod non in corde esset jucunditas: Ps. 146: Deo nostro jucunda sit laudatio; et ideo subdit, exultabo in salutari tuo. Non in mundo vel in carne, sed in tua salvatione qua me exaltas. Vel in Christo salvatore: Habac. 3: exultabo in Deo Jesu meo: 1 Reg. 2: laetata sum in salutari tuo. Therefore, he says, that I may declare all your praises. But these are sterile if they are in the mouth in such a way that it is not in the heart. To our God be joyful praise (Ps 146:1), and so he adds, I will rejoice in your salvation, not in the world or the flesh but in your salvation, by which you exalt me. Or in Christ the Savior: I will joy in God my Jesus (Hab 3:18). I have joyed in your salvation (1 Sam 2:1).
