      Innotuit enim per eum: Rom. 1: revelatur ira Dei de caelo super omnem impietatem et injustitiam hominum eorum qui veritatem Dei in injustitia detinent. He reveals it through this: for the wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness and injustice of those men who detain the truth of God in injustice (Rom 1:18). 126. Ascendit. Hic ponitur corporaliter exponendo effectus, qui est a summo. Effectus autem terrae a summo est, quando terra caelesti igne in aliqua sui parte comburitur: et circa hoc duo facit. 126. There went up a smoke. Here the effect which is from on high is described physically. Now the effect on the earth is from on high when the earth is burned somewhere with heavenly fire. And he considers two aspects of this. Primo tangit materiam ipsam. First, he touches on the matter itself; Secundo accensionem ignis et combustionem. second, on the rising of the fire and its burning. Materia ejus est fumus siccus resolutus ascendens quousque inflammetur; et ideo dicit, ascendit fumus in ira ejus, id est in voluntate ejus, id est Dei per quam sic punit. Its matter is dry smoke, set loose, and it ascends as long as it is kindled, and so he says, there went up a smoke in his wrath, that is, in his will—that is, God’s, through which he punishes. A facie, id est a potestate ejus, ignis exardescit, id est accenditur; et carbones, id est materia combustibilis hic incenditur. From his face, that is, from his power, a fire flamed, that is, rose, and coals, that is, combustible material is kindled here. Mystice per hoc innuuntur duo: Mystically, two things are expressed by this: scilicet devotio orationis, namely, the devotion of prayer, et inflammatio caritatis. and the inflammation of charity. Ascendit: et ex hoc consideratur ira Dei contra peccatores. There went up a smoke in his wrath. By this the wrath of God kindled against sinners can be understood. Ascendit fumus, devotae orationis: Apoc. 8: ascendit fumus aromatum, id est ignis caritatis: a facie ejus, id est Christi, exardescit: Luc. 12: ignem veni mittere in terram. There went up a smoke of devout prayer. The smoke of the incense ascended (Rev 8:4), that is, of the fire of charity; from his face, that is, Christ’s, it flamed. I am come to cast fire on the earth (Luke 12:49). Carbones succensi sunt ab eo, scilicet isti susceptivi accensionis. Carbo aliquando habuit ignem; sic homo a principio habuit caritatem, sed extinctus erat; sed isti succensi sunt a Christo. Item carbones non humidi sic incenduntur, sed humidi, non: sicut humidi fluxu carnalium: Ps. 119: sagittae potentis acutae cum carbonibus et cetera. Coals were kindled by it, namely, those able to be lit. A coal had fire at one time, like man who had charity in the beginning, but it went out; but these have been rekindled by Christ. Likewise, coals which are not wet burn, but those which are wet do not, like those who are wet from the flow of carnal lust. The sharp arrows of the mighty with coals (Ps 119:4). Commota est et contremuit terra; fundamenta montium conturbata sunt et commota sunt, quoniam iratus est eis. Deus irasci dicitur, quia ad modum irati se habet non in se, sed quantum ad effectum: dominus autem iratus facit tremere servum, et leo catulum. Pro quo sciendum, quod virtus continens membra dimittitur exterius, et revertitur interius, puta ad cor quasi fugiens, et cedens malo imaginato: The earth shook and trembled; the foundations of the mountains were troubled and were moved, because he was angry with them. God is said to be angry not because he has the qualities of angry in himself but because of his effect. Now when a lord is angry, he makes his servants tremble, and a lion, the cub. Through this we should know that the power controlling the members draws back from the exterior and returns to the interior, as though retreating towards the heart, and surrendering to the pictured evil. vel virtuti surgenti contra eam cui resistere non potest, et membra tremunt, sicut murus cum concutitur fundamentum. Anima enim continet corpus, et est quasi fundamentum ejus; et pars animae partem corporis. Unde concusso fundamento concutitur murus; et concussa virtute concutitur membrum. Sic ergo effectus irae in animali est tremor. Dicitur autem animal tremere, quando concutitur pars ejus, toto in eodem loco manente: et similiter quia contingit hoc in terraemotu, dicitur terra tremere per similitudinem ad animalia. Dicitur enim Deus irasci terrae in terraemotu. Or, he is not able to resist the powers surging up against him, and his members tremble as a wall does when its foundation is undermined. For the soul contains the body and is like its foundation, and part of the soul contains part of the body. So, as when the foundation is struck the wall trembles, when the power is struck the member trembles. So, therefore, the effect of anger in an animal is called a tremor. Now an animal is said to tremble when part of it is struck while the whole remains in place, and this also applies to an earthquake, when the earth is said to tremble because of its likeness to an animal. Therefore, God is said to be angry at the earth in an earthquake. Vel sic. In homine sunt quatuor: scilicet ratio, vires sensitivae, natura res et corpus. Sed in mundo sunt Deus, angeli, animalia, plantae, et elementa. Or it can be understood in this way. There are four things in man: reason, the sensitive powers, the natural faculties, and the body. But in the world are God, angels, animals, plants, and the elements. Videmus enim quod ad malum imaginatum, cui corpus non potest resistere, corpus statim tremit; non ex cognitione, sed quodam naturali ordine sive naturaliter, in quantum virtus mali imaginati est potentior. Now we see that the body immediately trembles at an evil depicted in the imagination which the body cannot resist, not from intellectual knowledge of it, but according to a certain natural order, or naturally, insofar as the power of the pictured evil is stronger than the body. Et similiter Deus cum vertit virtutem suam super terram, licet non cognoscat iram, naturaliter tremit. Fundamenta, id est aliquae concavitates sive terra concava, qua mota montes concutiuntur. Quoniam iratus et cetera. And so, when God directs his power to the earth, although it does not have knowledge of anger, it naturally trembles. The foundations: these are the cavities or caves in the earth, by whose motion the mountains are shaken. Because he was angry. Prima causa est voluntas Dei sive virtus ejus volens in eis agere: sed mediantibus causis secundis hoc agit; ita quod omnes causae secundae comparantur ad terram sicut imaginatum malum commovens membra. The first cause is the will of God or his power willing to act in them, but he acts here through mediating causes so that all secondary causes are related to the earth as the imagined evil moving the members. Ascendit fumus. Ubi nota secundum Philosophum, quod a terra humida resolvitur virtute caloris solis vapor calidus et humidus; a terra autem sicca vapor siccus et calidus; sed naturaliter plus ascendit secundus quam primus. Hic enim assimilatur igni, ille aeri: et hunc vaporem psalmista vocat fumum, secundum calidum et siccum. Philosophus vero vocat eum materiam incendii. Sursum enim latus hic vapor cum modico augmento caloris factus, per modum circulationis accenditur. There went up a smoke. Here note that, according to the Philosopher, warm and moist vapor is released from the moist earth by the power of the sun, but hot and dry vapor is released from dry earth. In nature, the second ascends higher than the first. For this second is like fire, but that is like air, and the psalmist calls this vapor smoke because it is hot and dry. The Philosopher rightly calls it the material for fire. For this rising vapor, made to expand through the addition of a little heat, is lit as it circulates. Qui quidem fumus siccus si habeat longitudinem et latitudinem, postquam accensus est, vocatur flamma. Est enim flamma, secundum philosophum, spiritus sicci ardoris. Si longitudinem tantum, vocatur daly sive titiones et aegibes sive caprae et sidera. Daly quidem quando est materia illa incendii longa, continua sine scintillatione. Caprae vocatur quando est cum scintillatione, id est quando videtur salire et discurrere, sicut caprae. Sidera, quando est materia discontinua, et videtur volare sicut sidera: et hoc habet minimum de materia. This dry smoke, if it has length and width after it has been lit, is called flame. For, according to the Philosopher a flame is a breath of dry heat. If it has only length, it is called brands (or firebrands) and billy goats or goats and stars. It is a brand when the matter continues burning for a long time without shimmering. It is called a goat when it shimmers, that is, when it seems to dance and run, as a goat. It is called stars when the matter is discontinuous and seems to fly like the stars, and this has the least amount of material. Est et aliud genus siderum, quod est frigus expellens calidum: et talia sidera non videntur volare, sed magis projici, ut dicit Philosophus: et generantur non ex fumo omnino sicco, sed vapore magis humido et calido; qui secundum naturam suam non tantum ascendit sicut siccus, sicut dictum est. Et quia est siccum, patitur a frigido et repercutitur, et inferius projicitur. Et fit hoc in die et in sereno: alias extingueretur a densitate et humiditate aeris. Et quia videtur in die, signum est, quod est prope terram. And there is another type of star, which is cold, expelling what is hot, and these stars do not seem to fly, but rather to be thrown out, as the Philosopher says, and they are generated not from smoke that is entirely dry, but from a moist and hot vapor which, according to its nature, does not ascend like the dry, as was said. And since it is dry, it is affected by the cold and is reflected off and thrown down. And this takes place during the day and when it is clear; at another time it would be put out by the density and moisture of the air. And that it is seen during the day is a sign that it belongs to the earth. Accenditur autem dupliciter; Now, it is set afire in two ways: et per continuationem, sicut superior flamma accendit inferiorem lucernam; either through continuity, as a flame above lights a lamp below; sive per motum a frigore et constrictione, sive conglobatione calidi. or through motion from cold and compression or by the gathering together of the hot. Sic ergo dicit, ascendit fumus, id est exhalatio sicca: in ira ejus, id est per voluntatem ipsius volentem agere in eo. Therefore, he says, the smoke went up, that is, the dry exhalation, in his wrath, that is, through his willing to act in it. Et ignis, id est ille fumus qui vocatur ignis etiam a Philosopho in principio Meteor. quasi eo quod non habeat proprium nomen: sicut exhalatio humida quae vocatur vapor; sed dicitur ignis, quia disposita est ad ascensionem, et quia est calida et sicca sicut ignis. And a fire, that is, that smoke which is called fire by the Philosopher in the beginning of the Meteorology, as if it does not have a proper name, just like the exhalation of moisture which is called vapor, but it is called fire since it is disposed to rise upwards and since it is hot and dry like fire. Iste enim ignis exarsit, id est accensus est, scilicet a Deo tamquam a prima causa: qui quidem ignis accensus vocatur dalus, flamma et sidera: sidera dico generata primo modo, ut dictum est. For this fire flames, that is, it has been lit by God as the first cause, which rising fire is called brands, flames, and stars. I say that the stars are generated in the first way, as was said. Et carbones succensi sunt ab eo, id est sidera secundo modo generata. Coals were kindled by it, that is, the stars generated in the second way. Vel sic. Commota est et cetera. Vapor siccus virtute caloris solis a terra elevatus, aliquando est subtilis: et tunc elevatur superius, et facit intensionem, ut dictum est supra. Aliquando in superficie terrae est aliquantulum grossior; unde a frigore repercussus non tantum ascendit, et est ventus; aliquando in terram elevatur grossior vapor siccus, qui propter suam grossitiem et terrae soliditatem et profunditatem non expirat extra, sed clauditur in terra, et congregatur in aliqua concavitate terrae simili sibi, et coarctatur ab aliquo corpore non sibi simili in specie, et sic agitatur in terrae visceribus: et sic commovet eam: nec mirum, cum videamus ventum in mari facere undas quasi montes, et in terra elevare arbores et aedificia facere corruere, et in aere tempestates maximas facere. Or the passages can be understood in this way: the earth shook and trembled. Dry vapor lifted up from the earth by the power of the heat of the sun is sometimes fine, and then it is raised higher and expands as was said above. Sometimes it is on the surface of the earth and is somewhat thicker; then, because of the cold seeping into it, it does not rise so far, and it is wind. Sometimes a thicker dry vapor arises in the earth, which, because of its thickness and the solidity and depth of the earth, it does not breathe forth but is collected in some cave of the earth appropriate to it, and it is compressed by another body unlike it in kind, and so it is disturbed within the bowels of the earth and so moves them. Nor is this surprising since we see the wind on the sea raise up waves like mountains, and on the earth it lifts up the trees and makes buildings fall, and in the air it causes great storms. Quod autem ventus sit causa terraemotus, signum est quod ante terraemotum consuevit fieri tranquillitas a ventis; sed post terraemotum sunt venti. Materia autem terraemotus subtiliata per calorem solis expirat a terra: et sic cessat terraemotus et fit ventus. Causa terraemotus est impulsio unius venti ab alio: et propterea non potest esse in tota terra simul, sed durant per ducenta miliaria ad plus, ut dicit Seneca. Et dicit quod terraemotus divisit Siciliam a Calabria, et Hispaniam ab Africa. Et durat aliquando per quadraginta dies; aliquando per unum annum. Now, a sign that wind is the cause of earthquakes is seen in that before an earthquake the wind is usually still, but after an earthquake there is wind. Then the subtle matter moving the earth rises from the earth through the heat of the sun, and so the earthquake stops and there are winds. The cause of an earthquake is the pressure of one wind upon another, and as a result there cannot be an earthquake throughout the whole earth at the same time, but they continue for two hundred miles or more, as Seneca says. And he says that an earthquake divided Sicily from Calabria and Hispania from Africa. And sometimes one can last for forty days, sometimes for a year. Item nota quod terra solida a qua non potest vapor exire exterius, apta est ut cito moveatur: ea enim quae est de natura lapidea, non leviter movetur et concutitur; oportet tamen ab aliqua parte porosam esse, unde ingrediatur vapor; ut per poros intret, et per soliditatem contineatur. Et si dicas, si ingrediatur non potest egredi, dicendum quod non potest semper hoc facere: quia aliquando semper continuatur ingressus et elevatio vaporis ad locum illum. Also, note that solid earth, from which vapor cannot breathe forth exteriorly, is more likely to be moved rapidly, for it is not the nature of stone to be lightly moved and shaken; nevertheless, there must be some porous part where the vapor enters, since vapor enters through the porous and is contained by the solid. And if you were to ask why if it enters it cannot get out, it could be said that it cannot always do that, since sometimes the entry of the vapor and its rising continues to this place. Et iterum, quia calidum non vadit inferius, ad hoc cooperatur unda maris claudens poros, et pro frigore recludens inferius. Unde loca cavernosa circa mare faciunt frequenter terraemotum. And likewise, since heat does not go into the lower regions, the waves of the sea add to this by closing the pores and sealing off the lower regions by their coldness. So a cavernous place near the sea has frequent earthquakes. Item nota quod iste vapor continue egreditur de terra quantum ad aliquid, et propterea tempore terraemotuum animalia quae portant caput juxta terram saepe ex hoc inficiuntur per vaporem illum venenosum egredientem de terra. Likewise, note that this vapor is always entering the earth to some degree, and for this reason animals who have their head near the earth are often poisoned by the poisonous vapor issuing from the earth. 127. Inclinavit. Hic agit de ventis. 127. He bowed the heavens. Here, he treats of the winds. Ubi nota quod materia venti est vapor vel exhalatio sicca calefacta, sed non ita subtiliata quod possit ad supremum locum ascendere, nec ita calefacta: unde impeditur a frigore et ingrossatur et repercutitur inferius: et haec repercussa movet aerem. Habet tamen tantum de caliditate quod non ita vincitur a frigore ut convertatur ad terram; et dicitur, caligo, et dicitur, sub pedibus, quia non est alta sicut illa quae accenditur in flamma. Note here that the matter of wind is vapor or a dry, warm exhalation, but it is not so fine nor so hot that it can ascend to the highest reaches, and so it is impeded by the cold and made to thicken and rebound in the lower regions, and this rebounding moves the air. Nevertheless, it has enough heat that it is not overcome by the cold and converted into earth, and so it is called darkness, and it is said to be under his feet, since it is not as high as that which is lit into flames. Aliquando autem non statim repercutitur, sed agitat nubes, quia non totaliter vincitur, nec directe redit inferius ad terram: et propter hunc motum tortuosum quasi nititur sursum ascendere, et non valet propter repercussionem; et hoc est quod dicit. Now sometimes it does not rebound immediately, but it stirs up the clouds since it is not totally held down; nor does it go directly down to the earth; because of this has a twisting motion, so it seems to attempt to arise, but cannot because of it rebounding, and this is what he says.
