      Primo ergo ponit primam commutationem. Therefore, he first describes the first movement; Secundo secundam, ibi, prae fulgore. second, the second: at the brightness. Circa primum tria facit. He makes three points about the change from clear to cloudy. Primo ostendit nubilosi temporis obscuritatem. First, he shows the obscurity of the cloudy time. Secundo adhibet similitudinem. Second, he gives an image of this. Tertio ponit obscuritatis causam. Third, he describes the cause of the obscurity. Dicit ergo quantum ad primum: posuit tenebras latibulum suum. Dicitur quod Deus habitat in caelo. Unde quando nubes occultant caelum, videtur Deus habitare in occulto: Ezech. 32: caelum nube tegam. Et posuit similitudinem de tabernaculo: et ideo dicit, in circuitu ejus tabernaculum ejus. Tabernaculum enim ponitur et deponitur sicut nubes. Dicit, tenebrosa aqua in nubibus aeris. Therefore, as a description of the cloudiness, he says, he made darkness his covert. It is said that God dwells in heaven. Therefore, when the clouds hide heaven, God seems to dwell in concealment. I will cover heaven with a cloud (Ezek 32:7). And he gives an image related to the tabernacle, and so he says, his pavilion round about him. For a tabernacle is pitched and moved as the clouds are. He says, dark waters in the clouds of the air. Consequenter agit de secunda. Prae fulgore et cetera et utitur tali similitudine: quando venit lux, expelluntur tenebrae; et sic Deo ostendente lumen suum, fugit obscuritas nebularum. Et ideo dicit: prae fulgore in conspectu ejus nubes transierunt, prae fulgore luminis a facie tua nubes transierunt, sicut fulgore sive splendore solis, nubes fugiunt et liquefiunt, ut in Lib. Meteo. dicitur. Then he treats of the second movement in regard to clouds and rain: at the brightness that was before him. And he uses a similar image. When light comes, it expels the darkness, and so, when God shows his light, the obscurity of the clouds flees. And so he says, at the brightness that was before him the clouds passed: before the brightness of the light of your face, the clouds passed, just as clouds flee and evaporate at the light or splendor of the sun, as is said in the Meteorology. Dali vel titiones ponuntur in transitu nubium: quia similem causam generationis habet grando et fulgur, sive ignis. Antiqui vero dicunt, quod generantur in loco supremo; quod ostendit fortiorem congelationem a forti frigore causari. Unde plus requirit de frigore nix quam aqua: pluviae et grando plus quam nix: et tantum potest esse frigus, quod statim condensat in grandinem: aliquando prius in aquam, et postea in grandinem. Et dicunt, quod vapores superius elevati congelantur multum, et ideo generantur grossi grandines. Brands or firebrands occur during the passing of clouds, since the cause of their generation is like that of hail and lightning, or fire. Indeed, the ancients said that they are generated in the highest place and that they are caused by a more intense congealing from a stronger cold. So snow requires more intense cold than water, and rain and hail more than snow. So when it is very cold it immediately condenses into hail. Sometimes precipitation begins as water and afterwards becomes hail. And they say that the vapors that rise higher freeze more and so produce larger hailstones. Sed Philosophus e contra dicit, quod grossiores essent in montibus, et in hyeme: cujus contrarium videmus, quia grossiores sunt in valle, et fiunt in vere et autumno, et generantur in loco propinquo. But, on the contrary, the Philosopher says that they should be larger in the mountains and in winter, but we see the opposite, since they are actually larger in valleys and in spring and autumn, and they are generated in a nearby place. Item secundum Philosophum, aliquando veniunt angulares, quod est signum quod veniunt de propinquo: anguli enim citius liquefiunt. Likewise, according to the Philosopher, they are sometimes angular, which is a sign that they come from nearby, for those which are angular melt more quickly. Unde sciendum, quod naturale est quod oppositum fortius agat in oppositum. Constat autem quod in nubibus admiscetur frigidum et calidum; ergo quando calor aeris circumstans constringit frigidum quod non potest consumere, tunc frigidum agit interius circumdante extra calore. Now it is known that it is natural for an opposite to act more strongly on an opposite. Now it happens that in clouds the cold and hot are mixed together; therefore, when moving hot air encircles and compresses the cold which it is not able to consume, then the cold air circulates around within the surrounding heat. Titiones autem cadentes habent duplicem causam generationis: But falling firebrands can be generated from two causes. unam per fumum superius ascendentem usque ad locum inflammationis, qui inflammatur; et sic secundum inflammationem descendit quousque invenit materiam combustibilem. Et hoc tetigit quando dixit, carbones succensi sunt ab eo. In one way, from smoke, which ascends high up to a point where it ignites, and then descends until it finds combustible material. And he touches on this when he says, coals were kindled by it. Et hic tangit alium modum, qui est per contrariam resistentiam. In nube autem aliquando est aliquid calidum, et istud a frigido exteriori constringitur interius et multiplicatur, ita quod materiam grossam adducit et cadit: et ideo carbones, ignis, et grando habent similem generationem, scilicet constrictionem frigoris vel caloris, ut dictum est. Dicit ergo, prae fulgore in conspectu ejus et cetera. Et haec transierunt simul cum carbone et grandine, quae generantur ex nubibus, ut dictum est. And here he touches on the other way, which comes about through resistance against it. Now, sometimes in a cloud there is some heat, and it is constricted inside by the cold on the outside, and it increases, so that the material sticks together and falls, and so coals of fire and hailstones have a similar origin, namely, the constriction of the cold or the hot, as was said. Therefore, he says, at the brightness that was before him. And these pass at the same time as coals and hailstones which are generated from clouds, as has been said. 129. Hic agit de tertia permutatione. 129. Here he treats of the third movement. Et primo de tonitruo. First, he speaks about thunder; Secundo de fulgoribus, ibi, misit sagittas. second, about lightning, at he sent forth his arrows. Sciendum quod psalmista loquitur hic secundum hanc similitudinem, quod quicquid fit in caelo, attribuatur Deo. Unde sonum auditum in caelo accipit, quasi vox Dei esset. It should be known that the psalmist says this using an image so that whatever is in the heavens will be attributed to God. So the sound heard in heaven is taken as the voice of God. Est autem duplex sonus in caelo. Now, there are two sounds in heaven. Unus qui est in tonitruo; et hic, licet aliqui dicant extinctionem ignis in nube, psalmista reprobat, et dicit quod fit per concussionem ventorum: ita et nubes. Et ideo psalmista dicit, intonuit de caelo dominus. One is thunder, and although some say it is the extinguishing of fire in the cloud, the psalmist rejects this, and says that it occurs when winds and clouds crash together. And so the psalmist says, the Lord thundered from heaven. Item aliquando nubes grossae ex quibus grandines generantur quandoque cum sonitu: unde Philosophus dicit, quod aliquando ante grandinem est fragor nubium, aliquando non: sicut enim vapor calidus et siccus expulsus a frigido, scindes nubem facit sonum, ut patet in fulgure, sic vapor humidus congelatus in grandinem, et expulsus a calido, scindit aliqualiter et facit sonum. Likewise, sometimes there are thick clouds from which hailstones are generated with sound, so the Philosopher says that sometimes a noise comes from a cloud before hail is generated, and sometimes it does not, for just as when hot and dry vapor is expelled from the cold, it makes a sound as it tears the cloud, so does this happen in lightning: moist vapor freezes into hail and, expelled by the hot, also tears the cloud and makes a sound. Et ideo dicit, altissimus dedit vocem suam, id est manifestavit potentiam suam et sequitur, grando et carbones ignis, quae ex his nubibus generantur, ut dictum est. And so he says, the Highest gave his voice, that is, manifested his power, and it follows, hail and coals of fire, which are generated from the clouds, as has been said. Vel sic, intonuit de caelo. Nota quod aliquando ad locum superiorem ascendit vapor humidus: et quia est de natura aquae, fiunt ex eo impressiones humidae, quae sunt nebula, ros, caligo, pluvia, grando, et nix, et hujusmodi. Or so: he thundered from heaven. Note that sometimes moist vapor ascends to a higher place, and because it has the nature of water, from it come the forms of humidity which are clouds, dew, mist, rain, hail, and snow, and these kinds of things. Diversificantur autem ista aliquando diversitate quantum ad caloris et frigoris tenuitatem et spissitudinem. Aliquando enim ascendit vapor siccus; et si solus ascendit, facit ventos; si autem sit comprehensus ille vapor siccus in vapore humido, tunc quando vapor humidus sursum ascendit, et incipit inspissari propter frigus, vapor siccus in vapore illo humido inclusus facit agitationem magnam et inflammatur: talis enim vapor cito inflammatur, ut est videre in vapore qui egreditur de ventre hominis: et haec inflammatio causa est fulguris et coruscationis. Now, these different forms occur sometimes because of the difference in the fineness or density of the hot and cold. For sometimes dry vapor ascends, and if it ascends alone, it makes winds. However, if this dry vapor is surrounded by humid vapor, then, when the humid vapor ascends and begins to thicken because of the cold, the dry vapor enclosed in the humid vapor is greatly agitated and kindled, for such vapor is easily kindled, as is seen in the vapor which comes forth from the belly of a man, and this kindling is the cause of lightning and flashing. Agitatus autem vapor siccus in interioribus nubibus multiplicem sonum facit. Now, the dry vapor moving about inside the clouds makes many sounds. Si etiam sic inflammatus percutiat latera nubis, et non scindat, tunc micat non clare; sicut si aliquis aliquem splendorem videret per pannum: est enim nubes aliquantulum pervia, unde aliqualiter videtur. Sonat autem sicut sonus flammae in medio incendio. For if, when kindled, it beats against the side of a cloud and does not tear it, then it glitters indistinctly; as if someone should see a light through a cloth, for insofar as the cloud is somewhat translucent, it is seen partially. The sound is like that of a flame in the middle of a conflagration. Aliquando etiam sine inflammatione, et per consequens sine coruscatione fit sonus, quasi tumultuans: et hoc fit cum percutit, non inflammatus in lateribus nubis. Sometimes, it does not kindle, and, without flashing, it makes sound like a tumult, and this takes place when it pierces through the sides of clouds without being kindled. Si autem percutiat latera et scindat, tamen cum difficultate quadam, et hoc in parte grossiori nubis, tunc est terribilis sonus, quasi aliquis pannum immensae latitudinis scinderet, et tunc visus fulguris vel coruscationis est curvus: quia non recte egreditur de nube, ut dictum est. Now, if it pierces the side and tears through it, although with some difficulty—this takes place in parts of the thicker clouds—then there is a terrible sound, like someone tearing a great length of cloth, and then the lightning and flashing seen are curved, since they do not come forth directly from the cloud, as has been said. Aliquando scindit nubem virtute magna et quasi subito, et totus vapor simul egreditur; et tunc sonat sicut vesica inflammata, vel si uter inflatus frangeretur super caput alicujus: et percutit aerem percussione fortissima. Sometimes it tears a cloud with great power and almost immediately, and then the whole vapor issues forth at the same time, and then the sound is as though an inflamed bladder or an inflated skin were broken on someone’s head, and it strikes the air with a strong clapping sound. Aliquando vapor ille siccus ex inflatione crescit, et quaerens majorem locum facit dissolvere nubem subito, et sonare ad modum viridium lignorum crepidantium in igne, vel ovorum maxime; et hoc maxime apparet in castaneis, quibus in igne positis cum humidum incipit resolvi, et majorem locum quaerere, frangit testam resistentem, et cum impetu et sono magno exit. Sometimes, the dry vapor grows as it expands, and, seeking a larger place, it suddenly dissolves the cloud with a sound like green wood or a large egg crackling in the fire, and this is especially apparent in chestnuts: when they are placed in fire, the moisture within them begins to loosen, and seeking a larger place, breaks the resisting shell and exits with a great sound. Aliquando etiam non valens exire extinguitur; et sonat ad modum ferri candentis in aqua extincti; quem sonum vocat Philosophus sisinum, vel stridorem. Sometimes, it cannot entirely exit, and it sounds like white-hot iron extinguished in water, which sound the Philosopher calls sisinum or hissing. Aliquando etiam ille vapor facit diversa foramina in locis nubis minus spissis, et tunc facit quasi sonum sibili, sicut ventus quando exit per foramina. Sometimes this vapor makes various fissures in places in a less dense cloud, and then it makes a sound of whistling, like the wind when it exits through a fissure. Aliquando antequam incendatur erumpit de nube, et tunc sonat sicut folles fabriles cum sufflant. Sometimes it bursts forth from a cloud before lighting, and then it sounds like a workman’s bellows when they are pumped. 130. Fulgura. Hic describit motum fulgurum, et comparat ea sagittae propter vehementiam venti a quo moventur. 130. He multiplied lightnings. Here he describes the motion of lightning, and he compares it to arrows on account of the intensity of the wind by which it is moved. Et dissipavit eos, scilicet peccatores, qui aliquando ex eis moventur: secundum diversitatem enim ventorum est diversitas motus fulguris: nam sicut superius cum de modo ventorum agebatur dixit, volavit volavit et cetera ut ostenderet diversum modum ventorum, ita hic dicit, fulgura et cetera ut ostendat diversum motum fulgurum. And he scattered them, namely, sinners, who sometimes are moved by these. For there are different movements of lightning which correspond to the different winds. For just as above he said, he flew, he flew, about the ways in which the wind is agitated to show the different forms of the winds, so here he says, lightnings, to show the different types of lightning.
