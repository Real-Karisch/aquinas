      Qui dicitur noster specialiter pietate, et cultura, et unione naturae, et carnis assumptione, et redemptione. In hoc confunduntur Manichaei: quia hic est Deus et dominus visibilium, et quod Deus veteris testamenti est verus Deus, quia nullus Deus praeter eum. He is said to be ours particularly through piety, and worship, and the union of nature, and the assumption of flesh, and redemption. In this the Manicheans are confounded, since this God is also the Lord of visible things, and it testifies that the God of the Old Testament is the true God, since there is no God but him. 139. Deus. Hic ostendit quomodo habet a Deo idoneitatem ad vincendum, Deus enim aliquando dat virtutem alicui ad bene operandum; nec tamen sufficit nisi Deus protegat eum exterius. 139. God who has girt me with strength. Here he shows how he received from God the ability to conquer, for God sometimes gives someone the power to work well, although it is not enough unless God also protects him outwardly. Primo ergo ostendit quomodo Deus dedit virtutem interius. Therefore, first he shows how God gave him interior strength; Secundo, quomodo juvat exterius, ibi, dedisti mihi protectionem. second, how God helped him exteriorly, at you have given me the protection. Tria sunt necessaria alicui ad vincendum: Three things are necessary for someone to conquer: scilicet quod sit fortis: Prov. ult.: fortitudo et decor indumentum ejus: Luc. 11: fortis armatus custodit atrium suum: that he be strong: strength and beauty are her clothing (Prov 31:25); a strong man armed keeps his court (Luke 11:21); quod sit agilis, that he be agile; et quod sit doctus in bello; and that he be practiced in war. et haec tria dicit se habere. And he claims to have these three. Secundo, ibi, qui perficit pedes meos. The second is at who has made my feet. Tertio, ibi, qui docet manus. The third is at who teaches my hands. Circa primum duo facit. He says two things about strength. Primo confitetur sibi datam fortitudinem a Deo. First, he confesses that he has received fortitude from God. Secundo debitum fortitudinis usum. Second, he has been given the ability to use that fortitude. Dicit ergo, Deus qui praecinxit me virtute ad bellum et cetera. Milites praedicti praecinguntur armis et gladio ut sint expediti et parati ad pugnam: 1 Mach. 3: Judas Machabaeus induit se lorica sicut gigas: et succinxit se arma bellica in praeliis. Haec est fortitudo, scilicet virtus quae data est mihi a Deo, non solum in corporalibus bellis, sed et in spiritualibus, quae non vincerem sine virtute Dei. Et ideo dicit, praecinxit me virtute: Eph. 6: confortamini in domino et in potentia virtutis Dei: Isa. 40: qui dat lapso virtutem, et his qui non sunt, fortitudinem et robur multiplicat. Therefore, he says, you have girded me with strength unto battle. Soldiers are said to be girded with arms and with a sword so that they can go forth and be prepared to fight. Judas Maccabeus . . . put on a breastplate as a giant, and girt his warlike armor about him in battles (1 Macc 3:1, 3). This is fortitude, namely the strength which is given to me from God, not only for bodily wars, but also for the spiritual, in which I cannot conquer without the strength of God. And so he says, you have girded me with strength. Be strengthened in the Lord, and in the might of his power (Eph 6:10). He who gives strength to the fallen, and increases force and might to those who are not (Isa 40:29). Vel praecinxit ad modum currentis ne impediatur ex defluxu vestium. Ita virtus Dei retinet affectum ne defluat ad terrena. Or, you have girded me to run without being impeded by flowing garments. So the strength of God holds in the affections so that they do not flow down towards earthly things. Et ideo sequitur, et posuit immaculatam viam meam: 1 Reg. 25: benedictus Deus, qui custodivit servum suum a malo: Ps. 118: beati immaculati in via. And so it follows, and made my way blameless. Blessed be God . . . who has kept his servant from evil (1 Sam 25:39). Blessed are the undefiled in the way (Ps 118:1). Vel via ista est via caritatis, quae non agit perperam, ut supra dictum est. Or the way is the way of charity, which does not do anything wrongly, as was said above. 140. Qui. Hic ponitur agilitas, quae necessaria est ad pugnandum. 140. Who has made my feet. Here he describes the agility which is necessary for fighting. 1 Reg. 24, dicitur quod egressus est Saul contra David super petras abruptissimas, quae solis cervis perviae sunt; quasi dicat, tantam agilitatem mihi contulit Deus, quod quasi cervus ibam per montes. In 1 Samuel 24 it is said that Saul had come against David out over the precipitous rocks which only a deer could pass through; the text is as if to say, “God has conferred such great agility upon me that I was going through the mountains like a deer.” Et super excelsa statuit me. In montibus declivibus vestigia hominis non figuntur: sed Deus dedit ei tantam gratiam ut non laberetur in eis. And who sets me upon high places. The footsteps of man are not able to fasten themselves upon the steep mountains, but God gave him such grace that he did not slip in them. Mystice legitur sic. Cervus transcendit sine laesione spinas et silvas: sic spiritualis affectus pertransit sine laesione et infectione mala, sive delectationes mundi: Gen. Pen.: Nephtalim cervus emissus dans eloquia pulchritudinis: Is. 35: tunc saliet sicut cervus claudus. Mystically, it can be read in this way: a deer passes through the thorns and forests without being torn by spines, and so the affections of the spiritual man pass without being torn or infected by evil or the delights of the world. Naphtali, a hart let loose, and giving words of beauty (Gen 49:21). Then shall the lame man leap as a hart (Isa 35:6). Et super excelsa statuit me, id est super caelestia statuit mentem meam defixam: Habac. 3: super excelsa mea deducet me victor. And who sets me upon high places, that is, he makes my mind be fixed upon heavenly things. The conqueror will lead me upon my high places (Hab 3:19). Consequenter ponitur doctrina militaris; unde ait, qui docet manus meas ad praelium. Doctrina militaris acquiritur scientia et perfectio exercitio. Primo ergo scientiam sive doctrinam quaerit, quia haec doctrina necessaria est militibus. Prov. 24: cum dispositione initur bellum. Sed iste edoctus a Deo dicit quantum ad secundum, posuisti ut arcum aereum brachia mea, id est brachia quasi infatigabilia mihi ad bellandum dedisti. Afterwards, he describes military knowledge; so he says, who teaches my hands to war. Military knowledge is an acquired science and is perfected by its exercise. Therefore, he first seeks this knowledge or teaching, since this teaching is necessary for soldiers. War is managed by due ordering (Prov 24:6). But he says he is taught by God in regard to the following: you have made my arms like a brazen bow, that is you have given my arms untiring strength for fighting. Vel, qui docet et cetera, id est contra vitia et Daemones, docet nos operari ad superandos hostes, qui caeli portas claudere conantur. Or: who teaches my hands to war, that is, he teaches us to act against vices and the demons to overcome the enemies, who try to close the gates of heaven. Postea mutans personam dicit, posuisti et cetera. Afterwards, changing the person, he says, you have made my arms like a brazen bow. Alia littera habet, confregisti arcum aereum, id est brachium meum. Ps. 143: benedictus dominus Deus meus, qui docet manus meas ad praelium et cetera. Another text has, you have broken a brazen bow, that is, my arm. Blessed be the Lord my God, who teaches my hands to fight (Ps 143:1). Nota, quod excellentia agilitatis et excellentia fortitudinis est in leonibus, qui ex siccitate nimia, non habent medullam in ossibus; et haec contingunt ex magna inaequalitate elementorum immixta: et ideo parum vivunt; et hoc non decet in homine propter operationes ejus: unde hujusmodi dicuntur ex speciali munere data David, ut dicitur Eccl. 47: lusit cum leonibus quasi cum agnis: et ursis similiter fecit sicut cum agnis eorum. Notice that excellent agility and excellent strength are found in lions, which, from their great dryness, do not have marrow in their bones, and this results from a great inequality in the mixture of the elements and so they live only briefly. This does not apply to man in regard to his operations, so these things are said because of a special gift bestowed upon David, as is said, he played with lions as with lambs: and with bears he did in like manner as with the lambs of his (Sir 47:3). Et similiter sibi data est a Deo ex gratia doctrina pugnandi, mystice. Oportet nos in spirituali bello esse doctos. Eccl. 11: multae insidiae sunt dolosi quas non possumus evadere nisi habeamus et doctrinam et auxilium divinum. Job 39: gloria narium ejus, id est Daemonis, terror contemnit pavorem nec cedit gladio. 1 Cor. 4: licet is qui foris est noster homo corrumpatur, tamen qui intus est, renovatur de die in diem. And mystically, something similar was given by God of the grace of learning to fight. Now we must be taught in spiritual warfare. Many are the snares of the deceitful (Sir 11:31), which we cannot escape unless we have teaching and divine help. The glory of his nostrils, that is, of the demon, is terror. . . . He despises fear; he turns not his back to the sword (Job 39:20, 22). Though our outward man is corrupted, yet the inward man is renewed day by day (1 Cor 4:16). 141. Et dedisti. Hic ostendit quomodo praedicta sibi data conservantur a Deo exterius; unde dicit, praecinxisti me virtute: et tamen protegit me: et dedisti mihi protectionem salutis tuae, id est protexisti me ad salutem: quia non sufficiunt praedicta nisi adsit protectio Dei. Ps. 63: protexisti me Deus a conventu malignantium et cetera. 141. And you have given me. Here he shows how those things mentioned above are preserved by God exteriorly, so he says, you have girded me with strength, yet he also protects me: and you have given me the protection of your salvation, that is, you have protected me for salvation, since what was spoken about above is not sufficient unless the protection of God is added. God, you have protected me from the assembly of the malignant (Ps 63:3). Et dextera tua suscepit me, id est favor gratiae tuae me in bello confortavit. De hoc bello, Ezech. 3: manus domini erat mecum confortans me. And your right hand has held me, that is, the favor of your grace has strengthened me in war. About this war is said, the hand of the Lord was with me, strengthening me (Ezek 3:14). Vel, dextera tua, id est Filius tuus, suscepit me, id est naturam meam. Or, your right hand, that is, your Son, has held me, that is, my nature. Vel, me, infirmum ad curandum, et disciplina tua, correxit me in finem, id est finaliter et perfecte. Prov. 3: quem diligit dominus corrigit et castigat, et quasi pater in filio complacet sibi: unde, disciplina tua correxit me in finem. Or it means, me, weak and needing to be cured, and your discipline has corrected me unto the end, that is, finally and perfectly. Whom the Lord loves, he chastises and as a father in the son he pleases himself (Prov 3:12). So: your discipline has corrected me unto the end. Quantum ad secundum dicit, disciplina et cetera. In regard to the second he says, discipline. Contingit aliquando quod aliquis dubitat, aliquando errat. Ille autem, scilicet Deus errata corrigit; unde disciplina tua correxit me in finem, ut supra dictum est. Sometimes, someone may doubt; sometimes he may err. But he, namely, God, corrects the error, so: your discipline has corrected me to the end, as was said above. Item dubitata dirigit: et disciplina tua ipsa me docebit. Ps. 118: bonitatem et disciplinam et scientiam doce me. Likewise, he directs what is doubtful: and your discipline, the same shall teach me. Teach me goodness and discipline and knowledge (Ps 118:66).
