      Primo perversitatem vitiorum. first, the depravity of the vices; Secundo abominationem eorum. second, their abomination. In corporibus sequitur corruptio per exhalationem naturalis caloris expulsi ab extraneo calore. Calor namque naturalis animae est amor Dei. Quando ergo subintrat extraneus amor concupiscentiae, et aliorum peccatorum, recedit Deus. In physical things, corruption comes about through the exhalation of natural warmth driven out by alien heat. Now the natural heat of the soul is the love of God. Therefore, when the alien heat of concupiscence and other sins enters in, God withdraws. Et ideo cum dixit, non est Deus, statim subdit, corrupti sunt: Hier. 5: negaverunt Deum, et dixerunt, non est ipse, scilicet peccatorum damnator, justorum remunerator: Eccl. 21: cor fatui, scilicet peccatoris, quasi vas confractum. And so, after he says, there is no God, it immediately follows that they are corrupt. They have denied God, and said, “It is not he” (Jer 5:12), namely, the one who condemns sinners and rewards the just. The heart of the fool, namely, of the sinner, is like a broken vessel (Sir 21:17). Corrupti ergo sunt, peccatores per malum actum: Jo. 3: erant autem eorum mala opera: quia postquam per consensum amittunt bona gratuita, corrumpuntur in eis naturalia; et ideo sequitur eos poena: Ps. 96: ignis ante ipsum praecedet, et inflammabit in circuitu inimicos ejus. Therefore, they, the sinners, are corrupt, through their evil actions. For their works were evil (John 3:19), since after they lost, through consent, the goods given freely, their natural qualities became corrupted, and so punishment follows. A fire shall go before him, and shall burn his enemies round about (Ps 96:3). Item quando corpus putrescit, redditur abominabile. Sic anima hominis, quamdiu amor Dei est in ea, est Deo accepta; sed quando corrumpitur per peccatum, fit abominabilis. Just as when a body putrefies, it becomes abominable, so the soul of a man is acceptable to God when the love of God is in it, but when it is corrupted through sin it becomes abominable. Abominabile est quod humanus appetitus refugit: et ideo dicit, et abominabiles facti sunt, scilicet Deo et hominibus peccatores per consuetudinem peccandi: Hier. 2: quam vilis facta es nimis, iterans vias tuas! Osee 9: alienati sunt in confusione, et facti sunt abominabiles sicut ea quae dilexerunt. The abominable is that from which the human appetite flees, and so he says, they, namely sinners, are become abominable to God and men through the habit of sinning. How exceeding base you are become, going your ways! (Jer 2:36). They alienated themselves in confusion, and became abominable as those things were, which they loved (Hos 9:10). Et dixit: in studiis, quia per ea deveniunt abominabilia. And he said, in their eager ways, since through them they became abominable. Vel studiose faciunt, secundum Hieronymum. Plus enim abominatur Deus studiosam voluntatem peccandi, quam ipsum peccatum: Job 34: quasi de industria recesserunt ab eo: et vias ejus intelligere noluerunt. Or: they did them eagerly, according to Jerome. For God detests the will that is eager to sin more than the sin itself. Who as it were on purpose have revolted from him, and would not understand all his ways (Job 34:27). Alia littera habet, corrumpunt et abominati sunt studium, scilicet sapientiae et disciplinae: Prov. 1: exosam habuerunt disciplinam, et timorem domini non susceperunt: Ezech. 8: adhuc videbis abominationes majores. Another text has, they have corrupted and detested the pursuit that belongs to wisdom and discipline. Because they have hated instruction and received not the fear of the Lord (Prov 1:29). You shall see greater abominations (Ezek 8:6). Consequenter agit de vitatione boni. Non est qui faciat bonum, quia non est justus in terra qui faciat bonum, et non peccet, Eccl. 7. Next, he treats the ruin of the good: there is no one who does good, since there is no just man upon the earth who does good and sins not (Eccl 7:21). Usque ad unum, id est Christum, quia ipse solus nec peccatum contractum habuit, nec commissum. Beata virgo habuit contractum peccatum: Eccl. 7: virum de mille unum reperi, mulierem ex omnibus non inveni. One, that is, Christ, who alone neither contracted nor committed sin. The Blessed Virgin had contracted sin. One man among a thousand have I found, a woman among them all I have not found (Eccl 7:29). Vel, usque ad unum, quia nec unus est qui perfecte faciat bonum. Verum est, supposito quod dixerunt, non est Deus, et corrupti sunt. Or, not one, since there is not even one who does good perfectly. This is true, supposing that they have said, there is no God, and that they are corrupt. 97. Dominus. Hic primo certificat culpam. 97. The Lord has looked down. Here, he first makes certain of their guilt; Secundo exponit, ibi, sepulcrum. second, explains it, at sepulcher. Peccatum certificatur per iniquitatem; et ideo hic ponit Dei inquisitionem, dicens, vos dicitis quod non est Deus; sed falsum est, quia dominus de caelo prospexit super filios hominum: Prov. 16: omnes viae hominis patent oculis ejus. Prospexit ergo mittendo Filium suum dominus Pater de caelo, id est de sinu pietatis suae: Isa. ult. caelum mihi sedes est. Now, sin is proven through wickedness, and so he records the inquiry of God, saying, “You say that there is no God, but this is false, since the Lord has looked down from heaven upon the children of men.” All the ways of a man are open to his eyes (Prov 16:2). Therefore, the Lord, the Father, has looked down by sending his own Son from heaven, that is, from the inner heart of his piety. Heaven is my throne (Isa 66:1). Vel de caelo, id est Christus per quem judicabit peccatores. Or, the Lord has looked down from heaven, that is, Christ, through whom he will judge sinners. Vel aliter. Dicunt aliqui quod Deus non cognoscit singularia et mutabilia, quia est immaterialis et simplex et aeternus. Et sic non cognoscit secundum motus rerum, et secundum modum suae cognoscibilitatis. Or, it can be taken in another way, so that some say that God does not know singular and changeable things, since he is immaterial and simple and eternal. And so he does not know in accordance with the movement of things, but only according to the mode of his own ability to be known. Respondetur: immo: quia cognoscit materialia immaterialiter, ut Dionysius concludit. Et ita etiam cognoscit intellectus: et ideo dicit: de caelo, id est de altitudine suae dignitatis et naturae. Prospexit super filios hominum. The response is that, on the contrary, he knows material things immaterially, as Dionysius concludes. And so he knows intellectually, and so he says, from heaven, that is, from the height of his own dignity and nature, he has looked down upon the children of men. Et vult invenire in nobis voluntate antecedente, qua vult omnes salvos fieri, id quod pertinet ad salutem, scilicet ut cognoscamus Deum per intellectum, et amemus per affectum, et desideremus. And he desires, with his antecedent will by which he desires all to be saved, to find in us that which belongs to salvation—namely, that we know God with our intellect, and love him with our affections, and desire him. Et ideo dicit, ut videat, id est, ut videre faciat, quia ipse semper videt. Si est intelligens, per intellectum: Deut. 32: utinam saperent et intelligerent et cetera. Aut requirens Deum, per affectum. And so he says, to see, that is, to cause sight, since he himself always sees. If there are any who understand through the intellect: O that they would be wise and would understand (Deut 32:29). Or who seek God through the affection. Quid inveniet, cum hic quaesivit? Contrarium: quia omnes declinaverunt; Will he find what he is seeking? On the contrary, he will find that they are all gone aside. et ponit tria: He describes three things: scilicet declinationem a Deo, their going aside from God, inutilem operationem, their unprofitable action, et cessationem a bono. and their ceasing to do good. Dicit ergo, omnes declinaverunt, scilicet a Deo: Deut. 31: novi quod post mortem meam inique agetis, et declinabitis de via quam praecepi vobis: Oseae 4: non est veritas, non est scientia, non est misericordia Dei in terra: Hier. 8: nemo quod bonum est loquitur, nullus est qui agat poenitentiam de peccato suo. Therefore, he says, they are all gone aside from God. I know that after my death you will do wickedly, and will quickly turn aside from the way that I have commanded you (Deut 31:29). There is no truth, there is no knowledge, there is no mercy of God in the land (Hos 4:1). No man speaks what is good; there is no one who does penance for his sin (Jer 8:6). Item ex hoc quod declinat a Deo, efficitur inutilis: quia illud est inutile, quod non attingit ad id ad quod factum est. Homo autem factus est ad fruendum Deo: Sap. 4: multigena impiorum multitudo non erit utilis. Unde dicit: simul inutiles facti sunt. Also, because he has fallen away from God, he has become unprofitable, since that is unprofitable which does not attain that for which it was made. Now, man was made to enjoy God. The multiplied brood of the wicked shall not thrive (Wis 4:3). So he says, they are become unprofitable together. Item cessant a bono, quia non est qui faciat bonum et cetera. Hoc jam expositum est. Likewise, they have ceased to do good, since there is no one who does good. This has already been explained. 98. Sepulcrum. 99. Their throat is an open sepulcher. Hieronymus dicit, quod Apostolus utitur testimonio istorum versuum, sepulcrum patens et cetera. Et invenitur alibi in sacra Scriptura Rom. 3, ubi dicit quod aliquam partem accepit ex Isa. 59, aliam ex aliis partibus Psalterii, et non ex isto psalmo tantum, cum ipse Hebraeus esset, et sciret hoc in Hebraeo non haberi; Jerome says that the Apostle uses the witness of these verses: their throat is an open sepulcher. And this image is found elsewhere in Sacred Scripture in Romans 3, where he says something that is taken from Isaiah 59 and partly from other places in the Psalter, and not from this psalm alone, since he was Hebrew and would know that this is not found here in the Hebrew. habetur tamen in editione vulgata, qua vulgus utitur, vel quae non attribuitur alicui certae personae. Nevertheless, it is included in the edition called the Vulgate, either because the common people use it or because it is not attributed to any specific person. A sepulcro patenti exhalat foetor; et ideo supra psalmista certificavit malitiam, sive culpam inimicorum; hic autem exponit eam: An open sepulcher exhales a stench, and so while the psalmist verified the malice or the guilt of his enemies above, here he explains it further. et circa hoc duo facit. He explains their guilt through two points. Primo ostendit, quomodo sunt peccatores inutiles aliis. First, he shows how sinners are unprofitable to others; Secundo quomodo sibi, ibi, contritio et infelicitas. second, to themselves, at destruction and unhappiness.
