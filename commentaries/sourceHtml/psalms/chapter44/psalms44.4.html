      Hic gladius est ex utraque parte acutus, ut testatur Apoc. 1, quia de aeternis et temporalibus instruxit. Et est super femur, quia organo humanitatis verbo doctrinae usus est: Isa. 52: ego ipse qui loquebar, ecce adsum. This sword is sharp and two-edged (Rev 1:16), since it teaches about both eternal and temporal things. And it is upon his thigh, because the instrument of his humanity used the word of teaching. It was myself who spoke, “behold I am here” (Isa 52:6). Potentissime. Ubi ostenditur ejus virtus seu potentia naturalis: 1 Reg. 2: non est fortis sicut Deus noster: Job 9: si fortitudo quaeritur, robustissimus est. O you, Most Mighty. Here, his strength or natural power is shown. There is none strong like our God (1 Sam 2:2). If strength be demanded, he is most strong (Job 9:19). Specie tua. Secundum Hieronymum et Hebraeos hoc quod dicitur, specie, conjungitur cum hoc, quod dicitur, potentissime, et est totum sub uno versu, et sic uno modo legitur in glossa; et si sic conjungatur cum potentissime, sic est sensus: tu Christe es potentissimus, specie tua, scilicet humanitatis, secundum quam etiam est maximus virtute: Cant. 5: species ejus ut Libani. With your comeliness. According to Jerome and the Hebrews, the word comeliness is connected to O Most Mighty, and the whole idea is contained in one verse, and it is read as one idea in the gloss, and if it is thus joined with O Most Mighty, the meaning is: you, Christ are the most mighty in your species, namely, your humanity, according to which he is also mightiest in strength. His form as of Lebanon (Song 5:15). Et pulchritudine tua, scilicet divinitatis: Sapien. 13: quorum si specie delectati deos putaverunt, sciant quanto his dominator eorum speciosior est. And with your beauty, namely, of the divinity. With whose beauty, if they, being delighted, took them to be gods, let them know how much the Lord of them is more beautiful than they (Wis 13:3). Vel es potentissimus, specie tua, id est speciosa pulchritudine tua. Or, you are most mighty, with your comeliness, that is, your comely beauty. Unde Hieronymus habet, laude tua, quia ex hoc es laudabilis et gloriosus, quia es armatus et fortis. So Jerome has, with your praise, since you are praiseworthy and glorious because you are armed and strong. 454. Intende, prospere procede. Psalmista supra ad commendationem Christi posuit fortitudinem et apparatum regis; hic autem agit de ejus processu: 454. Set out, proceed prosperously. Above, the psalmist described Christ’s strength and kingly accoutrements to praise him; now here, he treats his going forth. et circa hoc duo facit. About this he does two things. Primo proponit processum regis. First, he describes the king’s going forth; Secundo ejus causam, ibi, propter veritatem. second, its cause, at because of truth. Circa primum sciendum est quod loco istorum trium quae hic ponuntur, scilicet, intende, prospere procede, et regna, in psalmo Hieronymi habetur unum tantum, prospere ascende. In ascensu designatur processus: Jerem. 49: leo ascendit, et superbia Jordanis ad pulchritudinem robustam. Unde manifestum est quod haec pertinent ad quamdam perfectionem et ascensum. Regarding the first, it should be known that, of the three things said here, namely, set out, proceed prosperously, and reign, there is only one in Jerome’s psalm, proceed upwards. Ascent signifies procession. A lion has come up from the swelling of the Jordan, against the strong and beautiful (Jer 49:19). So it is clear that these things pertain to a certain perfection and ascent. In ascensu bellicosi sunt tria; In going up to war, there are three things: principium, the beginning, medium the middle, et finis. and the end. Principium debet esse diligens et discreta consideratio: Prov. 24: cum dispositione initur bellum: et Luc. 14 dicitur, quod rex iturus ad bellum prius et cetera. Unde, intende, id est diligenter considera. In the beginning there should be careful and circumspect consideration. War is managed by due ordering (Prov 24:6). What king, about to go to make war against another king, does not first sit down, and think whether he is able, with ten thousand, to meet him who, with twenty thousand, comes against him? (Luke 14:31). So he says set out, that is, consider diligently. In Christo autem intendere designat dispositionem misericordiae ejus qua intendit ad salutem humani generis: Psalm. 37: intende in adjutorium meum. Now, in Christ, to set out signifies his disposition to have mercy, by which he pursues the salvation of the human race. Attend unto my help (Ps 37:23). Medium est prosper processus. The middle part of making war is a favorable advance. Processus autem Christi intelligitur dupliciter. Christ’s advance can be understood in two ways. Uno modo, secundum quod ex utero virginis processit in nativitate: Ps. 18: tamquam sponsus procedens de thalamo suo. Et hic fuit prosper processus, quia sine peccato natus est, et matri non abstulit virginitatem, nec dolorem intulit. In one way, as his proceeding from the womb of the virgin in the nativity. As a bridegroom coming out of his bride chamber (Ps 18:6). And this was a favorable proceeding, since he was born without sin and did not take away his mother’s virginity or cause her pain. Alio modo, secundum quod processit de homine ad hominem convertendum, hunc et illum. Et in hoc fuit prosper, quia tandem pervenit ad conversionem totius mundi: Isa. 55: faciet quaecumque volui, et prosperabitur in his ad quae misi illum: Psalm. 117: o domine bene prosperare. The other way Christ can be understood to have proceeded is as he proceeds from one man to another to convert him. And in this he was favored, since in the end he will achieve the conversion of the whole world. It shall not return to me void, but it shall do whatsoever I please, and shall prosper in the things for which I sent it (Isa 55:11). O Lord, give good success (Ps 117:25). Quod dicit, intende, potest conjungi cum hoc quod dicit, specie tua; quasi dicat, ergo speciose virtute humanitatis et cetera et pulchritudine divinitatis intende. Set out can be joined with your comeliness, so as to say, “Therefore, go forth with the comely strength of your humanity, and strive with the beauty of your divinity.” Finis ejus regnum ejus: Psalm. 46: regnabit Deus super omnes gentes. Finis ejus quod regnet per fidem in cordibus omnium: Luc. 1: et regnabit in domo Jacob. Et ideo dicit, et regna. His goal is his kingdom. God shall reign over the nations (Ps 46:9). His goal is to reign through faith in the hearts of all. He shall reign in the house of David (Luke 1:32). And so he says, and reign. Causa processus est propter veritatem. Et hoc vel causa dispositiva, vel finalis. The cause of his advancing is because of truth. And this can be understood as a dispositive or as a final cause. Si ly propter primo dicatur secundum quod est causa dispositiva, secundum quod in Hieronymo habetur, propter verbum veritatis et mansuetudinem justitiae, notandum quod duo sunt necessaria ut rex prospere agat. If because is a dispositive cause, as Jerome has—because of truth and meekness and justice—it should be noted that two things are necessary so that the king may prosper. Primo scilicet ut ei credatur: quia si nihil ei crederetur, et ipse aliis crederet, non posset plus quam unus homo: Prov. 17: non decent stultum verba composita. The first is that he be believed, since if no one believes in him, but he believes in others, he will not be able to do more than a single man can do. Eloquent words do not become a fool (Prov 17:7). Secundo quod diligatur; quia si non diligitur, non potest prosperari in regno nec in negotiis suis: et hoc facit fieri mansuetudo et clementia regis: Eccl. 3: fili in mansuetudine opera tua perfice. Second is that he be loved, since if he is not loved, he will not be able to prosper either in his rule or in his affairs. And the king’s meekness and clemency achieve this: my son, do your works in meekness (Sir 3:19). Et ita haec duo disponunt ad prosperitatem regis: Prov. 20: misericordia et veritas custodiunt regem: Ps. 36: mansueti hereditabunt terram. And so these things further the king’s prospering. Mercy and truth preserve the king (Prov 20:28). The meek shall inherit the land (Ps 36:11). Sed secundum litteram nostram, ad hoc ut rex possit prosperari in negotiis suis, debet habere tria: scilicet veritatem, mansuetudinem, et justitiam. Et ista tria fecerunt prosperari Christum: quia fuit verax in docendo, mansuetus in patiendo, justus in operando. But, according to our text, in order for the king to prosper in his affairs, he needs to have three things, namely, truth, meekness, and justice. And these three things made Christ prosper, since he was truthful in teaching, meek in suffering, and just in his deeds. De primo Matth. 22: scimus quia verax es et cetera. About the first, Matthew says, we know that you are a true speaker (Matt 22:16). De secundo 1 Pet. 2: cum pateretur, non comminabatur: Jer. 11: ego sicut agnus mansuetus et cetera. About the second, Peter says, when he suffered, he threatened not (1 Pet 2:23). I was as a meek lamb (Jer 11:19). De tertio, quia in nullo a justitia discessit: Ps. 144: fidelis dominus in omnibus verbis suis. Regarding the third, he never turned aside from justice. The Lord is faithful in all his words (Ps 144:13). Si autem ly propter designat causam finalem, sic est sensus: intende, prospere procede, et regna, id est ut facias veritatem. But if because means a final cause, the meaning is, set out, proceed prosperously, and reign so that you may accomplish truth. Christus autem fecit veritatem dupliciter: scilicet implendo promissiones, et adimplendo figuras: Rom. 15: dico autem Christum Jesum ministrum fuisse circumcisionis ad confirmandas promissiones patrum: 2 Cor. 1: quotquot promissiones Dei sunt in illo. Now, Christ accomplished the truth in two ways, namely, by fulfilling the promises, and by corresponding to the prefigurations. For I say that Christ Jesus was minister of the circumcision . . . to confirm the promises made unto the fathers (Rom 15:8). For all the promises of God are in him (2 Cor 1:20). Et iterum propter mansuetudinem derivandam in discipulos: Matth. 11: discite a me et cetera. And he also accomplished it through his meekness overflowing into his disciples. Learn of me because I am meek and humble of heart (Matt 11:29). Item, regna, propter justitiam: Joan. 5: Pater omne judicium dedit Filio. Likewise: reign for the sake of justice. The Father has given all judgment to the Son (John 5:5).
