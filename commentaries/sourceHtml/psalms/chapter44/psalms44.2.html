      Et notandum quod hujus editio psalmi attribuitur cordi, ex cujus magna devotione est compositus: quia non est iste de illis de quibus dicitur Isa. 29: populus hic labiis me honorat, cor autem eorum longe est a me; sed corde pronuntiat laudes Christi: 1 Cor. 14: psallam spiritu, psallam et mente. And it should be noted that the writing of the psalm is attributed to the heart, out of whose great devotion it was composed. What is said elsewhere is not the case here: this people honors me with their lips, but their heart is far from me (Matt 15:8; Isa 29:13). Rather, he pronounces the praises of Christ from the heart. I will sing with the spirit, I will sing also with the understanding (1 Cor 14:15). Hoc cor eructavit verbum, scilicet hujus psalmi: quod est bonum, quia consolatorium; loquitur enim mysteria Christi et Ecclesiae: 1 Tim. 1: fidelis sermo, et nullus sermo melior illo: Zach. 1: respondit dominus angelo qui loquebatur in me verba bona, verba consolatoria. This heart uttered a word, namely, the word of the psalm, which is good, since it is consoling. For he speaks about the mysteries of Christ and the Church. It is a faithful word (1 Tim 1:15) and there is no word better than it. And the Lord answered the angel that spoke in me, good words, comfortable words (Zech 1:13). Dico ego, id est pronuntio, opera mea regi, id est ad honorem regis Christi; Isa. 32: ecce in justitia regnabit rex; quasi dicat: canto hunc psalmum ad honorem Christi, cui opera nostra omnia debemus dicare; Col. 3: omne quodcumque facitis in verbo aut in opere, omnia in nomine Jesu Christi. I speak, that is, I announce, my works to the king, that is, to the honor of Christ the king. Behold, a king shall reign in justice (Isa 32:1), as if to say, “I sing this psalm to the honor of Christ, to whom we should speak all our works.” All whatsoever you do in word or in work, do all in the name of the Lord Jesus Christ (Col 3:17). Facite lingua mea calamus Scribae. Hic ponitur auctor psalmi qui est lingua; quasi dicat: non intelligatur quod ex proprio hunc fecerim, sed auxilio spiritus sancti, qui utitur lingua mea, sicut scriptor utitur calamo. Et ideo principalis auctor hujus psalmi est Spiritus Sanctus: 2 Reg. 23: spiritus domini locutus est per me, quasi per instrumentum: 2 Pet. 1: non voluntate humana allata est sapientia, sed Spiritu Sancto et cetera. Make my tongue as the pen of a scribe. Here is described the author of the psalm, the tongue, as if to say, “It should not be understood that I made this by myself, but by the help of the Holy Spirit, who used my tongue as a scribe uses a pen.” And so the principal author of this psalm is the Holy Spirit. The spirit of the Lord has spoken through me (2 Sam 23:2) as through an instrument. Wisdom came not by the will of man at any time, but . . . by the Holy Spirit (2 Pet 1:21). Et cujus calamus est? Scribae velociter scribentis, Spiritus Sancti qui velociter scribit in corde hominum. Qui enim per studium quaerunt sapientiam, per partes, et etiam longo tempore student; sed qui habent eam a Spiritu Sancto, velociter accipiunt: Act. 2: factus est repente de caelo sonus et cetera. And whose pen is it? That of a scribe who writes swiftly, the Holy Spirit who writes swiftly in the heart of man. For those who seek wisdom through study, study part by part and for a long time, but those who possess it from the Holy Spirit receive it swiftly. There came a sound from heaven (Acts 2:2). Illi qui habent scientiam per revelationem divinam, subito implentur sapientia, sicut illi sunt subito repleti spiritu sancto: Ps. 147: velociter currit sermo ejus: Eccl. 11: facile est in oculis Dei subito honestare pauperem. Those who possess knowledge through divine revelation are filled with wisdom immediately, like those who are immediately filled with the Holy Spirit. His word runs swiftly (Ps 147:4). For it is easy in the eyes of God on a sudden to make the poor man rich (Sir 11:23). Vel velociter operantis, quia dixit et facta sunt. Ps. 148. Or it is the pen of one working swiftly, since he spoke, and they were made (Ps 148:5). Potest autem lingua ad aliud referri, quia, scilicet non solum voluit dicere, sed corde primo cogitavit, secundo dixit ore, et tertio scripsit; quasi dicat: non solum profuit praesentibus qui audiunt, sed etiam futuris: Isa. 8: sume tibi librum grandem, et scribe in eo stylo hominis: Habac. 2: scribe visum, et explana eum. Haec ergo expositio est litteralis. Now, the tongue can also refer to something else, since he not only wished to speak, but first thought in his heart and only then spoke with his mouth, and then wrote, as if to say, “This was profitable not only to those present who hear it, but will also be to those in the future.” Take a great book, and write in it with a man’s pen (Isa 8:1). Write the vision, and make it plain (Hab 2:2). This is the literal interpretation. Sed aliqui dicunt quod haec verba proponuntur ad commendationem Christi secundum divinitatem, quasi sint verba Dei Patris. Sed hanc expositionem non approbant Augustinus et Hieronymus; tamen Dionysius utitur 2 cap. de divinis Nomin. ubi introducit hoc verbum, eructavit et cetera But some say that these words are proposed, as the words of God the Father, to praise Christ in his divinity. But Augustine and Jerome do not approve this interpretation; nevertheless, Dionysius uses it in The Divine Names, where he introduces this text. et secundum istam expositionem commendatur a Patre tripliciter. According to this interpretation, Christ is praised by the Father in three ways. Primo describitur ejus emanatio. First, his emanation is described; Secundo ejus virtus, ibi, dico. second, his virtue, at I speak, Tertio ejus operatio, ibi, lingua. third, his work, at tongue. Circa emanationem ejus a Patre ponit quatuor. Concerning his emanation from the Father, he proposes four points. Primo ejus naturalem processionem, cum dicit, eructavit: quod est quaedam emanatio de plenitudine; unde processus Filii a Patre est divina eructatio, quia procedit ex plenitudine divinae naturae: Joan. 3: Pater diligit Filium et cetera. The first is his natural procession, since he says, it has uttered, which is a certain emanation out of a fullness; so the Son’s procession from the Father is a divine utterance, which proceeds from the fullness of the divine nature. The Father loves the Son (John 3:35). Secundo ponit modum emanationis, quia non corporaliter, nec de aliqua alia natura emanavit, sed ad modum spiritualis. Cor meum, quasi non ex nullo, nec de alia essentia, sed de corde meo: Psalm. 109: ex utero ante luciferum genui te. Second, he describes the manner of the emanation, since he did not emanate corporeally, nor from some other nature, but spiritually. He says my heart to indicate that he emanated not from nothing, nor from another essence, but from my heart. From the womb before the day star I begot you (Ps 109:3). Tertio ponitur proprietas procedentis, quia, verbum. Joan. 1: in principio erat verbum. Third, he describes the property of the one proceeding: a word. In the beginning was the Word (John 1:1). Item ponitur perfectio procedentis: quia, bonum, quasi habens plenam bonitatem divinitatis: Lucae 18: nemo bonus nisi et cetera. Likewise, the perfection of the one proceeding is described, since it is good, as though having the full goodness of the divinity. None is good but God alone (Luke 18:19). Virtus ostenditur cum dicit, dico ego, id est per verbum facio, omnia mea opera regi, id est ad honorem regis, scilicet Filii, qui est unus Deus mecum: Joan. 1: omnia per ipsum facta sunt. His virtue is shown when he says, I speak, that is, I create through my word, all my works to the king, that is, for the honor of the king, namely, the Son, who is one God with me. All things were made by him (John 1:3). Propria operatio designatur cum dicit, lingua mea calamus scribae; quasi dicat, quod ipse est lingua mea, est etiam calamus Scribae. His proper operation is designated when he says, my tongue is the pen of a scribe, as if to say that he is my tongue, as he is also the pen of a scribe. In sacra Scriptura operationes metaphorice designantur per instrumenta vel membra quae sunt operationum principia; et sic per linguam et calamum operatio Dei designatur conveniens linguae et calamo. Operatio linguae est, quod per eam diffunditur sapientia cordis ad alios; per calamum autem designatur quod sapientia quae est in corde, transfunditur in materiam sensibilem, scilicet Pergamenum. In Sacred Scripture, operations which are the principles of operations are metaphorically signified through instruments or members; thus through the tongue and the pen, the operation of God is signified as like that of a tongue or a pen. The operation of a tongue is that through it the wisdom of the heart is passed on to others. Now, the pen signifies that wisdom which is in the heart is passed on into sensible material, like parchment. Deus autem et loquitur et scribit: loquitur, quando transfundit sapientiam suam in mentes rationales: Ps. 84: audiam quid loquatur in me dominus Deus. Et hoc dicitur verbum, quia per ipsum est omnis illuminatio: Joan. 1: et vita erat lux hominum. Now, God both speaks and writes. He speaks when he pours his wisdom into rational minds. I will hear what the Lord God will speak in me (Ps 84:9). And this is called a word, since through it comes every illumination. And the life was the light of men (John 1:4). Scribit, quia judicia suae sapientiae imprimit in rationabilibus creaturis: Rom. 1: invisibilia Dei et cetera. Ecclesiast. 1: Deus effudit illam super omnia opera sua. Sicut enim respiciens librum cognoscit sapientiam scribentis, ita cum nos videmus creaturas, cognoscimus sapientiam Dei. Calamus igitur est verbum Dei. He writes, since he imprints the judgments of his wisdom in rational creatures. The invisible things of God, from the creation of the world, are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made (Rom 1:20). God has poured her out upon his works (Sir 1:10). For as someone reading a book comes to know the writer’s wisdom, so when we see creatures, we come to know God’s wisdom. Therefore, the pen is the Word of God. 452. Speciosus forma prae filiis hominum. Praemisso prooemio secundum unum sensum, vel Christi divinitatem secundum alium sensum; hic ponitur commendatio Christi secundum humanitatem. 452. You are beautiful above the sons of men. The opening above could have one meaning or could refer to Christ’s divinity according to another meaning; here is laid out praise of Christ according to his humanity. Et quia dixit, dico ego opera mea regi, commendat Christum secundum similitudinem regis, scilicet David, a quatuor: And since he said, I speak my works to the king, he praises Christ using the image of a king, namely David, in four points: scilicet a gratiositate, his graciousness, a bellica virtute, his warlike virtue, a judiciaria potestate, his judicial power, et a deliciarum multitudine. and the multitude of his delights. Secunda pars, ibi, accingere. The second part is at gird your sword, Tertia, ibi, sedes, the third at your throne, quarta, ibi, mirrha. and the fourth at myrrh. Circa primum duo facit. Regarding the first he does two things: Primo describit gratiositatem regis. first, he describes the king’s graciousness, Secundo causam vel effectum, ibi, propterea. second, its cause or effect, at therefore. Nota quod duo sensus vigent in homine principaliter, scilicet visus et auditus: unde per haec duo aliquis gratiosus apparet; per pulchritudinem visui, per gratiosum verbum auditui. Unde haec duo praecipue fuerunt in Christo: unde Can. 2: ostende mihi faciem tuam, sonet vox tua in auribus meis: vox enim tua dulcis et facies tua decora. Ipse enim pulcher fuit et eloquens in his quae decuit suam eloquentiam. Notice that in man two senses particularly flourish, namely, sight and hearing, so someone appears gracious in both of these through visual beauty and through harmonious words. So these two existed especially in Christ. Show me your face, let your voice sound in my ears: for your voice is sweet, and your face comely (Song 2:14). For he was beautiful and eloquent in those things which befitted his eloquence.
