Petit ergo remotionem impedimenti, et promittit sacrificium laudis. Therefore, he asks the removal of the impediment and promises a sacrifice of praise. Impedimentum laudis divinae, sicut dictum est, est reatus culpae: Eccl. 15: non est speciosa laus et cetera. David autem erat gravi culpa reus: et ideo petit liberari ab ea; et ideo dicit, libera me de sanguinibus. Secundum glossam, hoc nomen, sanguis, non declinatur in plurali numero: tamen translator voluit uti ad expressionem peccati. The impediment to divine praise, so to speak, is the mark of guilt: praise is not seemly in the mouth of a sinner (Sir 15:9). David, however, was marked by a heavy guilt, and thus he asks to be freed from it; therefore, he says, deliver me from blood. According to the gloss, this word, “blood,” is not declined in the plural, but the translator wanted to use it to express sin. Et refertur hoc ad concupiscentiam carnis, quae est caro et sanguis: Matth. 16: caro et sanguis non revelavit tibi et cetera: quasi dicat. Libera me de peccatis commissis ex carne et sanguine. And this refers to the concupiscence of the flesh, which is flesh and blood: flesh and blood has not revealed it to you, but my Father who is in heaven (Matt 16:17). “Free me from sins committed from flesh and blood.” Vel dicendum quod David commiserat culpam adulterii et homicidii: et in utroque est sanguis: quia in homicidio sanguis effunditur: Ps. 5: virum sanguinum et dolosum et cetera. Adulterium autem procedit ex fervore sanguinis; et ideo dicit. De sanguinibus: Oseae 4: sanguis sanguinem tetigit. Or it should be said that David incurred the guilt of adultery and murder, and there is blood in both of these, because in murder blood is shed: the bloody and deceitful man the Lord will abhor (Ps 5:7). And adultery comes from the ardor of blood, and so he says, from blood: blood has touched blood (Hos 4:2). O Deus, libera ergo me de sanguinibus, quia tu solus potes: Isa. 43: ego sum qui deleo iniquitates tuas propter me et cetera. Et, quia tu es Deus salutis meae, id est qui potest me salvare. Therefore, O God, free me from blood, because you alone can: I am he who blots out your iniquities for my own sake (Isa 43:25). And because you are God of my salvation, that is, who can save me. Et exultabit lingua mea, scilicet cum delectatione et cum interiori gaudio cordis narrabo justitiam tuam: Isa. 30: canticum erit vobis sicut nox sanctificatae solemnitatis. Item ibidem cap. 35: venient in Syon laudantes, et laetitia sempiterna super capita eorum: Ps. 41: in voce exultationis et cetera. And my tongue shall extol, that is, with delight and with interior joy of heart I will tell your justice. You shall have a song in the night as the sanctified solemnity (Isa 30:29). They shall come into Zion praising, and everlasting joy shall be upon their heads (Isa 35:10). In the voice of exultation (Ps 41:5). Domine labia mea aperies. Est autem sciendum, quod homo aliquando impeditur a doctrina etiam propter impedimentum interius loquendi: et hoc contingit aliquando propter culpam auditorum: Ezech. 3: linguam tuam adhaerere faciam palato tuo, et eris mutus. Et infra: quia domus exasperans est. Et propter proprium peccatum: Ps. 106: omnis iniquitas oppilabit os suum. O Lord, you will open my lips. It should be known that man is sometimes also prevented from teaching through an interior impediment in regard to speaking, and this happens through the guilt of the hearers: and I shall make your tongue stick fast to the roof of your mouth, and you shall be dumb . . . for they are a provoking house (Ezek 3:26). And it happens because of one’s own sin: all iniquity shall stop their mouth (Ps 106:42). Quia ergo solus Deus linguas infantium fecit disertas, Sap. 10; ideo petit: domine remove impedimenta quae incurri per peccatum, a labiis meis. Et tu, labia mea aperies. Ephes. ult.: ut detur mihi sermo in apertione oris mei cum fiducia notum facere mysterium Evangelii. Because God alone made the tongues of infants eloquent (Wis 10:21), he asks, “Lord, remove from my lips the impediments that I have incurred through sin. And you will open my lips.” That speech may be given me, that I may open my mouth with confidence, to make known the mystery of the Gospel (Eph 6:19). Est autem notandum, quod in apertione oris intelligitur doctrinae profunditas, ubicumque in Scripturis invenitur apertio oris: ut Job 3: post haec aperuit Job os suum. Et Matth. 5: aperiens Jesus os suum, scilicet in profunditatem Scripturae; It should be noted that, wherever the “opening of the mouth” is found in Scripture, it indicates depth of teaching: after this, Job opened his mouth (Job 3:1). Jesus, opening his mouth, that is, in the depth of Scripture (Matt 5:2). et tunc, os meum annuntiabit laudem tuam; quasi dicat: quod in corde habeo, ore confitebor. And then he says: my mouth shall declare your praise, as if to say, “What I have in my heart I will confess with my mouth.” 508. Quoniam si voluisses sacrificium. Hic excusat se. 508. For if you had desired sacrifice. Here, he excuses himself. Et primo ostendit sacrificium non esse Deo acceptum; And first, he describes the sacrifice that is not acceptable to God; secundo ostendit quod sacrificium sit Deo acceptum, ibi, sacrificium Deo. second, the one that is, at a sacrifice to God. Dicit ergo: ego repromitto doctrinam et laudem, hoc enim sacrificium honorificabit te; sed carnale sacrificium non est tibi acceptum; et ideo dicit: si voluisses sacrificium, scilicet carnale, utique dedissem: sed, utique holocaustis non delectaberis. Thus he says, “I bind myself to teaching and praise, for this sacrifice will honor you; but carnal sacrifice is not acceptable to you”; and therefore, he says, if you had desired sacrifice, namely, carnal sacrifice, I would indeed have given it, but with burnt offerings you will indeed not be delighted. Sed numquid non vult sacrificia carnalia? Si illa sacrificia non approbat Deus, quare ergo mandavit fieri in veteri lege? But does he really not desire carnal sacrifices? If God does not approve those sacrifices, why in the Old Law did he command them to be made? Dicendum, quod mandavit ea fieri non propter se, sed quia erant figura interioris veri sacrificii, quo Christus se obtulit: et sunt signa interioris sacrificii, in quantum homo animam suam offert Deo: He commanded them to be made not for their own sake but because they were figures of the interior true sacrifice in which Christ offered himself. And they are signs of interior sacrifice insofar as man offers his own soul to God. et iterum fuerunt instituta propter rudes, qui Deum non noverant; et ideo oportebat quod in rebus honorarent et cognoscerent Deum, ne sacrificia idolis immolarent, ad quod erant multum proni. And, alternatively, they were instituted on account of the common people who did not know God and who needed to honor and know God in things lest they immolate sacrifices to idols, something to which they were very prone. Sed quia David ex Spiritu Sancto sciebat cordis sacrificium Deo acceptum, non dedit hic sacrificia corporalia. But because David knew from the Holy Spirit that the sacrifice of the heart is acceptable to God, he did not give him corporeal sacrifices. Inter omnia autem sacrificia holocausta erant magis Deo accepta. Et tamen illa propter se non erant accepta Deo; ideo dicit, holocaustis non delectaberis; quia etsi ipsa fuissent tibi accepta, simpliciter obtulissem ea. But among all sacrifices, burnt offerings were most acceptable to God. And nevertheless even these were not acceptable to God for their own sake; thus he says, with burnt offerings you will not be delighted, because if they were acceptable to you for their own sake, I would simply have offered them. Et si objiciatur, quod odor suavissimus erant domino, dicendum, quod erat hoc propter figuratum sacrificium, et in signum interioris sacrificii, quod Deo placet. And if someone objects that they were a most sweet odor to the Lord (cf. Lev 1:13), we should reply that this was because of the sacrifice that was figured, and in the sign of the interior sacrifice, which pleases God. Unde subdit, sacrificium Deo, scilicet acceptum, spiritus contribulatus. Augustinus, 10 de Civ. Dei: omne sacrificium, quod offertur exterius, signum est interioris sacrificii, in quo animam suam offert Deo. This is why he adds below, a sacrifice to God, namely, an acceptable sacrifice, is an afflicted spirit. Augustine, in book 10 of the City of God, says that “every sacrifice that is offered exteriorly is a sign of an interior sacrifice in which one offers one’s soul to God.” Sed sciendum quod anima hominis deducitur in peccatum primo per inane gaudium. Eccle. 2: risum reputavi errorem, et gaudio dixi: quid frustra deciperis, id est deduceris in peccatum. But it should be known that man’s soul first was led into sin through vain joy: laughter I counted error, and to mirth I said, “Why are you vainly deceived?” (Eccl 2:2), that is, led into sin. Secundo induratur ad spiritualia ex peccato. Eccle. 3: cor durum male habebit in novissimo. Rom. 2: secundum duritiam tuam et cor impoenitens. Second, by sin he is hardened to spiritual things: a hard heart will fear evil at the last (Sir 3:27). According to your hardness and impenitent heart, you treasure up for yourself wrath (Rom 2:5). Tertio, quia sufficit sibi in rebus corporalibus, et non curat de spiritualibus, tunc superbit, quod est initium omnis peccati. Eccl. 10. Third, because he fills himself with corporeal things and does not take care for spiritual ones, he grows proud, which is the beginning of all sin (Sir 10:15). Oportet ergo quod poenitens qui cor suum offert sacrificium Deo, contraria omnibus his faciat. Therefore, the one repenting who offers his heart as a sacrifice to God must do the opposite of all these things. Et primo contra inane gaudium oportet, quod assumat tristitiam poenitentiae: et ideo subdit, sacrificium Deo spiritus contribulatus, id est de omnibus peccatis simul tristatur, non de uno tantum. 2 Cor. 7: quae secundum Deum est tristitia, poenitentiam in salutem stabilem operatur. Baruch 2: anima quae est tristis super magnitudinem mali, et incedit incurva. Et infra: dat tibi gloriam, et justitiam domino. And first, against empty joy, he must take up the sadness of penitence, and thus he says that a sacrifice to God is an afflicted spirit, that is, he sorrows for all sins at once, not just for one. For the sorrow that is according to God works penance, steadfast unto salvation (2 Cor 7:10). The soul that is sorrowful for the greatness of the evil she has done and goes bowed down . . . gives glory and justice to you, the Lord (Baruch 2:18). Contra secundum opponitur contritio; unde dicit, cor contritum. Et nota differentiam inter confracta et contrita: quia confracta sunt quae dividuntur in magnas partes; contrita sunt quae in parvissimas partes dividuntur. Quamdiu ergo quis habet cor durum, tunc quasi integrum habet cor in malitia; sed quando totaliter deserit peccatum praebens se spiritualibus, dicitur tunc contritus. Job 16: ego ille quondam opulentus, scilicet in temporalibus, repente contritus sum. Second, against hardening to spiritual things he opposes contrition, where he says, a contrite heart. And note the difference between “broken” and “contrite”: things that are “broken” are split into large pieces; things that are “contrite” are split into very small pieces. Thus as long as someone has a hard heart, he has a heart whole in malice, as it were; but when he completely abandons sin, surrendering himself to spiritual things, then he is said to be “contrite.” I who was formerly so wealthy, namely, in temporal goods, am all of a sudden broken to pieces (Job 16:13). Contra tertium opponitur humilitas; et ideo dicit, et humiliatum Deus non despicies, quia superbis Deus resistit, humilibus autem dat gratiam. Jac. 4. To the third, he opposes humility, and therefore, he says, and a humbled heart, O God, you will not despise, because God resists the proud and gives grace to the humble (Jas 4:6). Et est sciendum, quod facit mentionem de corde et spiritu, et spiritus pertinet ad animositatem, et sic pertinet ad irascibilem. Isa. 25: spiritus robustorum, quasi turbo impellens parietem. Cor pertinet ad concupiscibilem: et sic datur per hoc intelligi, quod quicquid est in vi appetitiva, debet Deo offerri in sacrificium. And it should be known that he makes mention of heart and spirit, and the spirit pertains to spiritedness, and thus it pertains to the irascible part of the soul. For the blast of the mighty is like a whirlwind beating against a wall (Isa 25:4). The heart pertains to the concupiscible part, and so this should be understood to mean that whatever is in the appetitive power should be offered to God in sacrifice. 509. Benigne. Hic promittit quid debeat facere in futurum. 509. Deal favorably, O Lord. Here he promises what he should do in the future.
