      Hieronymus aliter habet, et competit mysterio sponsae, germinet gaudio universae terrae montis Sion, in lateribus Aquilonis civitatulae regis magni. Et exponitur secundum mysterium. Haec civitas laudatur ex civilitate quam colit, et ex humanitate Christi quam assumpsit. Jerome has it otherwise, and his way accords with the mystery of the bride: let all the lands of Mount Zion rise, on the sides of the north of the little city of the great king. And it is explained in the mystical sense. The city is praised for the civility he cultivated, and for the humanity of Christ which he assumed. Dico, quod est magna; et hoc est ex ipso specioso germine, id est Christo. Et hoc est gaudium universae terrae. I say that it is great, and this is from its lovely seed, which is Christ. And this is the joy of all the earth. In Hebraeo habetur, decorus nimis exultationis, scilicet Christus, et hoc est in monte Sion. The Hebrew has, the beauty of exceedingly great joy, that is, Christ, and this is on Mount Zion. Deus in domibus ejus cognoscetur. Hic commendat civitatem a sapientia civium: vera namque sapientia consistit in Dei cognitione. Jer. 9: in hoc glorietur qui gloriatur, scire et nosse me. Et ideo commendat eam ex hoc quod Deus in ea cognoscitur, et dicit, Deus in domibus ejus cognoscetur. In her houses shall God be known. Here, he praises the city for the wisdom of the citizens. For true wisdom consists in the knowledge of God. Let him who glories glory in this, that he understands and knows me (Jer 9:24). And so he praises it because God is known in it, and he says, in her houses shall God be known. Est autem triplex cognitio Dei: quia hoc potest referri Now the knowledge of God is threefold, since it can refer ad statum civitatis Jerusalem, to the state of the city of Jerusalem, et ad Ecclesiam, and to the Church, et ad futuram gloriam. and to future glory. Una ergo cognitio de Deo est figuralis et obscura; et haec cognitio fuit in veteri testamento: et talis cognitio fuit in civitate illa, scilicet Jerusalem, et in populo Judaico. Ps. 75: notus in Judaea Deus et cetera. Et secundum hoc dicitur, Deus in domibus ejus cognoscetur. Therefore, one knowledge of God is figural and obscure. And this knowledge existed in the Old Testament, and this knowledge was in that city, Jerusalem, and among the Jewish people. In Judea, God is known (Ps 75:2). And so it is said, in her houses shall God be known. Hieronymus habet, Deus agnitus est et cetera, scilicet non in uno loco, sed in omnibus domibus et civitatibus. Et dicit, in domibus; nam apud Athenas cognoscebatur Deus. Act. 17: in ipso vivimus, movemur et sumus. Ad Roman. 1: invisibilia Dei et cetera. Sed non cognoscebatur in domibus, sed in scholis apud aliquos: sed in gente illa omnes cognoscebant Deum. Jerome has, God is recognized, not in one place, but in all houses and cities. And he says, in the houses, for God was known among the Athenians. In him we live and move and are (Acts 17:28). The invisible things of God, from the creation of the world are clearly seen, but understood by the things that are made (Rom 1:20). But he was not known in their houses, but rather in the schools among some people; but in that nation, all knew God. Alia est cognitio realis, sed obscura et imperfecta; et haec est cognitio qua Deus cognoscitur per fidem. 1 Cor. 13: videmus nunc per speculum et cetera, et sic Deus in domibus cognoscitur cognitione reali, sed fidei; 2 Cor. 3: nos autem revelata facie gloriam domini contemplantes. Another kind of knowledge of God is real but obscure and imperfect, and this is the knowledge by which God is known through faith. We see now through a glass in a dark manner (1 Cor 13:12), and God is known in this way in her houses with a knowledge that is real but that of faith. But we, contemplating the glory of the Lord with an open face (2 Cor 3:18). Et dicit, in domibus; quia tota universalis Ecclesia continet sub se multas Ecclesias et multa collegia, quarum quaelibet domus dicitur habere cognitionem Dei. Jer. 31: me omnes cognoscent a minimo usque ad maximum. And he says, in her houses, since the entire universal Church contains many churches and many schools, of which certain houses are said to have the knowledge of God. All shall know me, from the least of them even to the greatest (Jer 31:34). Alia est realis, quae est cognitio perfecta et aperta. 1 Cor. 13: tunc cognoscam sicut et cognitus sum in domibus caelestis Jerusalem. Et dicuntur plures domus diversi ordines sanctorum, scilicet apostolorum, martyrum, confessorum et virginum et cetera. Joan. 14: in domo Patris mei mansiones multae sunt. Et secundum hoc dicitur in Psalterio Romano: Deus in gradibus ejus cognoscetur; quia non omnes aequaliter cognoscent, sed erunt diversi gradus cognitionis secundum quosdam. 1 Cor. 15: stella differt a stella in claritate. Next is the real knowledge which is perfect and clear. Then I shall know even as I am known (1 Cor 13:12) in the houses of the heavenly Jerusalem. And the various orders of saints—namely, the apostles, martyrs, confessors, virgins, and so on—are said to be many houses. In my Father’s house there are many mansions (John 14:2). And the Roman Psalter refers to this when it says, God is known in his gradations, since he is known unequally: there are various gradations of knowledge. Star differs from star in glory (1 Cor 15:41). Sed hoc erit, cum suscipiet eam, ad adjuvandum; quia ipse est susceptor et auxiliator noster. But this will be when he shall protect her to help her, since he is our protector and helper. Alia autem littera Hieronymi habet, in auxiliando. Now another text of Jerome has, while helping. 476. Quoniam. Hic probat dignitatem civitatis per testimonium. 476. For behold the kings of the earth assembled themselves. Here, he proves the dignity of the city through testimony. Et primo inducit testes; He first brings forward the witnesses; secundo eorum probitatem; second, their probity; tertio ipsorum confessionem. third, their testimony. Ad hoc quod testimonium sit credibile, tria sunt necessaria: scilicet dignitas testium, ut sint testes auctoritatis; quia si sint leves, eorum testimonium non debet approbari. Item numerositas, et concordia; et haec tria sunt in istis testibus. Three things are necessary to make testimony credible: the dignity of the witnesses, so that they will be witnesses of authority (since if they are of little weight, their testimony should not be approved); likewise, the number and the harmony of witnesses. And these three are found in these witnesses. Quia sunt magnae dignitatis; quia. Reges terrae, unus fuit Constantinus, alius fuit Justinianus et Carolus magnus qui Ecclesiam firmaverunt privilegiis. They are of great dignity, since they are the kings of the earth. One of these was Constantine, another was Justinian, and then Charlemagne who confirmed the privileges of the Church. Item multi fuerunt, quia congregati sunt de diversis nationibus et temporibus. Possunt etiam per reges intelligi sapientes et justi, qui testimonium perhibuerunt Ecclesiae conversi ad fidem. Ps. 46: principes populorum. Likewise, they were many, since they assembled themselves from various nations and times. For by kings can be understood those who are wise and just, who give testimony to the Church, having converted to the faith. The princes of the people (Ps 46:10). Item sunt concordes, convenerunt in unum, scilicet testimonium et sententiam. Ps. 101: in conveniendo populos in unum et reges et cetera. Likewise, they are in agreement: they gathered together into one—one testimony and opinion. When the people assemble together, and the kings (Ps 101:23). Potest iterum aliter exponi; tamen prima expositio est litteralis, quia in Hieronymo habetur, testati sunt. In Graeco habetur, suscipient eam, scilicet ad defendendum. This can be explained differently; nevertheless the primary explanation is literal, since Jerome has, they witnessed. In Greek, it has, they shall receive it, namely, to defend it. Et hoc necessarium est, quoniam ecce reges terrae congregati sunt, convenerunt in unum, scilicet contra Ecclesiam. Et isti qui aliquando testimonium perhibent aliquando contra Ecclesiam fuerunt, et aliquando persecuti sunt eam, postea eam firmaverunt. And this is necessary: for behold, the kings of the earth assembled themselves; they gathered together against the Church. And those who at one time gave testimony were at one time against the Church, and at one time persecuted her but afterwards established her. Ipsi videntes. Hic describit eorum probitatem, ubi septem fuerunt. So they saw. Here, he describes their probity, which involves seven things. Primum visio, id est cognitio fidei; unde dicit, ipsi videntes, id est cognoscentes per fidem miracula quae Christus et apostoli faciebant. Isa. 62: videbunt gentes justum tuum, et cuncti reges inclytum tuum. First is vision, that is, the knowledge of faith, so he says, they saw, that is, they recognized through faith the miracles which Christ and the apostles did. The gentiles shall see the just one, and all kings your glorious one (Isa 62:2). Secundum est admiratio in his quae videntur, quia sunt supra sensum et rationem humanam. Isa. 60: videbis et afflues et mirabitur et dilatabitur cor tuum. Ps. 138: mirabilia opera tua. Second is wonder at those things which are seen, since they are above human sense and reason. You shall see, and abound, and your heart shall wonder and be enlarged (Isa 60:5). Wonderful are your works (Ps 138:14). Tertium est conturbatio pro peccatis. Third is being troubled by sin. Propter secundum, admirati sunt. About the second he says, they wondered. Propter tertium, conturbati sunt. Ps. 59: commovisti terram, et conturbasti eam. About the third he says, they were troubled. As Psalm 59:4 says, you have moved the earth, and have troubled it. Quartum est commotio. Aliquando quis conturbatur de peccato, et labitur in desperationem, vel persistit in malo; sed isti commoti sunt ad poenitentiam. Isa. 24: commotione commovebitur terra. Fourth is movement. Sometimes someone is troubled by sin and falls into despair, or persists in evil, but these are moved to repentance. With trembling the earth shall be moved (Isa 24:19). Quintum est, quia haec commotio debet esse cum timore Dei, ut non attribuat sibi quod per se moveatur ab bonum, sed Deo; et dicit, tremor apprehendit eos. Ps. 2: servite domino in timore. Fifth is that the movement should be accompanied by the fear of God, so that he does not attribute to himself his own movement, but to God, and he says, trembling took hold of them. Serve the Lord with fear (Ps 2:11). Hic dolor et tremor est fructuosus; unde dicit, ibi dolores ut parturientis, qui convertuntur in gaudium propter spem prolis et fructus. Isa. 26: a timore tuo, domine, concepimus et peperimus spiritum salutis. Et hoc est sextum. This pain and trembling is fruitful, so he says, there were pains as of a woman in labor, which are changed to joy because of the hope of offspring and fruitfulness. From fear of you, O Lord, we have conceived and brought forth wind of salvation (Isa 26:18). And this is the sixth. Septimum est. In spiritu vehementi conteres naves Tharsis, id est mare universaliter, et sic conteres naves maris. The seventh is with a vehement wind, you shall break in pieces the ships of Tarshish, that is, the whole sea, and so it means that you shall break the ships of the sea. Vel dicendum, quod ibi est una provincia quae Cilicia vocatur, et Tharsis est metropolis ejus, ubi natus est Paulus, et ex illa civitate tota regio nominatur Tharsis, et ibi sunt multae naves; vel sicut in mari Mediterraneo primi navigantes fecerunt Carthaginem, et isti simul pugnantes cum Tyriis praevaluerunt. Et ideo Tharsis vocatur totum mare mundi. Or it should be said that there is one province which is called Cilicia, and Tarshish is its city, where Paul was born, and so from the city the whole region is named Tarshish, and there are many ships there; or as those first sailing the Mediterranean Sea built Carthage, so they, fighting against Tyre, prevailed. And so the whole sea throughout the world was called Tarshish.
