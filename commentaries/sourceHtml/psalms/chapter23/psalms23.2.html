      vel terra, id est Ecclesia, quae est bona terra quae facit multum fructum. Isa. 40: quis appendit tribus digitis molem terrae, et libravit in pondere montes, et colles in statera? Or he speaks of the earth, that is, the Church, which is the good earth which brings forth much fruit. Who has poised with three fingers the bulk of the earth, and weighed the mountains in scales, and the hills in a balance? (Isa 40:12). Sed addit, et plenitudo ejus. Gen. 1: terra erat inanis et vacua et cetera quia non erat plena arboribus et aliis quae pertinent ad ornatum terrae, sicut plantae et herbae. But he adds, the fullness thereof. The earth was void and empty (Gen 1:2), since it was not yet full of trees and the other things which belong to the ornamentation of the earth, like plants and herbs. Vel, plenitudo terrae, id est plenitudo gratiarum Ecclesiae. Et hic est Christus qui attulit omnem gratiarum plenitudinem in se. Esth. 15: facies tua plena gratiarum. Joan. 1: de plenitudine ejus omnes accepimus. Or he says, the fullness of the earth, that is, the fullness of the grace of the Church. And this is Christ who has brought all the fullness of grace in himself. Your face is full of graces (Esth 15:17). Of his fullness we have all received (John 1:16). Secundo modo etiam dicitur Dei orbis terrarum, id est nostra terra habitabilis. Et universi qui habitant in ea, id est omnes habitatores ejus. Ps. 9: judicabit orbem terrae in aequitate, et populos in justitia. Isa. 45: non in vanum creavi eam (scilicet terram) ut habitaretur formavi eam. Centrum est infimus locus in mundo, ad quod terra gravitate sua tendit et omnia alia cedunt sibi: et dicit Psalm. 103: qui fundavit terram super stabilitatem suam, quia per gravitatem tendit illuc: et super nihilum, quia nihil sibi subsidet; et Isa. 40, dicit: appendit tribus digitis molem terrae, frigiditate et partium suarum continuitate. Sed quia terra est elementum, et ex ea cum aliis elementis fit mixtum; terra autem est sicca et frigida, unde nec ex motu nec ex fluxu pars una continuatur, alteri indiget humore continente et continuante, et sic fundatur vel confirmatur super aquas; et quia in qualibet parte terrae quasi fit generatio aquarum, tota terra fere potest dici fundari super aquam: propter quod dicit, ipse super maria fundavit eam. In the second way, the world—that is, our habitable earth—is God’s. He shall judge the world in equity, and the people in justice (Ps 9:9). He did not create it (namely, the earth) in vain; he formed it to be inhabited (Isa 45:18). The center is the deepest place in the world, to which the earth’s weight tends and all things fall towards it. And the psalm says, who founded the earth upon its own bases (Ps 103:5), since it tends there through its weight, and it is upon nothing, since it rests upon nothing. And he says, he has poised with three fingers the bulk of the earth (Isa 40:12), by its coldness and the cohesion of its parts. But because earth is an element, it also comes about from a mixture of other elements. Now earth is dry and cold, so neither from motion nor from flux is it made one continuous part, but it needs another moist element containing and connecting it, and so it is founded or strengthened upon the waters. And since it seems water can be generated in any part of the earth, the whole earth is generally said to be founded upon the waters, on account of which he says, he has founded it upon the seas. Vel sicut fundamentum continet aedificium, sic aqua continet terram, ne discontinuetur. Discontinuatio etiam terrae facit quod aqua quae liquida est et gravis, fluat quasi per totam terram. Or, just as a foundation secures a building, so the water secures the earth so it is not disconnected. For the discontinuities in the earth happen because the water, which is liquid and heavy, flows through the whole earth. Item per orbem designatur Ecclesia et omnes habitantes in ea, scilicet fideles; et omnes sumus Christi: et sive vivimus, sive morimur, domini sumus, ut Apostolus dicit Rom. 14. Likewise by the earth is designated the Church and all those dwelling in it, namely, the faithful, and we are all of Christ. Whether we live or whether we die, we are the Lord’s (Rom 14:8), as the Apostle says. Consequenter reddit causam supradictorum, quia scilicet, super maria fundavit eam; quasi dicat: illud est artificis, quod ipse artifex facit. Sed Deus fecit terram et ea quae sunt in terra. Ergo ipsius est terra et plenitudo ejus. Afterwards, he gives the cause of what he said above, that he has founded it upon the seas, as if to say, “It is an artifact, because an artisan made it.” But God made the earth and all those things which are in it. Therefore, the earth and the fullness thereof are his. Sed quidam dicunt, quod non est providentia Dei in rebus terrenis. But some say that the providence of God does not extend to earthly things. Sed contra hoc est signum magnae providentiae dispositio aquarum ad terram, quia elementa levia debent esse super graviora. Similiter ergo sicut aer circumdat aquam, ita aqua debet circumdare terram. Et philosophi assignant super hoc multas causas. Sed causa est providentia divina, ut ibi esset habitatio hominum et animalium; unde Moyses in principio, quando posuit rerum creationem, posuit terram primum informem; unde terra erat inanis, id est informis, et vacua arboribus; et ideo posuit eam circumdatam sive opertam aqua, et tenebrae, id est aquae, erant super faciem abyssi, id est super terram, et spiritus domini, id est aer, ferebatur super aquas. But against this is the sign of a great providence in the ordering of the waters in relation to the earth, since lighter elements should be above the heavier. Therefore, just as the air encircles the water, so the water should encircle the earth. And the philosophers give many reasons for this. But the cause is divine providence, so that it should be a dwelling for men and animals. So Moses, in the beginning when he wrote down the creation of things, wrote that the earth was first unformed, so the earth was void, that is, unformed, and empty of trees, and so he describes it as encircled or covered with water, and darkness, that is, water, was upon the face of the deep, that is over the earth, and the spirit of the Lord, that is, air, was upon the waters (Gen 1:2). Vel terra erat inanis, id est invisibilis propter aquas; unde sequitur, congregentur aquae et cetera et appareat arida; quasi dicat: quia terra est prima secundum ordinem elementorum, a providentia divina factum est quod sit super aquas, ut homines et animalia possint in ea vivere, et aquae nihilominus occupent terram quantum durant maria: Or the earth was void, that is, unseen under the waters, so it follows, let the waters be gathered together . . . and let the dry land appear (Gen 1:9), as if to say, “Since the earth is first in the order of the elements, it was made to rest upon the waters by divine providence, so that men and animals could live on it, but let the waters, nevertheless, occupy the earth insofar as they continue as the seas.” unde dicit: Ipse super maria fundavit eam, id est juxta, sicut alibi. Ps. 136: super flumina Babylonis (id est juxta flumen Babylonis) illic sedimus et cetera. So he says, he has founded it upon the seas, that is, beside them, as is said in other places. Upon the rivers of Babylon, that is, beside the river of Babylon, there we sat (Ps 136:1). Fundavit, id est firmam statuit, ut mare eam non occupet: Job 38: posui mari ostia et vectes, et dixit, hucusque venies, et non procedes amplius; et hic confringes tumentes fluctus tuos: Hier. 5: qui posuit arenam terminum mari, praeceptum sempiternum quod non praeteribit. He founded it, that is, he established it as firm, so that the sea would not overtake it. I made doors and bars for the sea and I said, “Up to here you shall come, and shall go no further, and here you shall break your swelling waves” (Job 38:10–11). I have set the sand a bound for the sea, an everlasting ordinance, which it shall not pass over (Jer 5:22). Et super flumina praeparavit illam, id est juxta flumina; et dicit, praeparavit, non fundavit, quia ad praeparationem requiritur quod irrigetur flumine; Ps. 64: flumen Dei repletum est aquis: parasti cibum illorum, quoniam ita est praeparatio ejus: rivos ejus inebrians multiplica et cetera. And has prepared it upon the rivers, that is, next to the rivers, and he says, prepared, not founded, since its preparation requires that it be watered by a river. The river of God is filled with water; you have prepared their food for so is its preparation, fill up plentifully its streams (Ps 64:10–11). Vel Ecclesia, fundata est super maria, id est super tribulationes: Ps. 92: mirabiles elationes maris. Or the Church was founded upon the seas, that is, upon tribulations. Wonderful is the surging of the sea (Ps 92:4). Et super flumina, id est persecutiones quibus praeparata est Ecclesia ad coronas martyrum. And it is upon the rivers, that is, the persecutions which prepared the Church for the crowns of martyrs. Vel super maria, id est amaritudines; et tamen consolationem ejus praeparavit flumina consolationum: Ps. 45: fluminis impetus laetificat et cetera. Or it is upon the seas, that is, upon bitterness, but nevertheless he has prepared his consolation with the river of consolation. The stream of the river makes joyful (Ps 45:5). 207. Quis ascendet? Quasi dicat, magnus est, et quomodo accedetur ad eum? Eccl. 2: quis est homo qui possit sequi regem factorem suum; et ideo docet modum perveniendi: 207. Who shall ascend into the mountain? As if to say: “He is great, and how will anyone ascend to him?” What is man, said I, that he can follow the King his maker? (Eccl 2:12). And so he teaches the way to reach it. unde circa hoc duo facit. He makes two points regarding this. Primo ponit quaestionem. First, he puts forward a question; Secundo responsionem, ibi, innocens manibus. and second, a response, at the innocent in hands. In quaestione quaerit duo: In the question he asks two questions: scilicet de via seu motu, quis ascendet. namely, about the way or movement: who shall ascend; Et de termino, aut quis stabit. and about the end: or who shall stand in his holy place? Mons signat hic altitudinem divinae justitiae sive majestatis: Psalm. 35: justitia tua sicut montes Dei. Mons ergo est altitudo divinae majestatis, vel sublimitas Christi, qui mons dicitur; Isa. 2: erit in novissimis diebus praeparatus mons domus domini in vertice montium, et elevabitur et cetera. The mountain here signifies the height of divine justice or majesty. Your justice is as the mountains of God (Ps 35:7). Therefore, the mountain is the height of the divine majesty, or the sublimity of Christ, who is called a mountain. In the last days the mountain of the house of the Lord shall be prepared on the top of mountains, and it shall be exalted (Isa 2:2). Quis ergo ascendet tantum quod deveniat ad Christum et Deum? Sancti viri qui disponunt ascensiones in corde suo ascendent, ut dicit psalmista. Therefore, who shall ascend so far as to come to Christ and God? The holy men who prepare ascents in their heart, as the psalmist says. Item: quis poterit stare ibi, ubi ipse est qui est locus sanctus, locus gloriae? Hier. 17: locus sanctificationis nostrae expectatio Israel: Exod. 3: locus in quo stas terra sancta est: quasi dicat: quis stabilietur ibi? Also, who shall be able to stand there, where he himself is, which is the holy place, the place of glory? The place of our sanctification, the hope of Israel (Jer 17:12–13). The place where you are standing is holy ground (Exod 3:5), as if to say, “Who can stand there?” Sed alibi dicit Ps. 121, quod sic, stantes erant pedes nostri in atriis tuis Hierusalem. Unde consequenter ponitur responsio. But he says in another place, our feet were standing in your courts, O Jerusalem (Ps 121:2). So afterwards the response is given. 208. Innocens. Et primo ostendit hoc in generali. 208. The innocent in hands. And first he shows this answer in general; Secundo in speciali, haec est generatio. and second, in particular: this is the generation. Circa primum duo facit. He makes two points about this. Primo proponit meritum. First, he sets down the merit; Secundo praemium, ibi, accipiet. second, the reward, at he shall receive. In merito est unum quod pertinet ad innocentiam operis; unde dicit, innocens manibus: Job 22: salvabitur innocens; salvabitur autem munditia manuum suarum: Ps. 25: ego in innocentia mea ingressus sum. In respect to the merit, there is one which belongs to him who is innocent in works, so he says, innocent in hands. The innocent shall be saved, and he shall be saved by the cleanness of his hands (Job 22:30). I have walked in my innocence (Ps 25:1). Aliud pertinet ad puritatem cordis: et quantum ad hoc ponit, Another pertains to purity of heart, and in regard to this he describes two things: quod conservetur cor purum a concupiscentiis interioribus: unde dicit, et mundo corde: Matth. 5: beati mundo corde, quoniam ipsi Deum videbunt. that the pure heart is guarded from inward concupiscence, so he says, and clean of heart. Blessed are the clean of heart: for they shall see God (Matt 5:8). Item ab omni cupiditate rerum temporalium, qui non accepit in vanum animam suam: id est qui non effudit eam super res vanas, vel qui non vane gloriatur de virtutibus, vel qui non permittit sensualitatem suam pervenire usque ad consensum peccati. And he is guarded from every desire for temporal things: who has not taken his soul in vain, that is, he who has not poured it out upon vain things or who does not glory vainly in his strengths or who does not allow his sensuality to reach consent to sin.
