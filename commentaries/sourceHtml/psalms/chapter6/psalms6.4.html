Et haec tria dicit exaudita ordine retrogrado. And he speaks in reverse order of how these three have been heard. Et primo gemitum, dicens, exaudivit dominus et cetera. Gemitus sanctorum vocem habet apud Deum: Eccl. 35: non despiciet vocem pupilli, nec viduam, si effundat loquelam gemitus. Nonne lacrymae viduae ad maxillam descendunt, et exclamatio ejus super deducentem eas? A maxilla enim ascendunt usque ad caelum, et dominus exauditor non delectabitur in illis. Et Genes. 4: vox sanguinis fratris tui clamat ad me de terra. Propterea dicebat illa sancta domina Judith 8: indulgentiam ejus cum lacrymis postulemus. First, he mentions his groanings, saying, the Lord has heard the voice of my weeping. The groaning of the saints has a voice before God. He will not despise the prayers of the fatherless nor the widow, when she pours out her complaint. Do not the widow’s tears run down the cheek, and her cry against him who causes them to fall? For from the cheek they go up even to heaven, and the Lord who hears will not be delighted with them (Sir 35:17–19). And, the voice of your brother’s blood cries to me from the earth (Gen 4:10). Therefore, a holy lady said, with many tears let us beg his pardon (Jdt 8:14). Secundo deprecationem: est namque deprecatio proprie secundum glossam Cassiodori, pro removendis malis; Luc. 1: exaudita est deprecatio tua. Second, he mentions his petition, because according to the gloss of Cassiodorus, petition is properly made for the removal of evils. Your prayer is heard (Luke 1:13). Dicit ibi glossa, qua rogasti pro liberatione plebis. Ut ergo ostendat se liberatum a malis pro quibus rogaverat dicens: convertere et cetera, subjungit, hic, exaudivit dominus deprecationem meam. Here the gloss says it is the prayer by which you prayed for the deliverance of the people. Therefore, he says this to show that he has been freed from the evils about which he prayed, saying, turn to me, O Lord, and deliver my soul. He adds, the Lord has heard my supplication. Tertio orationem, ut scilicet intret in conspectu ejus: et hoc est quod subinfert, dominus orationem meam suscepit. Third, he mentions the prayer that he might enter into his sight, and so he adds, the Lord has received my prayer. Et nota, quod ponit hic ter dominus ut ostendat se exauditum a tota Trinitate: Ps. 66: benedicat nos Deus Deus noster, benedicat nos Deus. And notice that he says Lord here three times to show that he was heard by the whole Trinity: may God, our God bless us, may God bless us (Ps 66:7–8). 41. Consequenter cum dicit, erubescant, ponit inimicorum eventus; ac si diceretur, tu dicis inimicis tuis. Discedite. Sed quid erit eis discedentibus a te? Certe erubescant et cetera. 41. Next, when he says, let all my enemies be ashamed, he describes the outcome for his enemies, as if to say, “You say to your enemies, depart.” But what awaits those departing from you? Certainly let them be ashamed. Haec verba possunt exponi in bono et in malo. Si in bono, tunc dicta sunt per modum orationis. Ubi petit quatuor pro eis, These words can be explained in relation both to good and to evil. If in regard to good, then they are said as a prayer where he asks four things for them: scilicet erubescentiam de peccatis, quia hoc est principium emendationis vitae; Eccl. 4: est confusio adducens gloriam. Unde erubescant. shame for sin, since this is the beginning of amendment of life. There is a shame that brings glory (Sir 4:25). So, let them be ashamed. Secundo dolorem de peccatis, conturbentur: Ps. 59. Commovisti terram et conturbasti eam et cetera: debet enim poenitens habere vehementiam doloris, plus quam habuerit delectationem in peccato. Second, sorrow for sins: let them be very much troubled. Psalm 59:4 says: you have moved the earth, and have troubled it. For the penitent should have deep sorrow, more than he had enjoyment in sin. Tertio conversionem ad Deum: unde convertantur; Isa. 31: convertimini sicut in profundum recesseratis filii Israel. Third, conversion back to God; hence, let them be turned back. Return as you had deeply revolted, O children of Israel (Isa 31:6). Et quarto erubescentiam, erubescant et conturbentur vehementer. Est autem erubescentia principium et finis emendationis. And fourth, shame: let all my enemies be ashamed, and be very much troubled. For shame is the beginning and end of amendment. Sed aliter posset reddi ratio; quia scilicet a principio erubescit quis oculos hominum, et hoc dolet, et vitat mala; in fine vero erubescit oculum rationis suae et oculum Dei: Rom. 6: quem fructum habuistis tunc in illis, in quibus nunc erubescitis? De secundo Luc. 18: publicanus a longe stans nolebat nec oculos ad caelum levare. Hoc autem fieri debet valde velociter, ut non tardetur: Eccl. 5: ne tardes converti ad dominum et cetera. But the structure of this passage can be explained another way: in the beginning, someone is ashamed before the eyes of men, and then is sad, and avoids evil; in the end, he is ashamed before the eye of his reason and that of God. What fruit did you have in those things, of which you are now ashamed? (Rom 6:21). About the second: the publican, standing afar off, would not so much as lift up his eyes towards heaven (Luke 18:13). Now this should be done very speedily, so that there is no delay. Do not delay to be converted to the Lord (Sir 5:8). Si autem accipiantur in malo, tunc per modum praenuntiationis intelligenda sunt; quasi dicat, erubescant, prae detectione peccati eorum coram omnibus: Isa. 1: erubescetis super hortis quos elegeratis, cum fueritis velut quercus defluens foliis et cetera. But if let them be ashamed is taken in relation not to good but to evil, then it must be understood as a foretelling, as if to say, they shall be ashamed about the unveiling of their sin before all. You shall be ashamed of the gardens which you have chosen when you shall be as an oak with the leaves falling off, and as a garden without water (Isa 1:29). Et conturbentur, timore et tristitia mirabili: Sap. 5: turbabuntur timore horribili et cetera. Et ideo dicit, vehementer omnes inimici mei convertantur, recognoscendo culpam propriam et gloriam sanctorum: Sap. 5: hi sunt quos aliquando habuimus in derisum, et in similitudinem improperii: nos insensati et cetera. And they shall be very much troubled with incredible fear and sorrow. They shall be troubled with terrible fear (Wis 5:2). And so he says, let all my enemies be very much troubled, by recognizing their guilt and the glory of the saints. These are those whom we had some time in derision, and for a parable of reproach (Wis 5:3). Et erubescant, quia erit eis haec recognitio ad confusionem: et hoc, valde velociter: Isa. 30: subito dum non speratur, veniet contritio ejus et cetera. Job 21: in puncto ad inferna descendunt: 1 Thess. 5: dies domini sicut fur in nocte, ita veniet. Cum enim dixerint pax et securitas et cetera. And let them be ashamed, since this understanding that results in shame will come to them, and this very speedily. Their destruction shall come on a sudden, when it is not looked for (Isa 30:15). In a moment they go down to hell (Job 21:13). The day of the Lord shall so come, as a thief in the night. For when they shall say, “Peace and security,” then shall sudden destruction come upon them (1 Thess 5:2–3). Supra praemissi sunt psalmi pro liberatione ab hostibus; hic ponitur psalmus in quo petit vindictam de eis: et praeponitur titulus talis, in finem quem cantavit domino pro verbis Chusi filii Gemini. 42. The earlier psalms were written for liberation from enemies; but this is a psalm in which he prays for vindication from them, and thus the title is to the end, which he sung to the Lord for the words of Chusai the son of Jemini. Historia habetur 2 Reg. 17, quod David fugit a facie filii sui, et Achitophel sapientissimus adhaesit Absaloni. Chusi vero adhaesit David: quem remisit ut sciret consilia Achitophel, et ea sibi significaret. Cum autem Achitophel consilium dedisset Absaloni nocivum valde patri suo David, scilicet ut statim eum invaderet antequam tutaretur alicubi, Chusi secreto significavit David, ut de campestribus fugeret ubi erat, et transiret Jordanem ad loca munitissima. Isto ergo nuntio Chusi audito David, hoc canticum cecinit domino: et ideo dicitur hic, quem cantavit domino pro verbis Chusi filius Gemini. The story in 2 Samuel 17 tells how David was fleeing before the face of his son, and Ahithophel, who was very wise, remained with Absalom. Chusai, however, remained with David, who had sent him back to learn the plans of Ahithophel and tell them to him. Now when Ahithophel gave Absalom counsel which was very dangerous to David, his father—namely, to attack him immediately before he could make himself safe elsewhere—Chusai secretly informed David so he could flee from the plains where he was and cross the Jordan to a fortified place. Therefore, when David had heard the message from Chusai, he sang this song to the Lord, and therefore it is said here, which he sung to the Lord for the words of Chusai the son of Jemini. Dicitur autem filius Gemini, quia forte de tribu Beniamin erat: vel ex aliquo descenderat qui habebat hoc nomen. Now he is called the son of Jemini because he might have been from the tribe of Benjamin, or was descended from another who had this name. Hieronymus non habet, in finem: nec pro victoria, sed pro ignoratione, vel ignorantibus. Et dicit super verbis Aethiopis: tamen nescitur an fuerit Aethiops. Jerome does not have in the end. Neither does he have for victory but for ignorance, or for the ignorant. And he says for the words of the Ethiopian, although it is not known whether Chusai was Ethiopian. Mystice autem hic psalmus potest esse Ecclesiae, et contra persecutores. Et dicitur pro verbis Chusi, qui interpretatur silentium, sed Gemini dextera; quasi dicat: occulto divinae providentiae judicio factum est ut Achitophel, qui fratris ruina interpretatur, scilicet Judas Iscariotes, in ruinam, id est in mortem fratris sui, Christi videlicet, qui non confunditur eos vocare fratres, Hebr. 2 consilio et auxilio conspiravit. Sed quod ille ad perniciem unius hominis fecit, Christus ad salutem omnium inflexit, ut dicit Augustinus in glossa. Mystically, this psalm could be said by the Church against her persecutors. And then Chusai in for the words of Chusai would be interpreted to mean “silence,” but Jemini would mean “the right hand,” as if to say that by the hidden judgment of divine providence, Ahithophel, whose name means “the ruin of a brother” (that is, Judas Iscariot), conspired with counsel and help to bring to ruin (that is, to death), his brother, namely, Christ, who was not ashamed to call us brothers (Heb 2:11). But what he did for the destruction of one man, Christ turned to the salvation of all, as Augustine says in the gloss. Hic autem psalmus habet tres partes. Now this psalm has three parts.
