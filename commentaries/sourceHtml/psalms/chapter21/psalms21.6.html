      Vel assimilatur Christus aquae, quia aqua lavat; sic passio Christi de omnibus peccatis et omnes sordes lavat: Apoc. 1: dilexit nos et lavit nos a peccatis nostris in sanguine suo. Item aqua rigat et facit fructificare: sic passio Christi: Eccl. 24: dixi, irrigabo hortum meum et cetera. Et fructificat fructum vitae aeternae: Eccl. 24: flores mei (id est passionis meae) fructus honoris et honestatis. Item facit viam lubricam: sic passio Christi disponit Judaeos ad casum: 1 Cor. 1: nos autem praedicamus Christum crucifixum: Judaeis quidem scandalum, gentibus autem stultitiam; ipsis autem vocatis Judaeis atque Graecis Christum Dei virtutem et Dei sapientiam. Or, Christ is made like water, since as water cleanses, so the Passion of Christ washes away all filth and all sins. Who has loved us and washed us from our sins in his own blood (Rev 1:5). Likewise, as water rains down and makes fruitful, so does the Passion of Christ. I said, “I will water my garden” (Sir 24:42). And it makes fruitful with the fruit of eternal life. My flowers (that is, my Passion) are the fruit of honor and integrity (Sir 24:23). Likewise, as water makes the road slippery, so the Passion of Christ disposed the Jews to fall. But we preach Christ crucified, unto the Jews indeed a stumbling block, and unto the gentiles foolishness but unto those who are called, both Jews and Greeks, Christ the power of God, and the wisdom of God (1 Cor 1: 23–24). Consequenter exponit hunc effectum: et ideo dicit, dispersa sunt et cetera; quasi dicat, quicquid videbatur in me forte, est dissolutum: quidquid pulchrum, emarcuit. Et ideo dicit, dispersa et cetera. Afterwards, he explains this effect, and so he says, all my bones are scattered, as if to say, “Whatever in me seemed to be strong is dissolved; whatever seemed to be beautiful is dried up.” And so he says, scattered. In homine est duplex fortitudo. There is a twofold strength in man. Una est fortitudo corporis, et haec consistit in ossibus et nervis; et quantum ad hoc dicit, dispersa sunt omnia ossa mea: quasi dicat: omnis virtus mea corporalis defecit. One is the strength of the body, and this consists in the bones and nerves, and in regard to this he says, all my bones are scattered, as if to say, “All the strength of my body has failed.” Tamen de Christo dicitur spiritualiter: nam apostoli qui sunt ossa Christi, dispersi fuerunt: Zach. 13: percutiam pastorem, et dispergentur oves gregis. Nevertheless, it is also said according to the spiritual sense about Christ, for the apostles, who are the bones of Christ, were scattered. I will strike the shepherd and the sheep of the flock shall be scattered (Zech 13:7 as quoted in Matt 26:31). Alia est fortitudo animae, quae consistit in corde; unde dicit, factum est cor meum tamquam cera liquescens. The other strength is strength of soul, which dwells in the heart, so he says, my heart is become like wax melting. Quaerit Augustinus quomodo de Christo capite verum sit: quia hoc videtur provenire ex superabundanti timore, quod non est dicendum de Christo: quia etsi fuerit in eo timor naturalis, non tamen fuit tantus quod liquesceret cor. Augustine asks how this is true of Christ the head, since this seems to happen through an excess of fear, which should not be said about Christ, since even if there was natural fear in him, it would not be so great as to liquefy the heart. Et ita intelligitur de Christo non secundum se, sed quantum ad membra; quae quidem sunt cor Christi, quae et praecipue diligit: Phil. 1: eo quod vos habeam in corde. Et sequitur, testis est mihi Deus, quomodo cupiam vos esse in visceribus Jesu Christi. Hi fuerunt apostoli qui fuerunt ossa ad sustentandum infirmos in Ecclesia, sicut ossa sustentant carnes: Rom. 15: debemus nos firmiores imbecillitatem infirmorum sustinere. Et fuerunt corda eorum sicut cera liquescens. And so it should be understood about Christ not in regard to himself but in regard to the members, since indeed they are the heart of Christ, which he principally loves. For that I have you in my heart (Phil 1:7). And it follows: God is my witness, how I long after you in the bowels of Jesus Christ (Phil 1:8). It was the apostles, who were the bones given to sustain the weak in the Church just as the bones hold up the flesh. We who are stronger ought to bear the infirmities of the weak (Rom 15:1). And it was their hearts which liquefied like wax. Primo mala liquefactione per timorem, sicut in fuga discipulorum: Matth. 26: tunc relicto eo fugerunt omnes. Et in negatione Petri: Luc. 22: at ille negavit, dicens, homo nescio quid dicis. This occurs first in evil liquefaction through fear, as happened in the flight of the disciples—then all the disciples, leaving him, fled (Matt 26:56)—and in the denial of Peter: but he denied it . . . saying, “Man, I know not what you say” (Luke 22:57, 60). Secundo bona liquefactione, sicut in conversione discipulorum, ut patet in Petro et Andrea. Second, it occurs in good liquefaction, as took place in the conversion of the disciples, as is shown in Peter and Andrew. Vel dicendum quod liquefactio etiam est amoris. Cant. 5: anima mea liquefacta est. Res antequam liquefiat, dura est et constricta in se; si liquescit, diffunditur et tendit a se in aliud. Timor etiam quandoque indurat, quando scilicet non est magnus: et sic est etiam de amore: quia quando supervenit amor, tunc homo tendit in aliud quod ante in se erat. Et de hac liquefactione potest exponi etiam de Christo secundum quod est caput: nam hoc liquefieri et est a Spiritu Sancto, et est in medio ventris, id est affectus. Or it should be said that this is a liquefaction of love. My soul melted (Song 5:6). Prior to the liquefaction of something, it is hard and holds together tightly; if it liquefies, it is diffused and tends from itself to another. Now fear sometimes hardens when it is not great, and so it is with love that, when love comes upon him, then the man tends towards others who before was focused in on himself. And this liquefaction can be applied about Christ as the head, for this liquefaction is from the Holy Spirit and is in the midst of the bowels, that is, the affections. Vel per cor Christi intelligitur sacra Scriptura, quae manifestat cor Christi. Hoc autem erat clausum ante passionem, quia erat obscura; sed aperta est post passionem, quia eam jam intelligentes considerant, et discernunt quomodo prophetiae sint exponendae. Or, by the heart of Christ should be understood the Sacred Scriptures which manifest the heart of Christ. This was closed before the Passion, since it was obscure, but is open after the Passion, since the intelligent understand and discern how the prophets should be explained. 187. Aruit. Hic ostendit quod quicquid pulchrum fuit in Christo evanuit. 187. My strength is dried up. Here he shows that whatever beauty that was in Christ vanished. Florere videbantur in Christo ante passionem tria: Three things were seen to flourish in Christ before the Passion: operatio miraculorum, the working of miracles, facundia doctrinae, eloquence in teaching, fama et gloria populorum. his reputation and the glory of the people. De primo, Joan. 6: multitudo magna sequebatur eum, quia videbant et cetera. Et haec virtus aruit in passione quantum ad opinionem eorum: unde clamabant, alios salvus fecit et cetera. Aruit, id est viluit, tamquam et cetera. About the first: a great multitude followed him because they saw (John 6:2). And this power dried up in the Passion in regard to their opinion of him, so they cried, he saved others, himself he cannot save (Matt 27:42). It is dried up, that is, it has become worthless like a potsherd. Vel testa, quando arescit induratur: sic in passione virtus Christi fuit indurata ad sustentandum: Eccl. 27: vasa figuli probat fornax, et homines justos tentatio tribulationis. Or it is like a potsherd which hardens when it dries up, as in the Passion the strength of Christ was made hard to sustain him. The furnace tries the potter’s vessels, and the trial of affliction, just men (Sir 27:6). De secundo, Matth. 7: erat docens eos tamquam potestatem habens; sed in passione, adhaesit lingua mea faucibus meis, prae taciturnitate: Ezech. 3: adhaerere faciam linguam tuam palato tuo, et eris mutus. Et hoc est factum in passione; quia non respondebat Herodi: Luc. 23: interrogabat eum Herodes multis sermonibus. At ipse nihil respondebat ei. About the second: for he was teaching them as one having power (Matt 7:29), but in the Passion, my tongue has cleaved to my jaws, because of his silence. I will make your tongue stick fast to the roof of your mouth, and you will be dumb (Ezek 3:26). And this took place in the Passion since he did not respond to Herod. Herod questioned him in many words. But he answered him nothing (Luke 23:9). De tertio Matth. 21: plurima autem turba straverunt vestimenta sua in via. Alii autem caedebant ramos de arboribus et cetera. Tunc impletum est quod David prophetaverat de Christo dicens, (Ps. 117) domine salvum me fac, o domine bene prosperare; benedictus qui venit in nomine domini, Deus dominus et illuxit nobis. Sed in passione viluit; unde dicit, et in pulverem mortis deduxisti me, id est vilem mortem me pati fecisti: Sap. 2: morte turpissima condemnemus eum. About the third: a very great multitude spread their garments in the way and others cut boughs from the trees (Matt 21:8). Then was fulfilled what David prophesied about Christ, saying, O Lord, save me; O Lord, give good success. Blessed be he who comes in the name of the Lord. . . . The Lord is God and he has shone upon us (Ps 117:25–26, 27). But in the Passion he was reviled, so he says, and you have brought me down into the dust of death, that is, you have made me endure a vile death. Let us condemn him to a most shameful death (Wis 2:20). Vel si referatur ad membra, in pulverem mortis deduxisti me, id est membra mea quae sunt incinerata, non autem Christi. Or, it is referred to the members: and you have brought me down into the dust of death, that is, my members who were burned but not those of Christ. Vel in pulverem et cetera, id est in potestatem Judaeorum dedisti me qui sunt sicut pulvis et cetera. Or: into the dust of death, that is, you have given me into the power of the Jews who are like dust. 188. Quoniam. Hic ponitur modus passionis. 188. For many dogs have encompassed me. Here the manner of the Passion is described. Et primo ponit ea quae facta sunt ante crucifixionem. First, he describes those things which took place before the crucifixion; Secundo quae facta sunt in ipsa crucifixione. second, those which took place in the crucifixion itself; Tertio quae facta sunt post crucifixionem. third, those which took place after the crucifixion. Ante crucifixionem facta fuerunt duo. Two things were done before the crucifixion. Primo fuit captus; et quantum ad hoc dicit, circumdederunt me canes multi: Philip. 3: videte canes, videte malos operarios: Isa. 56: canes impudentissimi nescierunt saturitatem. First, he was captured, and in regard to this he says, many dogs have encompassed me. Beware of dogs, beware of evil workers (Phil 3:2). Impudent dogs never have enough (Isa 56:11). Item secundo quomodo fuit illusus; unde dicit, consilium malignantium obsedit me. Second is how he was mocked, so he says, the council of the malignant has besieged me. Consequenter ponit ea quae facta sunt in ipsa crucifixione. Afterwards, he describes those things which took place in the crucifixion. Et primo quantum ad affixionem, foderunt manus meas et pedes meos, grossis clavibus affigentes ligno: Zach. 13: quid sunt plagae istae in medio manuum tuarum? And first in affixing him: they have dug my hands and my feet, affixing him to the Cross with large nails: what are these wounds in the midst of your hands? (Zech 13:6). Item quantum ad distensionem dicit, dinumeraverunt omnia ossa mea, id est dinumerabilia fecerunt. Likewise, in regard to stretching him he says, they have numbered all my bones, that is, I was stretched out so they could be numbered. 189. Ipsi vero. Hic ponuntur illa quae facta sunt post passionem; unde dicit, ipsi vero consideraverunt, convenientes ad spectaculum ut illuderent: Luc. 23: milites illudentes impleverunt spongiam aceto dicentes, si rex Israel es, salvum te fac et cetera. Et inspexerunt, scilicet quid eveniret de me. 189. And they have looked. Here are describes those things which took place after the Passion, so he says, and they have looked, coming to see in order to mock. Soldiers, mocking, filled a sponge with vinegar, saying, “If you are the king of Israel, save yourself” (Luke 23:36–37). And stared at what happened to me. Diviserunt inter se vestimenta mea, quae erant plura et divisibilia: et super vestem meam, scilicet inconsutilem, miserunt sortem, et hoc fecerunt vel propter cupiditatem, vel propter quamdam illusionem. They parted my garments, which were many and divisible, among them, and upon my vesture, namely, which was sewn as one piece, they cast lots. And they did this either out of greed or for their amusement. Per haec vestimenta divisa signantur Ecclesiae sacramenta; sed per vestem quae non dividitur, signatur unitas Ecclesiae quam quilibet credit habere; sed non habet nisi unus, quia sola est unitas Ecclesiae: Cant. 6: una est columba mea, perfecta mea. The Church is signified by these garments, but by the vesture which was not divided is signified the unity of the Church which anyone might believe they have, but no one has it except one, since the unity of the Church is singular. One is my dove, my perfect one (Song 6:8).
