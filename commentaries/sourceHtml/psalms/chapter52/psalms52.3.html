      Primo investigat ipsum defectum poenitentiae. First, he investigates the very failure to repent. Secundo meritum poenae assignat, ibi, qui operantur. Second, he assigns the merit of the punishment, at workers of iniquity. Circa primum considerandum est quod frequenter contigit quod in statu prosperitatis homines non recognoscant Deum, sed per poenas a Deo inflictas recordantur quod sit Deus, quasi dicat: et hoc necessarium est propter culpam. Psalm.: cognoscetur dominus iudicia faciens. Regarding the first, we should consider that it frequently happens that in a state of prosperity men do not think of God, but through the punishments inflicted by God they remember that God exists—as if he were to say, “And this is necessary because of wrongdoing.” The Lord shall be known when he executes judgments (Ps 9:17). Et huius exemplum habemus in Pharaone. Exod. 5: nescio dominum. Et post poenas inflictas dixerunt (Aegyptii): fugiamus Israelem, dominus enim pugnat pro eis contra nos. And we have an example of this in Pharaoh: I know not the Lord (Exod 5:2). And after the punishments were inflicted, they (the Egyptians) said, let us flee from Israel, for the Lord is fighting for them against us (Exod 14:25). Dictum est autem supra quod dixit insipiens non est Deus, et ob hoc dicit: nonne scient omnes qui operantur iniquitatem, saltem per poenas quod sit Deus? Quasi dicat: immo scient quod nullus potest poenas infligere nisi Deus. It was said above that the fool said in his heart, “There is no God,” and because of this he says, shall not all the workers of iniquity know through punishments that God exists. As if to say, “They will indeed know that only God can inflict punishments.” Et hoc necessarium est valde propter culpam quam commiserunt. Et commiserunt duplicem culpam, scilicet in opere et in affectu. And this is absolutely necessary because of the wrongdoing that they committed. And they committed a double wrongdoing, namely, in deed and in desire. Culpa est operatio iniquitatis; et ideo dicit, qui operantur iniquitatem. Iniquitas proprie est peccatum in proximum, quia opponitur aequitati. Wrongdoing is the enactment of iniquity, and therefore, he says, workers of iniquity. Iniquity, properly speaking, is sin against one’s neighbor, which is opposed to equity. Hanc culpam exaggerat cum dicit: qui devorant plebem meam ut cibum panis. Cibus panis est assiduus et delectabilis, quasi dicat: isti in suis malitiis delectabiliter perseverant et in iniuriis inferendis delectantur. Michaee 3: comederunt carnes populi mei et cadavera eorum, et abstulerunt bona eorum. Jerem. 50: primus rex Assur. He portrays this wrongdoing in exaggerated form when he says, who eat up my people as they eat bread. Bread is sustaining and delightful, so it is as if he were saying, “These men persevere with delight in their evildoing and take delight in inflicting injustices.” They have eaten the flesh of my people and their corpses, and have taken away their goods (Mic 3:3). First the king of Assyria devoured Israel (Jer 50:17). Quantum ad effectum dicit, Deum non invocaverunt. Dupliciter sunt inordinati peccatores in affectu. After all this, he says about the effect, they have not called upon God. Sinners are doubly disordered in their desires: Primo per contemptum Dei. first, through contempt of God; Secundo per inordinatum amorem ad temporalia. second, through their disordered love for temporal things. Quantum ad primum dicit, Deum non invocaverunt propter duo. Regarding the first, he says, they have not called upon God for two reasons. Primo quia in Deum non credunt. Rom. 10: quomodo invocabunt in quem non crediderunt? First, they do not believe in God: how then shall they call on him, in whom they have not believed? (Rom 10:14). Secundo quia credunt sibi per se sufficere. Ps.: labia nostra a nobis sunt. Second, they believe that they are self-sufficient: our lips are our own; who is Lord over us? (Ps 11:5). Quantum ad secundum dicit, illic trepidaverunt ubi non fuit timor; quasi dicat: ex hac causa timuerunt ubi non est timendum. Regarding the second, he says, there have they trembled for fear, where there was no fear, as if to say, “For this reason they feared where there was nothing to fear.” Verbo illic non locum sed causam designat. Unde dicit Augustinus quod timor ex amore causatur. Isti, scilicet mali, vel peccatores non nisi temporalium amorem habent; et ideo solum damna temporalia timent, id est, propter illam causam ubi non est, id est, propter quod non timendum. Matth. 10: nolite timere eos, et cetera; et ideo non est timendum in amissione temporalium, quia in ipsis non est proprie verum bonum nostrum, et certum est quod si adhaereremus Deo etiam hoc dabit nobis. Matth. 6: primum quaerite regnum Dei, et cetera. The word there designates not a place but a cause. So Augustine says that fear is caused by love. Those men, namely evil ones, or sinners, have love only for temporal things; and therefore, they fear only temporal loss, something which ought not be feared. Fear not those who kill the body and are not able to kill the soul (Matt 10:28). And thus one should not fear the loss of temporal things, because our real good does not truly exist in them, and it is certain that if we were to cling to God, he would also give us all this: seek first the kingdom of God, and his justice, and all these things shall be added unto you (Matt 6:33). 521. Quoniam dominus dissipavit. Hic manifestat quaestionem quomodo correctio fiat, id est propter poenam. 521. For the Lord has scattered. Here he exposes the question of how correction may occur, that is, through punishment. Et inducit duplicem poenam, And he sets out a double punishment, scilicet interiorem namely, interior et exteriorem. and exterior. Primum ostendit cum dicit, Deus dissipavit ossa eorum qui hominibus placent, id est qui finaliter desiderant hominibus placere. He lays out the first when he says, God has scattered the bones of those who please men, that is, who desire in the end to please men. Per ossa intelligitur fortitudo. Haec autem fortitudo omnis (vel) est corporalis, et haec consumitur a Deo; (vel) est spiritualis, et haec consumitur per peccatum. Bones means courage. And this courage is either entirely corporeal, and this is consumed by God, or it is spiritual, and this is consumed through sin. Haec ossa sunt virtutes bonorum virorum; unde in cruce ossa Christi non fuerunt fracta. Sed latronum ossa fuerunt contrita. These bones are the virtues of good men; so on the Cross, Christ’s bones were not broken, but the bones of the thieves were crushed. Sed dicit, eorum qui hominibus placent. Numquid placere hominibus est pravum? Videtur quod non. 1 Cor. 10: sine offensione estote, et cetera. Item omnibus per omnia placeo, dicit Apostolus. But he says, those who please men. Is it wrong to please men? It seems not. Be without offense (1 Cor 10:32). The Apostle says in the same place, in all things I please all men (10:33). Dicendum, quod si aliquis velit placere alicui propter inanem gloriam, sive gloriam humanam habendam hoc est pravum in illis qui in hoc ponunt finem, quia tales confidenter peccant in occulto dummodo placeant in aperto. It should be said that if someone wants to please another for empty glory, or to have human glory, this is wrong in those who make this their goal, because such men sin with insouciance in secret even as they please outwardly. Aliquando autem aliquis vult aliis placere ut trahat eos ad Deum; et sic talis placentia est meritoria et bona; et sic volebat Apostolus nos aliis placere, et sic placebat ipse. But sometimes someone wishes to please others in order to draw them to God, and such pleasing is meritorious and good; and this is the sense in which the Apostle wanted us to please others and thus himself pleased them. Contrarium ponitur poena exterior: unde dicit, confusi sunt, id est confundantur, quia peccata eorum deteguntur. Job 20: revelabunt caeli iniquitatem ejus, quia deceperunt, et quia perdent temporalia per quae credebant habere felicitatem. Jer. 20: confundentur vehementer. Jer. 17: confundantur qui me persequuntur. After this interior punishment, he juxtaposes an exterior punishment: they have been confounded, because their sins have been uncovered. The heavens shall reveal his iniquity (Job 20:27), because they deceived, and because they lose the temporal things through which they thought they would have happiness. They shall be greatly confounded (Jer 20:11). Let those who persecute me be confounded (Jer 17:18). Et hoc patientur quoniam Deus sprevit eos. Illi sunt honorati quos Deus amat. Psalm.: nimis honorati sunt, et cetera; sed quos Deus sprevit sunt confusi. And they shall suffer this because God has despised them. They are honored whom God loves. Your friends, O God, are made exceedingly honorable (Ps 138:17), but those whom God has despised are confounded. 522. Quis dabit ex Sion? Hic agit de bonis. 522. Who will give out of Zion? Here he treats of good things. Et primo proponitur quaestio. And first, he proposes a question; Secundo responsio. second, a response. Quaestio est talis. Quis ex Sion procedens salvabit Israel? Quis? Christus quia salus ex Iudaeis est: ergo ex Sion, id est ex Iudaeis, et ex David, et ex coenaculo Sion ubi apostoli Spiritum Sanctum receperunt, processerunt praedicatores salutis. Et haec non dabit nisi Deus. The question is, “Who will save Israel coming forth from Zion?” Who indeed? Christ, because salvation is of the Jews (John 4:22). Therefore, the preachers of salvation came forth from Zion, that is, from the Jews, and from David, and from Zion’s upper room, where the Apostles received the Holy Spirit; and only God will give this. Et qualiter? Quando converterit captivitatem plebis suae. Nam plebs fidelis in captivitate, id est in carcere Diaboli, tenebatur, quam convertit Deus quando eam relaxavit. Ps.: converte, domine, captivitatem nostram, et cetera. And how? When God shall bring back the captivity of his people. For God brought back his faithful people who were held in captivity, that is, in the Devil’s prison, when he released them. Bring back our captivity, O Lord (Ps 125:4). Et quid sequetur ex hoc? Gaudium et laetitia. Ps.: in convertendo dominus captivitatem (Sion) facti sumus, et cetera. And what will follow on this? Joy and gladness. When the Lord brought back the captivity of Zion, we became like men comforted (Ps 125:1). Sed est duplex status fidelium, scilicet luctae et coronae, meriti et praemii; et utrobique est gaudium. But the faithful have two states, namely, sorrow and the crown; merit and reward; and there is joy in each. Et primum est gaudium de participatione gratiae. Rom. 14: regnum est iustitia. Et ideo dicit exultabit Iacob. 1 Reg. 2: exultavit cor meum, et cetera. And first is the joy of participation in grace. The kingdom of God is justice (Rom 14:17). And thus he says, Jacob shall rejoice. My heart has rejoiced in the Lord (1 Sam 2:1).
