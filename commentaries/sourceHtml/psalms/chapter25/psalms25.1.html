      Super Psalmo 25 Psalm 25 Judica me, Domine Judge me, O Lord, for I have walked 25:1 In finem. Psalmus David. Judica me, Domine, quoniam ego in innocentia mea ingressus sum, et in Domino sperans non infirmabor. 25:2 Proba me, Domine, et tenta me; ure renes meos et cor meum. 25:1 Unto the end, a psalm for David. Judge me, O Lord, for I have walked in my innocence, and I have put my trust in the Lord and shall not be weakened. 25:2 Prove me, O Lord, and try me; burn my reins and my heart. 25:3 Quoniam misericordia tua ante oculos meos est, et complacui in veritate tua. 25:3 For your mercy is before my eyes and I am well pleased with your truth. 25:4 Non sedi cum concilio vanitatis, et cum iniqua gerentibus non introibo. 25:5 Odivi ecclesiam malignantium, et cum impiis non sedebo. 25:4 I have not sat with the council of vanity; neither will I go in with the doers of unjust things. 25:5 I have hated the assembly of the wicked and with the impious I will not sit. 25:6 Lavabo inter innocentes manus meas, et circumdabo altare tuum, Domine: 25:7 ut audiam vocem laudis, et enarrem universa mirabilia tua. 25:6 I will wash my hands among the innocent, and will compass your altar, O Lord: 25:7 that I may hear the voice of your praise and tell of all your wondrous works. 25:8 Domine, dilexi decorem domus tuae, et locum habitationis gloriae tuae. 25:8 I have loved, O Lord, the beauty of your house and the place where your glory dwells. 25:9Ne perdas cum impiis, Deus, animam meam, et cum viris sanguinum vitam meam: 25:10 in quorum manibus iniquitates sunt; dextera eorum repleta est muneribus. 25:11 Ego autem in innocentia mea ingressus sum; redime me, et miserere mei. 25:9 Take not away my soul, O God, with the wicked, nor my life with bloody men 25:10 in whose hands are iniquities; their right hand is filled with gifts. 25:11 But as for me, I have walked in my innocence. Redeem me and have mercy on me. 25:12 Pes enim meus stetit in directo; in ecclesiis benedicam te, Domine. 25:12 For my foot has stood in the direct way; in the churches I will bless you, O Lord. 226. Supra psalmista proposuit orationem contra tribulationes; hic autem, ut sua oratio sit acceptior, proponit suam justitiam. 226. Above, the psalmist put forward a prayer against troubles; now here, he describes his justice in order to make his prayer more acceptable. Titulus planus est, quia non est novus. The title is clear, since it is not new. Circa hoc ergo psalmista duo facit. The psalmist, therefore, makes two points about this. Primo praemittit in generali totam intentionem. First, he gives his whole intention in general. Secundo exponit eam in speciali, ibi, non sedi cum concilio. Second, he explains it in particular, at I have not sat with the council. Circa primum tria facit. He makes three points about this. Primo petit judicium. First, he asks for judgment. Secundo allegat justitiam, ibi, quoniam ego. Second, he asserts his justice, at for I have walked. Tertio excusat praesumptionem, quoniam misericordia. Third, he excuses his presumption, at for your mercy. David autem ex persona sua vel justi, videns se commixtum peccatoribus loco, etsi non merito, petit ab eis discerni; unde dicit, domine judica, id est discerne me ab eis. Now David, in his own person or that of the just man, seeing himself in a place mingling with sinners, although not deservedly, asks to be distinguished from them, so he says, judge me, O Lord, that is, distinguish me from them. Sed contra. Non intres in judicium cum servo tuo, quoniam non justificabitur in conspectu tuo omnis vivens, Ps. 142. But on the contrary. Enter not into judgment with your servant, for in your sight no man living can be justified (Ps 142:2). Respondeo. Dicendum quod duplex est judicium, I respond. It must be said the judgment is twofold. scilicet severitatis et districtionis: et hoc judicium non petit, quia nullus posset ferre; First, that of severity and strictness; and he does not ask for this judgment since no one can bear it. aliud est judicium misericordiae et discussionis: et hoc petit: Hier. 10: corripe me domine, verumtamen in judicio et non in furore, id est severitatis. Another is the judgment of mercy and examination, and he asks for this. Correct me, O Lord, but yet with judgment and not in fury (Jer 10:24), that is, of severity. Et proponit innocentiam; et ideo dicit, quoniam ingressus sum in innocentia mea, id est progressus sum in innocentia mea, And he puts forward his innocence, and so he says, I have walked in my innocence, that is, I have gone forward in my innocence. vel ingressus sum, quia vita nostra est quidam progressus. Prov. 4: ducam te per semitas aequitatis, quas cum ingressus fueris et cetera. Or I have walked, since our life is a sort of progression. I will lead you by the paths of equity, which, when you have entered, your steps shall not be straitened (Prov 4:11–12). Et hoc in innocentia mea, quam propono servare: Job 22: salvabitur innocens, et salvabitur in munditia manuum suarum. And this in my innocence, which I propose to guard. The innocent shall be saved, and he shall be saved by the cleanness of his hands (Job 22:30). Non infirmabor: Hieronymus habet, non deficiam, scilicet in hac innocentia servanda: Job 27: donec deficiam non recedam ab ea: I shall not be weakened. Jerome has, I shall not fail, namely, in guarding this innocence. Until I die I shall not depart from it (Job 27:5). et hoc faciam non in viribus meis, sed confidentia divini auxilii, quia, in domino sperans non infirmabor: 1 Mach. 2: omnes qui sperant in eum, non infirmantur. And I will do this not in my strength, but in confidence of the divine help, since I have put my trust in the Lord and shall not be weakened. None who trust in him fail in strength (1 Macc 2:61). Sed contra: hoc videtur esse praesumptionis: Job 9: si justificare me voluero, os meum condemnabit me. But on the contrary, this seems to be presumption. If I would justify myself, my own mouth shall condemn me (Job 9:20). Respondeo. Dicendum, quod allegatio innocentiae potest quandoque procedere ex superbia, quando scilicet aliquis attribuit eam sibi, et hoc est malum: quandoque ex divina misericordia, et hoc est bonum; et ideo subdit, quoniam misericordia tua. I respond. It must be said that an assertion of innocence can sometimes come from pride, namely, when someone attributes it to himself, and this is evil. Sometimes it comes from the divine mercy and this is good, and so he adds, for your mercy. Sed unde scis quod tu sis innocens? Proba domine, tu hoc. But how do you know that you are innocent? Prove, O Lord, and try me; burn my reins and my heart, you do this. Et ponit tria: scilicet probationem, tribulationem et tentationem. Secundum sequitur ex primo, tertium ex secundo. And he describes three things: proving, tribulation, and trying. The second follows from the first, the third from the second. Dicit ergo, domine proba me, non ut scias aliquid ex hoc de me, sed ut aliis ostendas. Therefore, he says, prove me, O Lord, not so that you will know anything about me from it, but so that you may show others. Probatur autem aliquis optime per tentationem quae est sumptio experimenti: tentatio est acceptio experimenti de eo quod quis nescit; probatio vero est ejus quod scit, virtutis manifestatione. Et ideo dicit, tenta me, scilicet ad hoc ut probatus appaream mihi et aliis: non enim tentat Deus ad malum: Jac. 1: Deus enim intentator malorum est; sed dicitur tentare, quando aliqua ex difficultate immensa ostenduntur. Now someone is proved best through a trial, which comes from experience. Trial is acquiring experience about that which someone does not know, while proving is about that which he knows, to manifest virtue. And so he says, try me, namely, so that I may appear as proven to myself and to others. For God does not tempt to evil. For God is not a tempter of evils (Jas 1:13), but he is said to try when anything is shown through great difficulty. Haec autem tentatio fit per ignem, ut dicitur Sap. 3: tanquam aurum in fornace probavit illos. Et ideo dicit, ure renes meos et cor meum, id est immitte tribulationes; per quas pateat, quid sit in corde et renibus meis. Cordi attribuitur cogitatio, renibus delectatio. Et hoc apparet per tribulationes; quia qui delectantur in terrenis, multum commoventur quando perdunt ea, sed non illi qui non delectantur. Now this trial is through fire. As gold in the furnace he has proved them (Wis 3:6). And so he says, burn my reins and my heart, that is, send tribulations through which what is in my heart and my reins will be shown. To the heart, thought is attributed; to the reins, delight. And this is shown through tribulations, since he who delights in earthly things is greatly shaken when they perish, but not those who do not delight in them. Vel potest referri ad ignem sancti spiritus: Luc. 12: ignem veni mittere in terram: quasi dicat: ne deficiam in tribulationibus, adhibe auxilium, mitte ignem qui exurat quidquid carnalitatis est in delectationibus meis et vanitatibus in cogitatione. Or it can be referred to the fire of the Holy Spirit—I am come to cast fire on the earth (Luke 12:49)—as if to say, “Lest I fail in tribulations, send me help. Send the fire which burns what is fleshly in my delights and vain in my thinking.”
