      Ex parte inimicorum: quia, non delectasti. Hoc enim non est odiosum valde quod inimici gaudeant de eo; quia non gaudent nisi de malo suo, et nullus optat malum nisi sibi exoso: Eccl. 18: si praestes concupiscentias ejus animae tuae, faciet te in gaudium inimicis tuis. On the part of his enemies is that you have not made my enemies to rejoice over me. For it is not very hateful for enemies to rejoice about him, since they do not rejoice except in their own evil, and no one desires evil except to one hated by oneself. If you give to your soul her desires, she will make you a joy to your enemies (Sir 18:31). Sed certum est quod David non venit in gaudium inimicis, quia Saul non est assecutus propositum suum de eo. But it is certain that David did not come to be a joy to his enemies, since Saul did not accomplish his aim concerning him. De Christo autem non videtur: Matth. 27: insultabant enim ei jam crucifixo: vah qui destruis templum Dei et cetera. Item, etiam viris justis mali insultant, et laetantur super eos: Job 30: derident me juniores tempore, nunc in eorum canticum versus sum, et factus sum in proverbium eis. But this does not seem to be about Christ. Vah, you who destroy the temple of God (Matt 27:40). Also, the evil do insult just men and rejoice over them. But now the younger in time scorn me. . . . Now I am turned into their song, and am become their byword (Job 30:1, 9). Sed dicendum, quod si ad horam Judaei de Christo gavisi sunt, non tamen finaliter: quia Christo resurgente nomen ejus magis invaluit: Mich. 7: ne laeteris inimica mea, quia cecidi; consurgam. But it must be said that, although the Jews did rejoice over Christ during that hour, they did not in the end, since his name grew much stronger when Christ arose. Rejoice not, you, my enemy, over me, because I am fallen (Mich 7:8). 265. Deinde cum dicit, domine Deus meus, ostendit quomodo liberatus sit. 265. Afterwards, when he says, O Lord my God, I have cried to you, he shows how he was delivered: Et primo a malis interioribus. first, from interior evils; Secundo ab exterioribus, ibi, domine eduxisti. second, from exterior, at you have brought forth, O Lord. Malum interius est infirmitas, vel corporalis vel spiritualis. Haec duo potuerunt esse in David et in nobis, sed in Christo non nisi corporalis, propter passibilitatem: Interior evil is infirmity, either bodily or spiritual. These two could be both in David and in us, but in Christ there was only the bodily infirmity, because of his passibility. et ideo dicit, clamavi, scilicet David: Ps. 119: ad dominum, cum tribularer, clamavi. Item Christus clamavit, etsi in quantum Deus sit exauditor: Hebr. 5: cum clamore valido et lacrymis et cetera. And so he says, I, namely David, have cried. In my trouble I cried to the Lord and he heard me (Ps 119:1). Likewise, Christ cried, and insofar as he is God he is the hearer. With a strong cry and tears (Heb 5:7). Sequitur, et sanasti me. Dicit quia ab utraque infirmitate; Christum vero sanavit a corporali solum passibilitate. It follows: and you have healed me. He says this out of either infirmity, but Christ was healed only from bodily passibility. Consequenter dicit se esse liberatum ab exterioribus malis, domine eduxisti. Afterwards, he says that he was delivered from exterior evils: you have brought forth, O Lord, my soul from hell. Et primo ab imminentibus. First is from those which were actually threatening him; Secundo ab illis a quibus est praeservatus, ibi, salvasti. and second, from those from which he was preserved: you have saved me. Dicit ergo, domine eduxisti et cetera Therefore, he says, you have brought forth, O Lord, my soul from hell. hoc ad litteram non potest intelligi de David: quia non erat erutus de inferno, quando hunc psalmum fecit. Potest intelligi de eo secundum metaphoram, quasi liberatus sit a mortali periculo. This cannot be understood literally about David, since he was not yet brought forth from hell when the psalm said this. It can be understood about him according to metaphor, since he was delivered from mortal danger. Sed ad litteram intelligitur de Christo, cujus anima educta est de inferno a Deo: Ps. 15: ne derelinquas animam meam in inferno. But it is understood according to the literal sense about Christ, whose soul was brought forth from hell by God. Because you will not leave my soul in hell (Ps 15:10). Item convenit illis qui per Christum resuscitati sunt: Zach. 9: tu autem in sanguine testamenti tui eduxisti vinctos tuos de lacu in quo non erat aqua. Also, it fits those whom Christ resurrected. You also by the blood of your testament have sent forth your prisoners out of the pit, wherein is no water (Zech 9:11). Secundo dicit se praeservatum a mortali periculo, cum subdit, salvasti me a descendentibus in lacum. Second, he speaks about his preservation from mortal danger when he adds, you have saved me from those who go down into the pit. Ad litteram lacus accipitur pro concavitate: mos enim fuit antiquitus quod sepeliebantur in profundis concavitatibus: salvasti me, ait, a descendentibus in lacum; quasi dicat, liberasti me a periculis mortis. Literally, a pit is understood to be a cave, for it was the ancient custom to be buried in deep caves. You have saved me, he says, from those who go down into the pit, as if to say, “You have delivered me from danger of death.” Sed de Christo exponitur optime, quia per lacum intelligitur damnatio aeterna: quia licet Christus descenderit in infernum; non tamen illuc descendit tamquam ad damnationem, sed ut liberaret eos qui erant in lacu; quasi dicat, dedisti mihi, ut non assimilarer descendentibus in lacum: Ps. 87: factus sum sicut homo sine adjutorio, inter mortuos liber. But it is best expounded about Christ, since by the pit eternal damnation can be understood, because although Christ descended into hell, nevertheless he did not descend there to be damned, but to deliver those who were in the pit. As if to say, “You have given it to me that I am not made like those who descend into the pit.” I am become a man without help, free among the dead (Ps 87:5–6). Vel, in lacum, id est in peccatum; immunis enim fuit a peccato. Or he says, into the pit, that is, into sin, for he was immune from sin. 266. Deinde cum dicit, psallite domino sancti ejus et cetera, inducit alios primo ad gratiarum actionem. 266. Afterwards, when he says, sing to the Lord, O you his saints, he first leads others to give thanks. Secundo commemorat beneficia, ibi, quoniam ira in indignatione ejus. Second, he remembers the favors, at for wrath is in his indignation. Ubi duo facit: Here he makes two points: quia primo ostendit qui sunt qui gratias Deo debent. first, he shows who owes thanks to God; Secundo de quo, ibi, confitemini. second, for what, at give praise. Dicit ergo, psallite. Ecce qualiter psalmus cantici. Psallite, inquam vos sancti: quia Eccl. 15: non est speciosa laus in ore peccatoris: Apoc. 19: laudem dicite Deo nostro omnes sancti ejus. Therefore, he says, sing. Behold how the psalm is of a song. Sing, I say, you saints. Praise is not seemly in the mouth of a sinner (Sir 15:9). Give praise to our God, all you his servants (Rev 19:5). Sed de quo? Confitemini, gratias agendo, memoriae sanctitatis ejus. Uno modo memoriae potest intelligi, quia Deus memor est nostri: Hier. 2: recordatus sum tui, et cetera. But for what? Give praise, by giving thanks, for the memory of his holiness. One way in which for the memory of his holiness can be understood is that God remembers us. I remembered you (Jer 2:2). Et dicit, sanctitatis ejus. Et dupliciter potest intelligi: He says, of his holiness. And this can be understood in two ways: vel quia haec memoria provenit ex sanctitate, id est ex misericordia et bonitate sua: Levit. 11: sancti estote, quia ego sanctus sum: either that this memory came into being from holiness, that is, from his own mercy and goodness: be holy because I am holy (Lev 11:44); vel quia memor est nostri ut sanctificet nos: Levit. 20: ego dominus qui sanctifico vos. or that the memory is of us so that he may make us holy. I am the Lord who sanctifies you (Lev 20:8). Alio modo, memoriae, scilicet nostrae, quia nos recordamur sanctitatis Dei; quasi dicat, confitemini commemorando sanctitatem ejus: Isa. 63: miserationum domini recordabor. In another way, to the memory can be ours since we remember the holiness of God. As if to say, “Give praise by remembering his holiness.” I will remember the tender mercies of the Lord (Isa 63:7). 267. Secundo, cum dicit, quoniam ira, commemorat beneficia omnibus exhibita. 267. Second, when he says, for wrath is in his indignation, he remembers the favors shown to all. Ubi duo facit. Here he makes two points. Primo proponit haec beneficia, quae spectant ad Dei clementiam. First, he describes those favors which concern the clemency of God. Secundo clementiae ejus ostendit signum, ibi, ad vesperum. Second, he shows a sign of the clemency itself, at in the evening.
