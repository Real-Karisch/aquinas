      Secundo clementiae ejus ostendit signum, ibi, ad vesperum. Second, he shows a sign of the clemency itself, at in the evening. Dicit ergo, quoniam ira et cetera. Misericordia Dei non est sine justitia; ideo ponit primo justitiam. Secundo misericordiam: Therefore, he says, for wrath is in his indignation. The mercy of God is not without justice, and so he puts justice first; mercy, second. justitiam, cum dicit, quoniam ira. Hic ira accipitur pro effectu irae, id est pro vindicta; indignatio vero non pro commotione irae in Deo, sed pro justitia Dei, secundum quam detestatur impium: quia Sap. 14: similiter odio sunt Deo impius et impietas ejus; quasi dicat, in indignatione ejus, id est Dei justitia judicante peccata in ira, id est vindicta. He refers to justice when he says, for wrath is in his indignation. Here, wrath should be understood to indicate the effects of wrath, that is, vengeance, because indignation is not used to indicate the emotion of anger in God, but the justice of God according to which he detests the wicked. To God the wicked and his wickedness are hateful alike (Wis 14:9). As if to say, “In his indignation, that is, in the justice of God judging sin by wrath—that is, vengeance.” Hieronymus planius, quoniam ad momentum est ira ejus; quasi dicat, si aliquando irascatur suis, hoc est ad correctionem brevi tempore: Isa. 54: in momento indignationis abscondi et cetera. Ezech. 18: nolo mortem peccatoris morientis. Hieronymus, vita in propitiatione ejus; quasi dicat, punit ad momentum, et post repropitiatur, et reddit vitam: Job 5: percutit, et manus ejus medetur. Jerome more plainly says, for his wrath is for a moment, as if to say, “If he is ever angry, it is for a brief time to correct.” In a moment of indignation I have hidden (Isa 54:8). I desire not the death of he who dies (Ezek 18:32). Jerome has, life is in his propitiation, as if to say, “He punished for a moment and afterwards is propitiated and gives life.” He strikes, and his hands shall heal (Job 5:18). Ostendit autem clementiae et misericordiae ejus signum secundum litteram, cum subdit, ad vesperum demorabitur; quasi dicat, in brevi tempore dominus a tristitia ducit ad consolationem: quia si aliquis tristis sit vespere, mane erit laetus. Now, according to the literal sense he shows a sign of his clemency and mercy when he adds, in the evening weeping shall have place, as if to say, “He will lead you from sorrow to consolation in a brief time,” since if someone is sad in the evening, he will be joyful by morning. Triplex autem ratio assignari potest, quare vespere tristitia insit, et mane laetitia. A triple reason can be given for why sorrow is in the evening but joy is in the morning. Una est ex exteriori dispositione: quia vespere principium est tenebrarum, quae contristant; mane vero lucis, quae laetificat: unde caeci ut laetentur cantant: Tob. 5: quale gaudium mihi erit, qui in tenebris sedeo, et lumen caeli non video? The first comes from exterior order, since evening is the beginning of darkness which saddens, but there is light in the morning which brings gladness. So the blind sing to be glad. What manner of joy shall be to me, who sit in darkness, and see not the light of heaven? (Tob 5:12). Secundo ex interiori: mane est hora sanguinis, qua homo disponitur ad gaudium; vespere est hora melancholiae, qua homo disponitur ad tristitiam. The second is from the interior, for morning is the hour of blood by which a man is disposed to gladness, but evening is the hour of black bile, by which a man is disposed to sorrow. Tertio ex natura somni. Somnus enim est quies animalium: unde tristitia per somnum quietatur. The third is from the nature of sleep. For sleep is the rest of animals, and so sorrow is quieted by sleep. Mystice littera est plana: quia, ad vesperum dominicae sepulturae fuit tristitia, quia adhuc fideles flebant mortem Christi. Sed ad matutinum, propter nuntium resurrectionis, laetitia. Mystically, the text is clear, since the sorrow of the Lord in the tomb was in the evening so that the faithful wept for the death of Christ. But in the morning gladness because of the announcement of the Resurrection. Si ad totum genus humanum referatur, sic, ad vesperum, id est peccatum primi parentis, tristitia; quia, ut habetur Genes. 3, post meridiem vergente jam sole ad occasum peccavit Adam. Et iste fletus non potest dici brevis, quia etiam post reparationem gratiae manent reliquiae ejus. If this is applied to the whole human race, then in the evening, that is, at the sin of the first parents, there was weeping, since, as Genesis 3 says, the sun was moving past noon when Adam sinned. And this weeping cannot be said to be brief, since its remnants remain even after the healing of grace. Sed in matutino, id est in Christo, laetitia. But in the morning, that is, in Christ, is gladness. Vel vespere quando lux spiritualis incipit in homine deficere, et tunc in eo est fletus; sed quando relucet in eo, tunc est gaudium: Ps. 5: mane astabo tibi, et videbo. Or, in the evening is when the spiritual light begins to fade in man and there is weeping in him, but when it rekindles in him, then there is gladness. In the morning I will stand before you and I shall see (Ps 5:5). 268. Ego autem dixi in abundantia. Supra gratias egit de beneficiis divinis, hic autem prosequitur totum ordinem quomodo hoc sit adeptus. 268. And in my abundance. Above, he gave thanks for the divine favors, but here he describes the whole order of why this is fitting. Ubi tria facit. Here he makes three points. Primo ponit processum suae directionis. First, he describes the progress of his approach; Secundo suum recursum ad orationem, ad te domine clamabo. second, his return to prayer: to you, O Lord, will I cry. Tertio ostendit orationis exauditionem, ibi, audivit dominus. Third, he shows the hearing of his prayer, at the Lord has heard. Secundum glossam prima intelliguntur de Christo, et de quolibet homine. According to the gloss, the first should be understood about Christ and any man. Et primo exponamus quomodo intelliguntur de quolibet homine: Eccl. 10: initium omnis peccati superbia. Et ideo in processu directionis First, let us explain how it can be understood about any man. Pride is the beginning of all sin (Sir 10:15). And so, in the progress in his approach, primo ponitur praesumptio de se confidentium. first, the presumption of trusting in oneself is mentioned, Secundo falsitas praesumptionis, ibi, domine in voluntate. second, the falsity of presumption, at O Lord, in your favor. Tertio praesumptionis poena, ibi, avertisti faciem tuam a me. Third is the punishment of presumption, at you turned away your face from me. Dicit ergo, ego dixi, id est corde praesumpsi, in abundantia, corporalis prosperitatis: non movebor, id est non deficiam: Apoc. 18: sedebo regina et cetera. Eccl. 11: in die bonorum ne immemor sis et cetera. Therefore, he says, and I said, that is, I presumed in my heart, in my abundance, in bodily prosperity, I shall never be moved, that is, I shall not fail. I shall sit, a queen (Rev 18:7). In the day of good things be not unmindful of evils (Sir 11:27). Vel in abundantia, spiritualium bonorum, sicut Adam erat in Paradiso: Ezech. 28: plenus sapientia, perfectus decore in deliciis. Et in hac abundantia dicunt, non movebor. Contra quod dicitur 1 Corinth. 10: qui se existimat stare, videat ne cadat. Or he says, in my abundance of spiritual goods, as Adam was in paradise. Full of wisdom, and perfect in beauty in pleasure (Ezek 28:12–1). And in this abundance, they say, I shall not be moved. Against this is said, he who thinks himself to stand, let him take heed lest he fall (1 Cor 10:12). Falsitatem praesumptionis ostendit cum dicit, domine in voluntate tua et cetera. Decor hominis temporaliter est prosperitas temporalis: ideo comparatur flori: Isa. 40: omnis caro fenum. Hic autem decor de sui natura non habet virtutem per mensuram, sicut habetur Jac. 1: sicut flos feni pertransibit. Exortus enim est sol cum ardore, et arefecit fenum, et flos ejus decidit, et decor vultus ejus deperiit: ita et dives et cetera. He shows the falsity of this presumption when he says, O Lord, in your favor, you gave strength to my beauty. Temporal prosperity is a beauty to man, so it is compared to a blossom. All flesh is grass (Isa 40:6). Now this beauty of his nature does not have the strength to be measured. As the flower of the grass shall he pass away. For the sun rose with a burning heat, and parched the grass, and the flower thereof fell off, and the beauty of the shape thereof perished (Jas 1:10–11). Sed unde habet virtutem et firmitatem et constantiam? Certe, ex voluntate Dei. Et ideo dixi, sed praesumptuose, quia non movebor. Sed non est ita; immo quamdiu tibi placuit, praestitisti decori meo virtutem. Bene in perpetuum, quia haec sanctis in patria sunt aeterna; hic autem ad velle suum. But from where does he have strength and firmness and constancy? Certainly, from the favor of God. And so he said, but presumptuously, that I will not be moved. But it is not so; on the contrary, as long as it pleased you, you gave strength to my beauty. These will be well perpetually, since in the fatherland these are eternal in the saints, but here they exist at his will. Item decor potest accipi pro virtute spirituali: Prov. ult.: fortitudo et decor et cetera. Hic etiam ex sui natura non est fortis: quia habemus hunc decorem in vasis fictilibus, 2 Cor. 4, Luc. ult.: sedete in civitate, donec induamini virtute ex alto. Et ideo dicit, praestitisti decori meo virtutem, sed in voluntate tua: Rom. 9: cujus vult miseretur. Likewise, beauty can be understood as spiritual virtue. Strength and beauty are her clothing (Prov 31:25). Of itself this nature is not strong, since we have this beauty in earthen vessels (2 Cor 4:7). Stay you in the city till you be endued with power from on high (Luke 24:49). And so he says, you gave strength to my beauty, but in your favor. He has favor on whom he will (Rom 9:18). Et hoc probat per effectum: quia quando avertisti faciem tuam, perii. Dicitur autem Deus avertere faciem suam ab homine, ut eum non videat, vel ut non videatur ab eo. Videt autem omnes simplici visione et notitia; sed quosdam visione misericordiae: Ps. 24: respice in me, et miserere mei. Avertisti ergo faciem tuam a me, ne mei miserearis. Et statim, factus sum conturbatus, vel spiritualiter incidendo in peccatum, vel temporaliter in adversitatem. And he proves this through its effect, since when you turned away your face from me, I perished. Now, God is said to turn his face away from man so that he might not see him or so that he might not be seen by him. Now, he sees all things with simple vision and knowledge but some with the vision of mercy. Look upon me and have mercy on me (Ps 24:16). You, therefore, turned away your face from me, so as not to have mercy on me. And immediately I became troubled, either by spiritually falling into sin or temporally into adversity. Vel, avertisti faciem tuam, ne videaris a me. Et hoc videtur sonare littera Hieronymi. In adversis quibuscumque fortitudo hominis est habere oculos ad Deum: Ps. 76: memor fui Dei, et delectatus sum. Avertisti ergo ne viderem. Et factus sum conturbatus. Or, you turned away your face from me, so as not to be seen by me. And the text of Jerome seems to say this. In any adversities, it is strength to man to have eyes turned towards God. I remembered God, and was delighted (Ps 76:4). Therefore, you have turned away from me so that I might not see you, and I became troubled. Sed si exponatur de Christo tunc in hoc, ego dixi, non quidem praesumptuose, sed scientiae certitudine, in abundantia mea, id est virtutum et gratiarum: Joan. 1: vidimus gloriam ejus, gloriam quasi unigeniti a Patre. But if it is explained about Christ, then in this, I said, “I shall never be moved,” he is not presumptuous, but it comes from certain knowledge in my abundance, that is, of virtue and grace. We saw his glory, the glory as it were of the only begotten of the Father (John 1:14). Non movebor, a voluntate Dei: Joan. 8: quae placita sunt ei, facio semper. Et hoc, quia, praestitisti decori meo virtutem, scilicet faciendi miracula, et resistendi adversariis: Rom. 1: praedestinatus est Filius Dei in virtute. I shall never be moved from the will of God. I always do the things that please him (John 8:29). And this is because you gave strength to my beauty, by causing wonders and by resisting my adversaries. Who was predestined the Son of God in power (Rom 1:4). Et hoc patet: quia quando avertisti faciem tuam a me: in passione: Ps. 21: Deus Deus meus, quare me dereliquisti. Factus sum conturbatus, non in ratione, sed in sensualitate: Joan. 12: nunc anima mea turbata est. And when you turned away your face from me is clear: in my Passion. O God my God . . . why have you forsaken me? (Ps 21:2). I became troubled, not in my reason, but in sensuality. Now is my soul troubled (John 12:27). 269. Ad te. Consequenter recurrit ad orationem cum dicit, ad te domine, clamabo et cetera. 269. To you, O Lord. Afterwards, he returns to prayer when he says, to you, O Lord, will I cry. Et primo ponit orationem. And first, he puts down the prayer. Secundo assignat rationem, ibi, quae utilitas. Second, he gives the reason, at what profit.
