Qui ambulat in justitiis, et loquitur veritatem. Et dicit, in corde, contra illos qui loquuntur veritatem, a casu, non ex proposito: Proverb. 12: labium veritatis firmum erit: 1 Petr. 2: deponentes omne mendacium et omnem dolum, quasi modo geniti infantes. First is that he do good in his speech: he who speaks truth, that is, he speaks good words. Which of you shall dwell with everlasting burnings? He who walks in justice, and speaks truth (Isa 33:14). And he says, in his heart, contrasting with those who speak the truth by chance and not intentionally. The lip of truth shall be steadfast (Prov 12:19). Laying away all lying and all guile, as newborn babes (1 Pet 2:12). Secundo, ut vitet malum, scilicet dolositatem: Hier. 9: sagitta vulnerans lingua eorum dolum locuta est. Qui non egit dolum in lingua sua. Second is that he avoid evil, namely, deceitfulness. Their tongue, a piercing arrow which has spoken deceit (Jer 9:8). Who has not used deceit in his tongue. Alia littera, qui non est facilis in lingua sua: Prov. 25: urbs patens et absque murorum ambitu, vir qui non potest in loquendo coercere spiritum suum. Another version has, he who is not quick with his tongue. A city that lies open and is not compassed with walls, a man who cannot restrain his own spirit in talking (Prov 25:28). Alia littera, et non est accusatio in lingua ejus; quia scilicet non est detractor et relator; vel quia verba sua non sunt accusabilia: Eph. 4: omnis sermo malus de ore vestro non procedat. Another version has, and there is no accusation on his tongue, since he is not a detractor and tattletale, or since his words are not blameworthy. Let no evil speech proceed from your mouth (Eph 4:29). 105. Nec fecit. Supra egit psalmista de virtuosa operatione quam Deus acceptat, connumerando ea per quae homo bene operatur in se; hic autem connumerat ea per quae se bene habet ad proximum. 105. Nor has he done evil. Above, the psalmist treated the virtuous action which God accepts, listing those things through which man acts well in regard to himself. Now here he lists those by which he acts well in regard to his neighbor. Et hic quantum ad proximum petit tria. And here, he asks three things in regard to his neighbor. Primo, ut ei non noceat. First, that he do him no harm; Secundo, quod non consentiat nocenti: Rom. 1: qui talia agunt, regnum Dei non consequentur; sed digni sunt morte non solum qui faciunt ea, sed etiam qui consentiunt facientibus. second, that he does not consent to his harm: who did such things, not following the kingdom of God; but not only those who do them are worthy of death, but also those who consent to those who do them (Rom 1:32); Tertio, quod non decipiat ipsum. third, that he not deceive him. Dicit ergo quantum ad primum, nec fecit proximo suo malum, nec corporaliter nec spiritualiter: Rom. 12: nulli malum pro malo reddentes: Gal. 6: dum tempus habemus, operemur bonum. Therefore, he says in regard to his neighbor, nor has he done evil to his neighbor, either physically or spiritually. To no man rendering evil for evil (Rom 12:17). While we have time, let us work good (Gal 6:10). Quantum ad secundum dicit, et opprobrium non accepit adversus proximos suos. Aliquis dicit aliquid contra aliud, sed non est sustinendum; et ideo dicit, opprobrium non accepit, tunc scilicet, quando ille qui audit verba detractoria contra eum, et ex his detestatur illum de quo dicuntur, vel etiam ipse dicit aliis: Eccl. 19: audisti verbum contra proximum tuum, commoriatur in te et cetera. Eccl. 28: sepi aurem tuam spinis, et noli audire linguam nequam. In regard to the second, he says, nor taken up a reproach against his neighbors. Someone may speak something against someone else, but it is should not be endured, and so he says, he does not accept a reproach, that is, when he hears detracting words spoken against someone, he spurns the one by whom they are said, or he speaks to others. Have you heard a word against your neighbor? Let it die in you (Sir 19:10). Hedge in your ears with thorns, and do not hear a wicked tongue (Sir 28:28). Hieronymus: si non est auditor, non est detractor. Bernardus: detrahere aut detrahentem audire, quid horum damnabilius sit, non facile dixero: Prov. 25: ventus Aquilo dissipat pluviam, et facies tristis linguam detrahentem: quia, ad litteram, detrahens cessat cum audiens contristatur. Jerome: if he is not a listener, he is not a detractor. Bernard: I cannot easily say which of these is more damnable, to detract or to listen to detraction. The north wind drives away rain, as a sad countenance does a backbiting tongue (Prov 25:23), since, according to the text, detraction stops when the one hearing is saddened. 106. Ad nihilum. Hic ostendit quod non despiciat. 106. In his sight the malignant is brought to nothing. Here he shows what he does not despise. In homine sunt duo: scilicet vitium et virtus. In man there are two things: vice and virtue. Vitium est despiciendum; et ideo dicit malignus, in quantum talis, ad nihilum est deductus, id est nihil reputatur; Vice should be despised, and so he says, the malignant, insofar as he is such a person, is brought to nothing, that is, accounted nothing. et hoc est bonum: primo ad tollendum aemulationem: quandoque enim aliquis malus exaltatur: Hier. 12: via impiorum prosperatur et cetera. Sed propter hoc nullo modo debet eum reputare magnum, sed debet eum despicere: 1 Mach. 2: gloria hominis peccatoris stercus et vermis est: hodie extollitur et cras non invenitur, quia conversus est in terram suam, et cogitatio ejus periit. This is good: first, to put a stop to the imitation that occurs when an evil man is exalted. The way of the wicked prospers (Jer 12:1), but no one should repute him as great for this, but should despise him. The glory of a sinful man is dung and worms; today he is lifted up and tomorrow he shall not be found (1 Macc 2:62–63). Vel aliquis magnus intendit nocere; sed ex quo est malignus, contemnas eum: quia talium derogatio est nostrae vitae adprobatio: Ps. 26: si consistant adversum me castra, non timebit cor meum, id est peccatores. Or it can be that some great man intends to do harm, but because he is malignant you despise him, since such disapproval is an approval of our life. If armies, that is, sinners, in camp should stand against me, my heart shall not fear (Ps 26:3). Sed virtuosum hominem reputa magnum; ideo dicit, timentes autem dominum glorificat: Eccl. 25: quam magnus est qui invenit sapientiam et scientiam, sed non est super timentem dominum. But consider the virtuous man to be great, and so he says, he glorifies those who fear the Lord. How great is he who finds wisdom and knowledge, but there is no one above him who fears the Lord (Sir 25:13). Glossa aliter exponit, ad nihilum deductus est in conspectu ejus malignus, id est diabolus vinctus est ab eo: 1 Joan. 2: vicistis malignum et cetera. Dominus glorificat timentes dominum, scilicet se. Sed prima expositio est magis litteralis. The gloss explains this in another way: the malignant will be brought to nothing in his sight, that is, by him the devil shall be conquered. You have overcome the wicked one (1 John 2:13). The Lord glorifies those who fear the Lord, that is, himself. But the first explanation is more literal. 107. Hic prohibet ne decipiat proximum. In tribus autem proximus decipitur. 107. Here he prohibits deceiving one’s neighbor. Now a neighbor is deceived in three ways. Videlicet in promissis, et hoc per juramentum; et ideo dicit, qui jurat et cetera id est firmat ad decipiendum, quia non servat: Zach. 8: juramentum mendax non diligas: Levit. 19: non perjurabis in nomine Dei tui, et non pollues illud. Jurare non pertinet ad virtutem, sed juramentum servare. First, in promises, and this takes place through swearing, and so he says, he who swears, that is, declares so as to deceive, since he does not honor it. Love not a false oath (Zech 8:17). You shall not swear falsely in the name of your God, nor profane it (Lev 19:12). To swear does not belong to virtue, but to honor an oath does. Item in contractibus. Unde: qui pecuniam suam non dedit ad usuram: Luc. 6: mutuum date, nihil inde sperantes. Et munera super innocentem non accepit: Prov. 17: munera de sinu, id est Ecclesiae, impius accepit, ut pervertat semitas judicii Deut. 23, prohibetur, quod non detur fratri ad usuram, quia vendit quod non est, cum non habeat usumfructum. Second, in contracts, so he says, he who has not put out his money to usury. Lend, hoping for nothing thereby (Luke 6:35). Nor taken bribes against the innocent. The wicked man takes gifts out of his bosom, that is, the Church, that he may pervert the paths of the just (Prov 17:23). Deuteronomy 23 prohibits giving to one’s brother for usury, since he sells what he does not have, when he does not have the usufruct. Item in judiciis, quando dat sententiam contra innocentes; et ideo dicit, et munera: Isa. 5: vae qui justificatis impios propter munera: Job 15: ignis devorabit tabernacula eorum, qui munera libenter accipiunt. The third way is in judgments, when he gives sentence against the innocent, and so he says, woe to those who justify the wicked for gifts (Isa 5:23). Fire shall devour their tabernacles, who love to take bribes (Job 15:34). Consequenter ponitur praemium. Qui facit, id est servat, haec, omnia praedicta: Jac. 1: estote factores verbi. Rom. 2: non auditores legis justi sunt apud Deum, sed factores. Finally, he describes the reward. He who does, that is, upholds, these things, all that was said. Be doers of the word (Jas 1:22). Not the hearers of the law are justified before God, but the doers (Rom 2:13). Non commovebitur in aeternum id est hic habitabit in monte sancto meo: infra 124: qui confidunt in domino sicut mons Sion, et cetera. Ps. 54: non dabis in aeternum fluctuationem justo. He shall not be moved forever, that is, he will dwell here on my holy mountain. Those who trust in the Lord shall be as Mount Zion (Ps 124:1). He shall not suffer the just to waver forever (Ps 54:23). Super Psalmo 15 Psalm 15 Conserva me, Domine, quoniam speravi in te Preserve me, O Lord, for I have hoped in you 15:1 Tituli inscriptio, ipsi David. Conserva me, Domine, quoniam speravi in te. 15:2 Dixi Domino: ‘Deus meus es tu, quoniam bonorum meorum non eges.’ 15:1 The inscription of a title to David himself. Preserve me, O Lord, for I have hoped in you. 15:2 I have said to the Lord, “You are my God, for you have no need of my goods.” 15:3 Sanctis qui sunt in terra ejus, mirificavit omnes voluntates meas in eis. 15:3 To the saints, who are in his land, he has made wonderful all my desires in them. 15:4a Multiplicatae sunt infirmitates eorum: postea acceleraverunt. 15:4a Their infirmities were multiplied; afterwards they made haste. 15:4b Non congregabo conventicula eorum de sanguinibus, nec memor ero nominum eorum per labia mea. 15:4b I will not gather together their meetings for blood offerings, nor will I be mindful of their names by my lips. 15:5 Dominus pars haereditatis meae, et calicis mei: tu es qui restitues haereditatem meam mihi. 15:6 Funes ceciderunt mihi in praeclaris; etenim haereditas mea praeclara est mihi. 15:5 The Lord is the portion of my inheritance and of my cup; it is you who will restore my inheritance to me. 15:6 The lines are fallen for me in goodly places, for my inheritance is goodly to me. 15:7 Benedicam Dominum qui tribuit mihi intellectum; insuper et usque ad noctem increpuerunt me renes mei. 15:8 Providebam Dominum in conspectu meo semper: quoniam a dextris est mihi, ne commovear. 15:9a Propter hoc laetatum est cor meum, et exsultavit lingua mea; 15:7 I will bless the Lord, who has given me understanding; moreover, my reins also have corrected me even until night. 15:8 I set the Lord always in my sight, for he is at my right hand, that I be not moved. 15:9a Therefore, my heart has been glad, and my tongue has rejoiced; 15:9b insuper et caro mea requiescet in spe.15:10 Quoniam non derelinques animam meam in inferno, nec dabis sanctum tuum videre corruptionem. 15:11 Notas mihi fecisti vias vitae; adimplebis me laetitia cum vultu tuo: delectationes in dextera tua usque in finem. 15:9a moreover, my flesh also shall rest in hope. 15:10 Because you will not leave my soul in hell; nor will you give your holy one to see corruption. 15:11 You have made known to me the ways of life, you shall fill me with joy with your countenance; at your right hand are delights even to the end. 108. In praecedenti psalmo enumeravit justitias quas Deus requirit ab homine; hic autem ostendit quomodo justitiam sequebatur. 108. In the preceding psalm, he listed the just qualities which God requires from man; here he shows how he follows justice. Titulus talis est: inscriptio tituli ipsius David. The title is the inscription of a title to David himself.
