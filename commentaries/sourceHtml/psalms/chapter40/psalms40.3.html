      tertio causam petitionis assignat. third, he assigns a cause to his petition. Dicit ergo, domine, ego dixi miserere mei; quasi dicat: non recurro ad justitiam, sed ad misericordiam, quia non in justitiis nostris est salus. Isa. 64: quasi pannus menstruatae omnes justitiae nostrae. Therefore, he says, I said, “Lord, be merciful to me,” as if to say, “I do not have recourse to justice, but to mercy, since our salvation is not in our own righteousness.” All our justices are as the rag of a menstrual woman (Isa 64:6). Sed in quo petit misericordiam? Ubi est praecipue miseria. Et hoc datur, scilicet misericordia, ubi est praecipua beatitudo: quia beatitudo non est in rebus corporalibus, quia corporalia ordinantur ad ultimam beatitudinem. But where does he seek mercy? Principally, where misery is. And this—mercy—is given where there is a particular blessedness, since blessedness is not in corporeal things, since corporeal things are ordered to ultimate beatitude. Ergo praecipua miseria non est in rebus corporalibus, sed in his quae habent in se contrariam ad beatitudinem dispositionem: sicut in adulteriis, furtis, homicidiis et aliis peccatis: et qui hujusmodi agit, regnum Dei non consequetur, ut Apostolus dicit Gal. 5, sed est damnatus: Prov. 14: miseros facit populos peccatum. Peccatum enim deordinavit corpus, et corpus deordinavit animam: Sap. 9: corpus quod corrumpitur, aggravat animam. Et haec sanitas habetur a Deo solo: Sap. 16: non herba neque malagma sanavit eos, sed sermo tuus domine. Therefore, misery is not principally in corporeal things, but in those things which have in them a disposition which is contrary to beatitude, such as adultery, theft, homicide, and other sins, and he who does these things does not attain the kingdom of God, as the Apostle says in Galatians 5, but is damned. Sin makes nations miserable (Prov 14:34). For sin has disordered the body, and the body has disordered the soul. A corruptible body is a load upon the soul (Wis 9:15). And this healing comes from God alone. For it was neither herb, nor mollifying plaster that healed them, but your word, O Lord (Wis 16:12). Et ratio petitionis hujus est, quia peccavi tibi. And the reason for this petition is, for I have sinned against you. Sed numquid peccata merentur misericordiam? Non: But surely sins do not merit mercy? No. hoc ergo continuatur sic ut scilicet ly quia designet materiam misericordiae, non meritum. Nulla miseria est, ubi non sit locus misericordiae. Et ideo quia sum miser, peto misericordiam. Therefore the continuation with for designates the matter for mercy, not what merits it. There is no misery where there is not a place for mercy. And so, since I am miserable, I ask for mercy. Vel ut ly quia denotet causam meritoriam: et sic quia confessio peccati est meritum misericordiae, ideo dicit, peto misericordiam, quia confiteor quod peccavi tibi: Prov. 28: qui abscondit et cetera. Qui autem confessus fuerit, misericordiam consequetur. Or for denotes the cause of meriting, so, since a confession of sin merits mercy, he says, I ask for mercy, since I confess that I have sinned against you. He who hides his sins shall not prosper but he who shall confess . . . shall obtain mercy (Prov 28:13). Vel aliter, quia peccavi tibi; quasi dicat: quare peto misericordiam? Quia tu solus es qui potes sanare: quia in te facta est injuria et peccatum, ideo ad te pertinet remissio. Et in omni peccato offenditur Deus. Or it can be understood otherwise: I have sinned against you is as if to say, “How do I ask for mercy?” Since you alone are the one who can heal, and since wrongdoing and sin are against you, forgiveness belongs to you. And in every sin, it is God who is offended. Et cantatur hic versus in tertia, quia fide et operatione Trinitatis remittitur peccatum. And this verse is sung third, because sin is forgiven through faith and the work of the Trinity. 426. Inimici mei dixerunt. Supra psalmista petiit misericordiam sanantem; hic autem ponit necessitatem hujusmodi petitionis, quae causatur ab hostibus: et secundum mysticum sensum, quia litteralis est planus, in loco isto dicuntur ista verba ex persona Christi. 426. My enemies have spoken. Above, the psalmist asked for healing mercy; here he explains the need for this sort of petition: it is made necessary by his enemies. And according to the mystical sense, since the literal sense is plain, these words in this place are said in the person of Christ. Circa quod tria facit. Regarding this, he makes three points. Primo ponit affectionem malorum volentium malum ejus. First, he puts down the desire of the wicked who wish evil for him. Secundo ponit studium eorum insidiantium, ibi, et si ingrediebatur. Second, he puts down the eagerness of those plotting against him, at and if he came in to see me. Tertio ponit consilium tractantium, ibi, adversum me, quia inimicus. Third, he puts down the counsel of those discussing him, at they whispered together against me, because I am an enemy. Primo desiderat offendere. First, my enemy desires to offend. Secundo insidiatur. Second, he plots. Tertio cogitat quomodo perficiatur. Third, he considers how to accomplish it. Dicit ergo, inimici mei dixerunt, in corde, vel in ore, mala mihi: Matth. 12: malus homo de malo thesauro profert et cetera et haec verba mala sunt exprimentia desiderium eorum de morte Christi, vel viri justi. Therefore, he says, my enemies have spoken, in their heart, or in word, evils against me. An evil man out of an evil treasure brings forth evil things (Matt 12:35). And these evil words express their desire for the death of Christ or of the just man. Unde dicitur, quando morietur. Hic est modus expressivus desiderii: Ps. 41: quando veniam et apparebo et cetera. Sap. 2: morte turpissima condemnemus eum. Et volebant mortem Christi, ut fama ejus, quae tunc erat celebris, extingueretur: Hier. 11: eradamus eum de terra viventium, et nomen ejus non memoretur amplius. So it is said, when shall he die? This way of speaking expresses desire. When shall I come and appear? (Ps 41:3). Let us condemn him to a most shameful death (Wis 2:20). And they desired the death of Christ, so that his reputation, which was then celebrated, would be extinguished. Let us cut him off from the land of the living, and let his name be remembered no more (Jer 11:19). Unde dicunt, et peribit nomen ejus. Sed contrarium accidit: quia per mortem magis exaltatus est, et plures post mortem Christi conversi sunt: Joan. 12: si granum frumenti cadens in terram mortuum fuerit, multum fructum affert: etsi mali insidiantur bonis, ut extinguatur nomen eorum, sed, ut dicitur Prov. 10: memoria justi cum laudibus. Et hoc peccatores procurant insidiando. So they say, and his name shall perish. But the opposite came about, since through his death he was greatly exalted, and many were converted after the death of Christ. If a grain of wheat falling into the ground die . . . it brings forth much fruit (Job 12:24–25). Even though the evil lie in wait for the good to extinguish their name, nevertheless, as is said, the memory of the just is with praises (Prov 10:7). And sinners bring this about by plotting. Sic ergo ponit primo insidiandi modum. Therefore, he describes first their manner of plotting. Secundo ostendit insidiarum effectum, ibi, ingrediebatur. Second, he shows the effect of their traps, at he came to see me. Circa primum tria facit. Regarding this, he makes three points. Primo enim proponit dolosum ingressum. First, he puts forward their deceitful entry; Secundo fictum verbum. second, the lying word; Tertio insidiosum animum. third, the deceitful soul. Dicit ergo, si ingrediebatur ut videret. Hieronymus habet, ut visitaret; quasi dicat, non veniebant causa amicitiae, sed causa explorandi. Sic veniebant principes Judaeorum ut caperent Jesum. Sic etiam post mortem Christi multi ingressi sunt Ecclesiam, ut caperent et insidiarentur sanctis viris. Hoc est quod Apostolus dicit Gal. 2: propter subintroductos falsos fratres, qui subintroierunt explorare libertatem nostram. Therefore, he says, if he came in to see me. Jerome has, to visit, as if to say, “They did not come because of friendship but to sound me out.” This is the way in which the leaders of the Jews came to ensnare Jesus. In this way also they entered the Church after Christ’s death to trap and ensnare the holy men. This is what the Apostle means when he says, because of false brethren unawares brought in, who come in privately to spy on our liberty (Gal 2:4). Quantum ad secundum dicit, vana loquebatur, id est falsa loquebatur populus Judaeorum contra Christum, quia ore verba dulcia simulabat: Matth. 22: magister, scimus quia verax es: et interius moliebatur mortem: Ps. 11: vana locuti sunt unusquisque ad proximum suum, labia dolosa in corde et corde locuti sunt. In regard to the second, he says, he spoke vain things, that is the people of the Jews spoke falsely against Christ, since they gave the pretense of sweet words with the mouth: master, we know that you are a true speaker (Matt 22:16). They have spoken vain things, everyone to his neighbor, with deceitful lips and a double heart they have spoken (Ps 11:3). Quantum ad tertium dicit, cor ejus congregavit iniquitatem sibi. Animus insidiosus est ille qui congregat verba ex quibus virum justum offendat. Isti enim non faciunt sicut apes quae congregant mel; sed sicut scarabaei qui congregant stercus. Sed boni congregant mel, quia de divina dulcedine alios dulcorant. Et ideo dicit, cor ejus congregavit iniquitatem sibi. In regard to the third, he says, his heart gathered together iniquity to itself. A deceitful soul is one who gathers words by which he may hurt the just man. For these do not act like the bees which gather honey, but like the scarabs which gather dung. But the good gather honey, because they sweeten others with the divine sweetness. And so he says, his heart gathered together iniquity to itself. Et dicit, sibi, id est contra se: Prov. 1: ipsi contra sanguinem suum insidiantur. And he says, to itself, that is, against itself. They themselves lie in wait for their own blood (Prov 1:18). Vel, sibi, id est ad suum desiderium consequendum: quia si eum in aliquo malo comprehenderent, multum gauderent: Eccl. 18: si praestes animae tuae concupiscentias ejus, faciet te in gaudium inimicis tuis. Or, to itself means to accomplish its own desires, since if they catch him in some evil, they rejoice greatly. If you give to your soul her desires, she will make you a joy to your enemies (Sir 18:31). Sic dicebant contra Christum, Matth. 27: recordati sumus quia seductor ille et cetera. This is how they spoke against Christ: we have remembered that that seducer said, . . . “In three days I will rise again” (Matt 27:63). Consequenter ostenditur insidiarum effectus, quia egredientes detrahebant; unde dicit, egrediebatur foras, et loquebatur in idipsum. Afterwards, he describes the effect of their plots, since they went out to slander. So he says, he went out and spoke to that same purpose. Egrediebatur, vel a simulatione, vel a malitia, vel a familiaritate, vel ab amicitia: quia ante erat et videbatur quasi amicus, et sic loquebatur in idipsum, id est falsum, sicut prius et falsitatis verba. He went out, either by lying, or by wickedness, or by familiarity, or from friendship, since beforehand he was, and seemed to be, a friend, and spoke to that same purpose, that is, falsely, since earlier he was false in words. Vel egrediebatur foras a sinu veritatis, vel ab amicitia quam simulabat, vel ab Ecclesia, vel societate Christi: Joan. 6: multi discipulorum abierunt retro. Or he went out from the bosom of truth, or from the friendship which he pretended, or from the Church, or from the society of Christ. Many of his disciples went back (John 6:67).
