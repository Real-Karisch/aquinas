      Vel egrediebatur foras a sinu veritatis, vel ab amicitia quam simulabat, vel ab Ecclesia, vel societate Christi: Joan. 6: multi discipulorum abierunt retro. Or he went out from the bosom of truth, or from the friendship which he pretended, or from the Church, or from the society of Christ. Many of his disciples went back (John 6:67). 427. Adversum. Hic ostendit malum consilium eorum, quomodo noceant. 427. Against. Here he shows their evil plan to harm. Et primo ponit consilium inimicorum. And first, he puts down the counsel of his enemies. Secundo ostendit quod in hoc etiam convenerunt aliqui amici boni, ibi, homo pacis. Second, he shows that even some good friends gathered together in this, at the man of peace. Et primo ponit ordinem consiliandi, And first, he shows the order of their taking counsel, ostendens quod primo conveniunt consiliantes. showing that first they gather together to discuss, Secundo discutiunt de quo consiliant. second, argue about what they are discussing, Tertio determinant quid sit faciendum. third, determine what to do. Et haec tria describit hic. Dicit ergo, omnes inimici mei subsannabant adversum me, glossa, egrediebatur. Et post sequitur in idipsum et cetera. Et hoc in littera Hieronymi habetur, tamen non est vis. And he describes these three things here. Therefore, he says, all my enemies whispered together against me. The gloss has, they went out. And then: all together. And this is in Jerome’s text, although the force is not the same. Et dicit, subsannabant, id est silenter loquebantur; et hic est modus specialiter in consiliariis, et maxime de malis consiliis et malis hominibus, qui in occulto hoc faciunt: Lev. 19: non eris criminator nec susurro in populis: Eccl. 28: susurro et bilinguis maledictus et cetera. And he says, they whispered, that is, they spoke quietly, and this manner is particularly used in councils, and especially in evil counsels and by evil men who act in secret. You shall not be a detractor nor a whisperer among the people (Lev 19:16). The whisperer and the double tongued is accursed (Sir 28:15). Et dicit, adversum me. Et ita non sunt bona consilia, sed mala: Ps. 35: iniquitatem meditatus est in cubili suo. And he says, against me. And so they are not good plans but evil. He has devised iniquity on his bed (Ps 35:5). Et ideo cogitabant mala mihi: Ps. 1: beatus vir qui non abiit et cetera. And so, they devised evils to me. As Psalm 1:1 says, blessed is the man who has not walked in the counsel of the ungodly. Verbum iniquum constituerunt adversum me, id est injustum. Hoc ergo determinaverunt in eorum concilio: Joan. 11: expedit vobis ut unus homo moriatur pro populo. Et ex illa hora cogitaverunt et cetera. Matth. 21: hic est haeres, venite occidamus eum: Sap. 2: morte turpissima condemnemus eum. Hoc verbum iniquum habetur Hier. 18: tu scis, domine, consilium eorum adversum me in malum et mortem. They determined against me an unjust word, that is, one that is not fair. They determined this in their council. It is expedient for you that one man should die for the people; . . . and from that hour they devised to put him to death (John 11:50, 53). This is the heir; come, let us kill him (Matt 21:38). Let us condemn him to a most shameful death (Wis 2:20). This evil word is found in Jeremiah: O Lord, you know all their counsel against me for evil unto death (Jer 18:23). Numquid qui dormit non adjiciet ut resurgat? Hic ponit rationem eorum pravae determinationis. Ly non, superfluit: quasi dicat, qui dormit, non resurget: quasi dicat, si occiderimus eum, non resurget: Sap. 2: non est qui agnitus sit reversus ab inferis. Et ideo resurrectionem credebant seductionem. Shall he who sleeps rise again no more? Here he gives the reason for their depraved determination. The no is superfluous, as if to say, “He who sleeps shall not rise.” As if to say, “If we kill him, he will not rise.” No man has been known to have returned from hell (Wis 2:1). And so they believed the Resurrection to be a deception. Vel aliter. Numquid qui dormit etc. ut ly non construatur cum ly adjiciet, et sit sensus, ut sint verba Ecclesiae eis insultantis; quasi dicat, vos constituistis eum occidere, sed frustra hoc fecistis, quia resurget; unde dicit, numquid qui dormit non adjiciet ut resurgat? Or it can be taken in another way, so that no is construed with rise, and the meaning is that these are the words of the Church insulting them, as if to say, “You intended to kill him, but what you have done is frustrated since he rose up.” So he says, shall he who sleeps rise again no more? Et dicit, dormit, quia etiam hoc dicitur Dan. 12: multi de his qui dormierunt in pulvere terrae evigilabunt: nam simpliciter mors Christi dormitio fuit, quia ipse se ipsum posuit in mortem, et mortuus est non per violentiam, sed per propriam voluntatem. Et ideo habuit potestatem sumendi animam virtute divinitatis: 2 Cor. 13: si mortuus est ex infirmitate, vivit tamen ex virtute Dei. And he says, he sleeps, because of Daniel 12:2: many of those who sleep in the dust of the earth shall awake. For Christ’s death was simply sleep, since he laid himself down in death, and he died not through violence but through his own will. And so he had the power of taking up his life by the power of divinity. Though he was crucified through weakness, yet he lives by the power of God (2 Cor 13:4). 428. Etenim homo pacis meae in quo speravi. Supra proposuit psalmista consilium inimicorum tractantium mortem Christi; hic autem introducit consilium amicorum. Et quia ipse Christus, Joan. 13, istud verbum introducit de Juda, ideo nos de Christo hic exponamus. 428. For even the man of peace in whom I trusted. Above, the psalmist discussed the plan of the enemies plotting the death of Christ; here he introduces the plan of his friends. And since Christ himself said this about Judas in John 13, we will explain this regarding Christ. Ubi duo facit. Here he makes two points. Primo describitur conditio ejus. First, his condition is described, Secundo culpa. second, his guilt. Conditio personae Judae describitur ex tribus, quae aggravant peccatum: quia amicus, quia familiaris, et quia beneficiatus. The condition of the person of Judas is described with three things that aggravate a sin, since he is a friend, a member of his household, and one who received a good. Dicit ergo quantum ad primum: etenim homo pacis meae. Dixit supra quod dormivit, id procurante proditore. Vel, verbum iniquum constituerunt adversum me inimici. Sed non est mirum, quia, homo pacis meae, quia Judas inter amicos reputabatur. Therefore, he says in regard to the first, even the man of peace. Above, he said that he slept, while the traitor was attending to this. Or enemies determined against me an unjust word. But this is not surprising since even the man of peace does this, since Judas was considered to be among his friends. Haec autem prophetia contigit facto, quia osculo prodidit Christum, quod est signum amicitiae et pacis. Unde dominus dixit Luc. 22: Juda, osculo tradis Filium hominis? Ps. 27: loquuntur pacem cum proximo suo et cetera. Hier. 20: audivi contumelias multorum, et terrorem in circuitu ab omnibus viris qui erant pacifici ei, et custodientes latus meum. Item familiaris erat; unde, in quo sperabam. Now this prophecy touches on the fact that he betrayed Christ with a kiss, which is a sign of friendship and peace. So the Lord said, Judas, do you betray the Son of Man with a kiss? (Luke 22:48). Who speak peace with their neighbor, but evils are in their hearts (Ps 27:3). I heard the reproaches of many, and terror on every side . . . from all the men who were my familiars and continued at my side (Jer 20:10). Likewise, he was a member of the household, so in whom I trusted. Sed numquid deceptus est Christus in spe sua? Non. Et ideo dicit, in quo sperabam, id est in quo videbar sperare, id est confidere, quia commiserat ei dispensationem rerum suarum. But surely Christ was not deceived in his trust? No. And so he says, in whom I trusted, that is, in whom I seemed to trust—that is, to confide—since he entrusted to him the dispensation of his things. Vel, in quo sperabam, id est talis conditionis: et ita me habebam ad eum quod debebam sperare in eo. Or it means that such was his position, and so my relation to him was such that I ought to have trusted in him. Sed aliquando credit quis sperare in eo quem credit amicum, et de quo deberet confidere, et tamen decipitur: Hier. 9: in omni fratre tuo non habeas fiduciam: Mich. 7: nolite credere amico, et nolite confidere in duce. But sometimes someone believes he should trust in someone whom he considers his friend and in whom he should confide, but nevertheless is deceived. Let him not trust in any brother of his (Jer 9:4). Believe not a friend and trust not a prince (Mich 7:5). Vel, in quo sperabam, in membris meis quae in Christo sperabant: Matth. 25: quod uni ex meis minimis fecistis, mihi fecistis. Or, in whom I trusted is describing Christ’s members, whom he trusted. As long as you did it to one of these my least brethren, you did it to me (Matt 25:40). Quantum ad tertium dicit, qui edebat panes meos, quia Christus cum signo panis eum designavit: Joan. 13: cui intinctum panem porrexero. Et licet Judas ederet panem Christi, tamen perrexit contra eum: Eccl. 29: pascet et potabit ingratos. In regard to the third, he says, who ate my bread, since Christ pointed him out with a token of bread. To whom I shall reach bread dipped (John 13:26). And although Judas ate Christ’s bread, he still acted against him: he shall feed and give drink to the unthankful (Sir 29:31). Vel, panes meos, id est doctrinam meam: Gen. 49: Aser pinguis panis ejus, et praebebit delicias regibus. Talis panis est panis Christi, qui est pinguis propter dulcedinem doctrinae: Ps. 118: quam dulcia et cetera. Or, my bread means my doctrine. Asher, his bread shall be fat and he shall yield dainties to kings (Gen 49:20). The bread of Christ is bread that is rich because of the sweetness of his teaching. How sweet are your words (Ps 118:103). Magnificavit super me supplantationem. Ecce peccatum. He has greatly supplanted me. Behold his sin. Hieronymus habet, levavit contra me calcaneum suum. Et loquitur ad similitudinem ejus qui vult aliquem omnino comprimere; quasi dicat: attentavit ut me totaliter contereret. Jerome has, he lifted his heel against me, using an image of someone who wants to oppress another completely, as if to say, “He tried to crush me completely.” Et diversitas translationum videtur processisse ex aequivoco: quia quod in altum levatur, magnificatur. And the different translations seem to be equivalent, since what is lifted on high is magnified. Vel, magnificavit super me supplantationem, id est fecit magnam supplantationem contra me, quia mortem intulit: Hier. 9: omnis supplantans supplantabitur: Amos 5: odio habuerunt corripientem in porta. Or he has greatly supplanted me means that he made a great usurpation against me, since he brought about my death. Every supplanter will supplant (Jer 9:4). They hated him who rebukes in the gate (Amos 5:10). 429. Tu autem. Christus jam exposuit in generali suam petitionem; hic autem ostendit in quo misericordiam petat; 429. But you, O Lord. Christ has already put forward his petition in general; now here he lays out in what he asks for mercy. unde dicit, ipsi conturbaverunt me, sed non restat nisi ut recurram ad Deum; et ideo dicit, tu autem, domine, miserere mei. So he says that they threw me into confusion, but I can only turn to God, and so he says, but you, O Lord, have mercy on me. Misericordia debet esse ubi est miseria; Christus autem factus est particeps miseriae nostrae, non quantum ad culpam, sed quantum ad poenalitatem; et praecipue quantum ad poenam mortis. Et ideo, miserere mei: Ps. 88: misericordiam meam non dispergam ab eo. Where there is misery there should be mercy. Now Christ has been made a sharer in our misery, not in regard to guilt, but in regard to punishment, and principally in regard to the punishment of death. And so he says, have mercy on me. My mercy I will not take away from him (Ps 88:34).
