      Et quia haec omnia imperfecte sunt in Ecclesia, subdit, quare tristis es anima mea? Hic adhibet remedium contra causam tristitiae. And since all these things exist imperfectly here in the Church, he adds, why are you sad, O my soul? Here he offers a remedy against the cause of sadness. Et primo removet affectum. And first, he sets aside the emotion. Secundo removet effectum ejus. Second, he sets aside its effect. Dicit ergo, quare tristis es anima mea? Cum debeas gaudere, quia es in tabernaculo, et quia ibo ad domum domini. Quare ergo es tristis? Nam parva mala non sunt reputanda in consideratione ad bona aeterna: Eccl. 30: tristitiam, scilicet saeculi, longe expelle a te, 2 Cor. 7: tristitia saeculi mortem operatur. Effectus tristitiae est turbatio, quia ex inordinatione affectus ipsa ratio conturbatur. Therefore, he says, why are you sad, O my soul, when you should be rejoicing, since you are in the tabernacle, and since I shall go to the house of the Lord. Why, therefore, are you sad? For small evils should not even be noticed when considering eternal goods. Drive away sadness far from you (Sir 30:24). The sorrow of the world works death (2 Cor 7:10). The effect of sorrow is disturbance, since the reason is disturbed by disorder of the emotions. Et quis loquitur? Anima secundum hunc sensum. Anima habet duas partes: scilicet sensitivam, quam nominat hic animam propter animalitatem; et superiorem rationem, quae nominat se ipsam; quia unusquisque maxime est quod melius in eo est: et ideo dicit ratio superior inferiori: quare conturbas me. Et quae est causa? And who is speaking? The soul, from its sensitive part. The soul has two parts, namely, the sensitive, which here is called the soul because of its animating nature, and the higher reason, which is called itself, because whatever is better in a thing is called the best. And so higher reason speaks to the lower, why do you trouble me? And what is the cause? Ostendit autem quod non debet esse tristis, quia est spes futurae confessionis: et ideo dicit, spera in domino, quoniam adhuc confitebor illi; quasi dicat: spera in ipso Deo, quia adhuc venies ad hoc quod desideras, quia scilicet, adhuc confitebor illi, id est adhuc spero vultum ejus: vel in via, vel in patria, quod maxime est materia gaudii. Jac. ult. tristatur quis in vobis? Oret. Aequo animo est? Psallat et cetera. Now he shows that he should not be sad, since there is hope of future praise, and so he says, hope in God, for I will still give praise to him, as if to say, “Hope in God himself, since you will still come to what you desire,” because I will still give praise to him, that is, I still trust in seeing his face, either on the way, or in the fatherland, which latter is the principal matter for joy. Is any of you sad? Let him pray. Is he cheerful in mind? Let him sing (Jas 5:13). Salutare vultus mei. Hic ly salutare secundum glossam est nominativi casus, et est sensus: ego dico quoniam ego confitebor illi. Et cui illi? The salvation of my countenance. Here, salvation is in the nominative case according to the gloss, and this is the meaning: “I say that I will praise him.” And who is he whom I will praise? Scilicet Christo, in quo sunt duae naturae: humana scilicet qua est similis matri; et ideo dicit, salutare vultus mei, id est vultum meum gerens. Luc. 2: viderunt oculi mei salutare tuum et cetera. Gen. Pen.: salutare tuum expectabo domine. Item natura divina; et quantum ad hoc est Deus meus: ideo dicit, Deus meus. Rom. 9: ex quibus est Christus secundum carnem, qui est super omnia Deus benedictus in saecula. Christ, in whom there are two natures, the human, in which he is like his mother, and so he says, the salvation of my countenance, that is, bearing my countenance. My eyes have seen your salvation (Luke 2:30). I will look for your salvation, O Lord (Gen 49:18). Christ also has the divine nature, and, in this, he is my God, so he says, my God. Of whom is Christ, according to the flesh, who is over all things, God blessed for ever (Rom 9:5). Vel secundum Hieronymum, ly salutare est accusativi casus: quasi dicat, confitebor ei salutare, id est salvationem qua me salvavit. Or, according to Jerome, salvation is in the accusative case, as if to say, “I will confess unto him salvation,” that is, the salvation by which he saved me. Et quod sequitur, Deus meus, conjungitur cum sequentibus. And what follows, my God, is joined to what comes afterward. Consequenter adhibet remedium, scilicet divini auxilii. Next, he gives the remedy, divine help. Ubi primo proponit remedium. He first proposes the remedy; Secundo exponit. second, he explains it. Dicit ergo, ad me ipsum. Alia littera habet, a me ipso anima mea conturbata. Prima est melior; quasi dicat: quae est ratio quaero, quare anima conturbas me? Et haec turbatio venit, quia qualitercumque converto me ad me, invenio mihi tribulationem. Osee 13: perditio tua Israel ex te, tantummodo ex me auxilium tuum. Therefore, he says, within me. Another text has, my soul is troubled from me. The first is better: “I ask, what is the reason why, soul, you trouble me?” And this disturbance comes because wherever I turn within myself, I find trouble for myself. Destruction is your own, O Israel; your help is only in me (Hos 13:9). Et ideo Hieronymus habet, Deus meus in me ipso. Secundum illam litteram est sensus; quasi dicat: causa turbationis a me ipso est: quia si referamus ad humanum genus, corruptio sensualitatis a ratione venit, scilicet a sensu primi parentis. And so Jerome has, my God, within me. According to this text, the meaning is as if he were to say, “The cause of disturbance comes from myself,” since if we refer to the human race, the corruption of sensuality comes from reason, that is, from the sense of our first parent. Item homo quicumque peccando corrumpit naturam suam; et ideo post peccatum sensualitas magis resistit rationi. Again, man stains his nature by sinning, and so after sin, sensuality resists reason more. Et quia non est in me nisi causa turbationis, propterea memor ero tui, id est recurram ad te: Oseae ult.: memoriale ejus sicut vinum Libani. And since there is nothing in me which is not a cause of disturbance, I will remember you, that is, turn to you. His memorial shall be as the wine of Libanus (Hos 14:8). Et unde? De terra Jordanis et Hermoniim. Jordanis est fluvius in terra promissionis. Hermon est quidam mons propinquus Jordani. Et secundum historiam 2 Reg. 17, quando Absalon ivit in Jerusalem, et habuit consilium ut persequeretur David, qui erat citra Jordanem, et tunc Cusi impedivit; et ideo dicit, si essem in tanta tribulatione sicut tunc, contra, memor ero tui. And whence? From the land of Jordan and Hermon. Jordan is a river in the promised land. Hermon is a certain mountain near the Jordan. And according the history of 2 Samuel 17, when Absalom went to Jerusalem, he also took counsel to persecute David, who was near the Jordan, but Cusi prevented it, and so he says, if I am in any trouble like I was then, as a defense I will remember you. Tria tamen designantur hic quae sunt in Baptismo. Now, there are three things signified here which are in Baptism. Per Jordanem designatur descensus gratiae in Baptismum: Jac. 1: omne datum bonum et omne donum perfectum de sursum est. The Jordan signifies the descent of grace into Baptism: every best gift, and every perfect gift, is from above (Jas 1:17). Et de terra, quia per influxum gratiae terra cordis inde fecundatur. And from the land, since through the inpouring of grace the land of the heart is made fruitful. Hermon interpretatur anathema: et designat abrenuntiationem quam facimus Diabolo et pompis ejus in Baptismo: quia tunc quilibet anathematizat, id est separat se a Diabolo. Hermon means “anathema” and signifies the renunciation of the devil and his pomps which we make in baptism, since at that time one anathematizes such things, that is, one separates oneself from the devil. A monte modico, quia haec renuntiatio et gratia venit ab humili corde, quia humilibus Deus dat gratiam, Jac. ult. Et Diabolus est rex super omnes filios superbiae, Job 41. From the little hill, since this renunciation and grace come from a humble heart, since God grants grace to the humble (Jas 4:6). And the devil is king over all the children of pride (Job 41:25). 436. Abyssus abyssum. Exponit remedium quantum ad duo dicta: 436. Deep calls on deep. He lays out the remedy by saying two things, scilicet quantum ad turbationem quam habet in se, namely, in regard to the disturbance which he has in himself, et quantum ad memoriam de Deo, ibi, in die mandavit. and in regard to the remembrance of God, at in the daytime the Lord has commanded. Quantum ad primum proponit similitudinem et exponit causam. In regard to the first, he proposes an image and explains the cause. Abyssus in Scriptura signat multitudinem aquarum. Gen. 1: tenebrae erant super faciem abyssi, et spiritus domini et cetera. Contingit etiam quod aquae quando in multitudine generantur in aere, possunt vocari abyssus. Et in istis pluviis est quaedam circulatio: quia descendunt ad terram, et ex eis elevantur vapores qui generant alias aquas: et inter media sunt frequenter tonitrua, quae fiunt ex commotione nubium quae generantur a vaporibus et pluviis. Item contingit quod post tonitruum sequuntur fulgura. Ps. 134: fulgura in pluviam fecit. In Scripture, a deep signifies a great amount of water. Darkness was upon the face of the deep and the spirit of God moved over the waters (Gen 1:2). It also follows that when many waters are generated in the air, they can be called a deep. And in these rains there is a certain circulation, since they descend to the earth, and from them rise the vapors which generate other waters, and in their midst there is frequently thunder, which happens from the movement of clouds which are generated from vapors and rain. Again, it happens that lightning follows thunder. He has made lightnings for the rain (Ps 134:7). Dicit ergo, abyssus, id est multitudo aquarum, invocat abyssum, id est trahit ad se multitudinem aquarum aliarum: Therefore, he says, deep, that is, many waters, calls on deep, that is, it draws many other waters to itself. et hoc, in voce cataractarum tuarum, id est quaedam loca occulta sunt in quibus generantur aquae, et ex quibus descendunt. Unde cataractae, id est nubes, quae quasi cataractae quaedam sunt, ex quibus generantur. And this is at the noise of your flood-gates, that is, there are certain hidden places in which water is generated and from which it descends. So he says flood-gates to describe the clouds, which are in a certain way like flood-gates from which water comes forth. Et haec in Scriptura, scilicet abyssus et tempestas, important malum. Exo. 15: abyssi operuerunt eos: unde abyssus et cetera quasi dicat: una punitio Dei, abyssum invocat id est continuatur ad aliam; quasi dicat: judicium Dei, quo infert praesentia mala, invocat judicium quo futura mala infert. Psalm. 35: judicia tua abyssus multa. And in Scripture, these, namely, the deep and the storm, bring about evil. The depths have covered them (Exod 15:5), so deep calls on deep, as if to say, one punishment of God, the deep calls, that is, continues with, another, as if to say, “The judgment by which God imposes present evils calls to the judgment by which he imposes future evils.” Your judgments are a great deep (Ps 35:7). Et hoc sic, in voce cataractarum tuarum, id est per vocem Scripturarum tuarum, vel praedicatorum. Et hoc refertur ad hoc quod dicit, anima mea conturbata est. Quare? Quia abyssus: id est ex uno malo vel ex uno peccato sequitur magna poena. And so he says, at the noise of your flood-gates, that is, through the voice of your Scriptures, or of your preachers. And this refers to the earlier, my soul is troubled. Why? Because of the deep: a great punishment follows from an evil or from a sin. Vel aliter, propterea memor ero et cetera: ut per abyssus intelligatur sacra doctrina: et sic est sensus: abyssus, id est vetus testamentum vocat alium, id est novum testamentum. Et haec duo concorditer inducit ut memor sim tui, quia in utroque promittit homini auxilium Dei: et hoc, in voce cataractarum tuarum, id est praedicatorum et doctorum. Or, understood in another way: therefore will I remember, so that by the deep is understood sacred doctrine. And so this is the sense: deep, that is, the Old Testament, calls to another, that is, the New Testament. And these two together bring me to remember you, since in both he promises God’s help to man. And this happens at the noise of your flood-gates, that is, of the preachers and teachers. Vel aliter, propterea memor et cetera quia, abyssus: id est homo; qui vocatur abyssus. Quis scit hominis quae sunt hominis, nisi spiritus hominis qui in ipso est, 1 Cor. 2. Jer. 17. Secundum aliam litteram, profundum est cor hominis et inscrutabile. Ergo, abyssus, id est unus homo, invocat alium ad Christum. Et hoc fit non propria virtute, sed, in voce cataractarum tuarum; id est inspiratione spiritus sancti, ex qua habet efficaciam lingua praedicatoris. Or, taken in another way, it is man who is the deep: what man knows the things of a man, but the spirit of a man that is in him? (1 Cor 2:11). According to another text, the heart is deep beyond all things, and unsearchable (Jer 17:9). Therefore, the deep, that is, one man, calls another to Christ. And this happens not by his own power, but at the noise of your flood-gates, that is, by the inspiration of the Holy Spirit, from which the tongue of the preacher has its efficacy. Omnia excelsa tua. Supra psalmista posuit similitudinem suae tribulationis; hic autem ipsam similitudinem exponit. All your heights and your billows have passed over me. Above, the psalmist laid out an image of his trouble; here he explains that image. Supra dixit, abyssus abyssum invocat; et refertur ad signandum, quod non solum unam poenam passus est, sed multas: Above, he said, deep calls on deep, and it refers to what was signified—that not only one punishment was suffered, but many.
