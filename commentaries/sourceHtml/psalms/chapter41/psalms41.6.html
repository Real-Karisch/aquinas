      Vel, susceptor meus es, id est naturae meae. Isa. 42: ecce servus meus, suscipiam illum. Or, you are my support, that is, of my nature. Behold my servant; I will uphold him (Isa 42:1). Vel susceptor meus in Baptismo. Ps. 17: assumpsit me de aquis multis. Or, he is my support in Baptism. He received me out of many waters (Ps 17:17). Et si susceptor es, miror de malis quae patior. And if you are my support, I marvel at the evils I suffer. Et primo inquirit rationem de malis; And first, he asks the reason for the evils; secundo proponit quaedam quae sunt signa unius et causa alterius, ibi, dum affligit. second, he describes certain things which are signs of one thing and the cause of another, at while my enemy afflicts me. Ex malis duo considerat. And he considers two of the evils: Unum ex parte Dei: scilicet quod esset ejus oblitus. one on God’s part, namely, that he has forgotten him; Aliud ex parte sui, quia premebatur tristitia. another on his own part, since he is overwhelmed with sorrow. Ex parte Dei, quia permittebat eum affligi: et ideo dicit: quare oblitus es mei, quia olim sic suscepisti me. Isa. 49: dominus oblitus est mei. Sed in rei veritate non est oblitus The first is on the part of God, since he allows him to be afflicted, and so he says, why have you forgotten me, since formerly you supported me. The Lord has forgotten me (Isa 49:14). But actually he has not forgotten. ex parte sui qui affligebatur: unde dicit, et quare contristatus incedo, id est quae est causa qua das me tristitiae? Ps. 37: tota die contristatus ingrediebar, The second is on the part of him who is afflicted, so he says, and why go I mourning, that is, what is the reason for which you give me over to sorrow? I walked sorrowful all the day long (Ps 37:7). dum affligit me inimicus. Hic ostendit dolorem quem patitur ab adversariis: While my enemy afflicts me. Here he describes the sorrow which he suffers from his enemies. et assignat effectum persecutionis; quasi dicat: haec est causa quare contristatus incedo, quia affligor ab inimicis quandoque a temporalibus, quandoque a spiritualibus. Matth. 13: inimicus homo hoc fecit. And he proposes the effect of the persecution, as if to say, “This is the reason why I walk sorrowfully, since I am afflicted by enemies, sometimes by temporal and sometimes by spiritual ones.” An enemy has done this (Matt 13:28). Causa doloris seu effectus est, dum confringuntur ossa mea. Ossa Ecclesiae sunt fortes, ut praelati et viri perfecti. Et quandoque per adversarios temporales, quandoque per tentationes affliguntur. Ps. 21: dinumeraverunt omnia ossa mea. In quolibet homine quaelibet virtus est sicut quoddam os: et si contingat justum castum cadere, confringitur os ejus. The cause of sorrow, or its effect is, while my bones are broken. The bones of the Church are the strong, the prelates and holy men. And they are afflicted sometimes by temporal adversaries, sometimes by temptations. They have numbered all my bones (Ps 21:18). In any man, virtue is like a bone, and if it happens that the just and pure man should fall, his bone is broken. Exprobraverunt. Hic agit de causa tristitiae quam patitur ex verbis injuriosis: unde dicit, exprobraverunt mihi, scilicet verbis injuriosis. Jer. 20: factus sum in derisum tota die, omnes subsannaverunt me. My enemies have reproached me. Here he gives the cause of the sorrow which he suffers from injurious words, so he says, they have reproached me, namely, with injurious words. I am become a laughing-stock all the day, all scoff at me (Jer 20:7). Et hoc improperium est gravissimum. Tanto quis gravius fert improperium, quanto fit sibi de re de qua gloriatus confidit. Iste autem maxime gloriabatur de Deo: et ideo hoc opprobrium est sibi gravissimum. Et ideo inimici mei, dicunt mihi per singulos dies, ubi est Deus tuus? Ps. 12: ne quando dicat inimicus et cetera quare tristis es et cetera. And this derision is very serious. The weight of the derision that he bears will be in proportion to his relation to the thing in which, rejoicing, he trusted. Now he rejoiced the most about God, and so this derision is most serious to him. And so my enemies say to me day by day, “Where is your God?” As Psalm 12:5 says, lest at any time my enemy say: “I have prevailed against him.” Hic ostendit orationis effectum. Here he gives the effect of his prayer. Et oratio habet duplicem effectum. And the prayer has a twofold effect. Unus est expulsio tristitiae. One is the driving out of sadness. Alius est augmentum spei. Another is the increase of hope. Primus effectus est, quia per orationem ascendit mens hominis in Deum; et quia Deus est summe bonus; quando anima inhaeret ei, sentit delectationem maximam, et delectatio pellit tristitiam vel diminuit: et ideo dicit, quare tristis es et cetera ex quo oravi. Et hoc expositum est supra. The first effect occurs because, through prayer, the mind of man ascends to God, and, since God is the highest good, when the soul cleaves to him, it experiences the greatest delight, and delight drives out sadness, or lessens it, and so he says, why are you cast down, O my soul, since I prayed. And this is explained above. Alius effectus est, quia crescit in spe: quia si rex admittit aliquem ad familiare obsequium et colloquium, assumit fiduciam petendi et obtinendi. In oratione autem homo specialiter loquitur cum Deo. Ps. 27: in ipso speravit cor meum et cetera et ideo subdit, spera in Deo; et hoc expositum est supra. The other effect is that he grows in hope, since if a king admits someone to close service and conversation, he presumes the trustworthiness of the one asking and obtaining. Now, in prayer, man particularly speaks with God. In him has my heart confided (Ps 27:7). And so he adds, hope in God, and this was explained above. Judica me, Deus, et discerne causam meam de gente non sancta: ab homine iniquo et doloso erue me. 42:2 Quia tu es, Deus, fortitudo mea: quare me repulisti? Et quare tristis incedo, dum affligit me inimicus? 42:1 A psalm for David. Judge me, O God, and distinguish my cause from the nation that is not holy; deliver me from the unjust and deceitful man. 42:2 For you are God, my strength; why have you cast me off? and why do I go sorrowful while the enemy afflicts me? 42:3 Emitte lucem tuam et veritatem tuam: ipsa me deduxerunt, et adduxerunt in montem sanctum tuum, et in tabernacula tua. 42:4 Et introibo ad altare Dei, ad Deum qui laetificat juventutem meam. 42:5 Confitebor tibi in cithara, Deus, Deus meus. Quare tristis es, anima mea? Et quare conturbas me? 42:6 Spera in Deo, quoniam adhuc confitebor illi, salutare vultus mei, et Deus meus. 42:3 Send forth your light and your truth: they have conducted me, and brought me unto your holy hill, and into your tabernacles. 42:4 And I will go in to the altar of God, to God who gives joy to my youth. 42:5 To you, O God my God, I will give praise upon the harp. Why are you sad, O my soul? And why do you disquiet me? 42:6 Hope in God, for I will still give praise to him: the salvation of my countenance, and my God. 438. In praecedenti psalmo David narravit suum desiderium; nunc autem adhibet orationem ad implendum desiderium. 438. In the previous psalm, David described his desire. Now, he adds a prayer for the fulfillment of his desire. Et primo ponit orationem; And he gives the prayer first; secundo effectum ejus, ibi, quare tristis es. second, its effect, at why do I go sorrowful. Circa primum duo facit. In regard to the first, he does two things. Primo proponit orationem in generali; He proposes prayer in general first; secundo in speciali, ibi, ab homine iniquo. second, in particular, at deliver me from the unjust and deceitful man. Et primo petit judicium; And first he asks for judgment; secundo judicii effectum. second, for the effect of judgment. Petit ergo, judica me Deus. Therefore, he asks, judge me, O God. Sed videtur praesumptionis esse: quia ipse dicit, non intres in judicium et cetera. Ps. 142. But this petition seems to stem from presumption, because he himself says elsewhere, enter not into judgment (Ps 142:2). Respondeo. Dicendum, quod est duplex judicium: scilicet severitatis, et misericordiae seu aequitatis. I respond that there are two types of judgment: that of severity and that of mercy or equity.
