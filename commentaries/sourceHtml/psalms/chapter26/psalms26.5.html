      Primo petit exaudiri. First, he asks to be heard. Secundo proponit petitionem, ibi, tibi dixit cor meum. Second, he puts his petition forward, at my heart has said to you. Tertio ostendit fiduciam quam habet de exauditione, ibi, credo videre bona domini. Third, he shows the trust that he has in being heard, at I believe I will see the good things of the Lord. Ad hoc ergo quod exaudiatur, inducit duas rationes. Therefore, he introduces two reasons for being heard. Unam ex devotione propria. One is from his own devotion. Aliam ex sua miseria. Another is from his misery. Devotio est causa quod audiatur a Deo aliquis. Devotio est clamor cordis qui excitat Deum ad audiendum. Et ideo dicit, exaudi: quia clamavi non exterius, sed interius: Jac. 5: clamor eorum ad aures domini Sabaoth introivit. Devotion is the cause for anyone being heard by God. Devotion is a cry of the heart which brings God to hear. And so he says, hear, since I do not cry externally but interiorly. Their cry has entered into the ears of the Lord of Sabaoth (Jas 5:4). Item miseria nostra provocat ad exaudiendum: Exod. 3: videns vidi afflictionem populi mei, et descendi liberare eum; unde dicit, miserere mei, et exaudi me; quasi dicat, me miserum et meam miseriam cognosco, unde tuum est misereri: Judith 9: exaudi me miseram deprecantem. Likewise, our misery provokes him to hear. I have seen the affliction of my people and am come down to deliver them (Exod 3:7; Acts 7:34). So he says, have mercy on me and hear me, as if to say, “I know my miserable self and my misery, so it belongs to you to have mercy.” Hear me a poor wretch making supplication (Jdt 9:17). 240. Tibi. Hic ponit petitiones. 240. My heart has said to you. Here he gives his petitions. Et primo petit divinae faciei prospectum. First, he asks to see the divine face; Secundo divinum auxilium, ibi, adjutor meus es tu. second, for the divine help, at you are my helper; Tertio viae suae directivum, ibi, legem pone mihi domine. and third, for the direction of his way, at set me, O Lord, a way. Circa primum ostendit, quod de re petita habet magnum desiderium et intimum et anxium et assiduum. In regard to the divine face, he shows that he has a desire for the thing requested that is great, and intimate, and anxious, and assiduous. Intimum, quia tibi dixit cor meum. Aliquando homo aliquid petit ore, sed cor ejus ad alia versatur: Matth. 7: non omnis qui dicit mihi domine et cetera. Isa. 29: populus hic labiis me honorat et cetera. Sed quando petitio est ex intimo desiderio cordis, tunc est Deo accepta, sed tunc non os tantum, immo cor petit: 2 Regum 7: invenit servus tuus cor suum ut oraret te oratione hac: Psalm. 118: clamavi in toto corde. It is intimate, since my heart has said to you. Sometimes a man asks for something with his mouth, but his heart has turned away to something else. Not every one who says to me, “Lord, Lord,” shall enter (Matt 7:21). This people honors me with their lips but their heart is far from me (Isa 29:13, Matt 15:28). But when the petition comes from the intimate desire of the heart, then it is accepted by God, since then not only the mouth, but also the heart asks. Therefore has your servant found in his heart to pray this prayer to you (1 Sam 7:27). I cried with my whole heart (Ps 118:145). Anxium et aequum dicit habere cum dicit: exquisivit te et cetera. Contingit aliquando quod desiderium est intimum et quietum et non multum quaerit; sed quando est anxium, tunc vere quaerit: unde dicit, exquisivit te, id est frequenter et diligenter quaesivit. He is said to have anxiety and proportion when he says, I have sought you. Sometimes it happens that what is desired is intimate and quiet and not much sought, but when desire is anxious, then one truly seeks. So he says, I have sought you, that is, I have asked frequently and diligently. Et hoc etiam ostendit aequum desiderium: quia imago non perficitur nisi pertingat ad exemplar ad quod est facta; unde dicit, exquisivit te facies mea. Facies hominis interior est, in qua visus est interior, id est anima sive mens rationalis, et haec, scilicet facies mea quae est facta ad imaginem tuam, exquisivit te. Unde non potest reformari et perfici nisi jungatur tibi, domine. Unde sicut quaelibet res quaerit suam perfectionem, ita mens nostra quaerit Deum. This shows a proportionate desire, since the image is not perfected unless it attains to the exemplar according to which it was made. So he says, my face has sought you. The face of a man is interior in that sight is interior—that is, the soul or the rational mind—and this, namely, my face, which is made to your image, has sought you. So he cannot be reformed and perfected unless he is joined to you, Lord. So as everything seeks its own perfection, so our mind seeks God. Et ostendit quod sit assiduum, quia requiram, id est iterum et iterum quaeram: Isa. 21: si quaeritis, quaerite: Matth. 7: quaerite et invenietis. Hoc est proprium diligentis, quaerere saepe rem dilectam. Et quid quaerit, ostendit cum dicit, faciem tuam domine requiram. Hoc petebat Moyses Exo. 33: ostende mihi faciem tuam. Et dominus non statim ostendit, sed dixit, ostendam tibi omne bonum: Luc. 10: beati oculi qui vident quae vos videtis. Et ideo David non erat extra spem, sed adhuc quaerebat; unde alibi dicit, ostende nobis faciem tuam et cetera. Job 33: deprecabitur Deum suum, et placabilis erit, et, videbit faciem ejus in jubilo. And he shows that it is assiduous, since I will seek, that is, I will ask again and again. If you seek, seek (Isa 21:12). Seek and you shall find (Matt 7:7). It is proper for a lover to seek the beloved thing often. And he shows what he seeks when he says, your face, O Lord, will I seek. Moses asked for this. Show me your face (Exod 33:13). And the Lord did not immediately show him, but he said, I will show you all good (Exod 33:19). Blessed are the eyes that see the things that you see (Luke 10:23). And so David was not without hope, but still sought, so he says, in another place, show us your face (Ps 79:4). He shall pray to God, and he will be gracious to him, and he shall see his face with joy (Job 33:26). 241. Ne avertas. Hic proponit triplicem petitionem. 241. Turn not away your face from me. Here he proposes a threefold petition. Et primo petit non fraudari a re desiderata. First, he asks not to be cheated of the thing desired. Secundo petit amoveri causam per quam posset fraudari. Second, he asks that the cause through which he might be cheated be removed. Tertio petit dirigi in via, ibi, legem pone. Third, he asks to be directed in the way, at set me a law. Dicit ergo, faciem tuam, domine, requiram. Et rogo, ne avertas faciem tuam a me; quasi dicat, sicut avertit homo ab homine, quando non vult eum audire. Sed aliter est in Deo quam in homine. Homo enim avertens faciem mutatur. Ipse Deus autem immobilis est; sed dicitur avertere faciem, in quantum nos avertimur et immutamur. Therefore, he says, your face, O Lord, will I seek. And I ask, turn not away your face from me, as if to say, “As a man turns away from a man when he does not desire to hear him.” But it is otherwise in God than in man. For a man is changed by turning away his face. God himself is unmovable, but he is said to turn away his face insofar as we are turned away and changed. Et per hoc quod in corde nostro fit aliquod velamen quo inepti reddimur ad videndum faciem suam. Et ideo littera Hieronymi habet, ne abscondas: Isa. 8: expectabo dominum qui abscondit faciem suam a domo Jacob. And through this, understand that in our heart there is a veil by which we are made unable to see his face. And so the text of Jerome has, do not hide. I will wait for the Lord who has hid his face from the house of Jacob (Isa 8:17). Causa vero aversionis est ira Dei in poenam peccati. Et haec aversio est maxima poenarum; et hoc est quod dicit, et ne declines in ira a servo tuo, id est ne irascaris mihi in hoc quod declines faciem tuam a me. Now the cause of turning away is the wrath of God in punishment of sin. And this turning away is the greatest punishment, and this is what is said, turn not away in your wrath from your servant, that is, do not be angry with me so that you turn your face away from me. Et dicit, in ira: quia aliquando declinat in misericordia, cum scilicet non respicit peccata: Ps. 50: averte faciem tuam a peccatis meis. And he says, in your wrath, since sometimes he turns away in mercy, as when he does not regard sins. Turn away your face from my sins (Ps 50:11). Aliquando declinat in providentia, quando scilicet permittit aliquem cadere ut fortius resurgat, quia diligentibus Deum omnia cooperantur in bonum, Rom. 8. Sometimes he turns away in providence, when he allows someone to fall that he may rise up stronger, since to those who love God, all things work together unto good (Rom 8:28). 242. Adjutor. Hic petit divinum auxilium in agendis antequam veniat ad faciem, ne scilicet impediatur a visione faciei. 242. You are my helper. Here he asks for divine help in acting before he comes to his face, so that he may not be blocked from the vision of his face. Et primo ponit petitionem. First, he gives his petition; Secundo dictorum rationem, ibi, quoniam pater meus. second, the reason for speaking, at for my father and my mother have left me. Petit ergo divinum auxilium dicens, peto videre faciem tuam, sed ad hoc pervenire non possum per me: ergo, tu esto adjutor meus, ut ad hoc perveniam: Ps. 120: auxilium meum a domino. Therefore, he asks for divine help, saying, “I desire to see your face, but I cannot come to this on my own; therefore, be my helper, that I may reach it.” My help is from the Lord (Ps 120:2). Sed quantum ad superficiem non videtur ista littera recta esse, quia melius videtur dicendum esse, adjutor meus es tu, et sic habetur in Hebraico, scilicet auxilium meum fuisti. Et secundum hoc commemorat beneficium; quasi dicat, adjutor fuisti. Non ergo de cetero, derelinquas me. But in regard to the superficial wording, it does not seem that the text is correct, since it seems to me to be better to say, you are my helper, and it is so in the Hebrew, namely, you have been my helper. And according to this, he remembers favors, as if to say, “You have been my helper; therefore, in regard to these things, forsake me not.” Et petit duo removeri, scilicet ipsam desertionem et contemptum interiorem: nam si homo sibi derelinquitur, perit. Oseae 13: perditio tua, Israel, ex te. And he asks that two things be removed: namely, his desertion and interior contempt; for if a man is forsaken, he is destroyed. Destruction is your own, O Israel (Hos 13:9). Deserit autem aliquis aliquem, quia despicit eum. Et Deus despicit nos, quia sumus fragiles per naturam, et corrupti per culpam; et ideo dicit, neque despicias me Deus. Now, someone is deserted by another because he despised him. And God despises us, since we are fragile by nature and corrupt by guilt, and so he says, do not despise me, O God. Et quare hoc? Quia tu me creasti, et es, salutaris meus, id est tu me salvasti. Nullus autem despicit opera sua: Ps. 137: opera manuum tuarum ne despicias. And why? Since you created me, and you are my Savior, that is, you saved me. Now, no one despises his own works. Despise not the works of your hands (Ps 137:8). Consequenter ponitur ratio dictorum; unde sequitur, quoniam pater meus et mater mea dereliquerunt me, dominus autem assumpsit me; quasi dicat: quia inveni te adjutorem in omnibus aliis deficientibus, ne despicias me. Afterwards the reason for his speaking is put down, so it follows, for my father and my mother have left me, but the Lord has taken me up. As if to say, “Since I have found you to be a helper when all others have failed, do not despise me.” Et sic primo ponit defectum humani auxilii. And so he first describes the failure of human help.
