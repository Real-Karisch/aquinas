      Tertio ponit desiderii impletionem, ibi, exaudi domine. In the third, he describes the fulfillment of his desire, at hear, O Lord. Circa primum tria facit. He considers three aspects of the first. Primo commemorat beneficia sibi a Deo praestita, propter quae non timet, sed securus est. First, he remembers the favors God had supplied to him, on account of which he does not fear, but is secure. Secundo commemorat impedimenta parata hostibus a Deo, ibi, dum appropiant. Second, he remembers the obstacles prepared by God for his enemies, at while the wicked draw near. Tertio ostendit fiduciam quam habet a Deo, ibi, si consistant. Third, he shows the trust that he has in God, at if a camp should stand. Notandum autem, quod ad timendum concitatur quis aliquando ex interiori causa, quandoque ex exteriori causa. Now, it should be noted that sometimes someone is aroused to fear from an interior cause and sometimes from an exterior cause. Primo ergo ponit auxilium contra primam causam. Therefore, he first describes help against the first cause, Secundo contra secundam, ibi, dominus protector. and second, against the second: the Lord is the protector. Est autem duplex causa intrinseca timoris, ignorantia et debilitas: unde in tenebris magis timendum est. Now there are two interior causes for fear: ignorance and weakness. So there is greater fear in darkness. Secunda causa timoris est debilitas; The second cause of fear is weakness. et contra has est remedium a Deo. And there is a remedy given by God against these. Contra primum est illuminatio; unde dicit, dominus illuminatio mea: Mich. 7: cum sedero in tenebris, dominus lux mea est. Light is against the first, so he says, the Lord is my light. When I sit in darkness, the Lord is my light (Mic 7:8). Contra secundum est salus; unde sequitur, et salus mea: Psalm. 61: in Deo salutare meum et gloria mea, Deus auxilii mei, et spes mea in Deo est. Salvation is against the second, so it follows, and my salvation. In God is my salvation and my glory; he is the God of my help, and my hope is in God (Ps 61:8). Et ideo ostendit fiduciam: quem timebo, sic illuminatus? Isa. 51: quis es tu ut timeas ab homine mortali, et a filio hominis, qui quasi fenum sic arescet? Rom. 8: Deus qui justificat, quis est qui condemnet? Et si Deus pro nobis, quis contra nos? And so he shows his trust: whom shall I fear, so illuminated? Who are you that you should be afraid of a mortal man and of the son of man who shall wither away like grass? (Isa 51:12). It is God who justifies, who is he that shall condemn? . . . If God is for us, who is against us? (Rom 8:34, 31). Causa extrinseca est homo, qui adversatur; sed adhuc non est timendum, quia dominus opponit se sicut scutum; unde dicit, dominus protector vitae meae: Gen. 15: ego protector tuus et merces tua magna nimis. The external cause is the man who opposes, but he should no longer be feared, since the Lord acts against him like a shield. So he says, the Lord is the protector of my life. I am your protector and your reward exceedingly great (Gen 15:1). Et ideo dicit: a quo trepidabo. A quo, si sumatur masculine, tunc est sensus, a quo, scilicet a quo homine. Si neutraliter, a qua re. Et sic nihil est timendum, nec homo, nec res aliqua. And so he says, of whom shall I be afraid? If of whom is taken in the masculine, the sense is “by whom,” namely, by what man. If it is taken as neuter, it means, “by what thing.” And so nothing should be feared, neither man or any thing. Dum appropiant super me nocentes. Et quia posset dici quod Deus est illuminator etiam hostium, ideo hoc removens dicit quod Deus obsistit eis. While the wicked draw near against me. And since it can be said that God is the illuminator even of his enemies, he says here that God opposes them by removing this. Et primo dicit eorum conatum. First, he speaks about their attempt. Secundo ponit impedimentum eis superveniens, ibi, ipsi infirmati sunt et cetera. Second, he describes the obstacle coming upon them, at they themselves have been weakened. Circa primum tria facit. He makes three points about their attempt. Primo praemittit praesumptuosum insultum. First, he puts forward their presumptuous attack; Secundo perversum actum. second, their perverse act; Tertio malignum effectum. and third, their wicked effect. Quantum ad primum dicit, dum appropiant super me nocentes, id est habentes animum nocendi, super me, id est mihi se praeferentes: Thre. 1: facti sunt hostes ejus in capite, inimici ejus locupletati sunt, quia dominus locutus est super eam propter multitudinem iniquitatum ejus, parvuli ejus ducti sunt in captivitatem usque ante faciem et cetera. In regard to the insult, he says, while the wicked draw near against me, that is, having the intention of harming, against me, that is, displaying themselves to me. Her adversaries are become her lords; her enemies are enriched because the Lord has spoken against her for the multitude of her iniquities; her children are led into captivity before the face (Lam 1:5). Quantum ad secundum, ut scilicet graviter affligant, ut edant carnes meas, id est carnalem vitam: Prov. 1: deglutiamus eum, sicut infernus, viventem et integrum: Mich. 3: carnem populi comederunt, et pellem eorum desuper excoriaverunt. In regard to the action, he says that they come to afflict me more seriously, to eat my flesh, that is, my bodily life. Let us swallow him up alive like hell and whole (Prov 1:12). Who have eaten the flesh of my people, and have flayed their skin from off them (Mic 3:3). Vel ut ly ut teneatur consecutive: ut sit sensus: edant carnes, id est carnalitates meas: quia quando mali persequuntur bonos, aliud intendunt ipsi mali, sive ipsi persecutores, scilicet offensionem corporalem; et secundum hoc est prima expositio: aliud intendit Deus hoc permittens, scilicet purgationem ab omni carnalitate; et sic secunda expositio. Et hoc modo dicit Apostolus Gal. 5: qui Christi sunt, carnem suam crucifixerunt cum vitiis et concupiscentiis. Or, to should be understood as indicating a consequence, so that the sense is, they eat my flesh, that is, my fleshly desires, since when the evil persecute the good, the evil or persecutors intend something, that is, bodily harm, and the first explanation follows this. But God, in permitting this, intends something else, namely, to purify from all carnal desires, and this is the second explanation. And the Apostle speaks in this way. Those who are Christ’s have crucified their flesh, with the vices and concupiscences (Gal 5:24). Quantum ad tertium dicit, qui tribulant me inimici mei: Psalm. 12: qui tribulant me exultabunt si motus fuero. In regard to the effect, he says, my enemies who trouble me. Those who trouble me will rejoice when I am moved (Ps 12:5). Ipsi infirmati sunt, quia non valuerunt implere propositum, et ceciderunt, quia superati sunt, et absorpti: Hierem. 20: dominus mecum est tamquam bellator fortis; idcirco qui persequuntur me, cadent et infirmi erunt. They have been weakened, since they are not able to fulfill their intention; they have fallen, since they are overcome and swallowed up. But the Lord is with me as a strong warrior; therefore, those who persecute me shall fall, and shall be weak (Jer 20:11). 234. Si consistant. Homo debet habere securitatem in duobus. 234. If a camp should stand against me. A man should have security against two things: Primo in praeparatione malorum. first, against the schemes of the evil; Secundo in eorum passione, ibi, exurgam. second, against suffering from them, at if a battle should rise. Dicit ergo: dominus sic est illuminatio mea, quia inimici cadunt coram me. Therefore, he says, the Lord is my light, so that my enemies have fallen before me. Glossa. Si consistant adversum me castra, castra sunt ubi steterunt milites; non timebit cor meum. Quamdiu homo est in castris, non pugnat, sed disponit, et consiliat ad pugnandum. Per castra intelliguntur consilia et conjurationes malorum contra aliquem. 4 Reg. 19: angelus domini percussit castra Assyriorum: Exod. 14: factum est in vigilia matutina, et ecce ascendit dominus super castra, et percussit. Following the gloss: if a camp—camps are where soldiers rest—should stand against me, my heart shall not fear. While a man is in the camps, he does not fight, but plans and counsels fighting. Through a camp should be understood the counsels and plots of the wicked against someone. An angel of the Lord slew in the camp of the Assyrians (2 Kgs 19:35). The morning watch was come, and behold, the Lord ascends over the camp, and slew (Exod 14:24; 1 Sam 11). Non timebit cor meum, quia dominus mecum est: Job 17: pone me juxta te, et cujusvis manus pugnet contra me. My heart shall not fear, since the Lord is with me. Set me beside you and let any man’s hand fight against me (Job 17:3). Sed, si exurgat adversum me praelium, id est si jam invadant me, et praelientur contra me, quamvis sint multi: in hoc ego sperabo: quia ut dicitur 1 Mach. 3: non in multitudine exercitus victoria belli, sed de caelo fortitudo est. Est enim consuetudo amicorum juvare amicos, cum impugnantur ab hostibus: Ps. 93: consolationes tuae laetificaverunt animam meam. But if battle should rise against me, that is, if they attack me, and do battle against me, although they are many, in this will I be confident, since as is said, the success of war is not in the multitude of the army, but strength comes from heaven (1 Macc 3:19). For it is the custom of friends to help friends when they are attacked by enemies. Your comforts have given joy to my soul (Ps 93:19). 235. Unam petii. Supra psalmista posuit fiduciam ex oratione conceptam; hic autem ponit desiderium, quod ex hac fiducia oritur: et circa hoc duo facit. 235. One thing I have asked of the Lord. Above, the psalmist described the trust engendered by prayer; now here he describes his desire that arose from this trust, and he makes two points about this.
