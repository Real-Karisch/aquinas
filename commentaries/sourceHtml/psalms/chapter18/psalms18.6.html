      Quantum ad primum dicit ista quae dicta sunt: desiderabilia super aurum: Prov. 8: melior est sapientia cunctis opibus pretiosissimis, et omne desiderabile ei non potest comparari: Ps. 118: dilexi mandata tua super aurum et cetera. Et tangit illa duo quae homines desiderant; unde dicit, aurum; et pompas, unde lapidem pretiosum. Lapides pretiosi non serviunt nisi ad apparentiam: unde ibi multum potest referri, vel ad lapidem, vel ad pretiosum. In regard to the first, these things are said to be more to be desired than gold and many precious stones. Wisdom is better than all the most precious things and whatever may be desired cannot be compared to it (Prov 8:11). Therefore have I loved thy commandments above gold (Ps 118:127). And he touches on these two things which men desire, so he says, than gold, and pomps, so he says, precious stones. Precious stones have no use except appearance, so here, many can be applied either to the stones, or to their preciousness. Item quantum ad secundum dicit, et dulciora super mel et favum: Ps. 118: quam dulcia faucibus meis eloquia tua. Super mel ori meo. Dicitur mel et favum, quantum ad delectationes corporales. Mel extrahitur a cera; sed favus est mel et cera. Delectationes corporales quandoque sunt manifestae, et sic est mel; quandoque sunt absconditae et dulciores, et sic est favus: Prov. 9: aquae furtivae dulciores sunt et cetera. In regard to second, he says, and they are sweeter than honey and the honeycomb. How sweet are your words to my palate! More than honey to my mouth (Ps 118:103). He speaks of honey and the honeycomb as examples of bodily pleasures. Honey is extracted from the wax, but the comb is the honey and the wax. Bodily pleasures are sometimes manifest, as honey is; sometimes they are hidden and so sweeter, as the comb is. Stolen waters are sweeter, and hidden bread is more pleasant (Prov 9:17). Vel praeferuntur favo propter vetus testimonium, in quo est mel in favo, veritas in figura: melli propter novum ubi est veritas manifesta. Or, by the honeycomb, the Old Testament is understood, in which the truth is found in the figures just as honey is in the comb. By honey is understood the New Testament, where the truth is manifest. Sed numquid spirituales delectationes sunt delectabiliores? Dicendum est quod sic: et est triplex ratio. But is it really possible that spiritual pleasures are more delightful? It must be said that they are, and there are three reasons why. Una ex parte boni delectati quod est potius bonum, et ex causa delectationis, quod est majus bonum, ergo magis delectabile. The first is because of the delightful good, which is a greater good, and therefore more delightful. Secunda ex parte potentiae delectantis, quia vis intellectiva est fortior sensitiva. The second is because of the power of delighting, since the intellectual power is stronger than the sensitive. Tertia ex modo delectationum. Corporales delectationes consistunt in fieri et in motu; sicut in cibis et in aliis. Motus autem est quid imperfectum; et quoddam futurum et praeteritum importat; quia non habetur totum simul. Spirituales autem delectationes non sunt in motu: quia consistunt in amando et intelligendo bonum, quod non est in motu; The third is because of the way of delighting. Bodily delights consist in becoming and in change, as happens with food and other things. Now, change belongs to things that are imperfect, and indeed implies the future and the past, since the whole is not possessed at the same time. Spiritual delights are not in motion, since they consist in loving and knowing the good, which does not change. sed per accidens plus desiderantur illae, in quantum abundant aliqui in sensu, et deficiunt in intellectu. Now, the others may be desired more accidentally, because some people have strong senses but are lacking in intellect. Vel sancti homines plus diligunt praecepta Dei quam se ipsos. Et sancti designantur per aurum et lapidem pretiosum: 1 Cor. 3: si quis aedificaverit super fundamentum hoc aurum, argentum, lapides pretiosos. Item per mel et favum significantur illi qui alieni sunt a rebus mundi; sed favus sunt illi qui adhuc rebus mundi delectantur: et tales, quia non sunt totaliter alieni, diligunt plus se quam praecepta; alii diligunt plus praecepta quam se. Or, the text means that holy men desire the precepts of God more than themselves. And the saints are signified by gold and precious stones. Now if any man build upon this foundation, gold, silver, precious stones (1 Cor 3:12). Then honey and the honeycomb signify those who are far from the things of the world, but those are the comb who still delight in the things of the world, and these, since they are not entirely distant, love themselves more than the precepts, but the others love the precepts more than themselves. Et hoc probat dupliciter. And this is shown in two ways: Primo experimento. first, by experience; Secundo ex effectu. and second, from the effect. Experimento, cum dicit, etenim servus tuus custodit ea; quasi dicat: possum dicere quia dulcia sunt, quia probavi ea: nam ego diligo et experior ea. Nullus autem testimonium potest ferre nisi qui experitur: Joan. 7: si quis voluerit voluntatem ejus facere, cognoscet de doctrina mea utrum ex Deo sit, an ego a me ipso loquar: Job 23: vestigia ejus secutus est pes meus. It is known by experience, when he says, for your servant keeps them, as if to say, “I am able to say that they are sweet, since I have tested them, for I love and experience them.” Now, there can be no testimony except from one who has experience. If any man do his will, he shall know of my doctrine, whether it is of God, or whether I speak of myself (John 7:17). My foot has followed his steps (Job 23:11). Ex effectu, id est remuneratione probat idem, cum dicit, in custodiendis illis retributio multa: Matth. 5: gaudete et exultate, quia merces vestra copiosa est in caelis. From the effect—that is, from the reward—he proves the same thing, where he says, in keeping them there is a great reward. Be glad and rejoice, for your reward is very great in heaven (Matt 5:12). Et non dicit pro custodia; sed, in custodiendis illis, quia ipsa custodia eorum est magna retributio, scilicet gloria cordis et munditia: 2 Cor. 1: gloria nostra haec est, testimonium conscientiae nostrae. Et sic commendatur lex ab honestate, quando dicit, in custodiendis illis et cetera quia honestum idem est quod virtus; et est per se appetibile. And he does not say, for keeping them, but in keeping them, since the very keeping is itself the great reward, namely, glory and cleanness of heart. For our glory is this, the testimony of our conscience (2 Cor 1:12). And so the law is praised for its honorableness when he says, in keeping them there is a great reward, since the honorable is the same as virtue, and it is desirable for its own sake. 157. Delicta. Hic orat pro delictis illorum qui praetereunt legem. 157. Who can understand sins? Here he prays for the sins of those who disregarded the law. Et primo proponit occultationem peccatorum. First, he discusses hidden sins. Secundo petit eorum remissionem, ibi, ab occultis. Second, he asks for their remission, at from my secret ones cleanse me, O Lord. Tertio ponit rationem petitionis, ibi, si mei et cetera. Third, he gives the reason for his request, at if they shall have no dominion over me. Dicit ergo: legem frequenter offendimus, sed, delicta quis intelligit? Quasi dicat, nullus. Therefore, he says we frequently sin against the law, but who knows his sins? As if to say, “No one.” Et hoc propter tria. There are three things to be said about hidden sins. Primo, quia delictum excaecat oculos peccantis, unde non de facili discernuntur parva peccata: Sap. 2: excaecavit eos malitia eorum. First is that sin blinds the eyes of sinners, so they cannot easily recognize light sins. Their own malice blinded them (Wis 2:21). Secundo, quia peccata sunt multa: Ps. 39: circumdederunt me mala quorum non est numerus; comprehenderunt me iniquitates meae, et non potui ut viderem. Second is that there are many sins. Evils without number have surrounded me; my iniquities have overtaken me, and I was not able to see (Ps 39:13). Item propter eorum subtilitatem: Ps. 39: multiplicati sunt super capillos capitis mei, ex subtilitate: sed non dicit peccata, sed, delicta. Also, they are subtle. They are multiplied above the hairs of my head (Ps 39:13), because of their subtlety, but they are not called sins but delicts. Duplex est peccatum: Sin is twofold. scilicet transgressionis: et hoc facilius cognoscitur, quia homo scit se fecisse actum pravum. There is a sin of transgression, and it is more easily known, since man knows when he has acted evilly. Item omissionis: et hoc difficile cognoscitur, quia haec non obligant ad semper, sed pro loco et tempore. Et distingue. Quando est locus et tempus, est difficile affirmare: et quando danda est eleemosyna, qualiter et cetera et ubi. There is also that of omission, and this is difficult to recognize since obligations do not always bind a person but only do so in a particular time and place. Consider: it is difficult to affirm when the time and place is and when alms should be given and how and where. Consequenter ponit peccatorum remissionem. Est autem duplex genus peccati. Afterwards, he discusses the remission of sins. And there are two kinds of sin. Unum quod sumit initium a nobis; et hoc est praecipue originale, et quae ex corruptione fomitis procedunt, ut peccata carnalia; et haec animam inquinant, quia conjungunt eam rebus terrenis. Et ideo dicit, ab occultis meis munda me, id est quae ab occulta radice procedunt, vel quae in occulto fiunt, vel quae a secreta voluntate oriuntur: Eph. 5: quae in occulto fiunt ab ipsis, turpe est et dicere. One is that which we have received from the beginning, and this is chiefly original sin and that which comes from the corruption of the fomes, such as carnal sins, and these dirty the soul since they join it to earthly things. And so he says, from my secret ones cleanse me, O Lord, that is, from those things that come forth from this hidden root, or which are done in secret, or which arise from a secret desire. The things that are done by them in secret, it is a shame even to speak of (Eph 5:12). Aliquando sumunt originem ab alio: ideo dicit, ab alienis parce servo tuo. Sometimes they arise from another source, so he says, and from those of others spare your servant. Sed numquid alicui imputatur alienum peccatum? Ezech. 18: anima quae peccaverit, ipsa morietur. Dicendum, quod non, quando totaliter est alienum; sed quando transit ad te per imitationem: Eccl. 13: qui tetigerit picem, inquinabitur ab ea. Vel persuasionem, vel consensum: Prov. 1: fili mi, si te lactaverint peccatores, non acquiescas eis. Vel per dissimulationem tempore et loco debito, tunc fit tuum, et imputatur: Rom. 1: non solum qui faciunt ea, digni sunt morte, sed etiam qui consentiunt facientibus. Et hoc maxime in praelatis quando dissimulant scienter scelera subditorum. But surely the sins of one would not be imputed to another? The soul that sins, the same shall die (Ezek 18:4). It must be said that no, they would not be when they completely belong to another. But when they pass to you by imitation—he who touches pitch, shall be defiled with it (Sir 13:1)—or by persuasion, or by consent: my son, if sinners entice you, consent not to them (Prov 1:10), or by hiding the due time and place, then they are yours and are imputed to you. Not only those who do them, but those also who consent to them that do them (Rom 1:32). And this especially applies to prelates when they knowingly hide the sins of their subordinates. Et dicit, parce servo tuo, quia haec peccata videntur in nobis ex divina ira provenire, quod scilicet hujusmodi occasiones peccati dentur nobis. And he says, spare your servant, since these appear to arise in us because of the divine wrath in that occasions of such sin are given to us. Vel, ab alienis, superbis hominibus: Ps. 17: filii alieni mentiti sunt mihi. Or save us from those of others, proud men. The children that are strangers have lied to me (Ps 17:46). 158. Si mei. Hic ponitur ratio petitionis. 158. If they have no dominion over me. Here he gives the reason for his petition. Et primo ex parte David petit immunitatem a malo. First, on the part of David, he asks for freedom from evil. Secundo perfectionem in bono, et erunt ut complaceant. Second, he asks for perfection in the good: and the words . . . shall be such as may please.
