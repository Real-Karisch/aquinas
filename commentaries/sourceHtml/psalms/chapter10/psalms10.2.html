      Dicit ergo, sic faciunt isti; sed justus dominus auctoritate videns hoc, quid facit? Hier. 33: hoc est nomen quod vocabunt eum dominus justus noster. Solvit quaestionem, et ponit quid facit hic justus. Therefore, he says, they act in this way; but what does the just Lord do with authority when he sees this? This is the name that they shall call him: the Lord our just one (Jer 33:16). He answers the question and describes what the just one does here. Et primo ejus judiciariam potestatem, vel conditionem judicis. First, he explains his power of judging or the situation of the judge; Secundo judicii discussionem, oculi ejus. second, the examination of the judge: his eyes look; Tertio condemnationem concussorum, qui autem diligit. third, the condemnation of those examined: for he who loves. Judex si semper punit, crudelis est; si semper parcit, remissus. Deus autem utrumque facit; et ideo locum habet tribunal misericordiae; et hoc est templum: Hebr. 4: adeamus cum fiducia ad thronum gratiae; et ideo dicit, dominus in templo sancto suo: 1 Cor. 3: nescitis quia templum Dei estis, et Spiritus Sanctus habitat in vobis? If a judge always punishes he is cruel; if he always pardons he is remiss. Now God does both, and so his tribunal of mercy has a place, which is the temple: let us go therefore with confidence to the throne of grace (Heb 4:16), and so he says, the Lord is in his holy temple. Know you not that you are the temple of God, and that the Spirit of God dwells in you? (1 Cor 3:16). Habet etiam locum justitiae, unde punit, scilicet caelum; et hoc est etiam, dominus in caelo sedes ejus. Ubi enumeratur potestas ejus: Isa. ult., caelum mihi sedes est. He also has a place of justice from which he punishes, namely heaven: the Lord’s throne is in heaven. This is where his power is considered to be. Heaven is my throne (Isa 66:1). Templum dicuntur fideles propter devotionem, caelum propter sapientiam. The faithful are called a temple because of their devotion and heaven because of their wisdom. Item caelum dicitur cuna in qua Deus sedet ut faciat eum caelum, id est secreta scientem. Item dicitur anima templum Dei, quia in templo Deus adoratur. Similiter in anima fideli: Matth. 6: intra in cubiculum tuum, id est secreta animae tuae, et clauso ostio ora Patrem tuum in abscondito. Item ibi offeruntur sacrificia: similiter in anima fideli: Ps. 50: sacrificium Deo spiritus contribulatus. Item in templo funduntur orationes ad Deum, ita in anima operatur Deus: Isa. 26: omnia opera nostra operatus es in nobis domine. Item, quia Deus eam sanctificat, et in eam descendit sicut in templum: Sap. 4: gratia Dei in sanctos ejus, et respectus et cetera. Also, heaven is said to be the cradle of the soul in which God sits, so that he makes him be a heaven—that is, one who knows secrets. Likewise, the soul is called the temple of God because God is adored in the temple as he is in the soul of the faithful. Enter into your chamber, that is, the secret of your soul, and having shut the door, pray to your Father in secret (Matt 6:6). Likewise, here sacrifices are offered, and they are also offered in the soul of the faithful. A sacrifice to God is a contrite spirit (Ps 50:19). Likewise, just as prayers are poured forth to God in the temple, so also God acts in the soul. You have wrought all our works in us (Isa 26:12). Likewise, God sanctifies it and descends into it, just as he does into the temple. The grace of God is with his saints, and his respect (Wis 4:15). 85. Oculi. Hic agit de discussione judicii. 85. His eyes look. Here he begins to treat judgment. Et primo ponit divinae cognitionis perfectionem. And first, he describes the perfection of the divine knowledge; Secundo diligentem examinationem, ibi, palpebrae ejus interrogant filios hominum. second, its careful examination, at his eyelids examine the sons of men; Tertio judicii aequitatem, ibi, dominus interrogat justum et impium. and third, the equity of his judgment, at the Lord tries the just and the wicked. Per primum nullus potest occultari, quia videt affectum pauperis et impii, peccatoris et justi: Eccl. 23: oculi domini lucidiores sunt super solem, circumspicientes super omnes vias hominum, et profundum abyssi, et hominum corda intuentes: Prov. 16: omnes viae hominum patent oculis ejus; spirituum ponderator est dominus, id est cogitationum, vel animarum secundum glossam; et ideo dicit, oculi ejus in pauperem respiciunt, ad miserandum: Ps. 30: miserere mei domine, quoniam tribulor. Item Ps. 27: dominus protector meus, et in ipso speravit cor meum et cetera. Nothing can be hidden from the first, since he sees the disposition of the poor and of the wicked, of the sinner and of the just. The eyes of the Lord are far brighter than the sun, beholding round about all the ways of men, and the bottom of the deep, and looking into the hearts of men (Sir 23:28). All the ways of a man are open to his eyes; the Lord is the weigher of spirits (Prov 16:2), that is, of thoughts or of souls, according to the gloss. And so he says, have mercy on me, O Lord, for I am afflicted (Ps 30:10). Likewise: the Lord is my protector, in him has my heart hoped (Ps 27:7). Ad protegendum etiam probando: Ps. 33: oculi domini super justos. Item Ps. 31: in via hac qua gradieris, firmabo super te oculos meos. He also looks upon him to protect him by testing: the eyes of the Lord are upon the just (Ps 33:16). Likewise: in this way, in which you shall go I will fix my eyes upon you (Ps 31:8). Et sic respiciens examinat diligenter. Palpebrae ejus interrogant et cetera. And so, watching him, he examines him carefully: his eyelids examine the sons of men. Per palpebras significatur discretio: quia sicut palpebrae oculi dirigunt visum, sic discretio moderatur humanam sapientiam: Prov. 4, palpebrae tuae praecedant gressus tuos. By eyelids, discernment is signified, since just as the eyelids direct the sight, so discernment orders human wisdom. Let your eyelids go before your steps (Prov 4:25). Ipsa ergo discretio Dei interrogat, id est probat et examinat: Sap. 1: cogitationibus impii interrogatio erit: sermonum autem illius auditio ad Deum veniet. Therefore, the discernment of God examines him, that is, it probes and tests. Inquisition shall be made into the thoughts of the ungodly and the hearing of his words shall come to God (Wis 1:9). Et quomodo? Quia quandoque punit, quandoque parcit, quandoque dat beneficia, quandoque aufert: Job 6: sagittae domini et cetera. Etiam claudit palpebras et aperit: Matth. 15: misereor super turba et cetera. Item ibidem 21: auferetur a vobis regnum Dei: Ps. 146: dat jumentis escam ipsorum et cetera. And how? He sometimes punishes and sometimes pardons; he sometimes gives good things and sometimes takes them away. The arrows of the Lord are in me (Job 6:4). For he closes his eyelids and opens them. I have compassion upon the multitude (Matt 15:32). The kingdom of God shall be taken from you (Matt 21:43). Who gives to beasts their food (Ps 146:9). Interrogat filios hominum, quia efficiuntur quidam ex ipsis meliores, quidam pejores; vel quidam ex Scriptura efficiuntur mali, quidam boni: quia quidam eam bene intelligunt et non fatigantur, sed exercentur; mali vero e converso faciunt. His eyelids examine the sons of men, since by them some are made better, some worse; or some are made bad by the Scriptures and some good, since some understand them well and are not wearied by them but strengthened, but the opposite happens to the evil. Judicii aequitas ponitur: quia, interrogat justum et impium, quia immittit flagella justis et injustis, et tribuit bona temporalia bonis et malis: Sap. 11: hos, scilicet bonos, tamquam pater monens probasti; illos autem, scilicet malos, tamquam rex durus interrogans condemnasti. Propterea dicit, dominus interrogat justum et impium, id est per tribulationes probat. Gregorius: poena inflicta interrogat, si quis in tribulatione positus Deum veraciter amat. The equity of his judgment is described: the Lord tries the just and the wicked, since he sends his scourges upon the just and the unjust, and he gives temporal goods to the good and to the evil. You did admonish and try them, namely, the good, as a father, but the others, namely the evil, as a severe king, you did examine and condemn (Wis 11:11). So he says, the Lord tries the just and the wicked, that is, he tries them through tribulations. Gregory: by inflicting punishments, he questions whether someone in tribulation truly loves God. Justum interrogat, id est examinat dominus ut praemietur: Sap. 10: reddidit Deus mercedem laborum sanctorum suorum. Sed impium sive peccatorem interrogat, ut condemnetur: Matth. 23: ligatis manibus et pedibus projicite eum in tenebras exteriores. He tries the just, that is, the Lord examines him to reward him. God rendered to his saints the wages of their labors (Wis 10:17). But he tries the wicked and the sinful to condemn them. Bind his hands and feet, and cast him into the exterior darkness (Matt 23:13). 86. Hic ponitur condemnatio: et circa hoc duo facit: 86. He describes their condemnation, and he discusses two aspects of it: quia primo ponit culpam; first, describing their guilt; secundo poenam, ibi, pluet super peccatores. second, their punishment: he shall rain upon sinners. Ostendit quod quantum est ex parte Dei, non est perditio malorum, sed ex parte nostra; unde dicit, qui diligit iniquitatem, id est peccatum: 1 Joan. 3: omnis qui facit peccatum, et iniquitatem facit, quia peccatum iniquitas est. Odit animam suam: Sap. 16: homo per malitiam occidit animam suam. He shows that the destruction of the evil does not come from God but from us, so he says, he who loves iniquity, that is, sin. Whoever commits sin also commits iniquity, because sin is iniquity (1 John 3:4). He hates his own soul. A man kills his own soul through malice (Wis 16:14). Sed quaeritur, quomodo quis se odire possit. Dicendum, quod quodammodo odit peccator se ipsum; sed simpliciter nullus odit: Ephes. 5: nemo unquam carnem suam odio habuit. But it is asked, how can one hate himself? It must be said that the sinner does hate himself in some respect, but he does not hate himself simply. No man ever hated his own flesh (Eph 5:29). Sed quod mali quodammodo odiunt se ipsos, et etiam boni quodammodo se odiunt, declaratur hoc modo. Anima nostra duas habet facies: unam versus Deum secundum rationem, aliam versus carnem secundum naturam sensitivam, quae tantum corporalia comprehendit. Et sicut quaelibet res diligit proprium bonum, ita homo diligit illud quod aestimat animam suam. Peccatores enim aestimant animam suam quod principaliter intendunt: quia quaelibet res est illud quod est praecipuum in ea, sicut rex dicitur regnum. Qui ergo naturam sensitivam habent principale diligunt eam; qui autem intellectivam, eamdem amant. Nullus ergo odit animam quantum ad id quod aestimat principale. Boni ergo odiunt quantum ad naturam sensitivam; mali quantum ad intellectivam. Now, that the evil do hate themselves in some respect and that the good also hate themselves in some way is explained this way: our soul has two faces, one turned to God by reason, another turned to the flesh according to the sensitive nature, which grasps only bodily things. And just as each thing loves its own proper good, so the man loves that which he understands his soul to be. For sinners consider their soul to be that to which it primarily inclines, since each thing is that which is primary in it, as the kingdom is called the king. Therefore, he who holds his sensitive nature to be primary loves it, but he who holds the intellectual as primary loves that. Therefore, no one hates his soul in regard to that which he considers primary. Therefore, the good hate the sensitive nature, but the evil hate the intellectual nature. Secundo ponit poenam. Et hoc possumus exponere de praesenti, vel de futuro. Second, he describes the punishment. And we can explain this as being about the present or about the future. De praesenti sic, et servat metaphoram. Dixit quod dominus est in caelo, quia ibi est sedes ejus; sed de caelo venit pluvia: ideo eorum poena signatur cum dicitur, pluet super peccatores. Et quid? Laqueos. The metaphor helps show how it is about the present. He says that the Lord is in heaven since his throne is there; but rain comes from heaven, and so when he says, he shall rain snares upon sinners, he signifies his punishment. And what is it? Snares. Et si exponatur hic in praesenti, notandum est, quod in peccato sunt quatuor. And if this is explained in regard to the present, it should be noted that there are four aspects of sin. Primum est seductio; unde dicit: pluet super peccatores laqueos. Quasi justo judicio permittit eos illaqueari et seduci: Isa. 8: et offendentur ex eis plurimi et cadent, et conterentur et irretientur et capientur. First is seduction; so he says, he shall rain snares upon sinners. It is as if the just judge allows them to be snared and seduced. And very many of them shall stumble and fall, and shall be broken in pieces, and shall be snared, and taken (Isa 8:15). Secundum est concupiscentia; et ideo dicit, ignis concupiscentiae: Ps. 57: supercecidit ignis, et non viderunt solem, scilicet carnalis concupiscentiae et cetera. Second is the desire, and so he mentions the fire of concupiscence. Fire has fallen on them, and they shall not see the sun (Ps 57:9), namely, of carnal desire. Tertium est foetor infamiae; et ideo dicit, sulphur. Sic pluit dominus super Sodomam et Gomorrham igne et sulphure. Third is the foul odor of infamy, and so he says, brimstone. So the Lord rained fire and brimstone upon Sodom and Gomorrah. Potest addi et quartum, scilicet inquietudo mentis; et ideo dicit, spiritus procellarum: Isa. 57: cor impii quasi mare fervens. A fourth can be added, namely, restlessness of mind, and so he says, storms of winds shall be the portion of their cup. The heart of the wicked is like the raging sea (Isa 57:20). Pars calicis eorum: quia calix est quaedam mensura, et ipsi poenas habent secundum eorum peccata: Isa. 27: in mensura contra mensuram, cum abjecta fuerit judicabit. It is the portion of their cup, since a cup is a certain measure, and these have punishments in accordance with their sins. In measure against measure, when it shall be cast off, you shall judge it (Isa 27:8). Sed si exponatur de futuro, sic punientur ibi motus: quia ligabuntur ut nec mala vitare, nec bona consequi possint; ideo dicit, pluet super peccatores laqueos, id est colligantes sensus: et sic referuntur ad odoratum sulphur, ad tactum ignis: Isa. 66: ignis eorum non extinguetur: Apoc. 20: missi in stagnum ignis. Item affectus, quia non quiescunt: quia, spiritus procellarum pars calicis eorum, id est daemones inquietantes, molestantes, et affligentes. But if it is explained about the future, they are punished in regard to their movement, since they will be bound so that they can neither do evil nor seek the good, so he says, he shall rain snares upon sinners, that is, by binding their senses, and so they are brought to the smell of brimstone and the touch of fire. Their fire shall not be quenched (Isa 66:24). They were cast into the pool of fire (Rev 20:14). Their desires are also bound, since they do not rest, since storms of winds shall be the portion of their cup, that is, restless, disturbing, and afflicting demons.
