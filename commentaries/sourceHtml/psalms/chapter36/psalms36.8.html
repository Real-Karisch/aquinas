      Secundo cum subdit, confirmat autem et cetera, ostendit quod justi sunt diuturni, et firmi, et stabiles. Dicit ergo, confirmat autem justos dominus. Next, when he adds, but he strengthens the just, he shows that the just are lasting and firm and stable. Therefore, he says, but the Lord strengthens the just. Licet justitia sit virtus et firmitas animi, tamen non inest homini a se ipso: unde 1 Cor. 1: non glorietur omnis caro in conspectu ejus; ex ipso autem vos estis in Christo Jesu et cetera usque in domino glorietur. Although justice is a virtue and a strength of soul, nevertheless it is not in a man from himself alone. So 1 Corinthians 1:29–31 says: that no flesh should glory in his sight but of him are you in Christ Jesus, who of God is made unto us wisdom, and justice, and sanctification, and redemption, that as is written: he who glories may glory in the Lord. Homo enim in se factus est infirmus: Ps. 6: miserere mei Deus, quoniam infirmus sum. Et ideo indiget confortari ab aliquo, maxime a Deo, a quo quidem confirmatur, quandoque in temporalibus, in quantum expedit justo ad salutem; sed in spiritualibus semper, et hoc interiori gratia. For in himself a man is weak. Have mercy on me, O God, for I am weak (Ps 6:3). And so he needs to be strengthened by another, and most of all by God, from whom he indeed is strengthened, sometimes in temporal things insofar as this will aid the salvation of the just, but always in spiritual things, and this is by interior grace. Item bonis verbis. Likewise, he is strengthened by good words. Item bonis exemplis. Also by good example. De primo Rom. 1: gratiae spiritualis ad confirmandos vos. 2 Thess. 2: in gratia exhortetur corda vestra, et confirmet et cetera. Ps. 50: spiritu principali confirma me. About the first is, some spiritual grace to strengthen you (Rom 1:11). May he exhort your hearts in grace and confirm you (2 Thess 2:15–16). Strengthen me with a perfect spirit (Ps 50:14). De secundo Psalm 118: confirma me in verbis tuis: Act. 15: verbo plurimo consolati sunt fratres, et confirmaverunt eos. About the second is, strengthen me in your words (Ps 118:28). They comforted the brethren with many words and confirmed them (Acts 15:32). De tertio Luc. 22: conversus confirma fratres tuos: 1 Pet. 2: Christus passus est pro nobis et cetera. Et 4: Christo igitur in carne passo et cetera. Unde in 5: ipse perficiet confirmabit et cetera. About the third is, converted, strengthen your brethren (Luke 22:32). Christ also suffered for us (1 Pet 2:21). And, Christ therefore having suffered in the flesh (1 Pet 4:1). So he will himself perfect you and confirm you (1 Pet 5:10). Confirmatur per exemplum crucis de qua dicitur (ab Augustino Enar. in hunc psalm. Ser. 2, n. 4), crux enim finita est in poena, sed manet in gloria: a locis enim suppliciorum transit ad frontes imperatorum. Qui tamen dedit honorem suis poenis, quid faciet fidelibus suis? He is strengthened by the example of the Cross, about which is said in regard to this psalm, for the Cross is limited in pain, but continues on in glory. For from a place of suffering it has passed over to the brows of emperors. What will he who gives honors to his sufferings do for his faithful? 383. Consequenter cum dicit, novit dominus, manifestat propositum. 383. Afterwards, when he says, the Lord knows the days of the undefiled, he shows his proposition. Et primo dicit de stabilitate justorum. First, he speaks about the endurance of the just; Secundo de contritione injustorum, ibi, quia peccatores. second, about the breaking of the unjust, at because the wicked shall perish. Justi autem dupliciter confirmantur a Deo: Now, the just are strengthened by God in two ways, quia primo bona eorum stabiliuntur; since first their goods are made stable; secundo a malis curantur, ibi, non confundentur. second, they are guarded from evils, at they shall not be confounded. Sed duplex est bonum, scilicet bonum viae et bonum finis: et in utroque justi stabiliuntur. But the good is twofold: the good of the way and the good of the end, and the just are made stable in both. Quantum ad primum dicit, novit dominus. In regard to the first, he says, the Lord knows the days of the undefiled. Quantum ad secundum dicit, et hereditas. In regard to the second, he says, and their inheritance. Dicit ergo, novit dominus: Heb. 4: omnia nuda et aperta sunt oculis ejus. Sed praecipue quod sibi affinis et familiaris fuerit: 2 Tim. 2: novit dominus qui sunt ejus; et eos quos approbat: et sic dicitur hic, novit dominus, id est approbat, dies immaculatorum. Therefore, he says, the Lord knows. All things are naked and open to his eyes (Heb 4:13). But this principally means that he will be close and familiar. The Lord knows who are his (2 Tim 2:19). And he approves of these, and so he says, the Lord knows, that is, he approves, the days of the undefiled. Sed Job 15, dicitur: quis est homo ut immaculatus sit, et ut justus et cetera. But in Job it is said, what is man that he should be without spot, and he who is born of a woman just? (Job 15:14). Respondeo dicendum, quod verum est quod ex se nullus est immaculatus, si macula intelligatur peccatum mortale; sed tamen per gratiam, sic. Si vero macula intelligatur originale peccatum, sic nullus est immaculatus. I respond that it must be said that it is true that, of themselves, no one of them is undefiled, if deadly sin is understood as defilement, but nevertheless through grace they are so. If defilement is understood as original sin, no one is undefiled. Dies isti tripliciter possunt intelligi. The days can be understood in three ways. Quia primo dies praesentis vitae; et licet sint communes bonis et malis, tamen boni eis bene utuntur: Gen. 25: mortuus est in senectute bona provectaeque aetatis et plenus dierum. Impii autem male utuntur eis: Ps. 54: viri sanguinum et dolosi non dimidiabunt dies suos. Et quia dies justorum sunt pleni et ideo approbati, illorum vero dimidiati, et ideo non approbantur. Item et isti sunt mali et pauci, tamen Deus novit eos. First are the days of this present life, and although they belong to the good and evil universally, nevertheless they are used well by the good. He died in a good old age and having lived a long time and full of days (Gen 25:8). The wicked make bad use of them. Bloody and deceitful men shall not live out half their days (Ps 54:24). And the days of the just are full and approved, but those of the others are incomplete, and so are not approved. Likewise these are also evil and few, but the Lord nevertheless knows them. Alii opera virtutum: Rom. 13: sicut in die honeste ambulemus: et isti sunt approbati a Deo. In another understanding of the text, they are acts of virtue—let us walk honestly, as in the day (Rom 13:13)—and these are approved by God. Alii dies aeternitatis et justitiae: et isti sunt dies justorum, sed soli Deo noti: Isa. 64: oculus non vidit absque te, quae praeparasti expectantibus te. In another understanding, they are the days of eternity and justice and so these are the days of the just, but are known to God alone. Eye has not seen . . . besides you what you have prepared for those who wait for you (Isa 64:4). Bonum vero finis ostendit, cum dicit, et hereditas eorum in aeternum erit. Istud dicitur hereditas in quo est stabilimentum et finis alicujus. Justi finem suum habent in re aeterna: Ps. 15: dominus pars hereditatis meae: Thren. 3: pars mea. Et ideo non potest eorum hereditas deficere. Impii autem ponunt finem suum in mundanis: Sap. 2: haec est pars nostra et cetera et ideo non manet. Et de istis dicitur 1 Pet. 1: in hereditatem conservatam in caelis. Indeed, he shows their good end when he says, and their inheritance shall be forever. The inheritance mentioned is that in which there is stability and is the goal of some. The just have their end in eternal reality. The Lord is the portion of my inheritance (Ps 15:5). My portion (Lam 3:26). And so their inheritance cannot fail. But the impious place their end in worldly things. This is our portion (Wis 2:9). And so it does not remain. And about this is said, unto an inheritance . . . reserved in heaven (1 Pet 1:4). 384. Deinde cum dicit, non confundentur, ostendit quomodo justi tueantur contra mala. 384. Afterwards when he says, they shall not be confounded in the evil time, he shows how the just are protected from evil. Et hoc dupliciter: And this happens in two ways: quoddam enim est malum, quod bono est contrarium; for there is evil which is contrary to the good, et quoddam est malum per defectum boni, ibi, et in diebus famis. and there is evil which is a defect in the good, at in the days of famine. Dicit ergo quantum ad primum, non confundentur in tempore malo, id est in tempore adversitatis: Amos 5: prudens in tempore illo tacebit, quia tempus malum est. Therefore, he says in regard to the first, they shall not be confounded in an evil time, that is, in the time of adversity. The prudent shall keep silent at that time, for it is an evil time (Amos 5:13). Est enim tempus malum dupliciter: For an evil time is twofold: vel de praesenti, et hoc est tempus adversitatis quod est malum; unde non confundentur in tempore malo, id est in tempore adversitatis. Mali enim in tempore adversitatis confunduntur, non boni: tunc nempe confunditur quis, quando perdit in quo sperat; sed quando id manet in quo sperat, non confunditur. In adversitate autem perduntur temporalia, in quibus boni non sperant: et ideo non confunduntur tempore adversitatis. either the present, and it is the time of adversity which is evil, so they shall not be confounded in an evil time, that is, in the time of adversity. For the evil are confounded in the time of adversity, not the good, for he is truly confounded when that in which he trusts perishes, but when that in which he trusts remains he is not confounded. Now temporal things, in which the good do not trust, perish in adversity, and so they are not confounded in the time of adversity. Vel de futuro in die judicii: et in eo impii confundentur per erubescentiam de peccatis: Ps. 6: confundantur et erubescant valde velociter. Justi autem honorabuntur: Rom. 2: his quidem qui secundum patientiam boni operis gloriam et honorem et cetera. Or it is about the future day of judgment, and the wicked will be confounded in it through their shame for their sins. Let them be confounded and very speedily ashamed (Ps 6:11). But the just will be honored. To those indeed who, according to patience in good works, seek honor and glory (Rom 2:7). Quantum ad secundum malum, quod est per defectum boni, subjungit, et in diebus famis saturabuntur. In regard to the second evil, which is a defect in the good, he adds, and in the days of famine they shall be filled. Hoc tripliciter exponitur. This can be explained in three ways.
