      Quod si referatur ad casum peccati mortalis, quo aliquando justus cadit, ut David per adulterium et homicidium, et sicut Petrus negando Christum; sic peccatores ex casu colliduntur, dum desperati nolunt ad poenitentiam redire. Unde Eph. 4: desperantes semetipsos tradiderunt et cetera. 2 Cor. 12: lugeam multos ex his, qui ante peccaverunt, et non egerunt poenitentiam et cetera. If this is applied to the case of mortal sin, into which the just sometimes falls, as David did by adultery and homicide and as Peter did by denying Christ, then sinners in this case are bruised when they despair and refuse to do penance. Despairing, they have given themselves up (Eph 4:19). I mourn many of those who sinned before and have not done penance (2 Cor 12:21). Sed justus non colliditur per desperationem, sed redit ad poenitentiam: unde David dicit, peccavi domino: et dictum est ei, 2 Reg. 12: dominus transtulit peccatum tuum. Item Petrus flevit amare, Matth. 26. Mich. 7: non laeteris inimica mea, quia cecidi, consurgam. But the just is not bruised through despair, but he does penance. So David says, I have sinned against the Lord, and it is said to him, the Lord has also taken away your sin (2 Sam 12:13). Likewise, Peter wept bitterly (Matt 26:75). Rejoice not my enemy over me because I am fallen; I shall arise (Mich 7:8). Ratio autem quod non colliditur, est, quia dominus supponit manum suam, scilicet gratiae confortantis eum: Ezech. 13: manus domini erat mecum confortans me: Ps. 138: et tenebit me dextera tua. Now the reason that he is not bruised is that the Lord puts his hand under him, by strengthening him by grace. The hand of the Lord was with me, strengthening me (Ezeck 3:14). And your right hand shall hold me (Ps 138:10). 387. Deinde cum dicit, junior fui, ostendit experimento quod bona justorum sunt fructuosa, sed non malorum. 387. Afterwards, when he says, I have been young, and now am old, he shows by experience that the goods of the just are fruitful, but not those of the evil. Et ponit illud diuturnum de duobus. And he describes this endurance in regard to two things. Primo de immunitate bonorum a malis. First, he describes the freedom of the good from evils; Secundo de profectu eorum in bono, ibi, tota die. second, their maturing in the good, at all the day long. Circa primum duo facit. He makes two points regarding the freedom. Primo enim ostendit immunitatem a malo, quantum ad ipsum justum. He first shows freedom from evil in regard to the just man himself; Secundo quantum ad semen ejus, ibi, nec semen ejus. second, in regard to his seed, at nor his seed. Circa primum duo facit. He considers two aspects of the just man. Primo enim ponit diuturnitatem experimenti. First, he describes the length of his experience; Secundo ipsum experimentum, ibi, non vidi. second, the experience itself, at I have not seen. Dicit ergo, junior fui. Ac si diceret ei aliquis: unde habes haec omnia quae dicis de bonis justorum et cetera. Respondet, quia ab experimento, junior fui et cetera. Therefore, he says, I have been young, as if someone had said to him, “Where did you get everything that you are saying about the good of the just?” He would respond that is from experience: I have been young. Hoc dupliciter potest exponi. This can be explained in two ways. Primo de aetate corporali secundum quod homo primo floret in juventute, et postea senex fit: Ps. 89: mane floreat, et transeat. The first is about physical age, as a man first flourishes in the time of youth, and afterwards becomes old. In the morning he shall flourish and pass away (Ps 98:6). Sed contra hoc dicit Augustinus, tu David, semper fuisti in terra tua: et si in illa non fuit justus derelictus, non tamen est mirum si alibi est derelictus. Et ideo vult Augustinus quod loquatur in persona Ecclesiae. Et haec habet aetatem pueritiae in Abel, juventutis in patriarchis, senectutis in apostolis, senectam in fine mundi. Praetermittit duas medias aetates, et ponit extremas: et sic fuit a principio mundi, et erit usque ad finem. Et haec distinctio ponitur Gal. 4. But against this is what Augustine says, you, David, always were in your own land, and if in it you did not see the just forsaken, it would not be surprising if in another place he was forsaken. And so Augustine wants this to have been spoken in the person of the Church. And this had its age of boyhood in Abel, youth in the patriarchs, adulthood in the apostles, and will be elderly at the end of the world. And so it has lasted from the beginning of the world and will continue even to the end. And this distinction is found in Galatians 4:21. Vel potest intelligi de spiritualitate: quia quamdiu homo delectatur in puerilibus et vanis, sic est puer mente; sed quando delectatur in maturis, fit senex. Et haec distinctio ponitur 1 Cor. 13: cum essem parvulus, loquebar et cetera. Cum autem factus sum et cetera. Et ideo dicit: sive in statu puerili, sive in statu senectutis, hoc judicavi, quod scilicet ipse justus sit immunis a malis, et quantum ad se, et quantum ad semen ejus. Ideo ostendit dicens, non vidi justum derelictum, quantum ad se. Or it can be understood about spirituality, since as long as a man delights in childish and vain things, he is a boy in his mind, but when he delights in mature things, he is adult. And this distinction is found in 1 Corinthians: when I was a child, I spoke as a child . . . but when I became a man, I put away the things of a child (1 Cor 13:11). And so he says, whether in the state of boyhood or in the state of old age, I have judged this to be so, that the just man is free from evils both in regard to himself and in regard to his seed. Therefore, he shows it saying, I have not seen the just forsaken, in regard to himself. Si hoc referatur ad bona temporalia, non videtur hoc verum, quia de justis dicitur Hebr. 11, egentes, angustiati et cetera. But if this is referred to temporal goods it does not seem to be true, since it is said about the just: being in want, distressed (Heb 11:37). Respondeo. Dicendum, quod licet subtrahantur eis bona temporalia, non tamen deseruntur a Deo, quia totum hoc cedit in bonum eorum, vel pro bonis, vel ut tollatur causa alicujus mali, ut dicit Augustinus: Matth. 6: primum quaerite regnum Dei, et haec omnia, scilicet spiritualia, adjicientur vobis. Verum est, sed si Deus videt expedire ad nostram salutem. I respond. It must be said that although temporal goods are taken from them, nevertheless they are not forsaken by God, since all this falls under their good or is for their good, or so that a cause of evil may be removed, as Augustine says. Seek you first the kingdom of God and all these things, namely, spiritual things, shall be added unto you (Matt 6:33). This is true, if it seems good to God to aid our salvation. Quantum ad semen ejus dicit: nec semen ejus quaerens panem: Exod. 20: ego sum Deus Zelotes, reddens misericordiam in millia his qui diligunt me, et custodiunt praecepta mea: Luc. 1: misericordia ejus a progenie in progenies timentibus eum. Et ideo sicut ipsi non derelinquuntur, ita nec semen eorum. In regard to his seed he says, nor his seed seeking bread. I am a jealous God . . . showing mercy to thousands to those who love me and keep my precepts (Exod 20:5–6). His mercy is from generation to generation to those who fear him (Luke 1:50). And so, just as these are not forsaken, neither are their seed. Et potest referri ad panem temporalem, quantum ad vetus testamentum; sed secundum spiritualem intellectum panis iste intelligitur participatio Christi, scilicet veritas, sapientia et justitia: Joan. 6: ego sum panis vivus et cetera. Hic est panis qui dat delicias regibus, Gen. 49. Et dicit, quaerens panem, quia iste panis praesto est quaerentibus eum. And this can be applied to temporal bread, in regard to the Old Testament, but according to a spiritual understanding this bread is understood to be participation in Christ, that is, in truth, wisdom, and justice. I am the living bread (John 6:41). This is the bread which he gives as dainties to the kings (Gen 49:20). And he says, seeking bread, since this bread is given to those seeking it. Sed contra: Abraham justus fuit et Isaac: Ismael autem et Esau, qui fuerunt filii justorum, non habent spiritualem panem. But on the contrary, Abraham and Isaac were just, but Ishmael and Esau, who were the sons of just men, did not have spiritual bread. Respondeo. Apostolus solvit, quod filii promissionis computantur filii justorum. Ergo semen est ille qui imitatur eum. Unde tam filii carnales qui imitantur patres in bono, quam discipuli qui imitantur magistrum, sunt semen spirituale, scilicet illi qui imitantur eorum doctrinam in opere. I respond. The Apostle solves this because the sons of the promise are counted as the sons of the just. Therefore, his seed is the one who imitates him. So just as physical sons who imitate their fathers in good are spiritual seed, so those who imitate their teaching in deed are disciples who imitate their master. Tota die. Hic ostendit aliud signum experientiae, sive aliud experimentum, et dicit quod aliud quod expertus de viro justo est quia tota die, id est toto tempore vitae suae, miseretur, miseriis aliorum, et commodat, scilicet spiritualia et temporalia: Ps. 111: jucundus homo qui miseretur et commodat: vel actu, vel promptitudine. Et ideo, semen illius in benedictione erit: Gen. 22: multiplicabo semen tuum sicut stellas caeli et cetera. He shows mercy and lends all the day long. Here he shows another sign from experience, or another experience, and he says that he has experienced from the just man that all the day long, that is, throughout his whole life, he shows mercy, to the miseries of others, and lends, spiritual and temporal goods. Acceptable is the man who shows mercy and lends (Ps 111:5), either in action, or in readiness. And so, his seed shall be a blessing. I will multiply your seed as the stars of heaven (Gen 22:17). 388. Declina a malo. Supra ostendit psalmista quod bona justorum sunt meliora quantum ad fructum; hic autem prorumpit in exhortationem ad illa. 388. Turn away from evil and do good. Above, the psalmist shows that the goods of the just are better in their fruitfulness; now here he bursts forth in exhortation to them. Circa hoc duo facit. He makes two points in regard to this. Primo enim ponitur exhortatio ad ista. First, he exhorts them. Secundo ponitur fructus, ibi, dominus amat. Second, he describes the fruit, at the Lord loves. Justitia habet duas partes: recedere a malo, et facere bonum. Et ideo dicit, declina a malo, et fac bonum. Hae autem duae partes justitiae correspondent praeceptis legis: justitia enim regulatur lege. In lege sunt quaedam praecepta affirmativa, quae implentur faciendo bonum; et quaedam sunt negativa, quae implentur declinando a malo. Justice has two parts: to avoid evil and to do good. And so he says, turn away from evil and do good. These two parts of justice correspond to the precepts of the law, for justice is regulated by the law. In the law there are certain affirmative precepts, which are fulfilled by doing good, and there are some negative precepts which are fulfilled by avoiding evil. Item per haec duo perficitur naturalis inclinatio appetitus, cujus sunt duo objecta; scilicet bonum et malum; quia appetitus naturaliter tendit in bonum, et refugit malum falsum. Sed dicit, declina a malo. Likewise, the inclination of the natural appetite, which has two objects, namely, good and evil, is perfected by these two, since the appetite naturally tends to the good and flees from evil. He says, turn away from evil. Est autem duplex malum. Now there is a twofold evil. Unum, quod facit homines malos, et hoc vere dicitur malum. One is that which evil men do and this is truly called evil. Aliud malum est, quod non facit homines malos, scilicet malum poenae. There is another evil which is not done by evil men, namely, the evil of punishment. Primum est peccatum; et de hoc intelligitur cum dicit, declina a malo, id est a peccato: Eccl. 9: non zeles hominem in peccato suo. Et non dicit quod non faciat malum, quia in hoc intelligitur sola negatio, sed, declina a malo, ut scilicet voluntatem faciendi non habeat. Isa. 1: quiescite agere perverse, discite benefacere. The first is sin, and about this can be understood what he says, turn away from evil, that is, from sin. Do not be jealous of a man in his sin (Sir 9:16). And he does not say not to do evil, since in this only a negation would be understood, but turn away from evil, that is, do not have the desire to do it. Cease to do perversely; learn to do well (Isa 1:16–17). Haec duo frequenter introducuntur in sacra Scriptura. Sed quandoque praemittitur bonum, sicut ibi, Ps. 44: dilexisti justitiam, et odisti iniquitatem. Quandoque praemittitur vitatio mali, sicut Isa. 7: ut sciat reprobare malum, et eligere bonum. These two are frequently mentioned in Sacred Scripture. But sometimes the good is put first, as here: you have loved justice and hated iniquity (Ps 44:8). Sometimes avoidance of evil is put first: that he may know to refuse the evil and to choose the good (Isa 7:15).
