      Apostolus autem, qui sic pro omnibus factis suis recurrit ad orationem, insinuat nobis quod omnes viae eius et facta ordinabantur ab ipso, secundum Dei dispositionem. Iob c. XXXVII, 11 s.: nubes spargunt lumen suum, quae lustrant cuncta per circuitum, quocumque eas voluntas gubernantis duxerit. Per nubes enim praedicatores et Apostoli intelliguntur. Is. LX, 8: qui sunt isti qui ut nubes volant? But the Apostle, who thus has recourse to prayer for all his deeds, suggests to us that all his ways and deeds were ordained by him according to God’s plan: the clouds spread their light, which go round about, whithersoever the will of him that governs them shall lead them (Job 37:11). For by the clouds are understood preachers and apostles: who are these that fly as clouds? (Isa 60:8). 765. Deinde cum dicit Deus autem pacis, orat Apostolus pro ipsis, et 765. Then when he says and may the God of peace, the Apostle prays for them: primo orat; first, he prays; secundo aliquid ab ipsis petendo se excusat, ibi rogo autem vos, fratres. second, he excuses himself by asking something of them, at and I beseech you, brethren. 766. Circa primum, prius describit eum quem orat, dicens Deus autem pacis. Proprius enim effectus Dei est facere pacem. Non enim est Deus dissensionis, sed pacis, I Cor. c. XIV, 33. Item II Cor. XIII, 11: pacem habete, et Deus pacis et dilectionis erit vobiscum. 766. In regard to the first, he describes the one whom he seeks, saying, the God of peace. For God’s proper effect is to make peace, because he is not a God of dissension but of peace (1 Cor 14:33), and, have peace: and the God of peace and love shall be with you (2 Cor 13:11). Pax enim nihil aliud est, nisi unitas affectuum. Quos unire est proprium solius Dei, quia per caritatem, quae a solo Deo est, uniuntur corda. Deus enim novit colligere et unire, quia Deus est caritas, quae est vinculum perfectionis. Unde ipse habitare facit unanimes in domo, Ps. LXVII, 7. Homo inter se et Deum, pacem fecit per mysterium Christi. For peace is nothing more than unity of affections, which God alone can make one, because hearts are united by charity, which is from God alone. For God knows how to gather and unite, because God is love, which is the bond of perfection. Hence, he makes men of one manner to dwell in a house (Ps 68:7). For man made peace between himself and God through the ministry of Christ. 767. Et ideo dicit qui eduxit de mortuis pastorem magnum ovium. Quandoque autem dicitur Christus suscitatus per virtutem patris. Rom. VIII, 11: si Spiritus eius, qui suscitavit Iesum Christum a mortuis. Quandoque vero dicitur seipsum suscitasse. Ps. III, 6: ego dormivi, et soporatus sum. Quae tamen non sunt contraria, quia surrexit virtute Dei, quae est una, Patris et Filii, et Spiritus Sancti. Eduxit ergo de mortuis, id est, de sepulchro, quod est locus mortuorum. Rom. VI, 4: quomodo surrexit Christus a mortuis per gloriam Patris, ita et nos in novitate vitae ambulemus. 767. Hence, he says, who brought again from the dead the great pastor of the sheep, our Lord Jesus Christ. But sometimes Christ is said to have been raised up by the Father’s power: if the Spirit of him who raised Jesus Christ from the dead (Rom 8:11), and sometimes he is said to have raised himself: I have slept and taken my rest: and I have risen up (Ps 3:6). But these statements are not contrary because he rose by God’s power, which is one in the Father and Son and Holy Spirit. Therefore, he brought him again from the dead, i.e., from the tomb, which is the place of the dead: as Christ rose from the dead by the glory of the Father, so we also shall walk in the newness of life (Rom 6:4). Dicitur autem Christus magnus pastor ovium, id est fidelium et humilium. Io. X, v. 11: ego sum pastor bonus, et cetera. Oves enim sunt qui Deo obediunt. Ibidem v. 16: et oves meae, vocem meam audiunt. But Christ is called the great pastor of the sheep, i.e., of the faithful and the humble: I am the good shepherd, and I know mine, and mine know me (John 10:14). For the sheep are they who obey God: and my sheep hear my voice (John 10:27). Dicit autem ipsum pastorem magnum, quia omnes alii sunt vicarii eius, quia ipse pascit oves proprias; alii vero oves Christi. Io. ult.: pasce oves meas. I Petr. V, 4: cum apparuerit princeps pastorum, percipietis immarcescibilem gloriae coronam. But he calls him the great pastor because all others are his vicars, for he feeds his own sheep, but the others feed Christ’s sheep: feed my sheep (John 21:17); when the prince of pastors shall appear, you shall receive a never fading crown of glory (1 Pet 5:4). 768. Eduxit autem ipsum in sanguine testamenti aeterni, id est in virtute sanguinis Christi, per quem confirmatur Testamentum Novum, in quo aeterna promittuntur ad differentiam Veteris. 768. He brought him out in the blood of the everlasting testament, i.e., in virtue of the blood of Christ by whom is confirmed the New Testament, in which eternal things are promised, but not in the Old. Christus enim sanguinem suum dicit sanguinem Novi Testamenti, Apostolus autem dicit aeterni. Et ideo in consecratione sanguinis in forma ponitur utrumque. For Christ calls his blood the blood of the New Testament; but the Apostle says: of the everlasting testament. Therefore, both are mentioned in the words of the consecration of the blood. Ipse vero Christus per passionem suam meruit sibi et nobis gloriam resurrectionis, ideo dicit quod eduxit Dominum nostrum Iesum Christum de mortuis in sanguine testamenti aeterni. Phil. II, 8: humiliavit semetipsum, et cetera. Zach. IX, 11: tu vero in sanguine testamenti tui eduxisti vinctos de lacu, in quo non erat aqua. But Christ by his passion merited the glory of his resurrection for himself and for us; hence, he says, who brought again from the dead . . . our Lord Jesus Christ, in the blood of the everlasting testament: he humbled himself, being made obedient unto death (Phil 2:8); by the blood of your testament you have sent forth your prisoners out of the pit, wherein is no water (Zech 9:11). 769. Consequenter subiungit petitionem suam, cum dicit aptet vos in omni bono. 769. Then he adds his petition when he says, may he fit you in all goodness. Voluntas enim humana, cum sit quaedam inclinatio rationis, est principium actuum humanorum, sicut gravitas est principium motus gravium deorsum, unde se habet ad actus rationis, sicut inclinatio naturalis ad actus naturales. Res autem naturalis dicitur apta ad illud ad quod habet inclinationem. Sic etiam homo quando habet voluntatem benefaciendi, dicitur aptus esse ad illud. Deus etiam quando immittit homini bonam voluntatem, aptat eum, id est facit ipsum aptum. Et ideo dicit aptet vos in omni bono, ut faciatis eius voluntatem, id est, faciat vos velle omne bonum. Prov. XI, 23: desiderium iustorum omne bonum. For the human will, since it is the inclination of reason, is the principle of human acts, as heaviness is the principle of downward movement of heavy bodies; hence it is related to the acts of human reason as a natural inclination to natural acts. But a natural thing is said to be fit for that to which it has an inclination. So, too, man, when he has the will to do good, is said to be fit for it. God, too, when he inserts a good will in a man, fits him, i.e., makes him fit. Therefore, he says, may God fit you in all goodness, that you may do his will, i.e., make you will every good: the desire of the just is every good (Prov 2:3). Haec est enim voluntas Dei, scilicet quod Deus vult nos velle. Aliter enim non est bona voluntas nostra. Voluntas autem Dei est bonum nostrum. I Thess. IV, 3: haec est voluntas Dei, sanctificatio vestra. Rom. XII, 2: ut probetis, quae sit voluntas Dei bona et beneplacens et perfecta. For this is God’s will, namely, what God wills us to will; otherwise, our will is not good. But the will of God is our good: this is the will of God, your sanctification (1 Thess 4:3); that you may prove what is the good and acceptable and the perfect will of God (Rom 12:2). 770. Dupliciter autem aptatur homo ad benefaciendum. Uno modo exterius operando, ut sic unus homo aptat alium persuadendo vel comminando. Alio modo aliquid interius exhibendo, et sic solus Deus aptat voluntatem, qui solus ipsam potest immutare. Prov. XXI, 1: cor regis in manu Domini, quocumque voluerit, inclinabit illud. 770. Now a man is made fit for doing well in two ways: In one way, by working outwardly; this is the way one man fits another, by persuading or threatening. In another way, by manifesting something inwardly; and this is the way that God alone fits a will, because he alone can change it: the heart of the king is in the hand of the Lord; whithersoever he will he will incline it (Prov 21:1). Unde dicitur faciens in vobis. Phil. II, 13: Deus est, qui operatur in nobis velle et perficere. Quid autem faciet? Quod placitum est coram se, id est faciet vos velle quod placet ei. Haec autem sunt fides et mansuetudo et timor Domini. Eccli. I, 34 s.: beneplacitum est ei fides et mansuetudo. Ps. CXLVI, 11: beneplacitum est Deo super timentes eum. Hence, he says, doing in you: it is God who works in us both to will and to accomplish (Phil 2:13). But what will he do? That which is well pleasing in his sight, i.e., he will make you will what pleases him. But this is faith and meekness and fear of the Lord: faith and meekness are agreeable to him (Sir 1:34); the Lord takes pleasure in those who fear him (Ps 147:11). Haec autem omnia habent per Christum. Nihil enim a Patre impetratur, nisi per Filium. Io. XVI, 23: si quid petieritis Patrem in nomine meo, dabit vobis. Et ideo dicit per Iesum Christum. II Petr. I, 4: per quem maxima et pretiosa nobis promissa donavit. Rom. V, 2: per quem accessum habemus, et cetera. Cui scilicet Christo, est gloria in saecula saeculorum, amen, id est, gloria sempiterna. I Tim. I, 17: regi saeculorum immortali, invisibili, soli Deo honor et gloria in saecula saeculorum, amen. Haec enim gloria sibi debetur inquantum est Deus. But all these are obtained through Jesus Christ, for nothing is obtained from the Father except through the Son: if you ask the Father anything in my name, he will give it to you (John 16:25); by whom he has given us most great and precious promises (2 Pet 1:4); by whom we have access by faith unto grace (Rom 5:2). To whom, namely, to Christ, is glory for ever and ever. Amen. That is, eternal glory: to the king of ages, immortal, invisible, the only God, be honor and glory forever and ever. Amen (1 Tim 1:17). For this glory is owed him, inasmuch as he is God. 771. Deinde cum dicit rogo autem vos, fratres, etc., subdit petitionem suam, in qua excusat se; deinde concludit epistolam, ibi gratia Dei. 771. Then when he says and I beseech you, brethren, he adds a petition in which he excuses himself; then he concludes the epistle, at grace be with you all. Circa primum tria facit quia In regard to the first he does three things: primo ponit excusationem suam; first, he gives his excuse; secundo recommendationem nuntii per quem scribit; second, he recommends the messenger through whom he writes; tertio ponit quasdam salutationes. third, he sends several greetings. 772. Dicit ergo quantum ad primum rogo vos, fratres, ut sufferatis verbum solatii, id est patienter portetis verba epistolae huius, in qua etsi in aliquo vos reprehendi, totum est ad consolationem vestram. Rom. c. XV, 4: quaecumque scripta sunt, ad nostram doctrinam scripta sunt. 772. He says, therefore, in regard to the first: and I beseech you, brethren, that you suffer this word of consolation, i.e., patiently bear with the words of this epistle, in which, even though I have rebuked some of you, it is all for your consolation: what things soever are written, are written for our consolation (Rom 15:4). Et quare debent patienter portare, ostendit, dicens quia perpaucis, id est valde paucis, scripsi vobis, quod verum est respectu mysteriorum, quae in ipsa continentur. In ista enim epistola fere omnia mysteria Veteris Testamenti continentur. Sermones autem breves valde accepti sunt, quia si sunt boni, inde avidius audiuntur, si vero mali, parum gravant. Eccle. V, 1: pauci sunt sermones tui. Then he shows why they should bear with it patiently, when he says, for I have written to you in a few words. This is true in regard to the mysteries contained in it; for almost all the mysteries of the Old Testament are contained in this epistle. But short talks are most welcome. For if they are good, they will be heard eagerly. If they are bad, they are a little boring: let your words be few (Eccl 5:1). 773. Consequenter recommendat illum, per quem scribit, dicens cognoscite fratrem nostrum Timotheum dimissum, scilicet a carcere, in quo cum Apostolo erat, vel a me dimissum ad praedicandum et ad vos missum. Cognoscite, id est gratiose recipite: tum quia, ut habetur Act. XVI, 3, fuerat circumcisus; tum etiam quia si celerius venerit, cum ipso videbo vos. In quo ostendit dilectionem quam ad ipsos habebat. Hoc etiam dicit quia etsi ad ipsos non venerit, quia Romae passus est, tamen incertus erat utrum vel ad tempus deberet dimitti. 773. Then he recommends the one through whom he writes, saying, know you that our brother Timothy is set at liberty, namely, from prison, where he was with the Apostle. Or, released by me to preach and come to you. Know you, that is, receive him kindly: both because he had been circumcised (Acts 16:3) and because, with whom (if he come shortly) I will see you. In this he shows the love he had for them. He also says this because, even though he did not visit them, since he was suffering in Rome, nevertheless he was uncertain whether he would be set at liberty for a while. 774. Consequenter ponit salutationes suas. Et primo iniungit eis salutationem aliorum, dicens salutate omnes praepositos, id est apostolos adhuc viventes, et omnes sanctos, scilicet alios discipulos. Istis autem non scribit, quia non intendebat scribere, nisi contra observantes legalia. Et ideo quia ista epistola est instructiva, non intendebat instruere apostolos, quia erant antecessores sui in fide. 774. Then he gives the greeting: first, he asks them to greet the others, saying, salute all your prelates, i.e., the apostles still living, and all the saints, namely, the other disciples. But he does not write to them, because his intention was to write only against the observances of the law. Therefore, because this epistle is instructive, it was not his intention to instruct the apostles, who preceded him in the faith. Secundo salutat eos ex parte aliorum, dicens salutant vos fratres de Italia. Scripsit enim epistolam istam a Roma. Second, he salutes them on the part of the others, saying, the brethren from Italy salute you. For he wrote the epistle from Rome. 775. Tertio, more solito concludens, et quasi pro sigillo, ponit salutationem, dicens gratia Dei cum omnibus vobis, amen, id est, peccatorum remissio, et omnia alia Dei dona, quae per gratiam Dei habentur, sint firmiter cum omnibus vobis. Amen, confirmatio est omnium. 775. Then he concludes in his accustomed manner, as though sealing it with a personal greeting: grace be with you all, i.e., the remission of sins and any other of God’s gifts, which are obtained through the grace of God, be firmly with all of you. The Amen is a confirmation of everything.
