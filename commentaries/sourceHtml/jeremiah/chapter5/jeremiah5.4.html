First, he shows their injustice with regard to their unjust and fraudulent oppression of the poor when he speaks of their fraud: lying in wait. He lies in wait that he may take the poor (Ps 10:29). Plenae dolo, divitiis quas dolo conquisierunt. Et doli effectum in multiplicatione divitiarum: ideo magnificati, in honoribus, ditati, in divitiis. Infra 17: perdix fovit quae non peperit, fecit divitias, sed non in judicio. Filled with treachery—riches that they have obtained by guile. And the purpose of guile by the multiplication of their riches: for this reason, they have grown great, in honors, and become rich, in wealth. As it says below: as the partridge has hatched what she did not lay, so he who has gotten riches, and not by judgment (Jer 17:11). Et in multiplicatione peccatorum, et praeterierunt. Deut. 32: incrassatus est dilectus et recalcitravit, incrassatus, impinguatus, dilatatus. Also, in the multiplication of their sins: they have transgressed. The beloved has grown thick and kicked, thickened and fattened, enlarged (Deut 32:15). Secundo ostendit eorum injustitiam quantum ad justi judicii omissionem, causam viduae. Isa. 1: pupillo non judicant, et causa viduae non ingreditur ad eos. Second, he shows their injustice as it pertains to their lack of just judgment: the cause of the widow. They do not judge for the orphan, and the cause of the widow does not come in before them (Isa 1:23). Hic arguit perversitatem doctrinae: 39. Here, he declares the perversity of their doctrine. et primo ponit admirabilem culpam: stupor, idest stupenda res, First, he discusses their astonishing guilt: wondrous, that is, something to be wondered at. prophetae prophetabant mendacium, quorum erat veritatem docere. Ezech. 22: prophetae ejus liniebant eos absque temperamento videntes vana, et divinantes eis mendacium. The prophets prophesy a lie, whose duty it was to teach the truth. Her prophets have daubed them without tempering the mortar, seeing false things, divining a lie for them (Ezek 22:28). Applaudebant, consentientes mendaciis, quorum erat prophetas corrigere. Infra 29: dedit te Dominus sacerdotem pro Jojada sacerdote, ut sis dux in domo Domini super omnem virum arreptitium et prophetantem ut mittas eum in nervum et carcerem. Et nunc quare non increpasti Jeremiam Anathothitem qui prophetat vobis? The priests clap with their hands, consenting to the lies—they whose duty it was to correct the prophets. As it is written below: the Lord has made you a priest in the place of Jehoiada the priest, that you may be the ruler in the house of the Lord over every madman and prophet, that you may put them in the stocks and in prison. And now, why have you not rebuked Jeremiah the Anatholite, who prophesies unto you? (Jer 29:26–27). Et populus meus. Eccli. 10: qualis rector civitatis, tales et inhabitantes in ea. And my people: as the ruler of a city, so the inhabitants thereof (Sir 10:2). Secundo innuit admirabilem poenam: quid igitur fiet in novissimo ejus? scilicet captivitatis, vel mortis. Isa. 27: in mensura contra mensuram cum abjecta fuerit, judicabit eam etc. Second, he intimates their astonishing punishment: what then shall be done in the end thereof? Certainly captivity or death. It is to be noted that poverty (Jer 5:4) can be understood in many senses: for example, of earthly possessions. Has not God chosen the poor of this world, rich in faith, and heirs of the kingdom which God has promised to those that love him? (Jas 2:5). Of humility: blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven (Matt 5:3). Of affliction: I am poor and afflicted; your salvation Lord, has sustained me (Ps 68:30). Of knowledge: you know not that you are poor (Rev 3:17). Of imperfection: I am a man seeing my own poverty in the rod of his indignation (Lam 3:1). Item notandum, quod multiplex est visitatio; scilicet consolationis. Lucae 1: visitavit nos oriens ex alto. Correptionis. Psal. 88: visitabo in virga iniquitates eorum. Damnationis. Infra 8: in tempore visitationis suae corruent, dicit Dominus. Congregans congregabo eos, ait Dominus. It is also to be noted that visitation (Jer 5:9) has several senses; for example, it is used of consolation. The one rising from on high has visited us (Luke 1:78). Of correction: I will visit your iniquities with the rod (Ps 88:33). Of damnation, as it says below, in the time of their visitation they shall fall down, says the Lord. I shall surely gather them, says the Lord (Jer 8:12–13). Item notandum, quod verbum Dei dicitur ignis, quia illuminat. Psal. 118: lucerna pedibus meis verbum tuum. Quia inflammat. Psal. 104: eloquium Domini inflammavit eum. Quia infirma penetrat. Infra 20: et factus est sermo Domini in corde meo quasi ignis aestuans, claususque in ossibus meis. Hebr. 4: vivus est sermo Dei, et efficax, et penetrabilior omni gladio ancipiti, et pertingens usque ad divisionem animae ac spiritus, compagum quoque ac medullarum, et discretor cogitationum et intentionum cordis. Quia liquefacit. Psal. 147: emittet verbum suum, et liquefaciet ea; flabit spiritus ejus, et fluent aquae. Quia consumit inobedientes. Isa. 30: labia ejus repleta sunt indignatione, et lingua ejus quasi ignis devorans. Joan. 15: si non venissem, et locutus non fuissem eis, peccatum non haberent. Nunc autem excusationem non habent de peccato suo. It is also to be noted that the word of God is called fire (Jer 5:14), because it illuminates: your word is a lamp to my feet (Ps 118:105); because it inflames: the word of the Lord has inflamed him (Ps 104:19); because it penetrates the weak, as it says below: and the word of the Lord became like a fire burning in my heart, shut up in my bones (Jer 20:9). The word of God is living, and effective—sharper than any two-edged sword, and penetrating to the division of soul and spirit, of joint also and marrow, and discerning the thoughts and intentions of the heart (Heb 4:12). Because it melts: he will send out his word, and it will melt them; he will blow forth his spirit and the waters will flow (Ps 147:7). Because it consumes the disobedient: his lips are full of indignation, and his tongue is as a devouring fire (Isa 30:27). If I had not come and spoken to them they would have no sin. But now, they have no excuse for their sin (John 15:22). Fixerunt in ea tentoria in circuitu, et pascet unusquisque eos qui sub manu sua sunt. 6:3 Shepherds and their flocks shall come to her; they have pitched their tents against her; each one shall pasture those which are under his hand. 40. Hic excludit secundum remedium in quo confidere poterant, scilicet in fortitudine urbis regiae, cujus destructionem prophetat in hoc capite: et circa hoc facit duo. 40. Here, he excludes the second remedy on which they might have relied, namely the strength of the royal city, whose destruction he prophesies in this chapter; concerning this, he makes two points. Primo ponitur civium praeparatio, scilicet ad resistendum, confortamini, ut designet quomodo se invicem, advenientibus hostibus, exhortabuntur. Vel irrisorie dicit. Fili Beniamin, quia Jerusalem in sorte illius tribus erat quantum ad superiorem partem; sed quantum ad inferiorem, in tribu Juda. Josue 15: ascenditque per convallem filii Ennon ex latere Jebusaei ad meridiem: haec est Jerusalem. Et in Thecua clangite buccina, et super Bethatarem, loca munita prope Jerusalem. Supra 4: levate signum in Sion. First, he speaks of the preparation of the citizens to resist—be strengthened—to indicate how they would exhort one another while the enemy was approaching. Alternatively, he says it ironically. Sons of Benjamin, because the upper part of Jerusalem was in the lot of that tribe, its lower part in that of Judah. And it goes up through the valley of the sons of Hinnom, from the side of Jebus to the south; this is Jerusalem (Josh 15:8). And sound the trumpet in Tekoa, and raise the banner over Beth Accarem, fortified places near Jerusalem. As it says above, raise a sign in Zion (Jer 4:6). Secundo ponit ipsius civitatis destructionem, ibi, speciosae et delicatae assimilavi filiam Sion. Second, he speaks of the destruction of the city itself, at I have likened the daughter of Zion to a beautiful and charming woman. Et primo ponitur conditio hostium vastantium, sub quibusdam metaphoris; First, he speaks of the nature of the destroying enemy with a number of metaphors; secundo describuntur aperte et expresse, ibi, haec dicit Dominus. second, they are described openly and expressly, at thus says the Lord (Jer 6:9).
