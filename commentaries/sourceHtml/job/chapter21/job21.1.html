      Caput 21 Chapter 21 Responsio Secunda Iob ad Sophar The Second Answer of Job to Sophar Lectio 1 Lecture 1 Cur mali vivunt? Why do the wicked live? 21:1 Respondens autem Iob dixit: 21:1 Then Job answered, and said: 21:2 Audite, quaeso, sermones meos et agetis paenitentiam; 21:2 Hear, I beseech you, my words, and do penance. 21:3 sustinete me ut et ego loquar, et post mea, si videbitur, verba ridete. 21:3 Suffer me, and I will speak, and after, if you please, laugh at my words. 21:4 Numquid contra hominem disputatio mea est ut merito non debeam contristari? 21:4 Is my debate against man, that I should not have just reason to be troubled? 21:5 Attendite me et obstupescite et superponite digitum ori vestro. 21:5 Hearken to me and be astonished, and lay your finger on your mouth. 21:6 Et ego quando recordatus fuero perstupesco, et concutit carnem meam tremor. 21:6 As for me, when I remember, I am afraid, and trembling taketh hold of my flesh. 21:7 Quare ergo impii vivunt, sublevati sunt confortatique divitiis? 21:7 Why then do the wicked live, are they advanced, and strengthened with riches? 21:8 Semen eorum permanet coram eis, propinquorum turba et nepotum in conspectu eorum. 21:8 Their seed continueth before them, a multitude of kinsmen, and of children’s children in their sight. 21:9 Domus eorum securae sunt et pacatae, et non est virga Dei super illos. 21:9 Their houses are secure and peaceable, and the rod of God is not upon them. 21:10 Bos eorum concepit et non abortivit, vacca peperit et non est privata fetu suo. 21:10 Their ox has conceived, and has not aborted: their heifer has calved, and is not deprived of her fruit. 21:11 Egrediuntur quasi greges parvuli eorum, et infantes eorum exultant lusibus. 21:11 They go out, their little ones like a flock, and their children dance and play. 21:12 Tenent tympanum et citharam, et gaudent ad sonitum organi. 21:12 They take the timbrel, and the harp, and rejoice at the sound of the organ. 21:13 Ducunt in bonis dies suos, et in puncto ad inferna descendunt. 21:13 They spend their days in wealth, and in a moment they go down to hell. 21:14 Qui dixerunt Deo: Recede a nobis, scientiam viarum tuarum nolumus; 21:14 Who have said to God: Depart from us, we desire not the knowledge of thy ways. 21:15 quid est Omnipotens ut serviamus ei, et quid nobis prodest si adoraverimus eum? 21:15 Who is the Almighty, that we should serve him? and what doth it profit us if we pray to him? 21:16 Verum tamen quia non sunt in manu eorum bona sua, consilium eorum longe sit a me. 21:16 Yet because their good things are not in their hand, may the counsel of the wicked be far from me. 21:17 Quotiens lucerna impiorum extinguetur et superveniet eis inundatio et dolores dividet furoris sui, 21:17 As often as the lamp of the wicked is put out, and there comes over them a deluge, and he divides the pains of his wrath, 21:18 erunt sicut paleae ante faciem venti et sicut favilla quam turbo dispergit. 21:18 they shall be as chaff before the face of the wind, and as ashes which the whirlwind scattereth. 21:19 Deus servabit filiis illius dolorem patris, et cum reddiderit, tunc sciet: 21:19 God shall lay up the sorrow of the father for his children: and when he shall repay, then shall he know. 21:20 videbunt oculi eius interfectionem suam, et de furore Omnipotentis bibet. 21:20 His eyes shall see their destruction, and he shall drink of the wrath of the Almighty. 21:21 Quid enim ad eum pertinet de domo sua post se, aut si numerus mensium eius dimidietur? 21:21 For what is it to him what befalleth his house after him, or if the number of his months be diminished by one half? 311. Respondens autem Iob dixit: audite, quaeso, etc. Quia Sophar in praemissis verbis quantum ad aliquid iam sententiae Iob consenserat ut poneret peccata puniri post mortem, quamvis adhuc in hoc suam sententiam retineret quod etiam in vita ista peccata temporaliter puniuntur, erectus fuit Iob in spem convertendi eos totaliter ad veram sententiam, et ideo primo humiliter inducit eos ad attentionem, cum dicit audite, quaeso, sermones meos. Et quia hactenus cum quadam irrisione eius verba audierant, subdit et agite paenitentiam, scilicet de hoc quod verba mea irrisistis vel quod veritati contradixistis; et quia iam omnes bis locuti estis, sustinete me ut et ego loquar, respondendo scilicet ad ea quae ultimo proposita sunt. Et ne antequam audirent iudicium condemnationis proferant, subdit et post mea, si videbitur, verba ridete, quasi dicat: si iudicatis meam hanc sententiam derisibilem esse; prius tamen meam responsionem audiatis, quae si vobis non satisfaciat postea poteritis iustius irridere. Et ne videantur eius verba despicienda fore, ostendit quod de magnis locuturus est, scilicet de divinis iudiciis non de humanis, unde subdit numquid contra hominem disputatio mea est ut merito non debeam contristari? Quasi dicat: si ad hoc tenderet meae disputationis intentio ut inquirerem an aliquis homo iuste vel iniuste me afflixerit, qualitercumque hoc accidisset, merito tristitia carerem; 311. Then Job answered and said: Hear, I beseech you. Since Sophar, in his previous words, had already somewhat agreed to Job’s judgment that sins are punished after death, although he still retained his own opinion on this matter that sins are also punished temporarily in this life, Job is elated in the hope of converting them completely to the true judgment. Therefore, he first humbly calls them to attention, when he says, hear, I beseech you, my words. And because up to this point they had listened to his words with a kind of derision, he adds, and do penance, namely, for you have derided my words or you have contradicted the truth. And because you all have already spoken twice, suffer me, and I will speak, namely, by responding to that which was proposed last. And lest they give a judgment of condemnation before they hear him, he adds, and after, if you please, laugh at my words, as if to say, “If you judge my opinion to be laughable, nevertheless first hear my response; if it proves unsatisfactory to you, then afterwards you can deride it more justly.” And lest it seem that his words will necessarily be despised, he shows that he is about to speak of a great matter, namely, of divine and not human judgments; and so he adds, is my debate against man, that I should not have just reason to be troubled? This is as if to say, “If the intention of my debate aimed at an inquiry into whether some man has afflicted me justly or unjustly, in whatever manner this had happened, I would deservedly not be troubled.” sed disputatio mea tendit ad inquirendum quomodo hoc iusto Dei iudicio acciderit. Et quia de magna re est ista disputatio, est audienda attente, unde dicit attendite me; est etiam audienda non leviter et cum irrisione sed magis graviter et cum stupore, unde subdit et obstupescite; est etiam audienda silenter et sine mussitatione, unde subdit et superponite digitum ori vestro. Et ne videatur hoc iactanter dicere quasi ad deferendum honorem suae auctoritati, ostendit se etiam in altitudine huius quaestionis stupere, unde subdit et ego quando recordatus fuero perstupesco, ne scilicet in tanta quaestione in aliquo a veritate deficiam aut irreverenter de divinis iudiciis loquar; timor autem iste non sistit in mente sed pervenit usque ad carnem, unde subdit et concutit carnem meam tremor: ex vehementi enim animi passione etiam caro immutatur. But my dispute pertains to the inquiry of how this happens according to the just judgment of God. Because this dispute is about such a great thing, they ought to hear attentively, and so he says, hearken to me. It also should not be heeded lightly and with derision, but with great seriousness and awe, and so he adds, and be astonished. Also, it should be heard in silence without muttering, hence he adds, and lay your finger on your mouth. Lest it seem that he is speaking boastfully, as if to bring honor to his authority, he shows that he also is in awe at the depth of this question; hence he adds, even I, when I remember, am stricken with awe, lest, namely, in such a question I may fall short in any way from the truth, or speak irreverently of divine judgments. This fear, however, does not stop in the mind, but pierces even unto the flesh; hence he adds, and trembling taketh hold on my flesh: for the flesh is also affected by a vehement passion of the spirit. 312. Quibus praemissis sufficientibus ad attentionem excitandam, procedit ad quaestionem; et quia Sophar dixerat quod prosperitates malorum, si quae sunt, brevi finiuntur et in malum eis vertuntur, ideo haec statim Iob improbat dicens quare ergo impii vivunt, scilicet diu? Quasi dicat: si impius sic, velut avis avolans et velut visio nocturna, cito transit, unde est quod multi impii habent vitae diuturnitatem? Item si gaudium hypocritae est ad instar puncti et eius ascensus cito deicitur, quare sublevati sunt, idest in honoribus promoti? Item si divitias quas devoravit evomit, quare sunt confortati divitiis, idest quare eorum divitiae sunt eis confirmatae? Contra hoc etiam quod dixerat filii eius atterentur egestate, dicit semen eorum permanet coram eis, idest filii, eis videntibus, perseverant; et idem subiungit de aliis personis coniunctis dicens propinquorum turba et nepotum in conspectu eorum; per quod duplicem prosperitatem designat, quia nec eorum propinqui subtrahuntur per mortem, quod significat in hoc quod dicit permanet, nec elongantur per exilium vel aliquid huiusmodi, quod designat per hoc quod dicit coram eis et in conspectu eorum. 312. With these premises, sufficient to awake their attention, he proceeds to the question. Because Sophar had said that the prosperity of evil men, if it exists at all, will end in a short while and will be turned into evil for them, Job immediately rejects these propositions, saying, why then do the wicked live, namely, for a long time? As if to say, “If the impious man is thus quickly passing, as a bird flying away and as a vision in the night, then why is it that many impious have longevity of life?” Likewise, if the joy of the hypocrite is a speck (Job 20:5), and his rising is quickly thrown down, why are they advanced; that is, why are they promoted to offices? Likewise, if the riches which he devoured, he vomits forth (Job 20:15), why are they strengthened with riches, that is, why are their riches confirmed for them? Against what was also said, that his children are wasted by extreme poverty (Job 20:10), he says, their seed continueth before them; that is, their sons persevere in their sight. He adds the same point about other related persons, saying, a multitude of kinsmen, and of children’s children in their sight. By this he designates a double prosperity, because neither are their relatives taken away by death, which is signified where he says continueth, nor are they far separated by exile or anything of this manner, which he signifies where he says before them and in their sight. 313. Deinde prosequitur per partes praemissam prosperitatem impiorum, primo quidem quantum ad ipsos, praemittens immunitatem a malo cum dicit domus eorum, idest familiae eorum cum necessariis rebus, securae sunt, scilicet ab insultu hostium, et pacatae, idest carentes interna dissensione; et etiam immunes sunt a divino flagello, et quantum ad hoc subdit et non est virga Dei super illos, quia scilicet pro peccatis suis non corriguntur in hac vita. Et subiungit de multiplicatione bonorum, quia scilicet bona eorum non sunt sterilia nec fructu suo privantur, et hoc manifestat in specie boum qui apud antiquos acceptissimi erant propter agriculturam. Unde dicit bos eorum, idest vacca, concepit, per quod tollitur sterilitas, est autem conceptio primum in fructificatione animalium; secundum est formatio fetus concepti in ventre et perductio ad perfectum, quod impeditur per aborsum, et quantum ad hoc dicit et non abortivit; tertium autem est partus, et quantum ad hoc dicit vacca peperit, idem significans nomine bovis et vaccae, et utitur utroque vel propter ornatum sermonis vel propter necessitatem metri; quartum autem est educatio prolis, et quantum ad hoc subdit et non est privata fetu suo, morte scilicet praeoccupante. 313. Then he pursues in its parts the premised prosperity of the impious, first with respect to the impious men themselves, premising their immunity from evil where he says, their houses, that is, their family with their necessary things, are secure, namely, from the assault of enemies, and peaceable, that is, lacking internal dissension; and they are also free from the divine scourge, and with respect to this he adds, and the rod of God is not upon them, because they are not corrected for their sins in this life. And he adds a point about the multiplication of goods, since their goods are not sterile nor are they deprived of their fruit. And he manifests this in the species of oxen, which were most pleasing to the ancients because of agriculture. Hence, he says, their ox, that is, heifer, has conceived, by which he rules out sterility. For conception is first in the fertilization of animals; second is the formation of the fetus conceived in the womb and the formation to perfection, which is impeded by abortion. With respect to this he says, and has not aborted. But third is birth, and with respect to this he says, their heifer has calved, signifying the same thing with the names ox and heifer, and using both terms either for the purpose of adorning his speech or because of the necessity of the meter; but fourth is the education of the young, and with respect to this he adds and is not deprived of her fruit, namely, by death taking it beforehand. 314. Consequenter autem subdit de prosperitate quantum ad filios, circa quod primo ponit prolis multitudinem cum dicit egrediuntur, scilicet in plateis ambulantes, non praeventi morte, quasi greges parvuli eorum, scilicet in multitudine et mutua concordia; 314. But following this he adds a treatment about prosperity with respect to sons, about which he first posits a multitude of offspring when he says they go out, namely, walking in the streets, not prevented by death, their little ones like a flock, namely, in a multitude and mutual concord. secundo ponit eorum incolumitatem cum subdit et infantes eorum exultant lusibus, quasi non gemunt infirmitatibus; Second, he posits their security when he adds, and their children dance and play, as if not groaning with infirmities. tertio ponit eorum disciplinam qua apud antiquos liberi instruebantur in musicis, unde dicit tenent tympanum et citharam, et gaudent ad sonitum organi, quasi instructi ad hoc quod et ipsi convenienter musica utantur et alios audiendo prudenter iudicent. Third, he posits their learning, in which, among the ancients, free men were instructed in music; hence, he says, they take the timbrel, and the harp, and rejoice at the sound of the organ, as if instructed both to practice music fittingly themselves and, hearing others, to judge them prudently. 315. Sed ne aliquis dicat quod haec eorum prosperitas modicum durat ad instar puncti, contra hoc subdit ducunt in bonis dies suos, quasi dicat: omnes dies vitae suae in prosperitate peragunt. Necesse est autem ex communi condicione quod finaliter sentiant mortem, sed tamen hanc patiuntur absque praecedenti angustia, unde subdit et in puncto ad inferna descendunt, idest in mortem: omnes enim antiqui ante redemptoris adventum, de quo supra locutus fuerat, ad Inferna descendebant; sed quidam adversitatibus pressi in vita, non subito sed per multas amaritudines ad Inferna descendebant, sicut Iacob dixit Gen. XXXVII 35 descendam ad filium meum lugens in Infernum; sed illi qui prosperitate vigent usque ad mortem quasi in puncto usque ad Inferna descendunt. 315. But lest anyone should say that this prosperity of theirs lasts but a short time, just a speck (Job 20:5), against this he adds, they spend their days in wealth, as if to say, “All the days of their life are spent in prosperity.” However, because of their common condition, it is necessary that they finally feel death, but nevertheless they suffer without preceding distress; hence, he adds, and in a moment they go down to hell, that is, into death. For all the ancients before the coming of the Redeemer, about which he had spoken above, descended into hell, as Jacob said: grieving will I descend to my son in hell (Gen 37:35); but those who thrive in prosperity until death descend into hell as if in an instant. 316. Posset autem responderi quod impii inter multa mala quae faciunt, meruerunt a Deo prosperitatem terrenam vel eum amando vel cognoscendo vel ei qualibuscumque operibus serviendo vel saltem bona temporalia ab ipso petendo, sed hoc excludit subdens qui dixerunt Deo, scilicet ex corde proposito quasi ex certa malitia peccantes: recede a nobis, quod pertinet ad defectum amoris, scientiam viarum tuarum nolumus, quod pertinet ad defectum cognitionis per ignorantiam affectatam: viae autem Dei dicuntur praecepta et iudicia eius quibus ab ipso disponimur; quis est omnipotens ut serviamus ei? Quod pertinet ad defectum bonorum operum provenientem ex Dei contemptu, et quid nobis prodest si adoraverimus eum? Quod pertinet ad contemptum petitionis propter defectum spei. 316. But one could respond that the impious, among the many evil deeds which they commit, also merit from God an earthly prosperity either by loving him, or knowing him, or serving him in whatever works; or at least by seeking temporal goods from him. But this Job excludes, saying, who have said to God, namely, sinning from a determined heart as if from a confirmed malice, depart from us, which pertains to a defect of love; we desire not the knowledge of thy ways, which pertains to a defect of knowledge through affected ignorance: for the precepts and judgments of God by which we are disposed by him are called his ways; who is the Almighty, that we should serve him? pertains to a defect of good works which comes out of a contempt for God; and what doth it profit us if we pray to him? pertains to a contempt for prayer because of a defect in hope. 317. Sic ergo evidentissime confutavit eorum sententiam, ostendens quod prosperitas temporalis non semper est praemium virtutis nec adversitas temporalis est poena peccati, quia impii frequenter prosperantur in hac vita nihil boni merentes a Deo, et cum hoc nullas graves adversitates patiuntur. Posset autem aliquis obicere: si malis prospera eveniunt et adversitatibus carent, ergo nulla est ratio a malitia declinandi, unde Eccl. IX 3 dicitur eadem cunctis eveniunt, unde et corda filiorum hominum implentur malitia; sed huic obiectioni respondet dicens verum tamen quia non sunt in manu eorum bona sua, consilium eorum longe sit a me. Circa quod sciendum est quod in manu hominis, idest in potestate ipsius, sunt quaedam bona, scilicet voluntaria virtutum opera quorum est dominus per liberum arbitrium gratia Dei iuvatum, et ideo huiusmodi bona semper possunt retinere virtuosi quandiu volunt, et propter hoc consilium sectandi huiusmodi bona est acceptandum; sed bona temporalis prosperitatis non sunt in potestate habentium ut possint ea acquirere vel retinere cum volunt, et ideo abiciendum est eorum consilium quo Deum et iustitiam contemnunt ut prospere vivant, quia per hoc non possunt assequi quod intendunt sed quandoque adversitatibus premuntur. 317. Thus, therefore, Job has most evidently refuted their judgment, showing that temporal prosperity is not always the reward of virtue, and neither is temporal adversity the punishment for sin, because the impious frequently prosper in this life, meriting nothing good from God, and along with this they suffer no serious adversities. But someone could object: if the wicked enjoy prosperity and lack adversities, then there is no reason to turn away from wickedness; hence, Ecclesiastes says, the same fortunes befall all; hence the hearts of the sons of men are filled with malice (Eccl 9:3). But to this objection Job responds, saying, yet because their good things are not in their hand, may the counsel of the wicked be far from me. And about this point, one should know that in the hand of man, that is, in his power, there are certain goods, namely, voluntary works of virtue, of which he is master through free will aided by the grace of God. And therefore virtuous men are always able to retain such goods for however long they wish, and because of this the plan to pursue such goods must be accepted. But the temporal goods of prosperity are not in the power of those who have them, so that they can acquire or retain them when they wish, and therefore their plan which despises God and justice so that they may live prosperously must be rejected, because by this they are not able to gain what they intend, but are sometimes pressed with adversities. 318. Est autem considerandum ulterius quod adversitas impii gravior est quam iusti, quia cum iustus adversitatem patitur temporalem remanet ei fulcimentum virtutis et consolatio in Deo, unde non totaliter dissipatur; sed malis, amissis temporalibus bonis quae sola sibi quaesierunt, nullum remanet fulcimentum: unde subditur quotiens lucerna, idest prosperitas, impiorum extinguitur, idest finitur, et supervenit eis, scilicet divino iudicio, inundatio, idest gravis tempestas adversitatis, et quotiens Deus dividit, idest sub certa mensura distribuit, dolores, idest afflictiones aliquas, furoris sui, idest ex eius furore provenientes, erunt, impii scilicet, sicut paleae ante faciem venti, quae scilicet propter levitatem resistere non possunt vento, et sicut favilla, quae scilicet remanet ligno combusto, quam turbo dispergit, quia scilicet non habet humorem ut simul se teneat: ita etiam impii, veniente adversitate, resistere non possunt quia carent fulcimento divinae spei, et per diversas cogitationes disperguntur carentes humore virtutis. 318. But it should be considered further that the adversity of an impious man is graver than that of the just, because when the just suffers temporal adversity, there remains for him the support of virtue and consolation in God; hence, he is not completely dissipated. But for wicked men, with the loss of temporal goods which they alone sought for themselves, there remains no support: hence he adds, as often as the lamp, that is, the prosperity, of the wicked is put out, that is, comes to an end, and there comes over them, namely, by divine judgment, a deluge, that is, a grave storm of adversity, and, as often as God divides, that is, distributes according to a certain measure, the pains, that is, any afflictions, of his wrath, that is, coming forth from his fury, they shall be, namely, the impious shall be, as chaff before the face of the wind, which, because of its lightness, is not able to resist the wind, and as ashes, namely, which remain when wood is burnt, which the whirlwind scattereth, because, namely, it does not have moisture to hold itself together. Thus, the impious, with the coming of adversity, are not able to resist, because they lack the support of divine hope, and they are scattered by diverse thoughts, lacking the moisture of virtue.
