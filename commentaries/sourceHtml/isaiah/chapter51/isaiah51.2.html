      Primo ponit exemplum: attendite ad petram, scilicet Abraham, propter ejus senectutem, excisi, quasi violenter, non secundum cursum naturae; cavernam, scilicet Saram, cui desierant fieri muliebria, Gen. XVIII 11, Ro. X non consideravit corpus suum, cum esset iam emortuum, in quo ostenditur quod Deus potens est fecundare; quia unum vocavi, Gen. XII 1, Ez. XXXIV unus erat etc. First, he gives an example: look unto the rock, namely, Abraham, because of his old age, whence you are hewn, as though violently, not according to the course of nature; and to the hole of the pit, namely, Sarah, to whom it had ceased to be after the manner of women (Gen 18:11): neither did he consider his own body, when it was already dead (Rom 4:19), in which is shown that God has power to make fertile; for I called him alone (cf. Gen 12:1): Abraham was one (Ezek 33:24). 924. Et concludit divinae consolationis beneficium: consolabitur, quantum ad reaedificationem civitatum, omnes ruinas, Ez. XIV 22 consolamini super malo quod induxi in Jerusalem in omnibus; quantum ad culturam agrorum: et ponet desertum, id est terram vestram quae prius erat deserta, Ier. XXXI 12 erit anima eorum; et quantum ad laetitiam hominum: gaudium, supra XXX 29 canticum erit vobis. 924. [Second,] he deduces the benefit of divine comfort: the Lord therefore will comfort, as to the rebuilding of cities, all the ruins: you are comforted concerning the evil that I have brought upon Jerusalem, in all things (Ezek 14:22); as to the cultivation of fields: and he will make her desert, that is, your land, which formerly was desert: their soul shall be as a watered garden (Jer 31:12); and as to the gladness of men: joy, above: you shall have a song (Isa 30:29). 925. Tertio <ponit> executionis modum: attendite ad me. 925. Third, he sets out the manner of execution: hearken unto me. Et primo ponit divinam intentionem: lex, praeceptum Cyri de liberatione populi, judicium, ex justitia Domini promulgata, in lucem, consolationem, supra IX 2 habitantibus in regione umbrae mortis lux orta est eis. And first, he sets out the divine intention: a law, the precept of Cyrus concerning the liberation of the people; my judgment, promulgated from the justice of the Lord; to be a light, comfort, above: to them that dwelt in the region of the shadow of death, light is risen (Isa 9:2). Secundo ponit judicii propinquitatem: prope est justus, scilicet Cyrus vel Christus, egressus, jam in praescientia mea, supra XLVI 13 prope feci justitiam. Second, he sets out the nearness of the judgment: my just one is near at hand, namely, Cyrus, or Christ; my savior is gone forth, already in my foreknowledge, above: I have brought my justice near (Isa 46:13). Tertio ponit judicii utilitatem: brachia, potentes, mea virtute, Cyrus et Darius; insulae, diversae gentes in me sperabunt, videntes populi liberationem, supra XLII 4 ponet in terra judicium. Third, he sets out the usefulness of the judgment: arms, powerful men, by my power, Cyrus and Darius; the islands shall look for me, various peoples will hope in me, seeing the liberation of the people, above: he will set judgment in the earth (Isa 42:4). 926. Levate in caelum. Hic ostendit divinae promissionis firmitatem per comparationem ad creaturarum mutationem. 926. Lift up your eyes to heaven. Here he shows the firmness of the divine promise by comparison to the changing of creatures. Unde primo ponit creaturae mutabilitatem: liquescent, quantum ad figuram et quantum ad substantiam, I Cor. VII; et similiter dicendum de aliis, Ps. initio tu, Domine, terram fundasti, et opera manuum tuarum sunt caeli. Hence he first sets out the changeableness of creation: the earth shall vanish, as to shape and as to substance (1 Cor 7:31); and the same thing is to be said concerning the others: in the beginning, O Lord, you founded the earth: and the heavens are the works of your hands (Ps 101:26[102:25]). Secundo divinae promissionis firmitatem: salus, quam promitto, vel quia hoc ex me numquam deerit quin sim paratus salvare et justitiam facere, Lucae XXI 33 caelum et terra transibunt, verba autem mea non transient. Second, the firmness of the divine promise: but my salvation shall be for ever, because I promise it, or because I will never lack, so as to not be prepared to save and do justice: heaven and earth shall pass away: but my words shall not pass away (Luke 21:33). 927. Audite me. Hic excludit impedimentum quod timeri poterat ex oppressione, quantum pertinet ad potestatem opprimentium. Et circa hoc duo facit: 927. Hearken to me. Here he excludes the impediment which could be feared from oppression, so far as pertains to the power of their oppressors. And concerning this, he does two things: primo ponitur Domini confortatio, first, the consolation of the Lord is set out; secundo prophetae disceptatio: consurge. second, the discussion of the prophet: arise (Isa 51:9). Circa primum quatuor. Concerning the first, he does four things. Primo excitat attentionem: qui scitis justum, id est justitiam quam in judiciis meis vobis revelavi, Ps. non fecit taliter omni nationi, et judicia sua non manifestavit eis; in corde, in cognitione, Prov. IV 21 ne recedant ab oculis tuis, custodi ea in medio cordis tui. First, he stirs up attention: you that know what is just, that is, the justice which I have revealed in my judgments: he has not done in like manner to every nation: and his judgments he has not made manifest to them (Ps 147:20); in your heart, in your understanding: let them not depart from your eyes, keep them in the midst of your heart (Prov 4:21). Secundo excludit timorem: nolite timere; hominum, qui vos contemptui habent, de sua potentia gloriantes, Ps. ego sum vermis et non homo, opprobrium hominum et abjectio plebis. Second, he excludes fear: fear not the reproach of men, who hold you in contempt, glorying in their own power: but I am a worm, and no man: the reproach of men, and the outcast of the people (Ps 21:7[22:6]). Tertio ostendit eorum fragilitatem: sicut enim vestimentum, Job XIII 28 qui quasi vestimentum quod comeditur a tinea. Third, he shows their fragility: for the worm shall eat them up as a garment: who am as a garment that is moth-eaten (Job 13:28). Quarto divini auxilii firmitatem: salus autem, supra XLV 17 Israel salvabitur in Domino. Fourth, the firmness of divine help: but my salvation shall be for ever, above: Israel is saved in the Lord with an eternal salvation (Isa 45:17). 928. Nota super illo verbo ponet desertum ejus quasi delicias, quod sancti habent duplices delicias. 928. Note on the words, he will make her desert as a place of pleasure (Isa 51:3), that the saints have twofold pleasures. Primas gloriae, quae consistunt First, those of glory, which consist primo in plena Dei visione, Job XXII 26 tunc super Omnipotentem deliciis afflues; first, in the full vision of God: then shall you abound in delights in the Almighty (Job 22:66); secundo in perfecta dilectione; second, in perfect love; tertio in copiosa refectione; third, in abundant refreshment; quarto in eminenti auctoritate. fourth, in eminent authority. Secundas gratiae, quae consistunt Second, those of grace, which consist primo in perceptione divini luminis, Ps. nox illuminatio mea in deliciis meis; first, in the perception of divine light: night shall be my light in my pleasures (Ps 138:11); secundo in proposito vel solicitudine boni operis, Eccl. V 19 non recordabor dierum; second, in the intention or the solicitude for good works: he shall not much remember the days of his life (Eccl 5:19); tertio in ornatu virtutum, Cant. VII 6 quam pulchra es et quam decora in deliciis; third, in the adornment of the virtues: how beautiful are you, and how comely, my dearest, in delights (Song 7:6); quarto in quiete cordis, infra LVIII 13 vocaberis sabbatum delicatum. fourth, in peace of heart, below: you will call the sabbath delightful (Isa 58:13). 929. Consurge. Hic proponitur prophetae ad Deum disceptatio, quasi: non contenderim quod hostes populi aliter quam divina fortitudine confundantur vel consumantur. 929. Arise. Here the discussion of the prophet with God is set out, as if to say: I have not contended that the enemies of the people are confounded or consumed otherwise than by divine strength. Unde primo ponitur prophetae petitio, Thus, first, the petition of the prophet is set out; secundo Domini responsio: ego, ego. second, the response of the Lord: I myself (Isa 51:12). 930. Circa primum duo. 930. Concerning the first, he does two things. Primo ponit suam petitionem, in qua invocat divinae potentiae brachium: et brachium Domini, id est virtus Dei; consurge, quasi a somno, Ps. exurge, quare obdormis Domine; induere, sicut miles arma bellica ad hostes nostros impugnandum, Ps. induit Dominus fortitudinem, et praecinxit se virtute, Job XXXIX si habes brachium sicut Deus. Allegat etiam divinae fortitudinis signum in occisione Pharaonis: numquid non tu percussisti superbum, draconem, Pharaonem, de quo Ez. XXIX 3 draco magne etc.; et in divisione maris: numquid etc., Iob XXXVI 22 in fortitudine ejus etc. First, he sets out his petition, in which he calls upon the arm of divine power: O you arm of the Lord, that is, the power of God, arise, as if from sleep: arise, why sleep you, O Lord? (Ps 43[44]:23); put on strength, like soldiers put on arms of war, to fight our enemies: the Lord is clothed with strength, and has girded himself with power (Ps 92[93]:1); and have you an arm like God? (Job 40:4). He also alleges a sign of divine strength in the killing of Pharaoh: have not you struck the proud one, and wounded the dragon?, Pharaoh, about whom it says in Ezekiel 29:3: behold, I come against you, Pharaoh king of Egypt, you great dragon; and in the division of the sea: have not you dried up the sea: by his power the seas are suddenly gathered together (Job 26:12). 931. Secundo annuntiat populo suae petitionis exauditionem: nunc, sicut fuit in exitu de Aegypto, qui redempti de captivitate Babylonis, Baruch quia reducit eos Dominus etc. 931. Second, he announces to his people that the Lord has heard his petition: and now, as it was when they came forth from Egypt, they that are redeemed, from captivity in Babylon: for the Lord will lead them back (Bar 4:23). 932. Ego, ego. Hic ponitur Domini responsio ad populum colloquentis. 932. I myself. Here is set out the response of the Lord speaking to the people. Et primo promittit consolationem: consolabor, II Cor. I 3 benedictus Deus et Pater Domini nostri, et infra et Deus totius consolationis, qui consolatur nos in omni tribulatione nostra. And first, he promises comfort: I will comfort: blessed be the God and Father of our Lord, and below this, and the God of all comfort: who comforts us in all our tribulation (2 Cor 1:3–4).
