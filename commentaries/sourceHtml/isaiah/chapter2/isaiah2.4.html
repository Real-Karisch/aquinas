      primo praesumptionem de divina misericordia; first, presumption of divine mercy; secundo confidentiam de propria potentia, ibi oculi sublimes; second, confidence in their own power, where it says, the lofty eyes of man are humbled (Isa 2:11); tertio remedium quod relinquebatur in aliorum fiducia, ibi in die illa projiciet homo idola. third, the remedy which remained in confidence in other [gods], where it says, in that day a man shall cast away his idols of silver (Isa 2:20). Et quia divina misericordia mensuram non habet, sed semper superexaltat judicium, Iac. II 13, ideo non removet eam praedicendo ut defecturam, sed imprecando ut justam. Duo ergo facit. And because divine mercy has no measure, but always exalts itself above judgment (Jas 2:13), he does not remove it by foretelling that it will fail but by calling it down as just. Therefore he does two things. Primo ponit imprecationem dicens: ex quo tam turpia mala faciunt, ergo ne dimittas eis, quasi dicat: justum esset ut in nullo eorum miserereris, Jer. V 9 numquid super his non judicabo, dicit Dominus, et super gentem hujusmodi non ulciscetur anima mea? First, he places the calling down, saying: since they do such filthy evils, therefore forgive them not, as if to say: would that there were justice, that you might have mercy on them in nothing: shall I not visit for these things, says the Lord? And shall not my soul take revenge on such a nation? (Jer 5:9). 87. Secundo ironice ponit consilium ad evasionem, dicens ingredere, quasi dicat: ex quo ipse non dimittit, restat ut abscondatis vos a facie ejus. Ingredere in petram, ad litteram: in fissuras petrarum ad ibi manendum; abscondere fossa humo, id est in cavernis terrae, a facie timoris Domini, id est a facie illorum per quos Deus se probat timendum et glorificandum, Jer. XVIII relinquite civitates; sed hoc non valebit eis, unde in Ps. quo ibo a spiritu tuo, et quo a facie tua fugiam? 87. Second, he places, ironically, the counsel to escape, saying, enter you; as if to say, since he himself does not send you away, it remains that you should hide yourself from his face. Enter you into the rock, literally, into the fissures of the rocks to remain there; hide you in the pit, that is, in the caverns of the earth, from the face of the fear of the Lord, that is, from the face of those through whom God proves that he is to be feared and glorified: leave the cities (Jer 48:28); but this does not avail them, hence in Psalm 138[139]:7: whither shall I go from your spirit? Or whither shall I flee from your face? 88. Bernardus exponit de Christo. Petra enim erat Christus, I ad Cor. X 4; in ipso est intrandum per devotionis affectum. Dicit ergo ego fidenter quod mihi deest usurpo de visceribus Jesu Christi. Viscera enim misericordiae affluxerunt, nec desunt foramina per quae effluat: foderunt enim manus ejus, et pedes ejus perforaverunt; Cant. II 13 surge, propera, amica mea, et veni in foraminibus petrae. 88. Bernard explains this as referring to Christ. And the rock was Christ (1 Cor 10:4); one must enter him through the affection of devotion. Therefore, Bernard says: that which is lacking to me I usurp from the heart of Jesus Christ. For out of his heart mercies overflow, and there is no lack of fissures by which they flow out: for they dug holes in his hands, pierced his feet. Arise, make haste, my love, and come . . . in the clefts of the rock (Song 2:13–14). 89. Oculi sublimes. Hic removet ab eis confidentiam de propria potentia, et circa hoc duo facit: 89. The lofty eyes. Here he takes away from them confidence in their own power, and concerning this, he does two things: primo excludit potentiam resistendi; first, he excludes the power of resistance; secundo repellit cautelam latendi, ibi et introibunt. second, he rejects the precaution of hiding, where it says, and they shall go into the holes of rocks (Isa 2:19). Circa primum tria facit: Concerning the first, he does two things: primo ponit intentum; first, he sets out the intention; secundo explicat modum, ibi quia dies; second, he explains the manner, where it says, because the day of the Lord of hosts (Isa 2:12); tertio concludit propositum: et incurvabitur. third, he concludes the proposition: and the loftiness of men shall be bowed down (Isa 2:17). Ponit autem duo ad excludendam potentiam resistendi. He sets out two things to exclude the power of resistance. Primum, ipsorum depressionem tum quantum ad superbiam cordis, cum dicit oculi, id est superbia, sublimis, id est hominis potentis, humiliati, id est humiliabuntur: praeteritum pro futuro propter certitudinem prophetiae, Prov. XXX 13 generatio cujus oculi excelsi sunt et palpebrae in alta surrectae, Job XL 7 respiciet cunctos superbos, et confundet eos, et conteret impios de loco suo. Tum etiam quantum ad nobilitatem generis, unde dicit altitudo virorum, id est nobilitas generis, Amos II 9 ego exterminavi Amorrhaeum a facie eorum, cujus altitudo cedrorum altitudo ejus. First, their lowering, both as to pride of heart, when he says, the eyes, that is, pride, of the lofty, that is, of powerful men, are humbled, that is, they will be humbled: the past tense is used for the future because of the certitude of the prophecy: a generation, whose eyes are lofty, and their eyelids lifted up on high (Prov 30:13); he will look on all that are proud, and confound them, and crush the wicked in their place (Job 40:7). Then also as to nobility of ancestry; hence he says, the height of men, that is, their nobility of ancestry: yet I cast out the Amorrhite before their face: whose height was like the height of cedars (Amos 2:9). Secundo ponit punientis exaltationem: exaltabitur, id est altus apparebit in punitione qui infirmus videbatur in expectatione, Ps. cognoscetur Dominus judicia faciens, infra V 16 exaltabitur Dominus exercituum in judicio. Second, he places the exaltation of the one who punishes: the Lord alone shall be exalted, that is he who seemed weak in expectation, will appear high in punishing: the Lord shall be known when he executes judgments (Ps 9:17[16]); below: the Lord of hosts shall be exalted in judgment (Isa 5:16). 90. Quia dies Domini. Hic ponitur modus expletionis praedictorum. 90. Because the day of the Lord. Here is set out the manner of fulfillment of the things that have been predicted. Et primo quantum ad depressionem hominum; And first, as to the lowering of men; secundo quantum ad subtractionem rerum, ibi et super omnes. second, as to the subtraction of things, where it says, and upon all (Isa 2:13). Dicit ergo primo: ideo erit hoc quod dictum est, quia dies Domini, id est ultionis tempus, infra LXIII 4 dies ultionis in corde meo, annus retributionis meae venit; exercituum, ut non dubitetur de victoria ejus cui tanta subest obsequentium multitudo; veniet quasi grave pondus ad comprimendum super omnem superbum et excelsum, id est super eum qui de magis quae habet extollitur; et super omnem arrogantem, qui elevatur de eo quod non habet, Gregorius: arrogans est qui sibi tribuit quod non habet; Prov. VIII 13 arrogantiam et superbiam et viam pravam et os bilingue detestor. Et humiliabitur, Lucae XIV 11 qui se exaltat humiliabitur. Thus he says first: therefore this will be which was said, because the day of the Lord, that is, the time of vengeance, below: the day of vengeance is in my heart, the year of my redemption is come (Isa 63:4); of hosts, that there be no doubt about victory of him, under whom there is such a multitude of servants; he will come as heavy weight to compress, upon every one that is proud and high-minded, that is, upon him who is raised up more from the great things he has; and upon every one that is arrogant, who is elevated because of that which he does not have. The arrogant man is he who attributes to himself what he does not have (Gregory). I hate arrogance, and pride, and every wicked way, and a mouth with a double tongue (Prov 8:13). And he shall be humbled: he that exalts himself shall be humbled (Luke 14:11). 91. Et super omnes cedros. Hic ponit subtractionem rerum quae faciunt ad defensionem. 91. And upon all the tall and lofty cedars. Here he places the subtraction of things which make for defense. Et primo quantum ad ea quae pertinent ad praeparationem machinarum: vel propter altitudinem, et sic dicit super omnes cedros; vel fortitudinem, et sic dicit super omnem quercum, Zach. XI 1 aperi Libane portas tuas, ut comedat ignis cedros tuas, et infra ululate quercus Basan, quia succisus est saltus munitus. And first, as to things that pertain to preparing machines of war: either because of their height, and thus he says, upon all the cedars; or because of their strength, and thus he says, upon all the oaks: open your gates, O Libanus, and let fire devour your cedars (Zech 11:1); and below this: howl, you oaks of Basan, because the fenced forest is cut down (Zech 11:2). Secundo quantum ad ea quae pertinent ad munitionem locorum, aut a natura: et super omnes montes excelsos, Ez. VI 3 haec dicit Dominus Deus montibus et collibus, rupibus et vallibus; aut ab arte: et super omnem turrim excelsam, Soph. I 16–17 dies tubae et clangoris super civitates munitas et super angulos excelsos, et tribulabo homines. Second, as to things which pertain to the fortification of places, either from nature: and upon all the high mountains: thus says the Lord God to the mountains, and to the hills, and to the rocks, and the valleys (Ezek 6:3); or from art: and upon every high tower: a day of the trumpet and alarm against the fenced cities, and against the high bulwarks. And I will distress men (Zeph 1:16–17). Tertio quantum ad ea quae pertinent ad abundantiam victualium: et sic dicit super omnes naves, in quibus deferuntur, Tharsis, id est maris, infra XXIII 1 ululate naves maris, quia vastata est domus unde venire consueverant. Third, as to things which pertain to abundance of provisions, and thus he says: and upon all the ships, in which provisions are borne, of Tharsis, that is, of the sea, below: howl, you ships of the sea, for the house is destroyed, from whence they were wont to come (Isa 23:1). Et super omne quod visu pulchrum est: hoc pertinet ad hostium implacationem vel augumentationem pugnantium, Judith X 18 quis contemnat populum Hebraeorum, qui tam decoras habet mulieres? And upon all that is fair to behold: this pertains to the placating of enemies, or the increase of fighters: who can despise the people of the Hebrews, who have such beautiful women? (Jdt 10:18). 92. Et incurvabitur. Hic quasi concludit propositum; et est omnino idem quod prius dictum est, nisi quod additur de destructione idolorum, ut ostendatur ipse solus excelsus, cum dicit et idola, Ez. VI 6 confringentur et cessabunt idola vestra, et conterentur delubra vestra. 92. Shall be bowed down. Here it is as if he concludes the proposition; and it is entirely the same as what was said before, except that something is added about the destruction of idols, so that he alone may be shown to be the highest, when he says, and idols (Isa 2:18). Your idols shall be broken in pieces and shall be no more, and your temples shall be destroyed (Ezek 6:6). 93. Et introibunt in speluncas. Hic ponit cautela latendi; et est idem quod prius, nisi quod hic praedicitur quasi futurum, quia etiam potentiam dejiciendam praedicendo et non imprecando exposuerat, Ps. introibunt in inferiora terrae, tradentur in manus gladii, partes vulpium erunt. 93. And they shall go into the holes of rocks. Here he places the precaution of hiding; and it is the same as before, except that here it is foretold as in the future, because he had also explained by foretelling and not by calling down that their power was to be thrown down; they shall go into the lower parts of the earth: they shall be delivered into the hands of the sword, they shall be the portions of foxes (Ps 62:10–11[63:9–10]). 94. In die illa. Hic removet tertium, scilicet idolorum fiduciam, 94. In that day. Here he removes the third, namely confidence in idols. et primo removet auxilium; ut adoraret, Ez. VIII 10 et ingressus vidi, et ecce omnis similitudo reptilium, et animalium abominatio, et universa idola domus Israel depicta, infra XXXI 7 in die illa abjiciet vir idola argenti sui, et idola quae fecerunt manus ejus in peccatum. And first, he takes away their help; to adore. And I went in and saw, and behold every form of creeping things, and of living creatures, the abominations, and all the idols of the house of Israel, were painted (Ezek 8:10). Below: in that day a man shall cast away his idols of silver, and his idols of gold, which your hands have made for you to sin (Isa 31:7). Secundo replicat latendi subsidium, ibi et ingredietur, Apoc. VI 15 omnis servus et liber absconderunt se in speluncis et petris montium. Second, he returns to the protection of hiding, where it says, and he shall go into the clefts of rocks: every bondman and every freeman hid themselves in the dens and in the rocks of mountains (Rev 6:15). 95. Quiescite ergo. Hic ponit sanum consilium ad evadendum: ergo ex quo omnia ista non valebunt vobis, quiescite ab homine, id est cessate ab offensa ipsius, scilicet Christi, cujus spiritus in naribus ejus, id est qui spirat naribus et vivit sicut alii homines, quantum ad humanitatem; quia excelsus reputatus est ipse, quantum ad divinitatem, a sanctis et ab angelis, Ps. excelsus super omnes gentes Dominus, et super omnes caelos gloria ejus. 95. Cease therefore. Here he places sound counsel for escape; therefore since all these things do not avail you, cease therefore from the man, that is, cease from what is offensive to him, namely Christ, whose breath is in his nostrils, that is, who breathes with his nostrils and lives like other men as to his humanity; for he is reputed high, as to his divinity, by the saints and angels: the Lord is high above all nations; and his glory above all the heavens (Ps 112[113]:4). 96. Vel aliter: loquitur de Deo ad similitudinem hominis per figuram antropospatos: ab homine, id est Deo, cujus spiritus in naribus; loquitur ad similitudinem irati, qui propter multiplicationem spirituum exufflat per nares, unde dicit Homerus quod fortes austeram virtutem per singulas nares exufflabant; Ps. ab inspiratione spiritus irae tuae, infra XXX 27 ardens furor ejus, et gravis ad portandum, et iterum spiritus velut torrens inundans usque ad medium colli, ad perdendas gentes in nihilum. Et hoc refertur ad primum, in quo removit praesumptionem de misericordia Dei. Quia excelsus ipse, discretive, quasi solus, contra secundum. 96. Or otherwise: it speaks of God in likeness to man through an anthropopathic figure: from the man, that is, from God, whose breath is in his nostrils; this speaks in likeness to an angry man, who because of the multiplication of his breaths, breathes out through his nostrils; hence Homer says that strong men breathed out harsh power through each of their nostrils. At the blast of the breath of your wrath (Ps 17:16[18:15]). Below: his wrath burns, and is heavy to bear (Isa 30:27), and again: his breath as a torrent overflowing even to the midst of the neck, to destroy the nations unto nothing (Isa 30:28). And this refers to the first, in which he takes away presumption of the mercy of God. For he is reputed high, separately, as if alone, against the second. Caput 3 Chapter 3 Lectio 1 Lecture 1
