Dicit ergo: vere sum exaudiendus, quia petitio continet honestatem et pietatem pro meo filio Onesimo, de quo est praesens mea sollicitudo. Et acquirens filium tempore defectus, magis eum diligit, ut senex filios in senectute genitos. Gen. XXXVII, 3: Israel autem diligebat Ioseph super omnes filios suos, eo quod in senectute genuisset eum. Hunc autem genuit in vinculis. 16. Therefore he says: truly I must be heard, because the petition involves honorableness and kindness for my son, Onesimus, who is his present concern. And acquiring a son in default of time, he loves him more, as an old man loves sons born to him in his old age. Now Israel loved Joseph above all his sons, because he had him in his old age (Gen 37:3). This one, Onesimus, was begotten in bonds. 17. Secundo est mutatio in moribus. Si enim in peccato perseverasset, non fuisset dignus venia. 17. Second, there is the change in status. For if he had persevered in sin, he would not be worthy of leniency. Et nota quod minus dicit, et plus significat. Docet enim Tullius, quod quis factum suum debet attenuare quantum potest. Sic Apostolus leviter de culpa huius loquitur, dicens inutilis, id est, nocivus in subtrahendo res tuas, nunc autem, conversus a malo ad statum virtutis, utilis est ad servitium Dei et hominum. II Tim. II, 21: si quis ergo emundaverit se ab istis, erit vas in honorem sanctificatum. Prov. XXV, 4: aufer rubiginem de argento, et egredietur vas purissimum. Note that Paul says little and means much. For as Cicero taught, one ought to make little of one’s own deed as much as possible. Thus the Apostle speaks lightly of his offense, saying, who was at one time unprofitable, that is, harmful in taking away your possession, but now, converted from evil to the state of virtue, is profitable for the service of God and man. If anyone, therefore, has cleansed himself from these, he will be a vessel for honourable use (2 Tim 2:21). Take away the rust from silver, and there shall come forth a most pure vessel (Prov 25:4). 18. Deinde cum dicit tu autem illum, ponit petitionem suam. Et 18. Then when he says, and receive him, he makes his request. primo ponitur petitio; First, he makes it, secundo respondet quaestioni, ibi quem ego. then he answers a question, at whom I would have retained. 19. Dicit ergo tu ergo illum ut mea viscera suscipe. Et hoc ideo, quia vidi eum emendatum, cuius signum est, quia eum remisi tibi. 19. And so he says, and receive him as my own heart. And this because I have seen him changed, the sign of which is the fact that I have sent him back to you. Contra Deut. XXIII, 15: non trades servum domino, qui ad te confugerit, et cetera. On the contrary, you shall not deliver to his master the servant that has fled to you (Deut 23:15). Respondeo. Verum est quando dominus quaerit eum ad mortem; et ideo dicit tu autem, et cetera. Phil. I, 7: eo quod habeam vos in corde, et in vinculis meis et in defensione, et cetera. I reply that this is true when the master seeks him in order to put him to death. Therefore, he says, and receive him. I have the right to feel so about you, because I have you in my heart, all of you, alike in my chains (Phil 1:7). 20. Et respondet quaestioni, quia posset dicere: si est utilis tibi, quare non retines eum usque ad mortem? Ideo dicit, quae est causa mittendi eum. 20. And he responds to a question, because it might be said: if he is useful to you, why do you not keep him unto death? And he gives the reason for sending him back. Primo autem ostendit propositum retinendi eum; First, he considers why he might keep him; secundo quare destitit a proposito, ibi sine consilio autem tuo. second, why he rejects that idea, at but without your counsel. 21. Dicit ergo Philemoni, qui licet esset magnus, tamen Apostolo solitus erat ministrare. Matth. XX, 26: quicumque voluerit inter vos maior fieri, sit vester minister. Unde ex hac fiducia proposuit eum tenere, ut loco eius sibi ministraret. Unde dicit quem ego volueram mecum detinere, ut pro te, et cetera. Quod erat maxime necessarium, quando erat in vinculis propter Christum. Est enim providendum quando quis patitur pro domino suo. 21. Therefore, he says to Philemon, who, although he is a great man, is accustomed to minister to the Apostle: on the contrary, whoever wishes to become great among you shall be your servant (Matt 10:26). Out of this confidence he proposed to keep him, so that in place of Philemon he might minister to him. Hence, he says, whom I would have retained with me, that in your stead he might have ministered to me. This was something especially needed since he was in bonds for the sake of Christ, for one is provided for when he suffers for his master. 22. Ratio autem quare destitit est quia noluit uti re aliena, ignorante domino. Unde dicit sine consilio, etc., quasi dicat: si retinuissem eum, iam placeret tibi non valenti resistere et esset quaedam coactio. Sed sic nolui, imo magis volui quod voluntarie fieret. Ex. XXV, 2: ab omni homine qui offert ultroneus, accipietis eas, scilicet primitias. II Cor. IX, 7: non ex tristitia, aut ex necessitate, hilarem enim datorem diligit Deus. 22. The reason he rejected the idea was that he did not want to use another’s property without the owner’s knowledge. Hence, but without your counsel I would do nothing. As if he said: if I should keep him, it would please you who do not wish to resist, but it would be a kind of force. But I did not want that, indeed I wanted it to come about voluntarily. Of every man who offers of his own accord, you shall take them (Exod 25:2), that is, the first fruits. Let each one give according as he has determined in his heart, not grudgingly or from compulsion, for God loves a cheerful giver (2 Cor 9:7). 23. Deinde cum dicit forsitan enim, ponit rationem quare debet eum recipere benigne. Et 23. Then when he says, for perhaps, he gives the reason why he ought to receive him kindly. primo ex parte Dei; First, on the side of God, secundo ex parte ipsius Apostoli, ibi si ergo habes; second, on the side of the Apostle, at if therefore you count me as a partner; tertio ex parte ipsius Philemonis, ibi confidens. third, on the part of Philemon himself, at trusting in your obedience. 24. Ex parte Dei, quia saepe Dei providentia id quod videtur malum permittit fieri, ut exinde sequatur bonum, ut patet de Ioseph vendito, ut liberaret Aegyptum et familiam patris. Gen. XLV, 5: pro salute enim vestra misit me Deus ante vos. Et dicit forsitan, quia incomprehensibilia sunt Dei iudicia, Rom. XI, 33. Et dicit pro servo, id est, loco servi. Matth. XXIII, 8: omnes autem vos fratres estis. Et non solum tibi, sed mihi in comparatione ad Deum, licet sit filius ministerio. 24. On the side of God, because the providence of God often permits what is evil to come about, in order that good might follow from it, as is clear from the sale of Joseph, that he might free Egypt and the family of his father. For God sent me before you into Egypt for your preservation (Gen 45:5). He says, perhaps because the judgements of God are incomprehensible (Rom 11:33). And he says instead of a servant, that is, in place of a servant. For one is your master and all are your brothers (Matt 23:8). And not only to you, but to me in comparison to God, though he is a son to the ministry. Quanto magis tibi, et in carne et in Domino? Quod dupliciter potest exponi: uno modo, quod referatur ad primam originem divinae creationis, et sic est frater. Deut. c. XXXII, 6: nonne est ipse Pater tuus, qui possedit, et fecit, et creavit te? Mal. II, 10: numquid non Pater unus omnium nostrum, numquid non Deus unus creavit nos? Item in Deo per fidem. Vel magis erat in bonum Philemoni, quia affinis eius secundum carnem, quia secundum eam erat servus eius, quia hoc totum quod erat ei carnaliter, erat suum. Unde duplici ratione quis movetur ex caritate, scilicet ex amore secundum carnis originem, et ex amore spirituali. But how much more to you, both in the flesh and in the Lord? This can be expounded in two ways. First, as referring to the first origin of the divine creation, and thus he is a brother. Is not he your Father, that has possessed you, and made you, and created you? (Deut 32:6). Have we not all one Father? Did not one God create us? (Mal 2:10). Again, by trust in God. Or it might rather be for the good of Philemon, because he is close to him in the flesh, since that is how he is his slave, because whatever he is bodily belongs to Philemon. Hence one is moved by charity for two reasons, namely, by love which has its origin in the flesh, or by spiritual love. 25. Ex parte autem Apostoli 25. On the part of the Apostle, primo allegat suam amicitiam, sub cuius obtentu vult eum suscipi; he first declares his friendship, under which pretext he wants Philemon to take Onesimus back; secundo fideiubet pro eo de damno; second, he offers to pay any damages; tertio ostendit receptionis officium. third, he shows the function of receiving. Secundum, ibi si autem. Tertium, ibi itaque, frater. Second, at and if he has wronged. Third, at indeed, brother. 26. Dicit ergo si ergo habes me socium, suscipe illum. I Io. I, 7: si autem in luce ambulamus, sicut et ipse est in luce, societatem habemus ad invicem. Et dicit sicut et me, quia iunctus est mihi. Matth. X, v. 40: qui vos recipit, me recipit. 26. Therefore, he says, if therefore you count me as a partner, receive him. But if we walk in the light as he also is in the light, we have fellowship with one another (1 John 1:7). And he says, as myself, because he is linked with me. He who receives you, receives me (Matt 10:40). 27. Secundo autem obligat se pro eo satisfacturum pro damno. Dicit ergo si aliquid nocuit, scilicet dimittens servitium tuum, hoc mihi imputa, quasi dicat: ego satisfaciam. Gal. ult.: alter alterius onera portate. 27. Second, he offers to make good any injury to Philemon, saying, and if he has wronged you in anything or is in your debt, namely by leaving his service, charge that to me. As if to say, I will make satisfaction. Bear one another’s burdens (Gal 6:2). Et plus: quia primo promittit se soluturum; secundo ostendit eum esse debitorem ad hoc, non necessitatis sed voluntatis. Dicit ergo ego Paulus, quasi dicat: ut certus sis de restitutione, scripsi manu. Et hoc non est necessitas, quia tu debes mihi teipsum, quem erui a morte aeterna. Et talis debet se liberatori. Tob. IX, 2: si meipsum tradam tibi servum, non ero condignus providentiae tuae. And more, because first he offers to make it good; second, he shows that Philemon is in his debt, not of necessity but of will. Therefore he says, I Paul, as if to say, that you might be certain of restitution, have written it with my own hand. And this not out of necessity, because you also owe me your own self, because I snatched you from eternal death, and thus he should do this for his liberator. If I should give myself to be your servant, I should not make a worthy return for your care (Tob 9:2). 28. Et subiungit dicens itaque, frater, ego te fruar, quasi dicat: si vis me habere socium, suscipe eum, et ego ita fruar te, frater; id est, si feceris, implebis voluntatem meam gaudiis. Nam frui est uti fructu, et sicut est uti ad utile, sic frui ad fructum. Importat autem fructus dulcedinem. Cant. II, v. 3: et fructus eius dulcis gutturi meo. Item finem, quia ultimum de arbore est fructus. Et ideo proprie est habere aliquid ut delectabile et finale. Et inde est quod dicit Augustinus fruimur cognitis, in quibus voluntas delectatur propter dulcedinem. Item frui est alicui inhaerere propter se. Aliquando ergo accipiuntur frui et uti communiter, prout important delectationem absque contrario. Eccli. c. VIII, 10: fruere magnatis sine querela. 28. And he adds, indeed, brother. May I enjoy you, as if to say, if you want me for a partner, take him back, and I will so enjoy you, brother; that is, if you do it, you will fill my wishes with joy. For to enjoy is to make use of the fruit, and thus as to use is to the useful, so is to enjoy to the fruit. It implies the sweetness of the fruit: and his fruit was sweet to my palate (Song 2:3), as well as the end, because the ultimate produce of the tree is its fruit. Therefore, to enjoy, properly speaking, is to have something that is pleasant and final. Hence Augustine says that we enjoy thinking of things in which the will delights because of their sweetness. Again, to enjoy is to adhere to something for its own sake. Sometimes ‘enjoy’ and ‘use’ are taken commonly as implying enjoyment without the contrary. And to serve great men without blame (Sir 8:10). Dicit ergo: sic fruar, quia in nullo mihi contrarius es. Etsi in hoc mihi satisfacies, nihil erit in corde meo de te, quod contristet me, et sic delectabis me. Therefore he says, may I enjoy you, because you are opposed to me in nothing. And if in this you please me, there will be nothing in my heart concerning you that saddens me, and thus you will delight me. Si autem accipitur frui prout finale quid est, sic non est fruendum homine, sed solo Deo. Contra quod est illud Sap. cap. II, 6: fruamur bonis quae sunt, et utamur creatura tamquam in iuventute celeriter, et cetera. Unde iste addit in Domino, id est, fruar te in delectatione Dei, gaudens de bono divino in te, quia eius actus est dilectio, et fruitio effectus, scilicet caritatis. Ideo additur in hoc refice viscera mea. Reficitur enim homo spiritualiter, quando satisfit desideriis animae suae. Ac si dicat: imple desideria intima cordis mei. Et non in malis, sed in Christo, et ideo bona est impletio desiderii. But if we take enjoyment as something final, then one does not enjoy man, but God alone. Contrary to this seems to be: come therefore, and let us enjoy the good things that are present: and let us speedily use the creatures as in youth (Wis 2:6). Hence he adds, in the Lord, that is, may I, too, make use of you in the delight of God, rejoicing in the divine good that is in you, because his action is love and the enjoyment of its effect, namely, charity. That is why he adds, refresh my heart. A man is consoled spiritually when the desires of his heart are fulfilled. As if he said: fulfill the deepest desires of my heart. And not with respect to evil, but in the Lord, and thus the fulfilment of desire is good. 29. Deinde cum dicit confidens, sumitur ratio ex parte Philemonis, et commendatio obedientiae eius. Et primo ostendit quomodo confidit de obedientia eius; secundo iniungit ei aliud simile. 29. Then when he says trusting in your obedience, he provides a reason on the side of Philemon, and a commendation of his obedience. First he shows how he is confident in his obeying; second he adds to it something similar. Dicit ergo confidens, et cetera. II Cor. VII, 16: gaudeo, quod in omnibus confido in vobis. I Reg. XV, 22: melior est obedientia quam victimae. Sed plus caute scribit, quia homo magis exaudit aliquem, quando sperat iterum ipsum visurum, quam si desperat. Et ideo dicit simul et para mihi hospitium. Solitus enim erat cum esset Colossis hospitari in domo sua. Therefore he says, trusting in your obedience. I rejoice that in all things I can have confidence in you (2 Cor 7:16). For obedience is better than sacrifices (1 Sam 15:22). But he writes more cautiously because a man listens more closely to someone if he expects to see him again than if he despairs of this. Therefore he says, but furthermore, also prepare a lodging for me. For it was his custom when he was in Colossae to stay in his home. Chrysostomus: Iucundum verbum, ut homo pauper diviti mandet ultra tot terrarum spatia per epistolam praeparationem hospitii. Quid enim pro eo parandum erat, qui pane et vili pulmento contentus erat? Dicendum ergo, quod non propter hospitii praeparationem, sed ad insinuandum familiaritatem et dilectionem hoc dicit; et magis per hoc provocat eum ad obediendum. Haec ille. Non ergo hoc Apostolus dixit propter apparatum exteriorem, sed ad devotionem eius. Nam spero per orationes vestras, et cetera. Chrysostom states: these are delightful words, that a poor man should command a rich man by letter from across the expanse of the earth to prepare a lodging for him. What would have to be prepared for one content with bread and cheap victuals? It should be said that it was not for the sake of the preparation of lodging that he says this, but to insinuate familiarity and love; in this way he will be prompt to obey. The Apostle therefore does not say this on account of external trappings but out of his devotion. For I hope that through your prayers I shall be given unto you. Contra: numquam fuit eis redditus, sed Romae est mortuus, ergo spes eius defecit. Against this is the fact that he never returned to them but died in Rome, and therefore his hope was dashed.
